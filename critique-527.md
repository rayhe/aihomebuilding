### 7. 📊 Technical Accuracy — 8.5/10

**Solar orientation (passive solar design):**
- East-west long axis with south-facing primary glazing reducing HVAC loads 20–30%: ✅ Standard passive solar design principle, well-documented in DOE literature and Building Science Corporation publications.
- "Costs nothing during the design phase and costs a fortune to fix after the foundation is poured": ✅ Correct — orientation is fixed at site planning stage.

**HVAC duct routing:**
- "A central air system needs a path from the air handler to every room, which means soffits, chases, or attic runs": ✅ Standard residential HVAC distribution paths.
- "Impossible duct routes without visible bulkheads": ✅ Common issue in residential plan review — floor plans that look open on paper often require soffit drops that weren't in the original design visualization.

**Plumbing stack alignment:**
- "Bathrooms stacked vertically share a plumbing wall, saving thousands in pipe runs": ✅ Vertical wet-room alignment is a foundational cost-saving principle in residential plumbing.
- "run supply and drain lines through a maze of floor joists to reach an island bathroom": ✅ Horizontal drain runs require minimum 1/4" per foot slope (IRC P3005.3), which becomes problematic over long horizontal distances and can conflict with floor joist depth.

**Structural grid:**
- "22-foot clear span...may be requesting a $4,000 steel beam that a 16-foot span wouldn't need": ✅ A 22-foot residential span typically exceeds engineered lumber (LVL) capacity for typical residential loads, requiring a steel W-beam. At 16 feet, a deep LVL or PSL beam is typically sufficient. The $4,000 figure is plausible for a steel beam (supply + installation) in 2026 residential pricing.

**IRC egress (R310):**
- Dimensions correct (24" height, 20" width, 44" sill). The 5.7 sqft figure is the grade-floor requirement; upper floors permit 5.0 sqft per 2021 IRC. See Legal Accuracy note above. Minor simplification, not an error.

**Construction cost ($200/sqft):**
- ✅ Defensible national median for mid-market new residential construction in 2026. Turner & Townsend, RSMeans, and NAHB data support a $150–$300/sqft range depending on market and quality tier. $200 is a reasonable midpoint.

**Energy calculation chain — minor note:**
The article states "Space heating and cooling account for roughly half of that bill" where "that bill" is the electricity bill ($2,040/yr). For all-electric homes, HVAC at ~50% of electricity is accurate per EIA RECS data. However, for homes with natural gas heating (which is ~47% of US households), the electricity bill's HVAC component is predominantly cooling, not heating. The 20–30% orientation savings from the DOE refer to total heating AND cooling energy (regardless of fuel type). This means:
- For all-electric homes: the math chain is accurate
- For gas-heated homes: orientation savings reduce gas costs (heating) and electricity costs (cooling), but the article's $200–$300/yr estimate derived purely from the electricity bill understates heating savings (gas) while overstating cooling savings (fraction of electricity)

**Impact assessment:** The net effect on the $200–$300/yr savings range is likely wash-to-slight-understatement for most climates (gas heating savings from orientation can be significant). The article's numbers remain defensible as a reasonable estimate, and the limitations section already flags regional variation. Not a scoring issue, but worth noting for technical completeness.

**Circulation percentages:**
- 6–10% for architect-designed homes: ✅ Consistent with residential design literature (Ching's architectural references, NAHB guidelines for efficient residential layout).
- 13–18% for AI-generated: Explicitly noted as author's assessment, not published data. Honestly disclosed. ✅
