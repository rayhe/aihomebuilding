The New Housing Law Funds Pre-Approved Home Catalogs. It Says Nothing About Who Draws the Plans.

By Catherine Chen · July 18, 2026

In June, Amber Long printed thirteen residential floor plans, posted them on a city website, and tried to solve a housing crisis in northwest Arkansas. Long is a planner in Rogers, population 74,000, where Walmart's orbit pulls in new residents faster than builders can frame walls. Her solution was old enough to have a precedent in the Sears catalog and new enough that most planning directors have never heard of it: a pattern book. Pick a design from the city's website, download it for free, skip months of plan review, and build.

"We tried to think about the character of some of the older parts of town," Long told Homes.com. Architects drew cottages and duplexes that echoed the pitch and setback of homes people already recognized from their own streets. Builders can vary materials and finishes, but the layout stays fixed unless they want to trigger a full review cycle. Thirteen designs for a city growing at 3% a year. Not many. But Rogers is not the only place gambling that a thin catalog beats a thick permitting backlog.

Eleven Programs, Twenty-Five Plans, Four Million Missing Homes

Nearly a dozen communities now run some version of a pre-approved building plan program. Ohio launched a statewide initiative with two architect-designed plans targeting 80% of the state's infill lots, a narrow single-family home and a front-back duplex, free to any municipality. Michigan's economic development corporation produced five open-source stamped plan sets, three duplexes and two quadraplexes, available across the state. Overland Park, Kansas, waived building permit fees entirely for its portfolio of pre-approved cottages and duplexes when the program launched in November 2025. Portland certifies builder-submitted house plans through a 15-working-day review, after which the approved design can be stamped onto any qualifying lot without repeating the structural review.

Count up every unique design across all active programs and you reach roughly 25 to 30 floor plans, and count up the national housing deficit using Realtor.com's post-Great Recession estimate and you get 4 million homes.

Which brings us to the question Congress just funded but did not answer: who draws the plans, and does it matter whether the author is a person or a machine?

What the ROAD Act Actually Says

On July 11, the 21st Century ROAD to Housing Act became law without the president's signature. Among its 47 provisions is a grant program for communities to develop "pattern books" of pre-approved housing designs, and it passed the House 358–32 and the Senate 85–5. CNN reported that the law also allows infill developers to skip environmental review when building between two previously reviewed structures, and removes the federal mandate requiring manufactured homes to sit on a permanent steel chassis, a rule that added $5,000 to $10,000 to units that are supposed to be affordable.

Read the provision on pattern books closely and you find something conspicuous in its absence: grant money for design catalogs, incentives for communities that adopt them, but not one clause specifying who draws the plans, what qualifications they need, whether the designs must reflect local architectural character, or how a community should evaluate whether its catalog is any good. Congress funded the printing press and forgot to discuss the author.

Enter the Algorithm

A commercial AI tool called PlanFinder, released as an Autodesk Revit 2026 addon, generates multiple residential floor plan variations from geometric and functional constraints using a custom machine learning model. Feed it a lot boundary, a unit count, and a set of room requirements, and it returns BIM-ready floorplans in minutes, not one option but a ranked set of variations optimized against whatever constraints the operator specifies. Academic researchers at Poznań University of Technology ran a comparison study this year, pitting PlanFinder outputs against architect-designed apartments of matching size and type. Separately, a Chinese research team built CorbuAI, an AI architecture platform that handles floor plan generation, facade design, and interior style transfer using generative adversarial networks trained on thousands of real residential layouts.

None of this work is theoretical anymore. Architects have known since Stanislas Chaillou's 2019 GAN experiments trained on 800 Boston apartment layouts that algorithms could generate plausible floor plans, but the outputs were curiosities then, not construction documents. PlanFinder is a shipping product with paying customers, researchers have published dedicated evaluation metrics for scoring AI-generated residential plans on both accuracy and diversity, and a federal law is about to fund the exact deliverable these tools produce.

Consider the math a small city faces when it decides to build a pattern book. Rogers paid architects through consultants, and a custom residential plan set runs $2,000 to $10,000 depending on complexity according to typical fees reported by the American Institute of Architects for schematic-through-construction-document phases of single-family projects, which means that for 13 designs the architectural work alone costs $26,000 to $130,000 before anyone touches site-specific structural engineering. An AI-generated catalog, run through PlanFinder or a comparable tool, requires a software license and a human operator who understands zoning constraints and building science, and every generated plan still requires a licensed professional engineer's stamp before any municipality will accept it. Cheaper, yes. But the design labor, the iterative sketching and revision that accounts for most of the architect's fee, compresses from months to days.

When a federal grant covers the cost, the question is not whether a city can afford an architect. It is whether a city that receives, say, $50,000 in pattern book funding will voluntarily spend $100,000 on architect-drawn plans when a faster, cheaper alternative exists. Few will.

Sears Shipped 75,000 Houses and Nobody Called It a Crisis

Between 1908 and 1940, Sears, Roebuck and Company sold more than 75,000 kit homes through its mail-order catalog, shipping precut lumber and a 75-page instruction manual by rail to wherever a boxcar could reach. At its peak, the program offered 447 models ranging from the $360 Golden Rod cottage to the $5,140 Magnolia. Pre-cut lumber reduced construction time by 40% according to Sears' own catalogs, and Aladdin Company of Bay City, Michigan, which actually beat Sears to market by two years, proved that the model scaled even without a massive distribution network behind it.

Nobody debated whether mail-order homes would destroy architectural diversity, and they did not — many Sears houses still stand, prized by preservationists who hunt them through county records and framing marks. But Sears also operated in an era when labor was cheap, land was available, and a family could order a three-bedroom home for less than a new car costs today, an era when housing starts were not falling for three consecutive months and builder confidence was not pinned at 34 on a 100-point index for the fifteenth straight month below 40.

The Counterargument Deserves Its Full Weight

Pattern book critics overstate the convergence risk. Homes already look alike. Drive through any subdivision built after 2015 and count the number of distinct elevations — you will run out of fingers before you leave the cul-de-sac, because production builders like D.R. Horton, which reported unsold inventory down 25% from December after pivoting from spec to build-to-order, already work from a finite set of repeatable plans that differ from each other primarily in the brochure photography. If a city's existing development looks like algorithmic output, an actual algorithm might not make it worse, and it might get homes standing two years sooner.

And the speed argument matters. Overland Park acknowledged that "hard numbers on time and cost savings aren't available yet" eight months into its program, but the structural logic is sound: removing the unpredictable design-review cycle shortens permitting timelines, which reduces carrying costs, which makes marginal projects pencil out. In a market where 37% of builders are cutting prices and 63% are running incentives for the sixteenth consecutive month, anything that lowers the cost of starting a home has theoretical value.

What This Analysis Cannot Tell You

No pattern book program has operated long enough to generate statistically meaningful data on permitting time savings, construction cost reductions, or adoption rates. Overland Park launched in November 2025. Rogers launched last month. Ohio's statewide initiative is still distributing its toolkit. I counted "roughly 25 to 30" unique designs across all programs by reviewing each city's public plan libraries; this is a manual tally, not an official census, and some programs may have added designs since publication. AI tool costs were estimated from published AIA fee ranges and commercial software pricing, not from invoices provided by the programs themselves. No city has publicly disclosed paying an AI tool to generate pattern book designs, and I am describing a trajectory, not documenting a practice that has already arrived, and the distinction matters because the grant money that could accelerate this trajectory has only just been appropriated.

Single-family housing starts fell 0.2% to 895,000 units in June, the third consecutive monthly decline. Permits dropped to 871,000, a ten-month low. Whether pattern books, AI-generated or otherwise, can move those numbers depends on whether the bottleneck is permitting friction or mortgage rates and buyer confidence, and the evidence so far suggests it is mostly the latter, which means pattern books may be solving a problem that is real but secondary to the one keeping builders on the sidelines.

The Grant Check Is Coming. The Design Brief Is Not.

Federal grant money for pattern book programs will begin flowing to communities in the coming months. Some of those communities will hire architects who sit with planning commissions and walk the neighborhoods where these homes will be built, the way Rogers and Ohio did. Others will discover that a software tool can produce a compliant catalog for a fraction of the cost in a fraction of the time, and they will have every economic incentive to use it.

Congress wrote a law that treats home design as a procurement problem: download the plans, skip the review, build faster. Sears proved a century ago that standardized design works when the market wants volume. But Sears also hired architects, tested materials, and built its reputation on quality that a buyer could verify by walking through a finished home in a nearby town. An AI-generated floor plan has no such showroom — it has a compliance checkbox and a rendering, and the community that adopts it may not discover what the algorithm missed until the first family moves in and finds that the morning light never reaches the kitchen because nobody asked the model which direction the lot faces. Whether that is enough depends on what you think a neighborhood is: a collection of structures that pass code, or a place where someone decided what the street should look like before the first wall went up.

If your community is applying for a pattern book grant, ask one question before the design contract is signed: will the person who draws these homes walk the streets where they will be built? That answer will tell you more about the catalog than any rendering.
