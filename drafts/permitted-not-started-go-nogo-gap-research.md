# Research: The Permitted-But-Not-Started Home Gap

## Core Angle
144,000 single-family homes were authorized for construction but never started as of May 2026 (Census Bureau). That number rose 2.1% in a single month. Each represents sunk permitting costs, carrying costs on land, and a builder's go/no-go decision made on instinct rather than data. AI feasibility tools exist for commercial real estate but not residential. The gap between pulling a permit and breaking ground is the most data-poor, highest-stakes decision in homebuilding.

## Original Contribution: The Carrying Cost Calculation

### Per-Lot Monthly Carrying Cost (Permitted But Not Started)
Inputs:
- Average finished lot cost: ~$91,100 (NAHB 2024: 13.7% of $665,298 sale price)
  - Updated: if avg sale price = $499,500, and lot share is ~13.7%, lot cost ≈ $68,431
  - But NAHB lot cost figures have been reported separately; use ~$75,000-$91,000 range
- Lot acquisition loan: 50-65% LTV, 8.5-10% interest rate (current market)
- Monthly carrying components:
  1. Loan interest: $75,000 × 60% LTV × 9% = $337.50/month
  2. Property taxes: varies, $100-300/month on unimproved lot
  3. Insurance (vacant land): $30-75/month
  4. Permit expiration risk: most permits expire in 6-24 months; extension fees $200-$500
  5. Opportunity cost: capital tied up in lot + permit fees not deployed elsewhere
- Conservative estimate: $500-750/month per permitted-but-not-started lot
- Aggressive estimate: $800-1,200/month per lot (higher-cost markets)

### Industry-Wide Carrying Cost of 144,000 Idle Permits
- At $750/month average: 144,000 × $750 = $108M/month = $1.30B/year
- At $1,000/month average: 144,000 × $1,000 = $144M/month = $1.73B/year
- NOTE: This is a crude estimate. Many factors affect actual carrying costs. But directionally, this is a multi-billion-dollar decision being made without data tools.

### What AI Feasibility Tools Would Need to Model
1. Local absorption rate (how fast homes sell in this submarket)
2. Construction cost trajectory (prices going up or down?)
3. Mortgage rate forecast (will buyers be able to qualify?)
4. Labor availability forecast (can I get crews when I need them?)
5. Competitive supply (how many other builders have permits in this area?)
6. Seasonal timing (optimal month to start for weather, demand, and completion timing)

### Commercial Real Estate Already Does This
- Prophetic AI: analyzed 183,000 parcels for D.R. Horton, identified 3,000+ sites
- TestFit: $20M Series A, 6,200+ users, connects pro forma to design
- Deepblocks: AI zoning interpretation + financial feasibility
- ALICE Technologies: schedule + cost optimization
- But these are all built for $50M+ commercial projects or 100+ unit developments
- Custom builder doing 5-15 homes/year has no equivalent tool

## Counterargument / Skepticism
- Not all 144,000 are "sitting idle" — some are phased developments, build-to-order waiting for buyer
- Builders may be strategically HOLDING permits as optionality (like options contracts)
- Carrying costs are real but may be acceptable if the alternative is building into a falling market
- The "information advantage" of commercial tools may not translate — residential markets are hyperlocal
- But: even if only 30% are genuine indecision (vs. strategic phasing), that's 43,000 homes and $400M+/year in carrying costs

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home?
YES. If you're a builder with a permitted lot, this gives you a framework for the start/wait decision and a sense of what that decision is costing you per month. If you're a buyer ordering a build-to-order home, it explains why your builder might be hesitant to start and what data would make them more confident.
