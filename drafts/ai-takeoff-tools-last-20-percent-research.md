# Research: AI Takeoff Tools — The Last 20%

## Angle
A new AI quantity takeoff startup launches every week. Most nail the first 80% of a plan — standard walls, rectangular rooms, regular doors and windows. They break on the last 20%: irregular geometries, complex facades, curved elements, site-specific details. That's where the money is, and where estimator oversight is still essential.

## Journalist: Jake Kowalski (Construction Technology)

## Key Sources

### 1. First Academic Study: ASC 2026 — Togal AI Accuracy (June 2, 2026)
- Sadeh, Geloso, Todorov — "Testing AI Accuracy in Quantity Takeoff"
- 29 line items spanning exterior, floor, ceiling finishes, windows, doors
- Key findings:
  - **AI best on:** "repetitive, clearly bounded, and orthogonal elements"
  - **AI worst on:** "irregular geometries and complex façades"
  - **Consistent underestimation** for area-based quantities (SF)
  - **Near-perfect alignment** for count-based items (EA — doors, windows)
  - **Accuracy by finish type:** Ceiling = highest, Floor = moderate, Exterior = greatest deviation
  - **Conclusion:** "estimator oversight remains essential for accuracy assurance in complex building elements"
- Published in Proceedings of ASC 62nd Annual International Conference, vol 7, pages 753-762

### 5. Data Points for Calculations
- Traditional takeoff: 8-20 hours for a 2,000 sq ft home (depending on complexity)
- AI takeoff: 15-45 minutes for initial pass
- Human review of AI output: 2-4 hours
- Estimator cost: $75-125/hr
- Traditional takeoff cost: $600-2,500
- AI-assisted cost: $150-500 (tool subscription + review time)
- But: underestimation on area quantities → ordering too little → crew downtime, reorders, delays
- Running short on drywall mid-build: $500-2,000 in extra delivery + crew idle time

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home?
YES — tells builders which AI takeoff tools to consider, where they fail, and how to evaluate them. Tells homeowners to ask their builder about estimating methods. Warns against trusting AI output without review on complex builds.

## Strongest Counterargument
Even at 80% accuracy, AI takeoffs save enormous time — and time is money. A builder who does 50 estimates a year and saves 10 hours per estimate recovers 500 hours annually. At $100/hr, that's $50,000 in estimator time. The 20% that needs human review still gets reviewed — you just don't have to do the first 80% by hand anymore. The tools don't need to be perfect; they need to be faster than perfect.

## Limitations
- Only one peer-reviewed study (Togal AI on commercial, not residential)
- Self-reported accuracy claims from vendors not independently verified
- No longitudinal data on cost impact of AI takeoff errors
- Tools improving rapidly — accuracy benchmarks may be stale by publication
