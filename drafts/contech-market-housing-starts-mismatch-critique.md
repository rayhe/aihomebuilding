## Critic 6: 🔬 Research Rigor
**Score: 8.0 / 10**

**Original Contribution:** YES. The novel analysis is the juxtaposition of real-time housing starts data (released same-day) against ConTech market sizing reports, combined with segmentation data showing where ConTech money actually goes (29% infrastructure/heavy-civil, 1% residential AI adoption). The ROI comparison at two different volume levels (30 homes vs. 18 homes) is a genuinely useful original calculation. The thesis — that ConTech growth is an infrastructure story misidentified as a residential story — is a non-obvious insight.

**Limitations:** YES. Dedicated section, honest about regional variation, production builder caveat for the 1% figure, pricing verification gap, and data timing mismatches. Meets the standard.

**Strongest Counterargument:** YES. The "What ConTech Actually Sells in a Downturn" section is legitimately strong. It calculates specific dollar savings, names specific scenarios, and calls the tools "a genuine survival mechanism." It's not strawmanned.

**Problems:**
1. **The $4,800 carrying cost figure fails methodology transparency.** The article states: "An AI scheduling tool that eliminates one 10-day weather delay saves $4,800 in carrying costs on a $300,000 construction loan at 9.5%." Running the math: $300,000 × 9.5% ÷ 365 × 10 = **$780.82** in interest. Even adding property taxes (~$17/day), insurance (~$5/day), and utilities (~$3/day), you get ~$1,050 for 10 days. The claimed $4,800 implies $480/day, which would require an all-in carrying rate of **58.4%** annually — absurd for any construction loan. This is either a calculation error or the word "carrying costs" is being used to include general overhead (superintendent time, equipment idle time, etc.) without disclosure. Either way, the Story Guide requires "show the math with inputs, assumptions, and calculation" for numerical claims. This one doesn't, and the unstated math doesn't add up.
2. **The $144,000 and $86,400 ROI figures cascade from the unverified $4,800.** If the per-home savings figure is wrong, the entire actionable section's ROI analysis collapses. $780 × 30 = $23,400 against a $38,000 license — a net loss. This would invert the article's actionable advice.
3. **The $5,400 takeoff savings figure also lacks transparency.** 3% of $180,000 = $5,400 — the math checks, but the 3% overestimate assumption and the $180,000 materials budget aren't sourced. Are these typical? Where do they come from?

**Problems:**
1. **$4,800 carrying cost is mathematically unjustifiable as presented.** (See Rigor critic above.) Interest on a $300K construction loan at 9.5% for 10 days is $781. The article frames the $4,800 as derived from these inputs ("carrying costs on a $300,000 construction loan at 9.5%") but no such derivation produces $4,800. This is the most critical data error in the piece. It must be fixed — either by:
   - (a) Reducing the figure to ~$800 and adjusting all downstream ROI math, or
   - (b) Explicitly breaking down what "carrying costs" includes beyond interest (overhead allocation, equipment idle time, etc.) with the calculation shown, or
   - (c) Removing the specific loan terms and stating the figure as an industry-average all-in delay cost with a source.
2. **"Binding constraint" said twice.** "construction efficiency isn't the binding constraint on your purchase price" then "the binding constraint on the buyer's purchase price isn't construction efficiency" — same data point expressed identically in the same paragraph. Delete one occurrence.
3. **Mortgage rate impact claim lacks nuance.** "Every quarter-point increase in the mortgage rate eliminates approximately 1.3 million households from qualification, based on NAHB's standard affordability calculation against a median-priced new home at $424,900." — This is a useful contextualization, but the 1.3M figure is contingent on income distribution assumptions. The NAHB methodology should be named or linked so a reader can check it.

## Required Revisions (Priority Order)

### 1. FIX: $4,800 carrying cost calculation (Critical — blocks ship)
**Location:** "What ConTech Actually Sells in a Downturn" paragraph and "What This Means for You" section.
The interest on a $300K loan at 9.5% for 10 days is ~$781, not $4,800. Options:
- **(Preferred)** Replace with realistic all-in carrying cost. A typical builder's 10-day delay cost including interest, property taxes, insurance, and overhead is ~$1,500–$2,000/house. Use that range, cite the components, show the math. Adjust downstream ROI: $1,800 × 30 = $54,000 vs. $38,000 license = still positive but tighter. $1,800 × 18 = $32,400 vs. $38,000 = net loss. This actually *strengthens* the article's thesis (the ROI math breaks faster at lower volume).
- **(Alternative)** If the $4,800 figure comes from a specific vendor case study or industry source, cite that source.
- Cascade fix into: $144,000 and $86,400 figures in actionable section.

**Location:** "What This Means for You" — homebuyer paragraph.

> "construction efficiency isn't the binding constraint on your purchase price" → then → "the binding constraint on the buyer's purchase price isn't construction efficiency"
