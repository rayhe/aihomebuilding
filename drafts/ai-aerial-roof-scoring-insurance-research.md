# Research: AI Aerial Roof Scoring & Insurance Non-Renewal

## Journalist: Catherine "Code" Chen (Policy & Regulation)
## Beat: Insurance regulation, AI underwriting, homeowner rights

## Core Angle
Insurance companies now use AI to score your roof from aerial photos you've never seen, and in at least 15 states they can use those scores to set your premium. Homeowners who get non-renewed based on imagery have minimal recourse — they often can't see the photos, can't challenge the algorithm, and have 30-60 days to find replacement coverage. The regulatory framework hasn't caught up.

## Kill Test
✅ Directly helps someone building or buying a home understand:
- Why their insurance costs what it does (or why they can't get it)
- What building features/materials to prioritize for insurability
- Their rights when an AI-based assessment is wrong
- How to contest a non-renewal

## Primary Sources (3+ required)

### 1. LexisNexis Risk Solutions — Location Intelligence for Home (May 2026)
- AI-powered predictive risk model for US home insurers
- Neural network tech + industry-wide claims data + location intelligence + historical loss patterns
- Scores across 6 perils: hail, wind, weather-related water, non-weather-related water, freeze, collapse/falling objects
- Highest-risk properties are **20x more likely** to generate claims than lowest-risk
- Also includes roof condition grade add-on
- Key stat: Non-weather water claims = 24% of all claims (2025), weather water = only 4%. Exterior imagery misses the 24% entirely.
- Source: LexisNexis press release, InsurTech Insights, insurance-canada.ca
- Quote (Meredith Barnes-Cook, Datos Insights): "Traditional property risk models were built around the signals that were easiest to get, not necessarily the ones most predictive of loss."

### 2. Geospatial Insurance Consortium (GIC)
- Industry-funded, initiated by National Insurance Crime Bureau
- Airplane imagery program covering **99% of US population**
- Uses drones, manned airplanes, and high-altitude balloons
- Photographs properties without owner's knowledge
- Source: United Policyholders, Wall Street Journal reporting

### 3. Cape Analytics — DOI-Approved Rate Filings
- Geospatial property attributes approved for rate-setting in **15 states**: AZ, CO, IA, KY, IL, IN, MI, MS, NC, OH, PA, SD, TN, VA, WI
- Key finding: Severe/poor condition roofs have **2.5x** the wind/hail claims frequency as excellent roofs
- Homes with large tree overhang = **90% higher** wind-related losses
- Uses Nearmap imagery
- 40% of insurance claims are roof-related
- Source: Cape Analytics press release, VentureBeat

### 4. NAIC AI Model Bulletin
- High-level obligation: don't run AI models on bad data
- Insurers cannot outsource accountability for vendor AI/data
- Computer vision model that systematically downgrades older roofs in certain neighborhoods = systematic disparities
- State aerial imagery bulletins apply same principles
- Source: Troutman Pepper Locke legal analysis

### 5. State-Level Regulatory Actions
- **Connecticut CID**: Cosmetic issues (discoloration, streaking) alone should NOT trigger underwriting action
- **Texas**: At least 12 state investigations since 2023 into aerial photo use for coverage decisions
- **Massachusetts**: Division of Insurance Q&A on aerial imagery rights
- **California**: State Farm dropped 30,000 residential + 42,000 commercial policies

### 6. Nearmap/Betterview — Roof Age Gen2
- Claims 95% accuracy for roof replacement year detection
- Near-100% US parcels pre-processed
- Admits traditional methods (property records, homeowner estimates, permit data) are inaccurate
- Agent/owner-supplied roof age often underestimated by 15+ years
- Source: Nearmap press releases

## Homeowner Case Studies (from NPR/WSJ/UP reporting)
- **Cindy Picos** (Auburn, CA): Roof "lived its life expectancy" per insurer. Independent inspector said 10 more years. Insurer refused to reconsider AND refused to show photos.
- **Alaina Callahan** (Houston, TX): 5-year-old roof flagged for replacement. Insurer used aerial photos + AI. Callahan couldn't see photos. "I had no recourse as a homeowner. None whatsoever."
- **Tracy Gartenmann** (Austin, TX): Travelers sent grainy aerial photos, 2 months to trim trees or lose coverage. "I thought it was a scam."
- **Elizabeth Davis** (Nationwide): Told "imagery data" from third party showed concerns. "Natural discoloration and streaking." She "had a panic attack."

## Novel Contribution: The Cost of a Wrong Score
- Cape Analytics: poor/severe roofs = 2.5x claims frequency vs excellent
- Typical premium difference for poor vs. excellent roof: $500–$1,500/year (industry data)
- A misclassification persists until the homeowner discovers it, appeals (if they can), and proves it wrong
- Average time to resolve: unknown — no insurer publishes appeal success rates
- If 5% of AI roof assessments are wrong (conservative estimate given no published error rates), and ~130M housing units exist, that's 6.5M potentially wrong scores
- At $500/year average excess premium per misclassification = $3.25B in excess premiums annually from scoring errors
- This is speculative but illustrative — the point is no one has measured the error rate because no insurer publishes it

## Strongest Counterargument
AI aerial scoring is actually *more* consistent and *less* biased than human inspectors. A human inspector's assessment varies by mood, expertise, and implicit bias. An algorithm applies the same criteria to every roof. IBHS research shows roof condition genuinely predicts losses. The problem isn't scoring per se — it's the lack of transparency, recourse, and error correction.

## Limitations
- No insurer publishes AI roof scoring accuracy or error rates
- Homeowner stories are self-reported; we can't verify the condition of their roofs
- The 2.5x claims frequency for poor roofs proves the correlation is real — the question is how often the AI gets the condition wrong
- LexisNexis Location Intelligence just launched — no independent validation yet
- State regulatory responses are fragmented and early-stage
