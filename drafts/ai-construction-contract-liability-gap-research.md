# Research: AI Construction Contract Liability Gap

## Angle
Standard construction contracts (AIA A201, ConsensusDocs) were written before AI entered jobsites. As builders increasingly use AI for estimating, scheduling, design, and quality inspection, these contracts contain zero provisions for who's liable when AI-generated outputs are wrong. When the AI estimate is $80,000 off or the AI schedule misses a critical path, the homeowner discovers the contract says nothing about it.

## Journalist: Catherine "Code" Chen (Policy & Regulation)

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? YES — anyone signing a construction contract in 2026 needs to know whether AI tools are being used and whether their contract addresses liability for AI errors. Actionable: ask your builder three questions before signing.

## Primary Sources

### 1. Bluebeam Global AEC Survey (Oct 2025)
- 1,000 AEC professionals surveyed
- Only 27% use AI in operations
- 94% of those 27% will increase usage in 2026
- 52% still use paper during design phase; 49% during planning
- 43% rely on physical signatures/approvals
- Source: press.bluebeam.com/2025/10/new-bluebeam-report...
- Reported by: ASCE Civil Engineering Source, Dec 18, 2025

### 2. NSPE Position Statement No. 03-1774 (Revised Feb 2025)
- "Individuals who design, develop, or oversee AI systems that have a direct impact on public safety should be held to the same standards as professional engineering licensure"
- Calls for transparency, accountability, continuous education
- Source: nspe.org/nspe-advocacy/explore-issues/professional-policies-and-position-statements/artificial-intelligence

### 3. Bilzin Sumberg Law Firm Analysis (Aug 2025)
- Philip R. Stein, "The Legal Risks of AI in the Homebuilding Industry"
- "An inaccurate cost projection or flawed design generated by AI can cause defects, overruns, or delays"
- "Contracts that incorporate AI need to explicitly allocate liability for such errors"
- Key areas: contractual liability, IP, data privacy, labor law, regulatory compliance
- Source: bilzin.com/insights/publications/2025/08/the-legal-risks-of-ai-in-homebuilding

### 4. Michelman & Robinson / Area Development (Q4 2025)
- Jane Kutepova, Counsel: "Construction contracts, by and large, don't address the use of AI, which is becoming dangerous"
- "Standard form agreements, including widely used documents from the AIA and ConsensusDocs, were not drafted with AI in mind"
- Key gaps identified: who selects/configures AI tools, who owns AI-generated data/content, who assumes liability for autonomous system decisions, whether AI malfunctions = force majeure
- AI in construction market projected at $48B by 2030
- Source: areadevelopment.com/construction-project-planning/q4-2025/whos-liable-when-ai-builds.shtml

### 5. AIA Contract Documents Spring 2024 Release
- Most recent update to AIA standard forms
- Zero AI-specific provisions added
- AIA A201 (General Conditions) is the most widely used construction contract in the US
- Used in approximately 80% of commercial projects; residential derivatives also widely used
- Source: learn.aiacontracts.com/articles/aia-contract-documents-spring-2024-release/

### 6. Arcadis Construction Disputes Report
- Global construction disputes averaged $43.4M (commercial scale)
- Time to resolve: 14.8 months average
- Top causes: failure to administer contract, unclear risk allocation
- Negative trend in "quality and transparency of contracts, specifications and designs"
- Source: arcadis.com/...2024-construction-disputes-report

## Novel Contribution
Cross-reference specific AIA A201 sections with AI use cases to show exactly where the contract breaks down:
- §3.3 (Superintendent) — contemplates a human superintendent, not AI scheduling
- §3.5 (Warranty) — contractor warrants materials/work, but what about AI-selected materials?
- §3.12.10 (Means and Methods) — contractor chooses means/methods; if contractor delegates to AI, is that still their responsibility?
- §4.2 (Architect's Administration) — architect reviews work; what if "architect's review" is AI-assisted?
- §15.1 (Claims) — claim resolution procedures don't address AI as a third party

## Actionable Takeaways
1. Ask your builder: "Are you using AI tools for estimating, scheduling, or design on my project?"
2. Add an AI disclosure clause to your contract (specify which tools, liability allocation)
3. Require that AI-generated outputs (estimates, schedules) be independently verified by a licensed professional
4. Ensure your builder's insurance covers AI-assisted errors (many general liability policies haven't been tested on this)
5. If using AI design tools, confirm that a licensed architect/engineer has independently reviewed and stamped all plans

## Strongest Counterargument
AI tools are still just tools — like a calculator or spreadsheet. A contractor who uses Excel for cost estimation doesn't add an "Excel clause" to the contract. The existing standard of care already covers tool use: professionals are responsible for their outputs regardless of what tools they use. Adding AI-specific clauses could create more confusion than clarity.

## Limitations
- No published case law specifically addressing AI tool failures in residential construction
- AIA hasn't commented publicly on AI provisions for future releases
- Bluebeam survey may over-represent tech-forward firms
- Insurance coverage questions for AI errors are theoretical — no test cases yet
