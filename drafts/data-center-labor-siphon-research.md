# Research: AI Data Center Boom Siphoning Residential Construction Workers

## Thesis
Meta, Google, OpenAI, and BlackRock are spending hundreds of millions training construction workers — but every one of those workers is being trained to build data centers, not houses. The AI boom is creating a massive labor siphon that pulls electricians, plumbers, and HVAC techs away from residential construction, making homes more expensive and slower to build.

## Original Analysis
- Data center spending ($86B) / construction employment ratio (3,450 jobs per $1B) = 296,700 jobs. In a market already short 439,000 workers, data centers absorb 68% of the unfilled demand.
- The wage premium is structural, not temporary: a residential electrician at $20/hr has no path to matching $35/hr data center work without leaving residential entirely.
- Combined Big Tech training investment: $115M (Meta) + $50M (Google) + $100M (BlackRock) = $265M+ for data center worker training. Investment in residential construction training: effectively $0 from Big Tech.
- Kill test: If you're building or buying a home in 2026, the labor shortage is directly adding ~2 months to your timeline and thousands to your cost. The data center boom is making it worse, and the "training programs" won't help you.

## Strongest Counterargument
- Training ANY worker for construction helps the entire ecosystem — workers who start in data centers may eventually move to other construction including residential
- These programs create net new workers who wouldn't have entered construction otherwise — growing the pie, not just redistributing it
- Gen Z construction participation already doubling without these programs
- Data center construction is temporary (2-3 year projects); those workers will return to the broader market afterward
- Response: The pay differential is too large for workers to voluntarily return to residential. Once an electrician earns $35/hr + benefits, they don't go back to $20/hr residential work. And the "temporary" argument assumes data center construction will slow down — with 400+ under development and 3,000 projected, the boom has years to run.

## Limitations
- Precise wage data for data center vs residential electricians is anecdotal (Abilene case study)
- HBI's $10.8B annual cost figure includes all causes of skilled labor shortage, not just data center competition
- The 296,700 jobs estimate (85% of need) is based on ABC's formula applied to projected spend — actual hiring may differ
- No comprehensive tracking of workers leaving residential for data center construction; we're inferring from local reports
- Training program graduation rates and retention are unknown (Meta's AWA just launched)
