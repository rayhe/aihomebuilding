# Research Notes: Teardown Carbon Debt — Green Rebuild

## Angle
When homebuyers tear down an existing house to build a "green" replacement, the combined carbon cost of demolition + new construction embodied carbon creates a carbon debt that takes decades to repay through operational energy savings. Deep energy retrofits of existing homes often reach carbon neutrality years faster. AI lifecycle tools are now making this calculation visible, and the numbers frequently favor renovation.

## Journalist: Priya Greenwood (Sustainability & Green Building)

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? YES — directly actionable for anyone considering teardown-rebuild vs. renovation. Affects the 7% of new SF homes that are teardowns, plus anyone evaluating a lot with an existing structure.

## Primary Sources

### 1. NAHB / Eye On Housing (Dec 2025)
- 6.9% of new single-family detached homes in 2024 were teardowns
- 20.1% were infill lots in older neighborhoods
- Teardown market concentrated in California (13.3% of demolition permits), New Jersey (10.4%), Texas (7.2%)
- Source: Builder Practices Survey by Home Innovation Research Labs
- URL: https://eyeonhousing.org/2025/12/about-7-of-new-homes-are-teardowns/

### 2. NAHB Economics (March 2026)
- Residential demolition permits in 2025 = 34.2% higher than 2018
- Activity dipped 0.1% from 2024 but well above pre-pandemic
- NJ surpassed TX for #3 in demolition permits (73% of NJ homes built pre-1980)
- Source: Construction Monitor data / NAHB analysis
- URL: https://www.nahb.org/blog/2026/03/aging-housing-stock-demolitions

### 3. EPA C&D Materials (2018 data)
- 600 million tons of C&D debris generated annually in US
- 90% from demolition, 10% from construction
- 145 million tons sent to landfills
- Residential demolition: ~115-170 lbs/sq ft of debris (from EPA 2003 residential study)
- URL: https://www.epa.gov/smm/sustainable-management-construction-and-demolition-materials

### 4. European Environment Agency (EEA)
- 20-25% of life cycle emissions of buildings are from embodied carbon in materials
- Most effective circular renovation action: extending lifespan of existing buildings
- 85% of today's buildings will still be in use in 2050
- Renovation uses far fewer materials than demolition + new construction
- URL: https://www.eea.europa.eu/en/analysis/publications/building-renovation-where-circular-economy-and-climate-meet

### 5. RMI — Reducing Embodied Carbon in Buildings (2021)
- Upfront embodied carbon reductions of 19-46% achievable with <1% cost premium
- Focus on 5 material categories: concrete, rebar, insulation, glazing, finish materials
- URL: https://rmi.org/insight/reducing-embodied-carbon-in-buildings/

### 6. University of Bath — AI Embodied Carbon Predictor (March 2026)
- First AI tool predicting embodied carbon from text descriptions
- Uses NLP + machine learning to estimate carbon from conversational building descriptions
- Trained on 150,000 synthetic buildings (real-world data too scarce)
- Tested with 43 building professionals
- Correctly identifies key materials 80% of the time
- Published: Architectural Engineering and Design Management, DOI: 10.1080/17452007.2026.2613773
- URL: https://techxplore.com/news/2026-03-ai-tool-emissions-simple-text.html

### 7. California AB 2446 / CARB Embodied Carbon Framework
- Requires CARB to develop framework for measuring/reducing embodied carbon
- Target: 40% net GHG reduction in building materials by 2035
- Embodied carbon now up to 50% of total building-related emissions
- Housing production in CA expected to ramp up significantly in next 10 years
- URL: https://ww2.arb.ca.gov/our-work/programs/embodied-carbon/about

## Novel Calculation: Carbon Payback of Teardown vs. Retrofit

### Teardown + Green Rebuild (1,800 sq ft):
- Demolition debris: ~135 lbs/sq ft × 1,800 = ~122 tons of material debris
- Demolition carbon (equipment, transport, landfill): ~10-15 metric tons CO2e
- New construction embodied carbon: ~50 metric tons CO2e (at ~300 kgCO2e/m² for residential)
  - Based on Oshidero et al. benchmark data and CLF database ranges
- Total carbon debt: ~60-65 metric tons CO2e
- Old home operational emissions: ~8.5 metric tons CO2/year (1970s vintage, ~100 MMBtu)
- New green home operational emissions: ~3.5 metric tons CO2/year (~40 MMBtu)
- Annual savings: ~5 metric tons CO2/year
- **Carbon payback: ~12-13 years**

### Deep Energy Retrofit Instead:
- Retrofit embodied carbon: ~10-15 metric tons CO2e (insulation, windows, heat pump, solar)
- Same operational improvement achievable (envelope + HVAC + solar)
- **Carbon payback: ~2-3 years**

### The Gap
- Teardown-rebuild takes 10 extra years to reach carbon parity vs retrofit
- During those 10 years, the planet accumulated an extra ~50 metric tons of CO2
- This is equivalent to ~110,000 miles of driving

### Limitations
- Calculations use US average grid carbon intensity; varies significantly by region
- Assumes comparable operational performance (not always achievable in retrofit)
- Doesn't account for structural condition of existing building
- Old homes with lead, asbestos, or structural failures may justify teardown
- New construction quality/durability advantages over time not captured

## Skepticism / Counterargument
- Some old homes are genuinely unrenovatable (structural, code, lead/asbestos)
- Retrofit can't always match new construction airtightness (ACH50 of 1.0 vs 3.0)
- When lot value >> improvement value, teardown may be economically inevitable regardless
- Code compliance for deep retrofits triggers "50% rule" in flood zones — may require full rebuild
- Embodied carbon data has huge uncertainty ranges (±30-50%)

## Actionable Takeaways
1. Before tearing down, run an AI lifecycle analysis comparing teardown-rebuild vs. deep retrofit
2. In CA, embodied carbon measurement is becoming mandatory — plan ahead
3. If you must tear down, maximize deconstruction/salvage (can recover 30-50% of material value)
4. Request embodied carbon estimates from your architect/builder
5. The "green new build" label doesn't automatically mean lower lifetime carbon
