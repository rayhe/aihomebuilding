## Critic 5: Residential Relevance (Kill Test)
**Score: 8.0/10**
Passes the kill test: the article helps any builder evaluating AI takeoff tools by identifying a specific failure mode and providing three concrete questions to ask vendors. BUT — the examples skew commercial (MEP assemblies, homeruns, panel loading, electrical schedule footnotes). Custom home builders think about structural modifications, cabinet layouts, bathroom relocations. Should weave in at least one residential-specific revision scenario to anchor the piece for the target audience.

## Critic 6: Scholarly Rigor
**Score: 7.5/10**
Has original contribution (strong). Has specific data. BUT:
- **Missing limitations section.** The 12% effective savings calculation makes assumptions (3-5 revisions per project, 20% fresh upload share, 60% vendor claim) that should be stated as assumptions, not facts. Different project types have different revision counts.
- **Missing strongest counterargument.** The article doesn't engage with the counter: some vendors (Togal.AI, IntoAEC) claim to have solved revision tracking. Is that claim credible? The article should acknowledge that the problem is being worked on, even if most tools haven't solved it yet.
Both are required by STORY_GUIDE.md.

## Critic 7: Structure & Engagement
**Score: 8.5/10**
Strong cold open (demo scenario). Good escalation through the three failure modes. The verification paradox section adds intellectual weight without losing the reader. The three vendor questions ending is actionable and memorable. The "They optimized for the demo" line is a strong closer concept, though the extended sentence after it dilutes the punch.
