# Research: AI Building Permit Review

## Thesis
Building permits cost the average US home builder $93,870 in regulatory compliance and 6 months of delays — yet 80% of applications contain deficiencies requiring 1.6 resubmissions. AI pre-screening tools are collapsing that timeline from months to days in early-adopter cities, but the vast majority of the 39,000+ US jurisdictions still run on paper and phone calls.

## Novel Contribution
Nobody has lined up the NAHB regulatory cost data ($93,870/home, 23.8% of home price) against the AI permit review results (Honolulu: 6 months → 2-3 days, Hamilton: 60% reduction) to calculate the dollar value of time saved per home. We'll build that calculation:
- NAHB says 95.9% of developers face ~6 month permit delays
- Carrying cost on a $400K home at current rates ≈ $2,000-3,000/month
- 6 months of carrying cost ≈ $12,000-18,000 per home
- If AI cuts that to 1-2 months, that's $8,000-15,000 saved per home
- Multiply by 1.5M single-family permits/year nationally = $12-22.5B in carrying costs alone

## Strongest Counterargument
"Permits exist to protect people. Speeding them up means cutting corners on safety." 

Counter: AI isn't replacing human reviewers — it's pre-screening submissions so human reviewers see clean applications instead of spending 80% of their time on deficiency corrections. Austin's Director Roig explicitly said it lets reviewers "focus on the unique aspects of an application, taking advantage of their expertise." The Honolulu number isn't "skip the review" — it's "time to reach a reviewer" went from 6 months to 2-3 days. The review itself still happens.

Second counterargument: "Only big cities can afford this." PermitFlow's $500M valuation and SaaS model suggests the opposite — smaller jurisdictions are exactly the target market. But adoption data from small jurisdictions is thin, which is a real limitation.
