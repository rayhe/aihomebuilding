# Research: Data Center Construction Draining Residential Labor

## Core Thesis
The AI data center construction boom is systematically draining the construction workforce — particularly electricians — from residential homebuilding. For the first time in modern economic history, private investment in computing infrastructure nearly matches single-family residential investment (~$400B each). The homes that don't get built, and the ones that cost more because of it, are the hidden housing cost of the AI revolution.

## Key Data Points

### Investment Scale (Federal Reserve / Census Bureau)
- Private fixed investment in computing equipment: ~$150B → ~$400B in less than 2 years (Fed data via Inman, May 2026)
- Single-family residential investment: flat at ~$400B over same period
- Two lines that spent six decades running parallel now nearly touching — first time ever
- Data center construction spending up 23% YoY in May 2026 (Census Bureau)
- Data centers = 8% of total private nonresidential construction spending (Census)
- AI-ready data center capacity growing 33%/year until 2030 (McKinsey)

### Housing Starts Decline
- Residential starts DOWN 4% through April 2026 (Dodge Construction Network)
- Single-family starts DOWN 11% through April 2026 (Dodge/ENR 2Q 2026 Cost Report)
- SF starts slid to 8-month low in May: 893,000 units SAAR, -2% MoM (Reuters)
- SF completions: 872,000 units — lowest level in 6 years (Census)
- NAHB projected national housing shortage: ~1.2 million homes
- "Rising mortgage rates between January and April have continued to price out first-time homebuyers" — Sarah Martin, Dodge

### Labor Drain — The Electrician Exodus
- Abilene, TX: Stargate (OpenAI/Crusoe/Oracle) 4M sq ft data center pulling electricians from homebuilders
- Builder Gene Lantrip: "My subcontractors don't have the people. My electrician lost two of his lead men and several of his helpers to the data center."
- Homes taking 2 months longer to complete in Abilene (Texas Tribune via Inman)
- Electricians on Stargate earning 2x residential subcontractors (Inman)
- Three electricians under 30 on a data center site in Plano, TX making $240K-$280K/year (CNBC)
- Data centers paying 32% more than residential projects overall (Randstad)
- Residential framer at $32/hr getting offered $42/hr on data center pour (LinkedIn/industry anecdotes)
- Electricians = 45-70% of data center construction budgets (IBEW)
- Construction worker demand up 30%, electrician demand up 18%, welder demand up 25% in 4 years (Randstad)
- Industry needs ~500,000 new construction workers in 2026 alone (Randstad)
- Residential-to-commercial electrician pay gap widened to 18% as of 2026 (Repair-CRM/BLS)

### Land Competition
- Northern Virginia data center land: $6.3-8M per acre (CBRE H2 2025)
- SDC Capital paid $615M for 97 acres in Leesburg (originally assembled for $57M in 2021)
- "I don't think a residential developer can compete" — Arif Gasilov, Gasilov Group
- Natelli Communities, 40-year residential developer, pivoting to data centers
- Ashburn, VA: developers offering $4.4M/acre to buy out 143-home Regency subdivision for data center
- PulteGroup (#3 US homebuilder) partnering with Nvidia/Span for "fractional data centers" on homes

### Utility Cost Pass-Through
- Virginia: Dominion Energy base rate increase $11.24/month for average residential customer (Jan 2026, VA SCC)
- SB 253 (Sen. Louise Lucas): estimated $5.52/month residential subsidy to data centers
- Data center power demand could cause 156 GW supply deficit by 2030 (Colliers)

### Material Competition
- Data center construction costs per MW up 20-25% since 2023 (Bernstein)
- HV transformer lead times: 80-100 weeks (Bernstein)
- Microsoft $500M data center in Chicago used 2,177 tons of copper (Copper Development Association)
- 50% tariff on copper imports compounds the problem
- 40% of construction firms raised bid prices due to tariffs (AGC)
- Construction inflation in Austin and Atlanta: 5% in 2025, highest in North America (Turner & Townsend)

### Immigration Factor
- 30% of US construction workers are immigrants (National Immigration Forum, Sept 2024)
- 1/3 of construction firms affected by immigration enforcement in last 6 months (AGC)
- Nearly 1/4 saw subcontractors lose workers (AGC)

## Original Contribution: Per-Home Cost Impact Calculation
Nobody has published this number. Calculate the all-in per-home cost impact in data-center-affected markets:

1. **Wage premium pass-through:** If residential electrical work requires ~200-250 labor hours per home, and the data center premium forces residential rates up by ~$8-12/hr to retain workers, that's $1,600-$3,000/home in electrical alone.

2. **Schedule delay cost:** 2-month average delay × carrying cost at ~7% mortgage rate on median new home ($420,000): $420,000 × 0.07 × (2/12) = ~$4,900/home in builder carrying costs (typically passed to buyer).

3. **Utility rate increase:** $11.24/month × 12 = $135/year × 30-year mortgage present value at 7% = ~$1,674 in lifetime utility cost per home.

4. **Total per-home impact in affected markets: ~$8,200-$9,574**

5. **National extrapolation:** If 15-20% of the 893K single-family starts are in data-center-proximate markets (~134K-179K homes), total annual residential cost: $1.1B-$1.7B/year

## Strongest Counterargument
Data centers create jobs and tax revenue that can fund housing — the Abilene Stargate project will generate property tax revenue and bring permanent tech jobs. Construction is cyclical; data center demand is creating a temporary surge, and the market will equilibrate as training programs expand and data center construction cadences stabilize. The real constraint on housing is mortgage rates and zoning, not labor competition from data centers.
