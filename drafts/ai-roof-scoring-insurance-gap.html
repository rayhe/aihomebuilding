Your Insurer's AI Scored Your Roof From Space. It Was Looking at a Roof You Replaced Eight Months Ago.

By Catherine Chen · May 17, 2026
← Back to all stories

A homeowner in central Florida replaced his roof in early 2024 with architectural shingles through a licensed contractor who pulled permits and passed final inspection. Eight months later, his insurer sent a non-renewal notice citing "roof condition concerns" identified through aerial imagery analysis, so he called a roofer to come out and look. The roofer found nothing wrong, because there was nothing wrong, because the roof was eight months old and the insurer's AI had scored a structure that no longer existed on the property.

This is not an isolated glitch. It is the predictable consequence of a system where insurance companies make coverage decisions based on aerial photographs that can be anywhere from five to twenty-four months old, according to a Troutman Pepper Locke analysis of emerging state regulatory frameworks governing image freshness standards. Between the moment you finish a roof replacement and the moment a new aerial capture enters your insurer's system, you inhabit what might be called a ghost roof window. During that period, the algorithm is evaluating a structure that was torn off and hauled to a landfill months ago, and it does not know the difference because nobody told it, and the system was never designed to ask.

How Your Roof Gets Scored Without You

Three companies dominate the AI property intelligence market that feeds insurer underwriting decisions. CAPE Analytics provides a Roof Condition Rating used by carriers nationwide and claims 95 percent accuracy on roof age estimation through a combination of historical aerial imagery and permit data. Nearmap integrates AI-enhanced aerial imagery directly into Zurich North America's underwriting systems, making automated roof scoring a default part of the renewal pipeline rather than an exception. EagleView operates drone-based inspection with AI anomaly detection at sufficient scale that Hastings Mutual Insurance eliminated its entire ladder-assist inspection program in favor of EagleView's Assess platform, which tells you everything about where the industry's investment thesis is pointing.

Score the roof from above, classify the risk, adjust the premium or decline the renewal. No adjuster climbs a ladder, no appointment gets scheduled, and the insurer processes thousands of renewals per day without a single human looking at a single roof. The economics are obvious: CAPE reports that 34 percent of all homeowner claims trace to wind and hail damage, which is overwhelmingly roof damage, which means getting roofs right at scale is worth billions in avoided losses across the industry.

What the system cannot do is distinguish a cosmetic issue from a structural one. Algae staining reads as deterioration, lichen growth reads as material failure, and moss on a north-facing slope reads as deferred maintenance. A Connecticut homeowner lost coverage over what the insurer's aerial analysis flagged as "discoloration," which on physical inspection turned out to be shadow patterns cast by adjacent trees at the time of the aerial capture. The United Policyholders advocacy group documented that case alongside others where homeowners faced up to $20,000 in forced roof replacement costs based on aerial assessments that a licensed roofer standing on the actual roof could not corroborate.

Five States Noticed. Sort Of.

California moved first with two bills working through the legislature simultaneously. Assembly Bill 75, backed by Insurance Commissioner Ricardo Lara, would require insurers to provide thirty days advance notice before aerial imagery affects a coverage decision and allow homeowners to request copies of the images used, while a companion bill, AB 1559, introduced by Assemblymember Lisa Calderon, goes further with mandatory disclosure of all aerial images used in underwriting plus expanded consumer rights to challenge adverse decisions. Neither has passed.

New York's Senate Bill 9156 establishes guardrails specifically targeting adverse decisions that rely on "outdated or misleading" aerial data. New Hampshire's insurance department issued guidance stating that insurers cannot cancel policies based solely on aerial photographs, noting that "aerial imagery is valuable" but "not infallible" and must be "complemented by physical assessments." Pennsylvania is drafting its own framework, which will make five states total if everyone follows through.

Forty-five states have done nothing. In those states, an algorithm can score your roof from a photograph taken before you owned the house, and your first notification that anything happened is the non-renewal letter arriving in the mail.

Even in states that are acting, the regulations target transparency rather than accuracy, which is a meaningful distinction with real consequences. Telling a homeowner their coverage was denied because of an aerial photograph is better than not telling them, but it does not address whether the photograph reflected reality at the time the decision was made. No current or proposed state regulation requires that the image postdate the homeowner's most recent permitted roof work, which is the single change that would close the ghost roof window entirely.

Ghost Roof Math

Troutman Pepper Locke's regulatory survey found that state freshness requirements for aerial imagery range from five to twenty-four months. That means an insurer can legally base a coverage decision on an image captured up to two years before the decision date.

A roof replacement takes one to three days, a new home takes seven to twelve months to build, and in both cases, the completion of the roof structure is a single point in time that may or may not coincide with the next aerial capture cycle for that property. If your roof was replaced in March and your insurer's data vendor last flew in January, the system is scoring a January roof on a March house, and if the vendor's next capture happens in November, you have an eight-month ghost roof window during which the AI evaluates a structure that does not exist while no mechanism in the current pipeline flags the discrepancy.

For new construction, the problem compounds. If aerial imagery is captured during the framing phase, the AI has no roof to score at all, which some systems interpret as missing data rather than construction in progress, and builders who pull permits and pass final inspection can still deliver a home to a buyer whose first insurance quote is inflated by an algorithm that last saw bare trusses where a finished roof now sits.

What Builders and Buyers Should Actually Do

If you are a contractor completing a roof replacement or a builder delivering new construction, timestamped documentation is now part of the job, and the documentation needs to be specific enough to override an algorithm. Photograph the completed roof from multiple angles on the day of final inspection, include the permit number and inspection approval in the package, and advise your client to submit this proactively to their insurer before the renewal cycle, because waiting for the insurer's AI to catch up means waiting for a system that was designed to look at the sky and was never designed to listen to the ground.

If you are buying a home, request the seller's most recent roof documentation and the date of the insurer's last aerial assessment, and if those dates diverge by more than six months, get an independent roof inspection before closing and submit the results to your insurer at the time you bind coverage. The DIY inspection movement has already processed over 65,000 homeowner inspections via smartphone apps, revealing $245,000 in underinsured property values in its first year, which suggests the information asymmetry runs in both directions. Proactive documentation is not paranoia; it is the rational response to a system where the default assumption is that the photograph is right and you are wrong.

If you already received a non-renewal notice based on aerial imagery, contest it immediately. In Texas alone, at least a dozen homeowners have challenged aerial-image-based decisions through their state insurance department, and in Illinois, a licensed inspection firm called Anchor Point overturned a cancellation in Oak Park where the insurer's aerial analysis had misidentified recent repairs as missing shingles, a distinction that required standing on the roof to make. Every state has an appeals process, though most homeowners do not know it exists because the non-renewal letter does not mention it.

What the Industry Says, and What It Means

CAPE Analytics claims 95 percent accuracy on roof age estimation, which is a self-reported number from a company selling the product, and no third-party audit of any AI roof scoring system's accuracy has been published anywhere. A 95 percent accuracy rate sounds reassuring until you apply it to a portfolio of millions of homes and realize it means tens of thousands of misclassified roofs, and if your home is one of them, the portfolio-level statistic is irrelevant to your experience of losing coverage on a roof you replaced last year.

The insurers' counterargument deserves its full weight, because it is genuine: without AI screening, they would need human adjusters to physically inspect millions of roofs, which is expensive, slow, and exposes adjusters to fall risk that itself generates workers' compensation claims. CAPE says its platform reduces wildfire risk by 76 percent and wind claims by 2.5 times for carriers that adopt it, and those are meaningful improvements in catastrophe management that reflect real reductions in losses which would otherwise be passed through to every policyholder as premium increases.

But reducing aggregate losses across a portfolio is a different problem than correctly evaluating any individual property, and the system was built for the former while being applied to the latter. Until the data pipeline includes a mechanism for homeowner-submitted documentation to override or augment aerial captures, every roof replacement creates a window where the algorithm is scoring a phantom, and the homeowner has no way to close that window from below.

Limitations of This Analysis

No publicly available data quantifies the false positive rate for any AI roof scoring system, and we do not know how many non-renewals are driven primarily by aerial imagery versus other underwriting factors, because insurers are not required to disaggregate the basis for adverse decisions in most states. CAPE's accuracy claims are marketing materials, not peer-reviewed research, and California's AB 75 and New York's S.9156 are proposed legislation that may or may not be enacted. Our ghost roof window calculation uses the range of freshness standards documented by Troutman Pepper Locke across multiple state regulatory frameworks and does not reflect any single insurer's actual capture frequency, which remains proprietary.

What we can verify: homeowners in at least four states have been non-renewed based on aerial imagery that did not match ground conditions, regulators in five states have concluded the practice requires new guardrails, and insurance premiums are rising faster than inflation, faster than income, and faster than construction costs. Something in the system is failing the people it was designed to protect, and the algorithm that was supposed to fix it is, in some documented cases, making the problem worse for the specific homeowners who did exactly what they were supposed to do and replaced their roofs before anything went wrong.
