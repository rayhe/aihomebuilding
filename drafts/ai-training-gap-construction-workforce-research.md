# Research: AI Training Gap in the Construction Workforce

## Journalist: Marcus "Steel" Washington (Workforce & Labor)
## Angle: The construction industry knows AI is coming. Nobody's training the workers.

### Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? **YES.** If you're a builder, this tells you whether to invest in AI training for your crew, what free programs exist, and what the realistic adoption timeline looks like. If you're hiring a builder, it tells you why the one who says "we use AI for scheduling" might be worth the premium — and why most builders are still years away from that.

### 1. DEWALT "AI in the Trades" Study (April 23, 2026)
- Source: Stanley Black & Decker / DEWALT national survey of construction professionals
- URL: https://dewalt.mediaroom.com/2026-04-23-New-DEWALT-Study-Identifies-Emerging-Gap-Between-AI-Training-in-Trade-Schools-and-Industry-Needs
- **Key findings:**
  - Only **8%** of U.S. construction professionals currently use AI on the job
  - **90%** believe AI will be indispensable within five years
  - **88%** expect adoption to increase over the next year
  - **83%** say AI will be standard within three years
  - **87%** say AI education must be embedded in trade schools and technical programs
  - **59%** need hands-on training tied to real construction tasks
  - **86%** feel "somewhat or very prepared" for AI — yet most rely on fragmented self-directed learning

  - Among early adopters: 35% cite increased productivity, 34% cost savings, 35% improved quality control
  - Adopter workflows: 46% site operations/monitoring, 46% planning/design, 41% estimation/procurement
- DEWALT response: pilot program with ABC Central Florida's Innovation and Technology Center + $75K committed to ABC Trimmer Construction Education Fund for AI education grants nationwide

## Original Contribution: The Training Arithmetic

BLS data: ~8.3 million U.S. construction workers
- 8% currently use AI = ~664,000 workers
- 90% expect to need AI = ~7.47 million workers
- Gap: ~6.8 million workers need AI training in the next 5 years
- That's **1.36 million workers per year**

Current training capacity:
- NABTU/Microsoft: trained 1,500 instructors (who each train... maybe 20-30 workers/year? = 30,000-45,000/year)
- DEWALT/ABC: one pilot program in Central Florida + $75K in grants (maybe hundreds of workers)
- DOL: targeting 1 million total apprentices (not all construction, not all AI-focused)
- TradesFutures: 270 ARPs across 34 states (pre-apprenticeship, not AI-specific)

Optimistic total trained through all programs: maybe 50,000-100,000/year
Need: 1.36 million/year

The programs cover roughly **4-7% of the annual need.** The rest are watching YouTube.

## Strongest Counterargument
Maybe the 90% number is aspirational, not real. Maybe "indispensable" in survey context means "I've heard it's important" not "I'll lose my job without it." The actual adoption curve could be much slower and more forgiving. Many construction tasks — framing, roofing, concrete work — remain stubbornly physical and may not benefit from AI for decades. The 8% who use AI today are disproportionately in offices (estimators, PMs, designers), not on scaffolding. The remaining 92% might not need AI — they need better pay.

## Limitations
- DEWALT study is industry-funded by a tool company with financial interest in tech adoption narrative
- Survey methodology not published — sample size, selection criteria unknown
- "Currently use AI" is vague — does ChatGPT for email count?
- BLS 8.3M construction workers includes many roles where AI relevance varies enormously
- Training capacity estimates are rough; no public enrollment data for most programs
- The DOL apprenticeship numbers are total, not construction-specific
