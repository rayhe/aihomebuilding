# Research: Builder Affordability Policy Collision

## Angle
Catherine Chen (Policy & Regulation). The collision between "most aggressive housing reform" rhetoric and the compounding policy costs that make homes unaffordable. AI tools target <4% of the regulatory burden. The math doesn't close.

## Primary Sources

### 1. NAHB 2026 Regulatory Cost Study (June 9, 2026)
- Regulations add **$131,734** (26.4%) to average new home price ($499,500, Census Jan 2026)
- Construction phase: $84,939 (17.0% of price, up from 13.3% in 2021)
- Land development phase: $46,795 (9.4% of price, down from 10.5% in 2021)
- Up **40% from $93,870 in 2021** — in just 5 years
- Building code changes over past 10 years: **>$40,000** per home (single largest line item)
- Permitting delays during construction: <$2,000 (smallest line item)
- US disposable income rose just 18.3% over same period — regulatory costs growing 2x faster
- Source: NAHB "Government Regulation in the Price of a New Home: 2026"

## Original Contribution: The $149,234 Policy Cost Stack

**Combined policy-imposed cost per home:**
- Regulation: $131,734 (26.4%)
- Tariffs: $17,500 (3.5%)
- **Combined: ~$149,234** (29.9% of $499,500 home price)

**What this means for builder economics:**
- Production builder margin: typically 3-8% ($15K-$40K on a $499,500 home)
- When 30% of price is policy-imposed and you're cutting prices 6% ($30K)...
- ...you're eating into a margin that was already $15K-$40K
- At 6% price cut: $499,500 → $469,530. Policy costs unchanged at $149,234.
- Policy costs now = **31.8% of the cut price**
- Builder margin on remaining $320,296: materials + labor + land + overhead + profit
- This math doesn't close for many small/regional builders

**What AI tools can realistically address:**
- AI permitting (GreenLite, Symbium): targets permitting delays ($2K line item = 1.5% of regulatory burden)
- AI plan review: faster approvals, but fees unchanged
- AI scheduling/estimation: reduce construction-phase inefficiencies, but doesn't reduce code compliance costs
- **AI addresses <$5K** of a $149K policy burden (<3.4%)
- The biggest chunks — building codes ($40K+), impact fees, zoning — are structural policy choices, not process inefficiencies

## Counterargument (must state at full strength)
- NAHB is a lobbying organization with a direct interest in reducing regulation
- Some "regulation" is safety-critical: energy codes reduce operating costs $2K-$4K/year, fire sprinklers save lives, structural codes prevent collapse
- The $40K building code figure includes energy efficiency requirements that DOE argues save homeowners $1,500-$3,000/year — break-even in ~15-20 years
- Regulation isn't inherently bad; it's the rate of increase (40% in 5 years) and cumulative burden that's the story
- Tariff criticism: CAP is progressive advocacy org, may overstate impacts

## Limitations
- NAHB survey is builder self-reported — may inflate regulatory burden estimates
- "Building code changes" bucket is broad — includes energy, fire, structural, accessibility, mixed value
- Tariff cost estimates are projections; actual pass-through varies by material, builder, market
- Combined $149K figure adds NAHB survey data + CAP modeling — different methodologies, some overlap possible
- No builder-level margin data available; 3-8% is industry convention, varies enormously by market

## Kill Test
If you're building or buying a home in 2026: $149,234 of your purchase price — nearly $1 in $3 — is government-imposed. That's 2x more than the cost of all the lumber and concrete in your frame. AI tools shave a few thousand off that, at best. The only thing that will meaningfully move this number is political will that doesn't currently exist.
