Your Building Permit Takes 92 Days in Chicago. In Honolulu, an AI Handles It in 20 Minutes.

By Catherine Chen · June 17, 2026

Dawn Takeuchi Apuna took over the Honolulu Department of Planning and Permitting in 2022 after a string of federal bribery indictments blew through the office like a category-five audit. Staff were demoralized. The permitting software was 30 years old. Submitting plans meant paper. Reviewing plans meant paper. Archiving plans meant boxes.

Now a residential applicant in Honolulu feeds their plans through CivCheck, an AI tool built by Canadian company Clariti. It flags code issues, identifies missing information, highlights potential problem areas, and walks the applicant through corrections before a human reviewer ever touches the file. Apuna calls it "TurboTax for permitting." Review time fell 70 percent, from 60 to 90 minutes per application down to 15 to 20.

Most building departments in the United States have not noticed.

Average regulatory cost added to a new single-family home, per a June 2026 NAHB study. That is 26.4 percent of the average $499,500 sales price, up nearly 40 percent in five years.

741 Cities. Fourteen Median Days. One 180-Day Tail That Nobody Talks About.

PermitPlace's 2026 national survey collected permit review timelines from 741 cities across 44 states. National average for initial plan review: 22.9 days. Median: 14. Half of American cities process an initial permit review in two weeks or less, which sounds almost reasonable until you read the footnotes.

Actual project timelines run two to five times longer than published guidelines. Initial review is just the first pass. Corrections, multi-department routing, resubmission, environmental review, fire department sign-off, public works approval, and utility coordination all stack on top, each department operating on its own schedule with its own standards and its own backlog. A builder in Virginia told the state Realtors association that simple permits might clear in weeks, but complex residential projects regularly spend six to twelve months in the queue.

Chicago averages 92 days for initial review alone. San Francisco, 60. Portland, 51. San Jose, 44. Phoenix, 45. Meanwhile Denver clears intake in two days, Houston in two, Miami in two. Same country. Same federal code framework. Wildly different bureaucratic machinery.

And here is what that variance costs. A Hawaii state auditor's report found that private-sector building permit delays in Honolulu cost $202 million during 2022 and 2023 combined, across 28,479 permits. That works out to roughly $7,094 per permit in delay-driven cost escalation, calculated using 4.7 to 4.9 percent annual construction inflation applied to the processing period. Average private-sector processing time was 104 days in 2023. State government projects averaged 632 days. Ninety-three percent of state projects blew past the 180-day standard.

Six Cities That Did Something About It

Honolulu is not alone, but the list is short enough to fit on a napkin.

Austin, Texas deployed AI pre-screening for residential applications. Through 190 submissions as of early April 2026, the system cut staff review time by roughly 50 percent and generated zero negative feedback from applicants. Janet Heit, Austin's chief administrative officer for development services, said staff adopted it quickly because it functions as a second set of eyes rather than a replacement. "We didn't realize how much of a great tool this was for our reviewers," she told Homes.com.

Jacksonville, Florida faces a 50,000-unit affordable housing shortfall. The city partnered with Florida-based SwiftGov, whose SwiftBuild.ai platform analyzes development plans for code and zoning compliance in minutes. After training on Jacksonville's local codes, the system reached 93 percent accuracy and saved more than 600 hours through automated reporting and real-time dashboards. Sabrina Dugan, SwiftGov's managing partner, framed the problem precisely: "What is standing between compliant projects and a building permit, and how fast can we get it out of the way?"

Seattle launched a citywide permitting reform under Mayor Harrell's executive order, with an AI pilot that began in April 2025 and public deployment expected this year. Officials project a 50 percent or greater reduction in housing review cycles. Denver selected Clariti to modernize its building permitting process. Malibu rolled out Australia-based Archistar's AI platform specifically to speed post-wildfire rebuild approvals. Los Angeles is in conversations with multiple vendors.

Clariti recently launched AI Studio, offering municipalities 45-to-60-minute workshops to demonstrate how the technology works. That the company feels it needs to offer tutorials says something about where the market is: the product exists, the results are proven, but most building departments have not progressed past the awareness stage.

What AI Can and Cannot See in Your Plans

CivCheck and its competitors are good at dimensional compliance. Setbacks, lot coverage, height limits, required window sizes, egress paths, parking ratios, Title 24 energy requirements. These are rule-based checks that a computer can apply faster and more consistently than a human reviewer flipping between a 300-page codebook and a 40-sheet plan set at 7 a.m. on a Monday with 14 more applications in the queue.

What AI cannot check is the site.

Soil bearing capacity. Subsurface drainage patterns. Mature trees whose root zones cross the foundation footprint. A retaining wall on the neighboring property that may need a geotechnical review. A historical review trigger that exists only in the institutional memory of a 28-year plan reviewer who retired in March. These are judgment calls that depend on local knowledge, physical context, and the kind of interdepartmental coordination that no amount of AI pre-screening addresses. And they account for most of the delay in complex residential permits.

A honest accounting of Honolulu's 70 percent reduction: it applies to the code-compliance review portion of residential permitting, not the entire permit-to-shovel timeline. Applicants still wait for environmental clearance, fire department sign-off, and any discretionary reviews their project triggers. Shrinking the code review from 90 minutes to 20 is meaningful, because it clears the staff bottleneck and improves first-round approval rates, which eliminates correction cycles that add weeks. But a city that deploys AI without fixing staffing, interdepartmental routing, or concurrent review protocols may find that the permit timeline barely moves.

Honolulu understood this. Apuna's team rewrote standard operating procedures, migrated to electronic plan review, and trained staff on the new system before layering AI on top. "That can be challenging, because you're asking a lot of staff," Apuna told GovTech. The AI was the capstone, not the foundation.

Why Most Cities Have Not Moved

Procurement. Building departments operate on municipal budgets approved by city councils who are not, as a rule, enthusiastic about writing checks for AI software they cannot explain at a town hall. Clariti does not publish pricing, and neither does SwiftGov. Enterprise government software typically runs six to seven figures annually, plus integration, training, and ongoing support.

Staff resistance is real. Plan reviewers have spent careers developing expertise in local code interpretation. Telling them a machine will now pre-screen their work sounds, to some ears, like the first step toward elimination. Apuna addressed this directly by involving staff in vendor selection and positioning the AI as an assistant rather than a replacement. Not every department director has the political capital or the inclination to run that process.

Liability. When an AI flags a plan as compliant and the building department stamps it, who is responsible if the AI missed a code violation that later causes a failure? Municipal law departments are still working through this question. Jacksonville's 93 percent accuracy rate means 7 percent of reviews required human correction. On a foundation setback or fire-separation issue, one wrong call is one too many.

And inertia. A building department that processes permits on a pace its community has grudgingly accepted faces no external pressure to change. Homeowners blame the builder. Builders blame the market. Nobody walks into a city council meeting and says, "Your building department's permit review process adds $7,000 to every home in this city and Honolulu figured out how to cut it by 70 percent three years ago."

Maybe they should.

Where This Is Going

A paper published June 16, 2026, in Scientific Reports presented an AI-driven framework for evaluating local and state permitting processes. Academic validation tends to lag market deployment by two to three years. The fact that peer-reviewed research is now catching up to what Honolulu has been running since 2022 suggests this is transitioning from experiment to expected practice.

A White House executive order on removing regulatory barriers to affordable home construction explicitly targets slow permitting. Congressional legislation on permitting reform has bipartisan support. NAHB Chairman Bill Owens stood at the podium two days ago and called regulatory costs a crisis. Robert Dietz, NAHB's chief economist, specifically named "permitting bottlenecks" as a target.

Honolulu's department is expanding CivCheck from residential to commercial this summer. Seattle plans public deployment in 2026. Clariti's CivCheck pilots are underway in Calgary. Denver is onboarding. The adoption curve looks like every other municipal technology wave: a few early movers prove the concept, a handful of fast followers validate the results, and within five years the laggards are explaining to frustrated builders why their city still reviews plans the way it did in 1997.

If you are a builder: ask your building department whether they have evaluated AI-assisted plan review. If they have not, ask why. Bring the Honolulu numbers. Bring the NAHB study. Bring a spreadsheet showing what a 50 percent reduction in review time would save on your next subdivision's carrying costs at current interest rates. Building departments respond to data from the people who pull permits, because those are the people who generate the fees that fund the department.

If you are buying a new home: understand that some fraction of your purchase price is a carrying cost passed through from the builder who waited 90 days for a permit that could have cleared in 20. You have no lever to pull on this directly. But you do vote in municipal elections, and permitting reform is one of the few housing affordability measures that does not require federal legislation, state budget allocations, or any philosophical consensus about the role of government in housing markets. It requires a building department that decides to answer the phone differently.

Limitations

Per-permit delay costs are calculated from a Hawaii state auditor report using construction inflation as the cost metric. This is an approximation; actual holding costs for any specific project depend on loan terms, project size, local labor market conditions, and whether delays are sequential or concurrent with other approvals. Honolulu's 70 percent review-time reduction is self-reported by the city department using Clariti's tool and has not been independently audited. PermitPlace's published timelines represent official guidelines, not measured actual processing times; the firm notes that real commercial timelines run two to five times longer. Jacksonville's 93 percent accuracy figure comes from SwiftGov and has not been externally validated. National projections extrapolating from Honolulu's results assume similar regulatory environments and construction costs, which vary substantially by jurisdiction. We contacted neither Clariti nor SwiftGov about pricing, and enterprise government software costs are estimated from industry ranges rather than confirmed quotes.
