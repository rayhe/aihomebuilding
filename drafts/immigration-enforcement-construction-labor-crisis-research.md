# Research: Immigration Enforcement Is Stalling Home Construction — and Native Workers Aren't Filling the Gap

## Journalist: Marcus "Steel" Washington
## Beat: Workforce & Labor
## Date: May 13, 2026

## Angle
Your contractor can't find roofers. Not because nobody wants the work — because the people who do it are afraid to show up. A Minnesota homebuilder lost 74 out of 80 roofers in a single week. An NBER study proves native-born workers don't replace them. And your house costs more because of it.

This story follows the workers, the builders, and the data to show how immigration enforcement is directly inflating residential construction timelines and costs. No policy prescriptions. Just the math.

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? YES. If you're building in Texas, Florida, Minnesota, or any metro where 30-60% of trades workers are immigrant — your project timeline and budget are directly affected by enforcement policy. You need to know how exposed your contractor is.

## Primary Sources

### 1. NAHB Report (April 2026)
- Foreign-born workers = 26.3% of US construction workforce (record high, 2024 data)
- Hispanics = 31% of total construction workforce (National Hispanic Construction Alliance, 2026 State of Hispanics in Construction Report)
- In top 7 homebuilding metros: immigrants = 54% of trades workers (Harvard JCHS study)
- Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington: 61% foreign-born trades workers, led with 350,000 permits 2019-2023
- Drywall installers: >50% immigrant workforce
- Roofers: >50% immigrant workforce
- Equipment operators (lower immigrant share) more common in nonresidential/infrastructure
- Source: scotsmanguide.com, homes.com, multiple agent magazine citations of NAHB data

### 2. NBER Working Paper (Cox & East, 2025) — CRITICAL SOURCE
- Title: First national, causal analysis of Trump admin immigration enforcement surge on labor markets
- Authors: Elizabeth Cox and Chloe N. East, University of Colorado Boulder
- Key finding: In areas where ICE arrests surged, likely undocumented immigrants worked less AND US-born men without college degrees saw employment in affected sectors DECLINE (not rise)
- Design: Event study with difference-in-differences, 58 geographic units, CPS data Jan 2024–Nov 2025
- "Treated" areas: ~50 additional arrests per 100,000 non-citizens in a single month (roughly doubling arrest rate)
- Key stat: 43% of immigrants worried about deportation (Pew survey, summer 2025)
- Implication: enforcement does not create job openings for native workers — it shrinks the labor pool overall
- Source: NBER working paper w35129, scienceofmoney.org writeup

### 3. Minnesota Ground Truth (CNN, Feb 2026)
- Large homebuilder in Minneapolis: only 6 of 80 roofers showing up after ICE stationed at apartment construction site
- ICE officers stationed at construction sites for weeks waiting to make arrests
- 9+ crews walked off after seeing officers (regardless of immigration status)
- "25-30% revenue decline" reported
- "I think most of us would probably take Covid over this" — unnamed homebuilder
- Even after White House announced operation wind-down, ICE interactions continued
- Legally authorized workers also avoiding sites (chilling effect)
- Source: CNN via kioncentralcoast.com, Feb 25, 2026

### 4. Industry Demand Numbers (ABC, Jan 2026)
- 349,000 new workers needed in 2026 (down from ~500K in prior years)
- 456,000 needed by 2027 (ABC workforce model)
- Data center spending = 85% of predicted demand growth (Bisnow)
- Labor shortages risk >$100 billion in lost output by 2026 (Pike Construction Services)
- Construction cost escalation: 4-6% projected for 2026, 7-10% risk scenario
- 200,000 unfilled construction jobs currently (RoofersCoffeeShop, May 2026)
- 93% of firms cite talent shortage as major barrier
- Source: ABC, Bisnow, Pike, Construction Executive, RoofersCoffeeShop

### 5. Net Immigration Decline (IndexBox, April 2026)
- Immigrant construction workforce peaked in 2024, now declining
- Net immigration fell significantly 2024→2025, further decline projected 2026
- Some analyses suggest net migration rate may already be negative
- Chilling effect: legally authorized foreign-born workers avoiding job sites for fear of detention
- Industry pushing for market-based visa system with legal pathways for employer-sponsored workers
- Source: IndexBox analysis of NAHB/Census data, April 18, 2026

## Novel Analysis Opportunity
Calculate the per-home cost impact: if labor is 40% of home construction cost, and labor costs rise 10-15% due to shortage, that's 4-6% on the total home price. For a median-priced new home ($420K-$450K), that's $17,000-$27,000 per house directly attributable to labor shortage. Nobody has done this math explicitly with the immigration enforcement link.

Cross-reference: drywall and roofing are >50% immigrant workforce. These are the trades with the longest current wait times. Correlation isn't causation, but the Minnesota anecdote + NBER causal study closes the loop.

## Strongest Counterargument
The enforcement argument is that unauthorized employment suppresses wages for native workers and that legal pathways should replace unauthorized ones. The NBER study directly addresses and refutes the "replacement" hypothesis, but the wage suppression argument has some economic support. However, in construction specifically, wages have been RISING even with immigrant labor — the issue is bodies, not pay rates. Nobody's refusing to roof because the pay is too low; they're refusing because the work is physically brutal and there simply aren't enough people willing to do it.

## Limitations
- NAHB data is 2024 (latest available); 2025-2026 numbers are projected/estimated
- NBER study covers Jan 2024–Nov 2025; no data on 2026 enforcement effects yet
- Minnesota anecdote is one market; other metros may have different dynamics
- Cost-per-home calculation uses industry averages; actual impact varies by metro, trade mix, project type
- No direct data on how many construction workers have been deported vs. self-deported vs. stayed but reduced hours
