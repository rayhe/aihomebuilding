# Research: AI-Powered Property Tax Appeals — The $339 Billion Blind Spot

## Angle
40% of US homes may be overassessed, yet only 3-5% of homeowners appeal. AI-powered platforms like Ownwell are automating the protest process with 86% success rates, exposing a massive structural inefficiency in residential property tax assessment. The regressive nature of assessments (cheaper homes taxed at higher effective rates) makes this an equity issue, not just a savings opportunity.

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? YES — every homeowner pays property taxes. This article gives them actionable tools to fight overassessment and specific numbers on potential savings.

## Novel Contribution
Original analysis: Calculate the "appeal gap" — the difference between how many homeowners COULD save vs how many actually do. At 40.5% potentially overassessed and 3-5% filing, that means ~35-37% of homeowners are leaving money on the table. At $539 median savings, that's billions annually. Cross-reference this with the racial equity data showing communities of color 33-40% less likely to protest, creating a regressive tax burden that AI platforms could theoretically democratize.

## Strongest Counterargument
AI property tax appeal platforms take 25% of savings — effectively a tax on people's lack of knowledge about a free process. The Harris County data shows DIY homeowners save MORE ($7,889 vs $5,125). Ownwell and its competitors profit from information asymmetry they could eliminate by simply educating homeowners. The Lanham Act lawsuit (Texas Tax Protest v. Ownwell) also raises questions about marketing claims.
