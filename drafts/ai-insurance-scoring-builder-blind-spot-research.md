# Research: AI Insurance Scoring — The Builder's Blind Spot

## Angle
Insurance companies now score individual homes from satellite imagery, lidar, and AI-driven climate models — pricing risk at the property level in real time. Builders design homes to pass building codes but have no visibility into what the insurer's AI will charge the buyer. The gap between "code compliant" and "insurable at a reasonable rate" is the builder's most expensive blind spot — and it's widening fast.

## Journalist
**Priya Greenwood** — Sustainability & Green Building. Climate risk, real cost impact, evidence-based urgency.

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? **YES.** If buying new construction, ask the builder what insurance will cost. If building, design for insurability — not just code. The new-construction discount is real (35-38%) but only if you build to the features insurers reward.

## Skepticism Points
- AI property scoring has equity/fairness concerns — lower-income neighborhoods, older housing stock
- Not all builders can absorb the cost of premium-reducing features
- Building codes are slow to incorporate insurability metrics
- The "new construction advantage" assumes code-compliant modern materials — but code minimum ≠ insurance optimal
- Cotality's approach is proprietary; builders can't access the same scoring models
- The feedback loop is broken: builders don't see the insurance price, insurers don't influence the design

## Actionable Takeaways
1. **For buyers:** Get an insurance quote BEFORE closing, not after. Budget for $3,000+/year. Ask the builder which specific features lower premiums (impact-rated roof, closed eaves, defensible space, modern electrical).
2. **For builders:** The 35% new-construction discount is real — but it's not automatic. Design to IBHS FORTIFIED standards where possible. Every $1 in resilience features saves the buyer $3-5 in insurance over a decade.
3. **For anyone in wildfire/wind/hail zones:** The premium gap between mitigated and unmitigated homes is widening annually. The time to retrofit is before your insurer reassesses.
