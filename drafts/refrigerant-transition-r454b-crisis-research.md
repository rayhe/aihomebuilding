# Research: R-454B Refrigerant Transition Crisis — Residential Builder Impact

## Topic
The AIM Act-mandated transition from R-410A to R-454B/R-32 refrigerants in residential HVAC is creating a supply chain crisis that's hitting new home builders hard in 2025-2026. The environmental mandate is sound (78% GWP reduction), but execution has been chaotic.

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? **YES**. Every new home built after Jan 1, 2025 must use A2L refrigerant equipment. Builders face equipment shortages, price spikes, and new safety requirements (leak detectors). Homebuyers absorb these costs.

## Primary Sources

### 1. EPA / AIM Act
- American Innovation and Manufacturing (AIM) Act passed in 2020 as part of Consolidated Appropriations Act, 2021
- Directs EPA to phase down HFC production/consumption by 85% over 15 years
- Key deadline: January 1, 2025 — manufacturers can no longer produce new residential HVAC equipment charged with R-410A
- Equipment must use refrigerants with GWP ≤ 700 (R-410A is 2,088)
- Source: EPA climate-hfcs-reduction/aim-act

### 2. R-454B vs R-410A — Key Numbers
| Property | R-410A | R-454B | R-32 |
|---|---|---|---|
| GWP | 2,088 | 466 | 675 |
| GWP reduction | baseline | 78% lower | 68% lower |
| Flammability class | A1 (non-flammable) | A2L (mildly flammable) | A2L (mildly flammable) |
| Efficiency | baseline | comparable to ~5% better | comparable to slightly better |
| Major OEMs using | legacy only | Carrier, Lennox, Trane | Goodman/Daikin |
- Source: EPA AIM Act docs, AHRI research data, ACDirect.com guide

### 3. R-454B Shortage Crisis (ACHR NEWS, 2025)
- "The R-454B refrigerant shortage is the HVAC story of 2025" — ACHR NEWS
- Causes: cylinder shortage + unexpected demand surge for R-410A units in H2 2024 + surge in R-454B demand
- Contractor hoarding (COVID toilet paper analogy)
- Trane (May 7, 2025): temporarily increasing factory pre-charge from standard 15-foot to longer line sets, with per-unit surcharge
- Carrier (May 16, 2025): increasing pre-charge from 15-foot to 30-foot line set on most residential splits, no surcharge
- Source: achrnews.com/articles/164654

### 4. Honeywell 42% Surcharge (April 2025)
- April 11, 2025: Honeywell 42% surcharge on R-454B
- February 2025: 15% increase
- March 2025: 8% increase
- Cumulative: ~65% price increase in 3 months
- Cited "unprecedented demand" that domestic production cannot meet
- Rising raw material costs + tariff anticipation
- Source: achrnews.com/articles/164475

### 5. OEM Earnings Calls (Q1 2025)
- Lennox: 2% YoY revenue increase to $1.1B, driven by R-454B product mix
- ~50% of Lennox equipment sales in Q1 2025 were R-454B product
- R-410A inventory "nearly depleted"
- Wall Street happy: OEMs beating expectations
- Source: achrnews.com/articles/164566

### 6. A2L Safety Requirements
- UL 60335-2-40: new safety standard for A2L refrigerant equipment
- Indoor units must include built-in refrigerant leak detection
- Leak detector must trigger equipment shutdown if refrigerant detected
- "Mildly flammable" — requires ignition source, propagation is slow
- In practice: very low risk in residential settings
- New York City published A2L heat pump design guidance (Rev1, 2025)
- Source: UL.com, NYC HPD guidance

### 7. Builder/Homebuyer Impact
- Equipment cost increase: exact premium unclear but Honeywell's 65% cumulative refrigerant price hike + OEM surcharges = $500-1,500 more per installation
- Lead times: cylinders stretching to months for some contractors
- Cooling season 2025: peak demand coincides with worst of shortage
- New construction advantage: builders ordering in bulk/advance have better access than replacement market
- Code variation: some states adopted updated mechanical codes for A2L, others haven't, creating jurisdictional confusion

## Novel Contribution
Most coverage focuses on homeowners with existing systems ("should I panic?"). We focus on the builder/new construction angle:

2. The environmental irony: a green mandate creating panic buying and waste
3. Leak detector requirements as a hidden cost in new construction
4. Which builders are winning (those who locked in supply) vs losing
5. The sustainability math — is the GWP reduction worth the transition pain?
