Meta Will Pay You to Learn Construction. Your Homebuilder Can't.

By Marcus Washington · June 14, 2026

On June 8, Meta announced it would pay for your plane ticket, your hotel room, your meals, and five weeks of construction training at a facility in Louisiana, Ohio, Indiana, or Texas. Finish, and you get a job. No prior experience required. A $115 million first-year investment backs the program, called America's Workforce Academy, open to veterans, recent graduates, career changers, and anyone else who wants in.

Five days later, Google announced $50 million in skilled-trades training for electricians, plumbers, pipe fitters, and welders, partnering with the International Brotherhood of Electrical Workers and North America's Building Trades Unions. Meta had already pledged a separate $250 million for broader data center construction training, and Oracle and Microsoft outlined their own workforce pipelines earlier this year, bringing combined spending past $400 million from four companies alone, every dollar aimed at one thing: building data centers.

Not houses.

$10.8 billion
Annual cost of the skilled labor shortage to U.S. homebuilders, according to the Home Builders Institute's Fall 2025 Construction Labor Market Report. That figure includes $2.7 billion in higher carrying costs from construction delays and $8.1 billion in lost single-family home production, amounting to roughly 19,000 homes that do not get built each year because there are not enough workers to build them.

An Offer Nobody in Residential Can Match

Meta's pitch is blunt and effective. Show up with nothing, leave with credentials from the National Center for Construction Education and Research, walk into a full-time job with a general contractor building Meta's data center network. CBRE manages logistics, while the U.S. Hispanic Chamber of Commerce, the National Urban League, and local workforce organizations handle recruitment in each pilot state. Meta covers every cost: tuition, airfare, lodging, a daily stipend during training.

A residential homebuilder in Columbus, Ohio, cannot do this.

A custom home builder running three to five projects at a time cannot offer free training with guaranteed employment, cannot fly apprentices to a centralized facility, cannot partner with CBRE to manage the logistics, and cannot spend $115 million on workforce development because most residential builders' entire annual revenue would not cover Meta's program catering budget. Median revenue for a single-family homebuilder in the United States runs under $5 million. Meta's one-year training investment exceeds the combined annual revenue of twenty-three such firms.

And this asymmetry matters, because Meta is recruiting from the same communities where residential framers, electricians, and plumbers are already scarce.

Already Happening

"The housing market is sluggish right now," Edward Brady, CEO of the Home Builders Institute, told Homes.com in March. "And if they don't have the work, they're going to leave the residential industry and go to the commercial. Then, residential construction suffers. Those commercial jobs are more stable and, in many cases, better paying."

Numbers confirm what Brady is describing. Residential construction workers earn $39.40 an hour nationally, a figure that looks reasonable until you compare it to what data center projects are paying. In Northern Virginia, the densest data center corridor on Earth, electricians wiring server infrastructure command $45 to $55 an hour with overtime guarantees that push annual compensation past $120,000, while a residential electrician wiring three-bedroom houses in the same labor market earns $38 to $44, with gaps between projects and no stipend for showing up.

In February, construction shed 11,000 jobs overall, according to the Bureau of Labor Statistics, but that aggregate figure masked a shift: residential was contracting while commercial, driven by data center and infrastructure spending, was growing. Workers were not leaving construction. They were leaving houses.

349,000
New construction workers needed this year alone to meet demand, according to Associated Builders and Contractors. That is before accounting for the 723,000 workers the industry must hire annually to replace retirements and departures, per NAHB analysis of Bureau of Labor Statistics projections. Homebuilders are competing for those workers against companies that can afford to pay trainees a stipend just to learn.

What $300 Million Buys When Spent on Data Centers

Meta's AWA trains workers in fiber-optic installation, electrical systems, welding, and plumbing, none of which are data-center-specific skills. A welder certified through Meta's program can weld pipe in a server cooling system or in a residential hydronic heating loop. An electrician trained on data center power distribution understands panel sizing, load calculations, and NEC compliance just as well as one who learned on houses. Portable credentials. Workers who could go anywhere.

But the incentive structure is not portable. Meta guarantees a job. Homebuilders guarantee nothing except a phone call when the next project breaks ground, if it breaks ground. Meta flies you to training. Homebuilders expect you to live near the jobsite. Meta pays you to learn. Homebuilders pay you to produce, and only once there is something to produce.

Predictable result. A 22-year-old in Baton Rouge choosing between Meta's free five-week program with a guaranteed job and cold-calling residential contractors to ask if they need a helper is not wrestling with a difficult decision, and thousands of potential residential workers are going to make the obvious choice, because Meta has spent $115 million to ensure they do.

Worse Math Ahead

Start with the deficit. Builders estimate the United States is short 1.5 million homes. Realtor.com puts the number at 4.03 million. NAHB says regulatory costs alone add $131,734 to the average new home, which is 26.4% of the $499,500 sticker price, up 40% from five years ago. On Capitol Hill last week, 1,100 builders and remodelers told lawmakers they need 2.2 million additional construction workers over the next three years to close the gap.

Now overlay the data center expansion. Four thousand one hundred forty-nine active data centers in the United States, another 2,788 planned or under construction, according to the American Edge Project. Those facilities are projected to create 5.4 million temporary and permanent jobs and generate $27 billion in tax revenue over the next decade. In Cheyenne, Wyoming, a developer proposed a "temporary workforce housing complex" for 5,600 data center construction workers, a complex that, if built, would be larger than 84 of Wyoming's incorporated cities.

Five thousand six hundred workers building server farms in a state with a population of 584,000, while the country cannot build enough houses for the people who already live here.

What Homebuilders Would Need to Compete

Residential construction has no centralized workforce development infrastructure comparable to what Big Tech is assembling in a single quarter. HBI operates the largest residential training program in the country, running through Job Corps centers, community colleges, and correctional facilities, on a budget that represents a fraction of Meta's single-year commitment. NAHB's legislative priorities include workforce development funding, but the ask is for federal support, not private capital, because no individual homebuilder or even consortium of builders can match the spend.

Brady laid out what a serious response would require: start in middle school with trade exposure, carry it through high school shop programs (most of which were shuttered decades ago), fund apprenticeships with stipends, and apply this consistently across all fifty states instead of the patchwork that exists now.

"You may get a great value in Tennessee, but you're not going to get it in New York," Brady said. "There should be some level of consistency."

None of that is happening at the scale or speed required. Google's $50 million and Meta's $365 million in combined workforce spending will produce measurable results within a year, while residential construction's best-case path to a comparable training pipeline requires federal legislation, multi-year appropriations, state-level implementation, and coordination among thousands of independent builders who are, by definition, competing with each other for the same labor.

In Big Tech's Defense

A fair counterargument: every trained construction worker is a net gain for the industry, regardless of where they start, because workers trained in data center construction learn skills transferable to residential work, and some will eventually migrate to homebuilding when data center projects slow, as they inevitably will. Residential construction's immigrant share has hit a historic 25.5%, and broader training programs that welcome veterans, career changers, and first-generation trades workers expand the total labor pool in ways that benefit every sector of construction over time. Google's partnership with NABTU's TradesFutures program specifically targets registered apprenticeship placement, a model that builds durable career pathways rather than project-specific labor.

Moreover, the housing slowdown itself bears partial responsibility for the labor drain. If mortgage rates drop and single-family starts recover, residential wages will rise, and some data-center-trained workers will follow the money back to houses.

Reasonable argument. May prove correct in the long run. Also useless to the builder in Columbus who needs a framing crew next month and just watched his best prospect board a flight to Meta's training center in Louisiana.

What Nobody Wants to Say

Construction labor is not a renewable resource with a fixed replenishment rate. It is a population of human beings making career decisions based on the information in front of them, and right now, that information says: go build data centers. Higher pay. Predictable hours. Free training. An employer who will fly you there and feed you while you learn. Against that pitch, "come frame houses for $39.40 an hour, no benefits, hope the market picks up" is not a competing offer.

Companies spending $400 million to train workers are the same companies whose AI models generated the data center demand that created the labor competition now stripping workers from residential construction. Meta needs data centers to run the AI that powers Instagram, WhatsApp, and its smart glasses platform. Google needs them for Gemini and cloud services. Workforce programs are not philanthropy. They are supply chain logistics for a product these companies cannot ship without physical infrastructure, and the labor to build that infrastructure has to come from somewhere.

It is coming from your neighborhood's housing supply.

Limitations of This Analysis

Big Tech workforce training programs are days to weeks old, and no employment data exists yet on how many participants they attract, how many complete training, or what percentage would have otherwise entered residential construction. Wage comparisons between residential and data center construction use national averages from HBI's analysis of federal data; local markets vary substantially, and data center construction wages in Northern Virginia are not representative of wages in rural Louisiana, where Meta's first AWA cohort will train. HBI's 19,000-homes-per-year figure represents lost production attributable to labor shortages broadly, not specifically to competition from data center construction. Immigration enforcement effects on the construction workforce, cited by Brady, are qualitative observations from an industry leader, not quantified in published research. NAHB's $131,734 regulatory cost figure reflects a national average and is not disaggregated by state or local jurisdiction.
