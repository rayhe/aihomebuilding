# Research: Homes Take Longer to Build Despite Getting Smaller — AI Scheduling Gap

## Core Thesis
Census Bureau data shows single-family construction time increased 36% since 1993 (5.6 → 7.6 months) even as average home size has DECREASED. The extra months aren't from bigger houses. They're from regulatory complexity, inspection requirements, and labor scarcity. AI scheduling tools exist but target commercial projects — residential builders still run on whiteboards and text messages.

## Original Analysis: The $5.3 Billion Carrying Cost

If the 1993 build time of 5.6 months is the baseline and 2024's is 7.6 months:
- Delta: 2.0 extra months per home
- Average construction loan amount: ~$400,000 (conservative, given median new home price ~$425K)
- Construction loan rate: ~8-9% (2024-2026 environment)
- Monthly carrying cost at 8%: $400,000 × 0.08 / 12 = $2,667/month
- Extra cost per home: $2,667 × 2 = $5,333
- Homes started in 2024: ~1.013 million single-family units (Census)
- Total industry carrying cost of extra time: ~$5.4 billion/year

This doesn't include:
- Subcontractor mobilization/demobilization costs
- Extended general conditions (trailer, dumpster, port-a-john)
- Project manager salary allocated over longer timeline
- Opportunity cost of capital tied up longer

Conservative total impact: $7-8 billion annually in waste from construction timeline inflation.
