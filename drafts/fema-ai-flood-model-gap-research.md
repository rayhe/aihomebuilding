# Research: FEMA vs AI Flood Model Gap

## Core Thesis
FEMA flood maps classify 8.7M properties as high-risk. AI-powered flood models (First Street Foundation) identify 14.6M — nearly 70% more. Approximately 6 million homeowners are unaware of their actual flood risk. Meanwhile, developers, lenders, and insurers have access to private AI flood models that give them an information advantage over individual homebuyers. The regulatory disclosure landscape is a patchwork — 21 states have zero statutory flood disclosure requirements.

## Primary Sources

### First Street Foundation Flood Model
- Covers 142 million properties in contiguous US
- Uses current climate data, precipitation modeling, sea-level rise projections
- Assigns Flood Factor score (1-10) based on 30-year cumulative flood risk
- Identifies 14.6M properties with substantial risk vs FEMA's 8.7M = 70% more
- By 2050, projects 16.2M properties at substantial risk
- Available on Realtor.com since 2020 (but not all real estate sites)
- Source: First Street Foundation, Scientific American

### FEMA Maps
- Many maps based on data 20+ years old
- Don't account for sea-level rise or changing precipitation patterns
- Don't model flooding at individual property level
- Not designed to be flood predictors — based on historical flooding
- Zones: A/V = high risk (flood insurance mandatory with federal mortgage), X = moderate-to-low risk

### Risk Rating 2.0
- Implemented October 2021
- Shifted from zone-based to property-specific pricing
- Uses multiple flood frequencies (surge, tsunami, coastal erosion, pluvial, riverine)
- Incorporates rebuild costs, structure-specific factors
- Up to 80% of policyholders saw rate changes (per QuoteWizard)
- Some increases up to $100/month
- Statutory annual increase cap: 15% for most policies

### Disclosure Laws (NRDC/Columbia Law School analysis)
- 21 states have ZERO statutory/regulatory flood disclosure requirements
- Only 10 states require disclosure of actual flooding history
- Florida — most hurricane-vulnerable state — has NO mandatory disclosure
- New York formerly allowed $500 opt-out (fixed 2023)
- Top states: Louisiana, Mississippi, Oklahoma, Texas
- 74% of Americans support federal disclosure mandate (Urban Institute)
- No federal law requires natural hazard risk disclosure

### Flood Cost Data
- $845 billion in flood losses since 2000 (Urban Institute)
- Flooding = costliest natural disaster type in US
- $1 trillion+ inflation-adjusted since 1980 (Mapbox/First Street)
- 25% of NFIP claims come from outside FEMA high-risk zones
- 41+ million Americans live in flood zones

### Information Asymmetry
- Developers and banks have had access to private AI flood models for years
- "For-profit companies and commercial actors are taking this data... and using it to have an advantage to buy or sell homes" — Matthew Eby, First Street
- "Democratization of this data is how we like to think about it" — Eby
- "Unfortunately, inaccurate FEMA flood maps and nonexistent or weak real estate disclosure laws make it extremely difficult for home buyers" — Joel Scata, NRDC

### First Street / FEMA Discrepancy Sources
- FEMA doesn't map precipitation as standalone risk
- FEMA has unmapped areas
- FEMA doesn't incorporate current/future climate data
- An additional 5.7M properties beyond FEMA maps are in 100-year floodplain (Urban Institute framing)

## Article Angle
For homebuyers and builders: the flood map your lender uses to decide whether you need flood insurance is likely outdated. AI models show dramatically different risk. And depending on what state you're in, the seller may not be legally obligated to tell you the house flooded three times.

## Actionable Takeaways
1. Check First Street's Flood Factor (floodfactor.com) for ANY property before buying — free
2. Don't rely solely on FEMA zone designation — 25% of flood claims come from "low risk" zones
3. If building new: spec foundation elevation above FEMA BFE, accounting for climate projections
4. Check your state's disclosure requirements — in 21 states, seller doesn't have to tell you about past floods
5. Budget for flood insurance even outside FEMA zones — Risk Rating 2.0 prices by property, not zone
6. Builders: First Street data is free and can inform site selection and foundation design
