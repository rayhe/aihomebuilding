Your Insurance Company Added a Clause About AI. It Takes Effect at Renewal. You Probably Won't Read It.

By Catherine Chen · June 7, 2026

Somewhere in the stack of endorsements attached to your commercial general liability policy, there may now be a form labeled CG 40 47 01 26. It is two pages long, carries no dramatic title, and will not trigger an alert from your insurance broker or generate a phone call from your carrier explaining what changed. It simply appears at renewal, tucked between the additional insured endorsement and the pollution exclusion you have never read either, and it says this: if something goes wrong because of generative AI, your insurance does not cover it.

Not might not cover it. Does not.

What ISO Actually Wrote

ISO, now a division of Verisk Analytics, publishes the standardized policy language that roughly four out of five U.S. property and casualty insurers use as their starting point. In July 2025, Verisk filed a multistate package of new endorsements addressing AI risk. Three of them landed in the commercial general liability space: CG 40 47, CG 40 48, and CG 35 08, all taking effect in January 2026.

CG 40 47 is the one that should keep you awake, because the way it defines its key term determines whether virtually every AI tool in your workflow falls under its scope. It defines "generative artificial intelligence" as "a machine-based learning system or model that is trained on data with the ability to create content or responses, including but not limited to text, images, audio, video, or code." Read that definition again, slowly, because it captures every AI tool you have touched in the last eighteen months: ChatGPT, which your estimator uses to draft scope narratives; Togal.AI, which your takeoff team uses to measure plans; whatever code-compliance chatbot your architect tested last quarter and then forgot to mention in the project documentation. An analysis by Cohen Seglias, a construction law firm, noted that the language "may be broad enough to successfully bar coverage for claims involving any aspect of AI, generative AI, or large language models."

The exclusion applies to both Coverage A, which handles bodily injury and property damage, and Coverage B, which covers personal and advertising injury claims. In construction terms, that means a structural calculation error, a hallucinated building code reference, a flawed load schedule, a defective specification generated or influenced by AI could all fall outside your policy.

Who Is Already Adopting It

CG 40 47 is optional, meaning carriers choose whether to attach it. That optionality is both the good news and the trap, because you have no way of knowing whether your carrier has adopted it until you read every endorsement page of your renewed policy or until a claim gets denied and your adjuster points you to the clause you missed.

According to Business Insurance, "a few carriers" had begun adopting the ISO exclusions by April 2026, with John Farley, managing director of Arthur J. Gallagher's cyber practice, describing the adoption as early-stage but warning that "we're just at the very beginning and we have to watch this very closely." Separately, The Insurer reported that at least half a dozen insurers filed to adopt Verisk's new wordings, while individual carriers including WR Berkley, Cincinnati Financial, Frederick Mutual, and Philadelphia Insurance filed their own AI exclusion language. Some of those filings go further than ISO's standard language. Berkley and Cincinnati proposed exclusions that cover the use, deployment, or development of AI in any form, not just generative models.

One carrier attempted to impose what industry sources described as an "absolute AI exclusion," barring coverage for any claim with any connection to AI whatsoever. Broker pushback forced a withdrawal, but the fact that a carrier wrote it, filed it, and expected regulators to approve it tells you where the underwriting sentiment is heading even if the market has not fully arrived there yet.

Why Construction Gets Hit Harder

A marketing firm that uses AI to write ad copy faces reputational risk if the copy is wrong. A construction company that uses AI to verify a fire-rating requirement faces a building that burns, and the physical consequences of AI errors in construction are categorically different from those in most industries because CGL policies exist precisely to cover bodily injury and property damage claims. Excluding AI from that coverage leaves the highest-consequence AI users with the largest gap, which is why construction law firms across the country have started flagging specific scenarios that demonstrate how quickly a routine AI interaction becomes a six-figure uninsured liability.

A general contractor asks ChatGPT whether a particular wall assembly meets the fire-resistance requirements of IBC Section 706 for a specific occupancy type. ChatGPT returns a confident, well-formatted, completely wrong answer because it hallucinated a code cross-reference that does not exist. The contractor builds the wall according to the AI's recommendation. An inspector, reviewing the same plans the AI validated, misses the same error. A fire breaks out, and the flames spread through the assembly exactly where the fire-resistance rating should have stopped them. Under traditional CGL coverage, the resulting bodily injury and property damage claim would be covered, because the policy was designed for exactly this kind of construction error. Under CG 40 47, the insurer could argue that the defect is traceable to a generative AI output and deny the claim entirely.

Or consider something subtler: an architect uses an AI-powered plan review tool to pre-check a residential design against local zoning setback requirements. The tool flags the design as compliant, the city approves the plans, and the house gets built four feet into a required side-yard setback because the AI misinterpreted a conditional overlay district rule. Nobody discovers this until the neighbor tries to build a fence, hires a surveyor, and files a complaint that triggers a correction costing $180,000, and that claim, under the new endorsement, could be excluded entirely because the original error traces back to an AI-generated compliance determination that a human professional should have verified independently.

Shadow AI Makes It Worse

There is a second, quieter problem that most contractors have not even considered. Your employees are almost certainly using AI tools you did not approve, did not purchase, and do not know about. The Sheet Metal and Air Conditioning Contractors' National Association identified this as the "Bring Your Own AI" dilemma in a September 2025 analysis: a foreman uses a free AI app to generate a materials list, unknowingly uploading proprietary assembly details to a public model. An estimator asks an AI chatbot for a code interpretation that has not been verified for the local jurisdiction, receiving guidance that sounds authoritative, reads cleanly, and may be completely fabricated. A project manager pastes a subcontractor's bid into ChatGPT to summarize the scope, exposing confidential pricing to a system trained on user inputs.

Each of these actions, taken individually, seems harmless. None of them involve a deliberate decision to rely on AI for a safety-critical function, yet if a defect or claim can be traced back to any of them, the exclusion language does not distinguish between intentional reliance and casual use. "A machine-based learning system or model that is trained on data with the ability to create content or responses" does not care whether you used it carefully or carelessly. It only cares that you used it.

And here is the compounding problem that nobody in the construction industry has fully reckoned with: you cannot prove you did not use AI. If a claim arises and the insurer suspects AI involvement, the burden of demonstrating that no generative AI contributed to the disputed work product may fall on you. Without documentation policies, without AI use logs, without a formal record of which tools touched which deliverables, you are left arguing that nobody on your team used ChatGPT, Copilot, Gemini, or any other AI tool at any point during a twelve-month project. Good luck.

What You Should Do Before Your Next Renewal

1. Read your endorsements. Pull your current CGL policy. Check for CG 40 47, CG 40 48, CG 35 08, or any carrier-specific AI exclusion language, and read every endorsement page, not just the declarations summary that your broker sends with the renewal invoice. If your broker cannot tell you whether these forms are attached, find a broker who can.

2. Ask your carrier directly. Call your underwriter. Ask whether AI exclusions are being added at renewal and whether you can negotiate their removal or modification. Some carriers are attaching them automatically; others are holding off. You will not know which category yours falls into unless you ask.

3. Establish an AI use policy. Document which AI tools are approved for use on your projects, by whom, and for what purposes. Prohibit the use of generative AI for code compliance determinations, structural calculations, or any output that a licensed professional would need to stamp. Require human verification of every AI-generated deliverable, and keep records of who verified what.

4. Add AI use disclosure to your subcontractor agreements. If your sub uses AI to generate shop drawings, material selections, or scheduling inputs, you need to know about it before a claim forces the discovery. Amend your subcontract language to require disclosure of AI tool usage on your project.

5. Explore supplemental coverage. Technology errors and omissions policies, cyber liability policies, and captive insurance structures like 831(b) plans are emerging as options for the gap that CGL exclusions create. None of them are perfect, all of them are new, and pricing is still volatile because actuaries have almost no claims data to work with. But an imperfect backstop is better than discovering at claim time that your primary coverage has a hole.

6. Never rely on AI for code compliance without a licensed professional in the loop. This was good practice before CG 40 47 existed. Now it is an insurance requirement, whether your policy says so explicitly or not, because any AI-generated code determination that turns out to be wrong is a potential coverage exclusion trigger. A PE or RA stamp on a calculation is not just a professional obligation. It is your documentation that a human, not a model, made the final call.

The Strongest Counterargument

The insurance industry's position is not unreasonable. Underwriters price risk based on actuarial data, and AI-related construction liability has almost none. No major AI-caused construction defect has produced a landmark court ruling. No insurer has published loss data on AI-generated errors in building design or project management. Carriers are operating in genuine uncertainty, and the standard response to genuine uncertainty in insurance is exclusion first, coverage later, once the risk is measurable.

There is also a reasonable argument that the exclusion will force better AI hygiene across the industry. If contractors know that sloppy AI use voids their coverage, they will implement verification protocols, restrict unsupervised AI deployment, and treat AI outputs with the skepticism that the technology warrants. In this framing, CG 40 47 is not a punitive measure. It is a behavioral nudge that aligns incentives correctly: use AI carefully, or pay the consequences yourself rather than passing them to the insurance pool.

Whether that behavioral argument holds depends entirely on whether contractors actually read their endorsements. The evidence suggests they do not.

Limitations

This analysis relies on the endorsement language described in publicly available legal analyses by Cohen Seglias, Bernstein Shur, and trade publications including Business Insurance and The Insurer. We have not reviewed the full text of CG 40 47 01 26 directly, as ISO endorsement forms are proprietary and require a Verisk subscription to access. No court has yet ruled on the application of the Generative AI Exclusion to a construction defect claim, which means every coverage scenario described above is analytical projection rather than established precedent. Carrier adoption rates are based on industry reporting as of April and May 2026 and may have changed. The "shadow AI" risk analysis assumes that employees at construction firms use consumer AI tools without formal approval, a claim supported by the SMACNA analysis but not verified through independent survey data specific to residential construction. Finally, supplemental coverage options described here are emerging products with limited track records, and their effectiveness in covering AI-related construction claims has not been tested in litigation.
