Your Lot Has a Wildfire Risk Score. Your Builder Doesn't Know What It Is.

By Catherine Chen · June 24, 2026

Before the Palisades fire reached its first house in January 2025, an AI model had already tagged 1,430 properties inside the fire zone as elevated wildfire risk. Federal hazard maps classified those same properties as low risk or gave them no rating at all. Total assessed value of the homes the feds said were fine: $1.14 billion.

That model was ZestyAI's Z-FIRE, a machine learning system trained on more than 2,000 historical wildfires, satellite and aerial imagery, topography, vegetation density, and individual building characteristics. It scores nearly every residential property in the United States for wildfire risk. More than a third of California's insurance market already relies on it for underwriting, including the CA FAIR Plan, the state's insurer of last resort. And it was right: post-fire validation showed that 94 percent of the Palisades burn zone and 87 percent of the Eaton burn zone had been rated High or Very High by the model before a single ember landed.

Statewide, Z-FIRE flagged 1.2 million California homes as elevated risk that FEMA's National Risk Index classified as low, approximately $1 trillion in residential property value stranded between two systems that do not agree on what "risk" means. Federal assessment works at the census-tract level, averaging risk across entire neighborhoods; AI models assess each structure individually, property by property, roof by roof.

A Score You Never Consented To

You probably know you have a credit score. You probably do not know you have a wildfire risk score, and that the gap between those two numbers could determine whether your home is insurable at all.

Multiple AI-powered risk models now operate in the insurance market simultaneously, each using different methodologies, and none of them require your knowledge or consent to evaluate your property. Verisk's FireLine scores properties on a 0-to-30 scale. About 73 percent of scores land at 0 or 1, negligible to low risk. Fifteen percent hit 4 through 30, high to extreme. Some insurers draw the line at a FireLine score of 8; others refuse coverage at 2, and Bamboo Insurance recently sent agents a blanket email declining anything above a FireLine of 2, which categorizes roughly 27 percent of scored properties as uninsurable through that carrier.

ZestyAI works differently, because Z-FIRE incorporates building materials, defensible space conditions, and vegetation patterns visible in aerial imagery, so two homes on the same block can get meaningfully different scores depending on whether one has a wood-shake roof and the other has Class A tile. Insurance companies do not have to disclose your property's score to you, though many agents will share it if asked. Under California Insurance Code Section 929, carriers with $10 million or more in direct written premiums must report their fire risk scores and sources to the CDI biennially, but that reporting obligation runs to the regulator, not to the homeowner whose premium it shapes.

Nearmap sells a third model built from high-resolution aerial imagery and historical loss data: transparent, explainable scores covering roof condition, stains, vegetation encroachment, defensible space compliance, and even the presence of pools, solar panels, or trampolines. Property Guardian introduced a wildfire recurrence risk score in 2026, incorporating time-aware fuel dynamics rather than just historical burn perimeters, and during the January 2025 LA fires, its scores identified the areas of highest destruction two to three days before fire arrival.

Colorado's Code Takes Effect in Seven Days

On July 1, 2026, Colorado begins enforcing the nation's first statewide mandatory wildfire building code.

Adopted by the Colorado Wildfire Resiliency Code Board in July 2025, the CWRC required local jurisdictions to adopt it by April 1, 2026, and enforcement starts next week. It applies to all new construction and significant remodels involving 25 percent or more of a home's exterior within designated wildland-urban interface zones. Existing homes are grandfathered unless they undergo major renovation.

Requirements are tiered by fire intensity classification: in low-fire-intensity WUI zones, Class 1 structural hardening mandates Class A roof coverings, noncombustible gutters, ember-resistant and flame-resistant vents, while moderate and high-intensity zones trigger Class 2 hardening with additional material and site requirements. Every property faces a three-year evaluation cycle for ongoing compliance.

Colorado joins California and Utah as states with mandatory statewide wildfire construction standards. California's Chapter 7A has been the national benchmark since 2008, but it addresses the home in isolation. Colorado's code incorporates defensible space into the building regulation itself, dictating vegetation placement, plant species selection within five feet of the structure, and mesh size for vent covers to prevent ember intrusion. Crystal Lambert, who chairs the CWRC Board, told CPR News: "I think these codes will save lives, I think they will save property, I think they will save people money."

If you are a builder working in Colorado WUI zones and you have not read the CWRC, you have seven days.

What It Actually Costs

Less than you think. Substantially less than the insurance consequences of skipping it.

Headwaters Economics, working with IBHS and using RSMeans national construction cost data, has published the most rigorous cost comparison available. For a new home in Northern California built to "Enhanced" wildfire resistance, meaning noncombustible siding, Class A roof, ember-resistant vents, tempered dual-pane windows, metal gutters, and a 5-foot noncombustible perimeter, total additional cost over conventional construction: approximately $2,800. In Southern California, $2,800 gets you Enhanced; "Optimum" resistance (metal roof, full fiber-cement exterior, upgraded everything) adds $18,200 in NorCal and $27,100 in SoCal.

IBHS ran its own cost analysis specifically for the Altadena rebuild after the Eaton fire, and the numbers are striking in their modesty: materials for a home built to Wildfire Prepared Home Plus standards totaled $40,307, versus $37,889 for the same home built to traditional code, a materials difference of $2,418 on a structure designed to survive three hours of direct fire exposure. Labor costs remain essentially identical because installing a solid-core wood door takes the same time as installing a hollow one, and nailing fiber-cement siding to studs requires the same crew hours as nailing wood lap. Only eave enclosure adds meaningful labor, because traditional code does not require enclosed eaves at all.

IBHS Just Rewrote the Playbook

On June 9, 2026, IBHS expanded its Wildfire Prepared program from a single-home standard into a three-tier system. Wildfire Prepared Home still exists, now with Essential and Enhanced designation levels. Two entirely new standards launched alongside it: Wildfire Prepared Multifamily, covering duplexes, townhomes, apartments, and condominiums, and Wildfire Prepared Neighborhood, addressing community-scale fire spread through structure separation, fuel pathway reduction, and coordinated material standards.

KB Home has already built two neighborhoods to this standard. Dixon Trail in Escondido, California, is a 64-home community where every house holds WFPH+ certification, structures sit at least 10 feet apart, fencing is all-metal, and connective fuel pathways between buildings have been systematically eliminated. Stone Canyon in Cameron Park, near Sacramento, applies the same program to 24 single-story homes in the Sierra Nevada foothills, making it Northern California's first IBHS-certified wildfire-resilient community. Both communities exceed California's already-stringent Chapter 7A requirements.

Paradise, California, went further than certification. After the Camp Fire destroyed 90 percent of the town's homes in November 2018, Paradise passed a law requiring all rebuilt homes to meet IBHS Wildfire Prepared Home standards, not voluntary, not incentivized, mandatory, because the town decided that losing 11,000 structures and 85 lives was a sufficient building code review.

What This Means If You Are Building

Before you break ground in any WUI area, look up your property's Fire Factor score on Risk Factor (First Street Foundation's public tool) or ask your insurance agent for your Verisk FireLine or ZestyAI Z-FIRE score, because these scores are not academic abstractions; they are already determining your insurance premium, and in some cases whether you can get coverage through the voluntary market at all.

If you are building to code minimum in a state without California's Chapter 7A or Colorado's CWRC, consider building to IBHS Wildfire Prepared Home Enhanced standards anyway. The materials premium is $2,800 or less on new construction. Your insurance savings will likely exceed that within two to three years, and your resale position improves in any market where wildfire risk is priced into buyer psychology, which at this point includes most of the western United States.

If you are a production builder, consider that KB Home is already marketing wildfire resilience as a feature rather than absorbing it as a cost, and IBHS certification is visible, credible, and insurance-industry-backed. When buyers are choosing between your subdivision and the one down the road, "first IBHS-certified neighborhood in the county" is a sales tool that shows up in listing descriptions and appraisal reports long after the model homes close.

If you are buying and your builder has never heard of Z-FIRE, FireLine, or IBHS Wildfire Prepared: ask why. A builder working in WUI zones in 2026 who cannot discuss AI risk scoring and current hardening standards is operating with a blind spot that your insurer does not share.

Why the Scores Are Not Enough

AI wildfire models are dramatically better than census-tract-level federal assessments, though that is a low bar given that the federal maps missed $1.14 billion in at-risk properties in a single fire zone. Z-FIRE's pre-fire accuracy in the LA burns is genuinely impressive, and property-level granularity represents a meaningful advance over the coarse geographic proxies that dominated risk assessment for decades, but impressive is not infallible, and several structural concerns deserve honest accounting.

Defensible space changes constantly. A homeowner who clears brush in March and lets it grow back by August has a risk profile that satellite imagery from February will not capture. ZestyAI and Nearmap update imagery periodically, but wildfire risk is a dynamic condition being measured with periodic snapshots. A score generated from six-month-old imagery is an assertion about a property that may no longer exist in that condition.

Model validation is also recursive: Z-FIRE proved accurate against the LA fires, but the model was partly trained on historical fire data that shares characteristics with the LA events. High-accuracy post-event validation on fires that resemble training data does not guarantee performance against novel fire behavior in areas with different fuel types, wind patterns, or topographic features. IBHS's Steve Hawks told The Builder's Daily that wildfire mitigation requires a "systems-based approach" because "under these extreme wildfires, when you have very strong winds, you have to address both the home and its surrounding area." No AI score captures the neighbor's unmaintained lot, the dead pine 80 feet uphill, or the propane tank behind the fence.

Third, NIST estimates 46 million residential structures sit in WUI areas, one-third of all U.S. homes, and scoring them is the easy part. Persuading their owners to spend $2,800 on hardening when the score is invisible and the fire is hypothetical requires something AI cannot provide: a political process that converts risk data into enforceable building standards. Colorado just did it; most states have not.

Limitations

This analysis relies on published cost data from IBHS and Headwaters Economics using RSMeans pricing, which reflects national and regional averages; actual material and labor costs vary significantly by market, supplier, and project scale. Z-FIRE performance statistics cited are from ZestyAI's own post-event validation and have not been independently audited by a third party for the specific LA fire events. Colorado CWRC enforcement timelines are as stated by the Wildfire Resiliency Code Board and participating jurisdictions, but actual enforcement capacity will vary by county. Insurance premium impact estimates are directional; no carrier has published a standardized discount schedule linked to IBHS certification or specific AI risk score improvements. NAHB's cost range of $1,827 to $44,888 for wildfire hardening reflects the full spectrum from minimal vent upgrades to complete exterior replacement with premium materials, and the wide range makes averages misleading for any specific project.
