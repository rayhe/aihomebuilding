# Research: AI-Powered Earth Construction Robotics — Terran Robotics

## Self-Critique Gate
- **Angle:** A robot in Central Texas is building homes from dirt — the same clay-straw-water mix humans used 10,000 years ago. Not 3D-printed concrete. Not steel framing. Adobe/cob shaped by an AI-powered cable robot running 24/7 with reinforcement learning and computer vision. IRC Appendix U makes it code-compliant. The material cost is literally zero because the clay comes from the building site excavation. Is this the path to genuinely zero-carbon housing, or a niche experiment that can't survive outside hot-dry climates?
- **Challenge:** We already covered 3D-printed homes (ICON, #7) and robotic bricklaying (Hadrian X, #5). Is this different enough? YES — fundamentally different material (earth vs. proprietary Lavacrete), different method (pick-and-place + hammering vs. extrusion), different code (IRC Appendix U cob vs. standard IRC), different cost structure (free on-site vs. $10K+ material), different environmental profile (80% vs. modest carbon reduction). Also the Appendix U code path is brand new, uncovered, and creates a genuine permitting maze.
- **Verdict:** PROCEED

- Reinforcement learning — gets better every day
- Runs autonomously 24/7
- First home completed April 2026 at Proto-Town
- Plans 20+ units over next year

### 3. IRC Appendix U — Cob Construction (Monolithic Adobe)
- First approved in the 2021 IRC by International Code Council
- Developed by Cob Research Institute
- Makes legal cob construction feasible across US — but requires state/jurisdiction adoption
- Compressive strength minimum: 60-85 psi per testing requirements
- Requires shrinkage tests and compressive strength checks
- Builder must test for 1-inch shrinkage
- Not all jurisdictions have adopted it — appendix adoption is opt-in
- Texas has favorable alternative building code provisions
- Washington Building Code Council delayed adoption — requested revisions to energy-code refs, inspection authority, seismic scoping
- Source: iccsafe.org (Building Safety Journal), motherearthnews.com, citizenportal.ai

### 4. Cob Thermal Performance (Academic Sources)
- Structural cob thermal conductivity: 0.4 W/(m·K) — about R-1.5 per inch (MDPI Energies 2021)
- A 24" cob wall ≈ R-3.6 — well below IRC code minimums for most climate zones
- BUT thermal mass effect: studies show 20-50% thermal load reduction vs conventional in hot/arid climates (ScienceDirect 2020)
- Phase change material integration can lower heating costs by 35%
- 75% less embodied energy during construction than conventional materials (Discover Magazine)
- Thermal mass works as flywheel — absorbs daytime heat, releases at night
- Poor insulation performance in cold climates — best suited for hot-dry regions
- Moisture vulnerability: cob + sustained humidity = degradation risk
- Source: MDPI Energies 14/7663, ScienceDirect (Dynamic behavior and economic analysis), espace2.etsmtl.ca

### 5. Embodied Carbon Comparison
- Terran claims 80% less embodied carbon than conventional construction
- Zero-mile sourcing: clay and soil from building site excavation
- No delivery trucks for concrete/lumber
- Walls replace framing, insulation, siding, AND drywall — single monolithic system
- Fireproof, mold-free, zero-VOC
- Source: Terran Robotics, TinyHouseTalk

### 6. Cost Context (2026)
- Average new home cost: $322,836 (Angi 2026)
- Average cost per square foot: $150-$300 (Autodesk 2026)
- Material costs risen 34% since 2020 (Terran claim)
- Construction labor shortage at 92% of firms reporting difficulty hiring (likely referencing NAHB/AGC data)
- Cob construction material cost: effectively near-zero for wall structure (earth is free)
- But: still need conventional roof, foundation, plumbing, electrical, windows, doors
- Cob walls replace: framing ($15-25/sqft), insulation ($1.50-3.50/sqft), siding ($6-15/sqft), drywall ($1.50-3/sqft)
- Combined conventional wall system cost: roughly $24-47/sqft
- Source: Angi, Autodesk, industry averages

### 7. Comparison to Other Construction Robots
- Hadrian X (FBR): 360 blocks/hour, masonry, conventional materials
- ICON Vulcan: 3D-printed concrete extrusion, proprietary Lavacrete
- ABB/Cosmic Buildings: AI-powered microfactory, modular, 70% faster, 30% cheaper (for LA wildfire rebuilds)
- Canvas: Drywall finishing robot, commercial focus
- Terran is unique: uses ancient material + modern AI; no proprietary material required

### 8. Limitations & Counterarguments
- R-value problem: cob walls don't meet energy code minimums in most US climate zones
- IRC Appendix U adoption is opt-in — many jurisdictions haven't adopted
- Moisture vulnerability limits geography (humid Southeast = risk)
- Seismic concerns — cob in high-seismic zones problematic (Washington state flagged this)
- Scalability: 20 units/year is tiny compared to production builders
- Aesthetics: not everyone wants adobe-style homes
- Resale/appraisal problem: same issue as 3D-printed homes — no comparable sales data
- Insurance/mortgage: non-traditional construction classification penalties
- Foundation, roof, MEP still conventional — cost savings limited to wall assembly
