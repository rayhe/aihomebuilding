Your Builder Bragged About a HERS Score of 45. The Materials He Used to Get There Emitted 55 Tons of Carbon Before You Moved In.

By Priya Greenwood • June 15, 2026 • 8 min read

In a warehouse laboratory outside Boston, a HERS rater named Sarah Kneeland is doing something that no residential energy assessor in America has done at scale before. She is measuring the carbon dioxide equivalent emitted by the physical materials in a newly built home, from the concrete in its foundation to the copper in its wiring, and she is producing a number that most builders have never seen and do not want to discuss.

Fifty-five and a half tons.

That is the average embodied carbon of a new single-family home in Massachusetts, according to the Massachusetts 100-Home Embodied Carbon Study released in December 2025. Funded by the Massachusetts Clean Energy Center, National Grid, and Eversource, the study is the first in the United States to measure both operational and embodied carbon emissions across a meaningful sample of newly constructed homes. And the number it produced upends a comfortable assumption that the building industry has carried for years: that a home's carbon footprint begins when someone turns on the furnace.

It does not. Thirty-two percent of a home's total carbon emissions over 25 years come from the materials used to build it. That carbon enters the atmosphere during mining, manufacturing, and transport, and it lands in the ledger before the drywall is finished. For the 100 homes in the Massachusetts study, the combined embodied carbon totaled 5,555 tons of CO₂ equivalent. A gasoline car would need to drive 14 million miles to produce that much.

The Insulation Paradox

The study's most uncomfortable finding is this: homes with the best operational energy ratings showed no improvement in embodied carbon compared to conventionally built homes. Read that again. A home with a HERS Index score of 40 and triple-pane windows and R-60 attic insulation emits, on average, the same amount of carbon from its materials as a code-minimum house next door.

Why? Because the materials that make a home energy-efficient are often the same materials that carry the highest embodied carbon. XPS rigid foam board, one of the most common insulation products in high-performance residential walls, carries a global warming potential of approximately 9,948 kilograms of CO₂ equivalent per 1,000 square feet of coverage. Closed-cell spray polyurethane foam clocks in at 4,306 kg CO₂e.

Dense-pack cellulose insulation achieves comparable R-values. Its embodied carbon is net negative. It stores more carbon in its fibers than was emitted to produce it.

This is not a marginal difference. On a 2,400-square-foot home insulated with XPS across walls and foundation, the insulation alone contributes roughly 48 metric tons of CO₂ equivalent, nearly the entire average embodied carbon budget of a complete house. Replace that XPS with dense-pack cellulose or wood fiber board, and the insulation contribution drops to zero or below. The delta is close to the weight of the rest of the building's material carbon combined.

The study found that three material categories account for 68 percent of all embodied carbon in new homes. Concrete leads at 39 percent. MEP systems (mechanical, electrical, plumbing) contribute 18 percent. Insulation adds 11 percent. Each of these categories has lower-carbon alternatives that are commercially available, and in many cases cost the same or less than the high-carbon defaults. The most common concrete mix used in the study sample carried a global warming potential 46 percent higher than the lowest-GWP alternative available from the same suppliers.

What Changed: Standards and Tools That Didn't Exist Last Year

Until recently, a builder who wanted to measure embodied carbon would have needed a consulting firm, a custom life cycle assessment, and several thousand dollars. The data formats were inconsistent, the methodology was debatable, and the effort was unjustifiable for a single-family project. That barrier is collapsing in 2026, driven by two developments that connect to the existing residential energy rating infrastructure.

The RESNET/ICC Standard 1550 is the first consensus standard for calculating and reporting the embodied carbon of homes. Developed by a committee that includes the Rocky Mountain Institute, Beazer Homes, the National Association of Home Builders, the California Energy Commission, and the International Code Council, the standard slots into the workflow that HERS raters already follow. It uses the same modeling data and reporting processes as ANSI/RESNET/ICC 301. A rater who already evaluates a home's energy performance can add an embodied carbon assessment using 60 to 70 percent of the information already in their files.

The Massachusetts study served as the standard's first real-world test drive. After completing five to ten assessments, participating raters reduced their completion time from 3.5 hours to 2.7 hours. Several finished in under two hours.

The BEAM Estimator, developed by Builders for Climate Action, is the calculator that makes 1550 assessments practical. It translates Environmental Product Declarations, the manufacturer-published carbon data sheets that most builders find impenetrable, into side-by-side material comparisons. A builder can model a baseline home, upgrade it to meet net-zero requirements, and then see what happens when cost-neutral material substitutions are applied. The tool integrates with Ekotrope, the energy modeling software already used by a majority of Massachusetts raters.

And a third tool, still in research but approaching deployment, suggests where this is headed. A team at the University of Bath led by Professor David Coley published a study in Architectural Engineering and Design Management in March 2026 demonstrating an AI system that predicts embodied carbon from natural language descriptions of buildings. An architect types a sentence describing a proposed house and its materials, and the model returns a credible carbon estimate within seconds. Trained on 150,000 synthetic building profiles, the system correctly identifies key materials from text 80 percent of the time and was tested by 43 building professionals who confirmed it fit their existing workflows. It is not a final answer. It is a conversational feedback loop that pushes carbon awareness to the earliest point in design, where the leverage to change things is greatest and the cost of changes is lowest.

The Numbers Behind the Trade-Offs

The Massachusetts study revealed variation that no previous research had quantified at this scale. The highest-embodied-carbon home in the sample, a sprawling residence exceeding 8,400 square feet with a walkout basement, extensive poured concrete, open-cell spray foam insulation, and a dual-fuel heating system, produced 144.3 tons of CO₂e. The lowest, a compact house under 700 square feet with minimal concrete and a single air-source heat pump, registered 18.5 tons. A factor-of-eight spread in a single building type.

Home configuration matters. Two-story detached homes averaged 59.2 tons. Single-story detached homes came in at 52.8 tons. Duplexes at 45 tons. Townhomes at 39 tons, with 26 percent lower MEP-system carbon, 32 percent lower building-enclosure carbon, and 38 percent lower operational carbon than the sample average. Shared walls are not just cheaper. They are measurably less carbon-intensive per unit.

The all-electric finding reinforced what energy modelers have been arguing for years, but with a carbon accounting dimension that changes the math. All-electric homes produced 60 percent lower total 25-year carbon emissions than fossil-fuel-only homes and 52 percent lower than mixed-fuel homes. The operational carbon difference is straightforward: electric homes averaged 57.7 tons over 25 years compared to 180.6 tons for fossil-fuel homes. But the study also found that all-electric homes had lower MEP-related embodied carbon, averaging 7.6 tons compared to 11.3 tons for mixed-fuel, because ductless heat pumps require fewer distribution components than furnace-and-duct systems.

The Counterargument, Taken Seriously

Not everyone believes embodied carbon should command this much attention. The case against making it a priority goes like this: operational carbon still dominates a home's lifetime emissions, especially for fossil-fuel homes. The electrical grid is decarbonizing every year, which means a home built today with high operational efficiency will emit progressively less carbon over its 50-to-80-year life. Embodied carbon, by contrast, is a one-time event. A home insulated with XPS foam will use less energy every winter for decades. The cumulative operational savings may exceed the upfront embodied penalty, particularly in cold climates where heating loads are heavy.

This argument has force. The Massachusetts study itself shows that all-electric homes dominate on total lifetime carbon regardless of their embodied carbon scores. If the goal is minimizing total emissions over a home's life, fuel choice matters more than material choice.

But there are two problems with using this as a reason to ignore embodied carbon. First, embodied emissions happen now. They are atmospheric reality by the time the permit closes. Operational improvements happen over decades, and they depend on grid decarbonization trajectories that no one controls. Second, the Massachusetts study's central finding is that reducing embodied carbon does not require sacrificing operational performance. Cellulose insulation achieves comparable R-values to XPS foam. Low-GWP concrete mixes meet the same structural specifications. The trade-off the counterargument assumes, operational excellence at the cost of high embodied carbon, is a false dilemma.

What This Means If You Are Building a Home

Three actions, ranked by impact and ease of implementation.

First: ask your concrete supplier for a low-GWP mix. Concrete accounts for 39 percent of a home's embodied carbon, and the most common mix in the Massachusetts sample carried a GWP 46 percent higher than the lowest available alternative. The structural performance is equivalent. The cost difference is negligible. If your builder does not know what a global warming potential number is, this is a signal worth paying attention to.

Second: specify cellulose or mineral wool insulation instead of XPS or closed-cell spray foam. This single decision can eliminate 30 to 50 percent of a home's total embodied carbon. The R-value per inch of cellulose (3.2-3.8) is lower than XPS (5.0), which means thicker walls. But wall thickness is a design decision, not a performance limitation, and the carbon savings are enormous.

Third: if your state has a HERS rating requirement, ask your rater about a RESNET 1550 embodied carbon assessment. The standard is nearing finalization, and early-adopter raters are already conducting assessments using the BEAM tool. The incremental cost is a few hours of rater time. The result is the first standardized, third-party-verified number that tells you how much carbon your home put into the atmosphere before you moved in.

Limitations of This Analysis

The Massachusetts study analyzed 100 homes. They were not randomly selected, and they may not represent the diversity of construction practices across the United States, particularly in regions with different climate zones, concrete mix traditions, or insulation preferences. The study measured cradle-to-gate emissions only (lifecycle stages A1 through A3), excluding construction-phase emissions, maintenance-related material replacement, and end-of-life demolition carbon. Biogenic carbon, the carbon stored in wood-framed walls and cellulose insulation, was excluded due to ongoing methodological disagreement about forestry accounting. MEP system carbon data is still thin, with limited product-specific environmental declarations available for HVAC equipment, plumbing fixtures, and electrical components. And the University of Bath's AI tool was trained on synthetic building data, not real-world projects. Its accuracy will improve when genuine embodied carbon datasets, which are still rare, become available for training.

What the study did prove, within its scope, is that embodied carbon is measurable at scale in residential construction, that HERS raters can do it without heroic effort, and that the number is large enough to change the definition of what a "green" home actually means.

For decades, the building industry measured one kind of carbon and ignored the other. The 55.5 tons that arrive before the moving truck are no longer invisible.
