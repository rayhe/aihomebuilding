# Research: AI Home Energy Score Disclosure Liability Gap

## Angle
Cities are mandating home energy score disclosure at point of sale. AI tools now generate these scores remotely from photos and public records. The accuracy gap between AI estimates and on-site assessments is 20-30%. Buyers are paying "green premiums" of up to 4% based on scores that may be significantly wrong. Nobody has clarified who's liable when the score misrepresents reality.

## Journalist
Catherine "Code" Chen — policy, legal, building codes

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? YES — directly affects any buyer or seller in a disclosure-required market. Actionable: know your rights, demand on-site assessments, understand the legal exposure.

## Primary Sources

### 1. Portland Home Energy Score Policy (2018)
- Source: portland.gov
- First major US city to mandate HES at point of sale
- 160,000 single-family homes, <2% had scores before mandate
- Score must appear in MLS listing Public Remarks
- Green Building Registry integration
- ~50 assessors in Portland
- URL: https://www.portland.gov/bps/climate-action/pdxhes/about-home-energy-score

### 2. Berkeley BESO Ordinance (Jan 1, 2026)
- Source: berkeleyca.gov
- Building Emissions Saving Ordinance
- Requires Home Energy Score PLUS upgrades at sale
- Options: complete upgrades (6 credits), qualify for heat pump exemption, or defer to buyer ($5,000 deposit, $2,500 each)
- Non-compliance: $150 filing fee, $5,000 escrow deposit as enforcement
- Extends to triplexes/fourplexes Jan 2028
- URL: https://berkeleyca.gov/construction-development/green-building/building-emissions-saving-ordinance-beso/time-sale-energy

### 3. RMI "MPG for Homes" Study
- Source: Rocky Mountain Institute
- Analyzed ~8,000 homes across 27 US states
- Compared automated estimates (ClearlyEnergy, UtilityScore) to DOE Home Energy Score on-site assessments
- **Key finding: 20-30% average absolute difference in total energy use**
- Nearly 3/4 of homes within 30% difference
- About half within 20% difference
- Only ~1/4 within 10% difference
- Automated tools better at energy COST estimates due to more granular utility rate data
- URL: https://rmi.org/is-there-a-trade-off-with-transparency-a-deep-dive-into-automated-home-energy-estimates/

### 4. UCL SERL Study — UK Energy Performance Certificates
- Source: University College London Smart Energy Research Lab
- 1,374 gas-heated British households
- EPCs vs smart-meter measured energy use
- Band C: 8% overprediction
- Bands F and G: 48% overprediction
- **Performance gap increases as rating worsens**
- Occupant behavior doesn't fully explain gap
- URL: https://www.ucl.ac.uk/bartlett/news/2023/apr/energy-performance-certificates-great-britain-overpredict-energy-use-study-finds

### 5. Notre Dame/U of Maryland AI Street View Study (2024)
- Source: ScienceDaily / Building and Environment journal
- AI analyzed Google Street View images of Chicago homes
- Predicted household energy expenses with 74% accuracy
- Used convolutional neural network to assess passive design (window size, type, shading)
- Combined with demographic data for 1,402 census tracts, ~300,000 households
- Purpose: identify energy-burdened neighborhoods
- Researchers: Ming Hu (Notre Dame), Chaoli Wang, et al.

### 6. Green Premium Data
- Portland data: green-certified homes sell for up to 4% more
- Based on analysis of 20+ worldwide studies
- A $500K home with high HES = up to $20K premium
- A $700K Berkeley home = up to $28K premium

### 7. DOE Home Energy Score Program Statistics
- Launched 2012
- 500+ assessors by 2017
- 75,000+ homes scored by Dec 2017
- On-site assessment takes ~1 hour
- 10-point scale
- Cost: $150-500 for on-site assessment (HomeAdvisor: average $437)

### 8. Massachusetts HEED Act
- Source: malegislature.gov (S.1761, S.1418)
- Proposed requiring energy assessment through Mass Save before listing
- No additional fees to seller (assessments free via program)
- Applies to <5 unit residential
- Shows legislative momentum

## Novel Analysis
Cross-reference green premium (4%) × accuracy gap (20-30%) = buyer's financial exposure:
- Portland median home ~$500K × 4% premium = $20K
- If score overpredicts by 25%: buyer paid $20K for ~$15K of actual efficiency value
- Annual energy cost difference: scored at $1,200/yr savings but actually delivers ~$900/yr
- 10-year phantom savings: $3,000
- Total buyer exposure: $5,000-$8,000 on a $500K home

## Strongest Counterargument
Automated scores, even with 20-30% error margins, are better than NO information. Before Portland's mandate, <2% of homes had any energy rating. The accuracy gap argument could paralyze disclosure efforts entirely, leaving buyers with zero transparency. Perfect shouldn't be the enemy of good.

## Limitations
- RMI study compared automated estimates to HES (itself a model, not metered data)
- UCL study is UK-specific; US building stock and climate zones differ significantly
- Green premium studies vary widely by market; 4% is high end
- No US case law specifically on energy score misrepresentation (Zillow Zestimate lawsuit is closest analogue but about property values, not energy)
- Notre Dame study was research, not a commercial product
