# Research: AI Insurance Exclusion & Construction Liability Gap

## Journalist: Catherine Chen (Policy & Regulation)
## Date: 2026-07-20

## Core Thesis
As of January 1, 2026, major E&O insurance carriers are actively excluding AI-related liabilities from professional liability policies for architects and engineers. At the same time, AI tools are being adopted across residential construction for specifications, design, and estimating. The result: a growing liability gap where AI-generated errors in your home's design or specifications may not be covered by anyone's insurance.

## Key Data Points

### Insurance Exclusion (PRIMARY SOURCE)
- **Verisk standardized AI exclusion forms CG 40 47 and CG 40 48** became effective January 1, 2026
- These provide ready-made language to exclude losses arising from generative AI use
- Specifically names tools like ChatGPT, Midjourney, DALL-E
- Major carriers filing: **Berkley, AIG, Great American**
- Source: Risk Specialty Group (Travis Landers, ARM, President & Founder)
- Quote: "AI creates liability exposures that traditional policies weren't designed to address. Firms renewing policies this quarter need to review the new language carefully."

### AI Reliability Data
- Stanford Law School: General-purpose AI hallucination rates **58-88%**
- Specialized "legal-grade" AI tools: error rates **20-33%**
- Construction-specific AI tools: no published reliability benchmark exists for residential specs

### Legal Framework
- US courts: Contractor remains liable for AI errors — "using AI does not lower that standard" (Suffolk Law Journal, Copper Mountain v. Industrial Systems)
- No US standard construction contract (AIA, EJCDC, ConsensusDocs) addresses AI usage, disclosure, or liability
- EU proposed AI Liability Directive would shift liability to AI developers (US has nothing comparable)
- MDPI Buildings paper (June 2026): First qualitative proportional-liability framework for GenAI in AEC lifecycle — identifies "autonomous hallucination entering safety-relevant design information" as a primary harm pathway

### Market Context
- AI construction market: $24.3 billion by 2030 (Mordor Intelligence)
- 500,000+ multi-housing units expected to be delivered in 2025 (NAIOP)
- AI being used for: proposals, estimates, blueprints, specifications, designs, plans, scheduling
- HCR Law (May 2026): "Traditional professional indemnity and construction insurance policies may not respond to AI related losses"

### Insurance Coverage Structure
- Standard A/E professional liability: $1M-$5M per claim
- Project-specific policy: ~$2M premium for $20M coverage (expensive)
- Owner's Protective Professional Indemnity (OPPI): costs ~half of project policy
- Key issue: AI exclusions may apply to ALL of these if the error originated from AI-generated output

## Original Contribution
Calculate the uninsured exposure for a typical custom home project:
- Average custom home: $400,000-$600,000 construction cost
- Design fees: 8-15% of construction ($32,000-$90,000)
- Typical A/E E&O coverage: $1M-$5M aggregate (shared across all projects)
- If architect uses AI for structural calcs, MEP specs, or code compliance checking and the output is wrong → claim may be excluded under new Verisk forms
- Result: Homeowner's only recourse is direct suit against architect (who may have no coverage) or contractor (who didn't generate the error)
- No state licensing board yet addresses AI use in stamped drawings

## Counterargument
The exclusions may be rational. Insurers can't price risk they can't quantify, and AI output reliability has no actuarial history. The exclusions may force the industry to develop proper validation protocols before coverage returns — similar to how cyber insurance evolved from blanket exclusions to risk-based underwriting with minimum security requirements. The coverage gap may be temporary and productive.

## Limitations
- Cannot independently verify which specific state insurance departments have approved the Verisk forms
- No public data on what percentage of A/E firms have already received AI exclusion endorsements on renewal
- Residential-specific AI adoption rates are estimates; most surveys cover commercial construction
- The Verisk forms are standardized templates — individual carrier implementations may vary
