# Research: AI Home Inspection Tools vs. the Inspection Waiver Epidemic

## Angle
Buyers are skipping home inspections at record rates to win bids — 52% waiver rate in 2022 bidding wars, 62% of first-time buyers skipped in hot markets. States are now legislating against it (Massachusetts banned seller-required waivers Oct 15, 2025; New York considering similar). Meanwhile, AI inspection tools are emerging that could make the whole debate moot: faster, cheaper, data-rich analysis that lets buyers inspect without slowing down the deal. The question: can AI close the gap between competitive bidding pressure and buyer protection?

## Kill Test
YES — directly helps homebuyers understand: should I use AI inspection tools, what do they cost, what are the limitations, and why skipping inspections entirely is a $63K gamble.

## Original Contribution (Novel Analysis)
Calculate the cost of skipping inspections at scale:
- 4.5M inspections/year at $450 avg = $2B spent on inspections
- But 52% waiver rate in bidding wars means ~2.3M transactions skipped
- At $63,688 average repair costs (OfferWise data), skipped inspections represent up to $146B in undiscovered repair liability annually
- Even if only 34% have major issues (Gitnux), that's $50B+ in surprise costs
- An AI pre-screen at $29-100/report could cover those 2.3M skipped deals for $67-230M — 0.05-0.15% of the risk exposure

## Limitations to Acknowledge
- OfferWise's $63,688 figure is average across all reports, not just waived ones — self-selection bias (buyers who inspect may target riskier properties)
- Waiver statistics from 2022 peak market — 2026 rates likely lower in cooling markets
- AI tools are analyzing existing reports, not replacing physical inspections
- No third-party audit of AI defect detection accuracy claims
- Massachusetts law is 7 months old — too early for outcome data

## Strongest Counterargument
AI inspection analysis is fundamentally parasitic on human inspection data. OfferWise analyzes reports that human inspectors wrote. RealReports costs come from human contractor bids. If AI makes inspections so fast and cheap that inspectors can't sustain a business, the training data dries up. The inspection workforce is already aging (average age 52) with only 3,200 new licenses per year against 8% turnover. AI could accelerate the very shortage it claims to solve.
