# Research: AI Safety Cameras on Construction Sites — Surveillance vs. Protection

## Journalist: Marcus "Steel" Washington (Workforce & Labor)
## Article #200

## Angle
AI camera surveillance is being sold as "safety" on construction sites. CompScience, OSHA, and insurers are deploying computer vision that can spot a missing harness or hard hat before someone falls. But no one asked the workers. The same cameras that catch fall hazards also enable productivity tracking, bathroom break timing, and phone usage monitoring. Who benefits — and who loses power?

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? YES — builders are being asked to deploy AI safety cameras. Workers on residential sites deserve to know what these cameras can/can't do with their data. Homeowners may face insurance requirements that mandate camera monitoring on their build. Direct impact on costs, timelines, and who wants to work your job.

## Original Contribution (Novel Analysis)
Cross-reference the CompScience insurance model (AI cameras → lower premiums) with BLS fatality data and NAHB labor shortage economics. Calculate: if AI cameras reduce the Fatal Four injuries by the 20-30% that vendors claim, how much of the $10.8B labor shortage cost do they actually address? Answer: almost none. The cameras don't fix the shortage — they make it cheaper to insure fewer workers doing more dangerous work. The math exposes whose interests AI safety really serves.

## Strongest Counterargument
AI cameras genuinely save lives. 1,075 people died on construction sites in 2023. If computer vision can catch one missing harness, one unsecured ladder, one worker in a fall zone — that's a life saved. Privacy concerns are real but secondary to death. Workers can't exercise their rights if they're dead. The moral urgency of 9.6 deaths per 100,000 FTE may justify surveillance that workers didn't ask for.

## Limitations
- No public data on residential-specific AI camera deployment rates (most data is commercial/industrial)
- CompScience claims are self-reported; no independent audit of injury reduction
- Worker sentiment data on AI monitoring is sparse for construction (more available for warehouse/logistics)
- Two-party consent legal analysis is state-specific and evolving
