Your City's AI Checked 250 Building Code Rules. It Got One in Five Wrong.

By Catherine Chen · June 13, 2026

Titusville, Florida, population 49,000, sits on the Space Coast between the Indian River and Kennedy Space Center. Lockheed Martin just broke ground on a $140 million manufacturing facility there. Rocket launches are projected to hit 120 this year. The city's development services department is drowning in permit applications, and last January it extended a contract with SwiftGov, an AI-powered plan review platform, after processing 132 real-world development projects through the system's pilot program.

Buried in the announcement was a number that should concern anyone whose building permit recently passed through an AI review engine: approximately 80% accuracy.

Eighty percent. On a system evaluating roughly 250 regulatory rules per engineering review. That means for every ten code checks the AI performed, two were wrong. Not "flagged for human review." Wrong.

The building code does not grade on a curve.

Regulatory costs added to the average new home in 2026, according to a NAHB study released June 10. That is 26.4% of the $499,500 average sale price, up 40% from $93,870 in 2021.

The Regulatory Pressure Driving Adoption

The rush to automate makes sense on paper. The National Association of Home Builders released a study this week showing that regulations at the federal, state, and local level add $131,734 to the cost of a typical new single-family home. Building code compliance changes over the past decade alone account for $40,200 of that figure, making it the single largest regulatory cost component at more than 8% of the sale price. Average regulatory delay: seven months. Over the same five-year period, disposable income rose 18.3%. Regulatory costs grew at more than twice that rate.

Those numbers are catnip for municipal leaders who need to look like they are doing something about housing affordability without actually changing the codes themselves. AI plan review tools promise to keep the same rules while cutting the time and cost of enforcing them. Jacksonville deployed SwiftGov alongside Microsoft and claims 600 hours saved. Naples partnered with Blitz AI, a Tampa company that trained its compliance engine on the entire 800-page Florida Building Code and produces redlined drawings in minutes instead of weeks. Hernando County, after Hurricane Helene buried its development office in rebuilding applications, reported that SwiftGov cut single-family home review times by 93%, from 30 days to two. Austin adopted Archistar's eCheck for single-family permits. Honolulu uses CivCheck. Seattle, Bellevue, Miami, Louisville, and Los Angeles have all followed.

Twenty-three percent of Canadian municipalities report using AI for permitting, per a MNP 2025 Municipal Report, with AI ranking as a top priority for 48% of them, double the figure from 2024. A national training initiative called Trax AI is teaching 2,500 Canadian building, fire, and planning officials to use AI technologies for permit reviews.

Nobody is publishing accuracy audits.

What 80% Accuracy Actually Means

Titusville's disclosure deserves closer scrutiny because it is the only municipality that has put a number on AI plan review performance. SwiftGov itself frames the tool carefully. From the city's announcement: "The AI does not automate approvals or replace professional judgment. Instead, it accelerates how staff identify applicable regulations, surface potential compliance issues and maintain consistency across reviews."

That framing is important. It means the AI is positioned as a first pass: it reads the plans, checks them against 250-odd rules, and hands a report to a human reviewer who is supposed to catch whatever the machine missed. The 80% accuracy figure describes this first-pass performance.

Reasonable enough, until you consider what happens to human attention when a machine has already done the checking. Aviation calls it automation complacency. Medicine calls it alert fatigue. The phenomenon is identical in every field where a human is asked to monitor a system that is right most of the time: the human stops looking. A 2016 NASA study on flight deck automation found that pilots missed critical anomalies at significantly higher rates when automated monitoring systems were active, precisely because they trusted the automation to catch what needed catching.

Building plan review is not air traffic control, but the cognitive dynamic is the same. A plan reviewer who receives an AI-generated report showing 200 rules checked and passed is not going to re-check all 200. They are going to focus on the 50 that the AI flagged as potential violations. The 20% of rules that the AI got wrong? They sit in the pile of green checkmarks that nobody questions, because the whole point of deploying AI was to stop making humans read every line of the code.

The Liability Gap Nobody Has Addressed

Consider a concrete scenario. An AI plan review engine checks a residential set of drawings against the Florida Building Code's stairway provisions. Section R311.7.5.1 of the IRC specifies maximum riser height at 7¾ inches. The AI scans the architectural stair detail, reads the noted dimension as compliant, and marks it green. The actual dimension on the drawing is 8⅛ inches, rendered in a hand-annotated note that the OCR module misread. The human reviewer, seeing the green checkmark, moves on. The permit issues. The builder builds the stairs as drawn.

Eighteen months later, someone falls. The stair riser exceeds code by ⅜ of an inch. The inspection should have caught it, but the inspection was a 15-minute visit on a day when the inspector had 11 other stops, and the riser height looked right to the naked eye because ⅜ of an inch is not visible without a tape measure and an expectation that something is wrong.

Who is liable? The software vendor's terms of service will include a standard limitation of liability clause disclaiming consequential damages. Every one of these companies positions its product as a "decision-support tool," not a code authority. Blitz AI's VP of Growth, Chris Prather, told the Business Observer that "human-in-the-loop AI" means "a human takes over. The AI gives them a head start." SwiftGov's Titusville deployment description says the AI "does not automate approvals or replace professional judgment."

Translation: if something goes wrong, the human reviewer bears the professional responsibility, not the AI. The city retains liability through its plan review function. The software vendor has sold a tool that promises speed, collects its fee, and contractually disclaimed the accuracy of its output.

No state has enacted legislation allocating liability for AI-assisted plan review errors. No insurance carrier has published underwriting guidance on how AI plan review adoption affects municipal errors-and-omissions coverage. The CFPB finalized rules governing automated valuation models in home appraisals in 2024, requiring safeguards for accuracy, anti-manipulation, and nondiscrimination. Nothing comparable exists for the AI systems now deciding whether your home's structural connections, fire separation, egress paths, and ventilation meet the minimum standards that exist because people died in buildings that lacked them.

Calculating the Cost of a Missed Violation

Plan review exists to catch code violations on paper, where fixing them costs a red pen and a revised drawing. Catching the same violation during construction costs demolition and reconstruction. Catching it after occupancy costs litigation and remediation. The ratio is not subtle.

When Caught Typical Fix Cost Multiplier
Plan review (on paper) $0 to $500 (designer revision) 1x
During construction (pre-drywall) $1,500 to $8,000 3-16x
During construction (post-drywall) $5,000 to $25,000 10-50x
After occupancy (remediation) $10,000 to $100,000+ 20-200x
After incident (litigation) $50,000 to $1M+ 100-2000x

These ranges come from published construction defect data. The National Institute of Standards and Technology estimated that inadequate interoperability in the capital facilities industry costs $15.8 billion annually, with two-thirds attributable to costs incurred during operations and maintenance rather than design and construction. The residential share is harder to isolate, but the multiplier effect is well-documented: the Construction Industry Institute's rule of thumb is that every dollar spent on quality during design saves five to ten dollars during construction and twenty to one hundred dollars during operations.

Apply that to NAHB's $40,200 code compliance cost. If AI plan review catches 80% of code issues at the paper stage (cost: minimal) but passes 20% through to the field, and half of those are caught during construction at an average remediation cost of $5,000 each, the math looks like this for a system reviewing 250 rules:

250 rules × 20% error rate = 50 rules incorrectly assessed. Not all incorrect assessments produce real-world violations. Many are false negatives on rules that the designer actually satisfied, or rules that a subsequent inspection would catch. But even if 10% of incorrect assessments result in a constructed defect, that is five defects per project that passed plan review unchallenged. At $5,000 average remediation per defect: $25,000 in rework costs that would have been $0 if a human had checked those 50 rules.

That $25,000 exceeds the entire schedule-delay savings that AI plan review claims to deliver.

The Strongest Case for AI Plan Review

The counterargument deserves its full weight, because it is not trivial. Human plan reviewers are not 100% accurate either. They are inconsistent. They get tired. They interpret the same code section differently depending on which reviewer pulls your application, a problem the Independent Institute compared to baseball umpires' varying strike zones. They are also disappearing: the municipal development workforce has been shrinking for over 15 years while the regulatory load has grown more complex, creating what HousingWire called a "de facto national underinvestment in permitting capacity measured in tens of billions of dollars."

An AI that catches 80% consistently may outperform a burned-out human reviewer working a 60-application backlog who catches 70% on a good day and 50% on a bad one. Hernando County's 93% reduction in review times is real. Blitz AI's reported 85% reduction in resubmittals suggests that its upfront AI screening catches enough issues early to save builders multiple rounds of revision, which saves real money even if the system is not perfect.

The problem is not that AI plan review is being deployed. The problem is that it is being deployed without independent accuracy benchmarking, without published error analysis, without liability frameworks, and without any regulatory body asking the question that should precede adoption: how accurate does an AI plan review system need to be before a municipality can rely on it, and who verifies that the threshold has been met?

What Nobody Is Requiring

Aviation has the FAA. Medical devices have the FDA. Financial algorithms have the SEC, CFPB, and a growing body of state law (Colorado's SB24-205, now being challenged by xAI with DOJ backing, attempted to regulate algorithmic discrimination). Building code plan review AI has nothing. No certification. No accuracy threshold. No mandatory audit. No disclosure requirement. A city can deploy an AI plan review system trained on its code, process thousands of permits through it, and never tell an applicant that a machine participated in the review.

A 2026 paper published in Springer Nature's Discover Cities journal audited LLM judgment against human professional judgment in urban infrastructure governance using the Delphi method. The researchers' recommendation: "LLMs should be treated as junior analytical assistants whose outputs require review and validation." They called for audit-based adoption with explicit readiness levels defining boundaries on permissible use, and warned that "outputs from models classified at higher readiness levels may support internal analysis but should not enter official records without independent verification."

None of the cities deploying AI plan review have published the results of such an audit. Titusville's 80% figure came from a press release about extending a contract, not from an independent evaluation. The number describes self-reported performance by a vendor with a financial interest in continued deployment.

What Should Happen Next

If you are a builder whose plans recently passed through AI-assisted review: request the AI-generated compliance report alongside your permit approval letter. You are entitled to the records that supported the permit decision under most state public records laws. Compare the AI's findings to your own code analysis. If you find discrepancies, document them before construction proceeds.

If you are a homeowner about to submit plans in a jurisdiction that uses AI plan review: hire your own code consultant to independently verify the critical systems, specifically structural connections, fire separation assemblies, egress dimensions, and energy code compliance. Cost: $500 to $1,500. Compared to the cost of a post-construction code violation: negligible.

If you are a municipality considering AI plan review: require your vendor to publish accuracy data. Not self-reported accuracy on a curated test set, but measured accuracy on your jurisdiction's actual permit applications, benchmarked against a human reviewer processing the same drawings blind. Publish the results. Annual audits. Accuracy by code section, because a system that nails setback calculations but misreads structural details is not 80% safe. It is 100% safe on setbacks and 0% safe on structure, and those two numbers should never be averaged.

Limitations of This Analysis

Titusville's 80% accuracy figure is the only publicly reported accuracy metric for a deployed AI plan review system in the United States. It may not be representative of other platforms (SwiftGov, Blitz AI, Archistar eCheck, CivCheck, AutoReview.AI). The $25,000 rework estimate assumes a 10% defect materialization rate from incorrectly assessed rules, which is an analytical assumption, not an empirical measurement, and the actual rate could be significantly higher or lower depending on the types of rules the AI gets wrong. Structural and fire safety errors are far more consequential than landscaping setback errors, and this analysis does not distinguish between them because no published data breaks AI accuracy down by code section. The automation complacency argument draws on aviation and medical research; no study has examined the phenomenon in building plan review specifically. NAHB's $131,734 regulatory cost figure reflects average national conditions and varies substantially by jurisdiction, with some markets significantly higher (coastal California) and others lower (rural markets with minimal overlay districts). The CFPB's AVM rule applies to automated home valuations, not plan review, and is cited here as a regulatory precedent, not a direct analog.
