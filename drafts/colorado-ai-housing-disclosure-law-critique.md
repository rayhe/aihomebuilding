## 🔍 Critic 1: General Editor

**Score: 8.5/10**

### Strengths
- The cold open is excellent — leads with a specific date and bill number, drops the reader into consequence immediately. No throat-clearing, no market size. The "They should." two-word follow-up lands perfectly.
- The "disclosure stack" concept (7 AI notices in one transaction) is a genuinely novel framing device that gives the whole piece its structural backbone.
- The pivot from "this is a disclosure law, shrug" to "but the insurance market isn't waiting" is the best turn in the article. It reframes urgency without feeling manipulative.
- Pacing is strong — each section builds on the last. The action items section is genuinely useful.

### Issues
1. **The article is front-loaded with legal exposition.** The "What the Law Actually Says" section, while necessary, is the densest stretch. The sentence starting "What replaced it is narrower in mechanism but broader in its practical reach, regulating..." is a 48-word construction that, while passing rhythm gates, may lose trade-pub readers who aren't lawyers. Consider breaking into two sentences here — this is the one place where the long-sentence bias overcorrects.
2. **The closing is slightly repetitive.** "The tools are shipping" / "the insurance market is pulling coverage" / "the rulemaking starts this year" — this three-beat pattern appears in both the insurance section ("the tools are shipping, the law now requires disclosure...") and the final paragraph. It's the same drum beat, which weakens the close.
3. **Section count is predictable.** Six sections, each 2-3 paragraphs. The uniformity is subtle but visible at a glance. One section that's notably longer or shorter would break the metronome.

### Suggested Fixes
- Split the long "What replaced it..." sentence around the sector list.
- Vary the closing's rhythm — the final paragraph shouldn't echo the insurance section.
- Consider merging or expanding one section to break the visual uniformity.

## ⚖️ Critic 5: Legal Accuracy

**Score: 6.5/10** ❌ BELOW THRESHOLD

### Strengths
- The structural analysis of developer vs. deployer obligations is accurate per Lexology and Ogletree analyses.
- The CFPB AVM rule reference is correctly scoped — algorithmic appraisals only.
- The 60-day cure period is confirmed in the statute.
- The SB 24-205 → SB 26-189 timeline is well-documented and matches multiple legal analyses.

### Issues — BLOCKING

1. **🔴 CRITICAL: "$20,000 per violation" is WRONG for SB 26-189.** The article states the law has "a potential fine of $20,000 per violation." According to Aguardic's detailed statutory analysis: *"SB 26-189 deliberately dropped the SB 24-205 framing of $20,000 per violation per consumer. The new framework rewards organizations that can document a credible response to a 60-day cure notice."* Penalties under SB 26-189 are discretionary (CUPA-style), not a fixed $20K per violation. The $20K figure was from the **repealed** SB 24-205. This is a factual error on a central legal claim.

2. **🔴 CRITICAL: Governor Polis HAS signed SB 26-189.** The "What We Don't Know" section opens with: "Governor Polis had not yet signed SB 26-189 as of this writing, though he is expected to, given his public support of the rewrite." Multiple legal sources (Lexology, Ogletree, Crowell, EPIC, Mondaq) confirm **Governor Polis signed the bill on May 14, 2026** — two months before this article's July 15 publication date. This is a significant factual error that undermines the entire limitations section and the article's credibility.

3. **⚠️ SIGNIFICANT: The article says the Assembly "passed" SB 26-189 on "May 12, 2026."** Lexology states the bill was "introduced" on "May 1, 2026" and signed "May 14." The exact passage date should be verified — it's plausible the Assembly voted on May 12, but the article should distinguish between legislative passage and gubernatorial signing.

### Suggested Fixes — REQUIRED
- **Remove the $20,000 claim entirely** or replace with: "penalties are discretionary under the Colorado Unfair Practices Act framework, with the AG determining appropriate civil penalties based on the conduct."
- **Rewrite the first paragraph of "What We Don't Know"** to reflect that the governor signed the bill on May 14, 2026. The uncertainty should focus on the AG's rulemaking timeline, not the signing.
- **Verify May 12 passage date** or change to "In May 2026" without the specific date.

## 📊 Critic 7: Data Presentation

**Score: 8.0/10**

### Strengths
- Numbers are specific: "$2.5 million and $6 million annually," "25 percent of inspection time," "10,000-plus users," "82 percent." No vague "thousands" or "millions."
- Sources are named inline: Cohen Seglias for 82%, Cotality for 75%, Stanford Law School for hallucination rates.
- The stat callout boxes are well-designed and draw the eye to the two most important numbers (7 and 82%).

### Issues
1. **Stanford hallucination rates are slightly misstated.** The article says "20 to 33 percent for specialized systems." The Stanford HAI follow-up study ("Hallucination-Free?") found 17% for Lexis+ AI and 33% for Westlaw AI-Assisted Research. The actual range for specialized legal AI tools is **17% to 33%**, not 20% to 33%. The article rounds up the lower bound, which inflates the floor by 3 percentage points. For a rigor-first publication, this should match the primary source.
2. **"Regulators are approving those filings at rates between 80 and 100 percent."** This claim has no source attribution. Where does the 80-100% approval rate come from? Cohen Seglias? A state filing database? Without attribution, this is an unverifiable claim about regulatory behavior.
3. **The 75% Cotality figure** ("75 percent of homebuyers already assume AI is embedded") is cited to "Cotality's 2026 AI in Housing report" — fine, but the claim should note whether this is a survey (and of how many homebuyers) or a model estimate. "Already assume" is doing work that the data source needs to support.

### Suggested Fixes
- Correct to "17 to 33 percent" or "roughly one-fifth to one-third" (which is what the Stanford authors themselves use in their own summary, and would sidestep the precision issue).
- Add source attribution for the 80-100% regulatory approval rate.
- Add a parenthetical for the Cotality stat: "(survey of N homebuyers)" or note the methodology.
