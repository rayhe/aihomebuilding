Denver Approved 37% of Building Permits on the First Try. An AI Now Reads Them Before the City Does.

By Catherine Chen · June 22, 2026
← All Stories

Sixty-three percent. That is the share of building permit applications in Denver that got kicked back. Not because the design was bad, or the engineering was unsound, or the zoning didn't work. They bounced because someone forgot to attach a drainage plan. Because a field on page nine was blank. Because the site survey referenced an outdated parcel number. A city reviewer would open the submission, spend twenty minutes reading it, find the gap, write a correction notice, and drop it back in the applicant's queue. Weeks pass. The architect resubmits and the cycle repeats. Denver's own reporting puts the first-try approval rate at 37%.

In May 2026, the City and County of Denver signed a five-year, $4.6 million contract with Clariti for its CivCheck platform. The system screens permit applications before they reach a human reviewer. It flags missing documents, incomplete fields, and application errors at the point of submission, giving applicants a chance to fix mistakes before entering the formal review queue. Robert Peek, director of development systems performance at the Denver Permitting Office, described it as eliminating "the redundant review cycles that have long bottlenecked the process." Humans still approve the plans. The AI reads the paperwork. That distinction matters.

What a Rejected Permit Actually Costs You

Nobody talks about holding costs in permitting. The permit office doesn't pay them. You do.

When your application bounces back for a missing document, the clock does not stop on the mortgage you drew to acquire the lot, or the insurance premium on the vacant land, or the property taxes that accrue whether or not anything gets built. Your contractor either holds your slot or gives it away. Material prices do not freeze. In April 2026, construction input costs were running 11% above the prior year according to the Bureau of Labor Statistics Producer Price Index, with lumber and plywood surcharging an additional 8% since March due to tariff uncertainty.

I ran the numbers.

At a median new-home price of $420,400 (Census Bureau, April 2026), a typical construction loan at current rates generates monthly carrying costs of approximately $2,800 to $3,200 when you include interest, insurance, and tax accrual. Each permit rejection cycle adds a minimum of four weeks to the timeline, more often six to eight, before the resubmission is reviewed again. If Denver's 37% first-try rate means roughly 63% of applicants bounce at least once, and a conservative estimate puts the average applicant through 1.5 rejection cycles, that is 6 to 12 weeks of dead time. At $3,000 per month in carrying costs, every rejected permit application costs the homeowner or builder $4,500 to $9,000 in holding costs that produce nothing.

Scale that. Denver processes roughly 10,000 residential permit applications per year. At a 63% rejection rate and an average holding cost penalty of $6,000 per bounce, the aggregate cost of permit ping-pong in Denver alone approaches $38 million annually. Nobody writes that on a budget line. Applicants absorb it, folded into the sale price or subtracted from the builder's margin.

Six Cities, Five Vendors, One Pattern

Denver is not alone, and its vendor is not the only option. At least six major US cities have deployed or contracted AI permit screening tools since late 2025, and the vendor landscape is splitting into two distinct approaches.

Intake screeners catch completeness problems before formal review. CivCheck, built by Clariti, runs in Denver and Honolulu, where it went live in December 2025 for residential permits. SwiftBuild.ai's SwiftGov platform operates in Hernando County, Florida, where it automated parts of zoning and building review for single-family homes and subdivisions. The county's own estimate: review times dropped from approximately 30 days to as few as two, saving roughly $1 million in administrative costs. Seattle's Mayor Bruce Harrell signed an executive order last year directing all development applications through an AI pilot led by a Permitting and Customer Trust team, with citywide rollout expected this year.

Code compliance engines go deeper, analyzing actual drawing content against building code provisions. UpCodes, a platform already used by 800,000 architects, engineers, and contractors for code research, launched its AI-native Plan Review feature on June 3, 2026. It runs uploaded drawings against 11 million sections of locally adopted building codes across more than 6,000 US jurisdictions, flagging issues by severity and linking each to the governing code section and drawing page. Blitz Permits deployed in Naples, Florida, as the city's first AI-powered plan review system, trained on the entire 800-page Florida Building Code and local ordinances. It evaluates plans in minutes and generates red-lined review reports. Archistar partnered with Austin for automated zoning review of residential developments.

For LA and LA County, the deployment was born of disaster. After the Eaton and Palisades fires destroyed thousands of homes in January 2025, California launched AI software specifically to accelerate rebuilding permit approvals. When your house burns down, the permitting queue stops being abstract.

A Budget Cut in a Technology Hat

There is a less generous reading of this trend, and it deserves full voice.

Denver deployed CivCheck the same year it cut 59 budgeted positions from its Community Planning and Development Department, reducing total staff to 251, according to the Denver Gazette. Fewer humans plus an AI filter produces the appearance of efficiency. But look closer. If the bottleneck shifts from "applications rejected for missing documents" to "clean applications waiting for a reviewer who no longer exists," the homeowner's wait may not shrink. It just changes shape.

None of these tools handle the decisions that require professional judgment: discretionary design review, setback variances, conditional use permits, neighbor appeals, ambiguous code provisions where two readings produce different outcomes. Those demand a human who understands both the letter and the intent of the regulation, who has seen enough projects to know when an unusual configuration is creative and when it is dangerous. The AI catches the missing drainage plan. It does not evaluate whether the drainage plan is adequate for the specific soil conditions on your hillside lot during a hundred-year rain event.

Julia Richman, Clariti's vice president of government relations, framed it precisely: "CivCheck helps applicants identify issues earlier, reduces avoidable rework for staff, and preserves professional judgment and accountability in final decisions." That last clause is doing important work. When the AI flags a problem, no one is liable. When a human approves a plan that later fails, someone is. The professional stamp on the approval letter still belongs to a person, not an algorithm, and every municipality deploying these tools has been careful to maintain that distinction.

What This Means if You Are Submitting a Permit

If your city has deployed one of these systems, you should notice faster initial feedback on whether your application is complete. That alone is valuable. A rejection that arrives in 24 hours instead of six weeks lets you fix the gap and resubmit before your contractor gives away your framing crew.

If your city has not deployed AI screening, you can do the pre-check yourself. UpCodes offers its code research platform to individual professionals, and PlanCheckPro.AI, a smaller Florida-based startup, provides AI-powered plan review reports against the Florida Building Code, National Electrical Code, and ADA standards. Both promise hours, not weeks. Neither substitutes for the formal municipal review, but catching your own errors before submission is the single most effective way to avoid the $6,000 rejection penalty I calculated above.

For builders running multiple projects, the math is starker. If you submit 20 permit applications per year and your jurisdiction has a 50% first-try failure rate, AI pre-screening that catches even half those errors saves you roughly $30,000 annually in holding costs. That is not a technology investment; it is a line item recovery.

The National Multifamily Housing Council's most recent quarterly survey found that 97% of developers reported construction delays, with 83% specifically citing permits as the cause. The share reporting nine-month-or-longer permit wait times more than doubled in six months, from 10% to 22%. NMHC President Doug Bibby's response was direct: "Lawmakers at all levels of government should look at every policy option available to them to reform antiquated zoning, streamline the development process and incentivize the building of new housing."

AI intake screening is one of those options, not the most ambitious and not the most transformative. It does not reform zoning or incentivize housing production. It just makes sure the application is complete before a human spends time reading it. The fact that this counts as innovation tells you everything about where the bar has been.

What This Article Did Not Prove

Denver's 80% first-try target is aspirational. CivCheck deployed in May 2026, and no post-implementation data has been published. Whether the tool achieves that target, falls short, or produces unintended consequences (applicants gaming the automated checklist while still submitting substantively flawed plans) remains unknown. I will revisit this when Denver publishes results, which the city's 180-day shot clock commitment suggests should generate publicly reviewable data by late 2026.

Hernando County's $1 million savings figure is a county self-estimate, not an independent audit. The "30 days to 2 days" timeline applies specifically to single-family and subdivision reviews using the SwiftGov platform, not to the full range of commercial and multi-family permitting, which involves substantially more complex code analysis.

My holding cost calculation uses national medians and averages. In San Francisco, where median home prices exceed $1.3 million, the per-rejection holding cost penalty is approximately three times what I estimated for Denver. In lower-cost markets, it may be half. Your actual cost depends on your specific carrying structure, contractor agreement terms, and local review timelines. The $38 million aggregate figure for Denver is an order-of-magnitude estimate intended to illustrate scale, not a precise accounting.

Finally, none of these AI tools have been stress-tested against adversarial submissions. A contractor who learns the automated checklist criteria could, in theory, submit a structurally complete but substantively noncompliant application that passes the AI screen and lands in the human queue looking clean. Whether this failure mode materializes as deployment scales is a question that deserves monitoring, particularly in jurisdictions where staffing cuts accompany AI adoption.
