Your Insurer Inspected Your Roof from a Satellite. It Found Damage That Doesn't Exist. You Lost Your Policy Anyway.

By Catherine Chen · May 3, 2026

Tracy Gartenmann had insured her Austin home with Travelers for more than a decade. In January 2025, she received an email informing her the company would not renew her policy. Trees had edged too close to her roof, a representative explained. Attached were two aerial photographs of her property that Gartenmann had never seen, taken from above by a third-party company called Nearmap. No inspector had visited her property, no one had knocked on her door, and no one had asked her permission to photograph her home from above. An algorithm looked down from the sky and decided her home was too risky to cover.

"I thought it was a scam," Gartenmann told KUT News. It wasn't. When she confirmed the email was real, her reaction shifted to something closer to alarm: "It felt like an infringement on my rights."

She is not alone. This is not a Texas quirk or a single insurer's policy. It is an insurance industry transformation happening across the country that most homeowners do not know is happening, built on technology that works at scale and accountability structures that have not caught up.

How It Works

Nearmap, EagleView, and Cotality (formerly CoreLogic) operate fleets of aircraft and license satellite imagery that covers most residential areas in the United States. Their cameras capture rooftops at resolutions high enough to distinguish individual shingles. Machine learning models trained on millions of roof images then classify what they see: moss growth, missing shingles, ponding water, branch encroachment, sagging ridgelines, flashing deterioration. Cotality markets a specific product called "AI-Powered Age of Roof" that estimates when your roof was last replaced based entirely on aerial imagery and property records.

Insurers feed this data directly into their underwriting pipelines, using algorithmic risk scores to decide whether to renew a policy, raise a premium, or drop a customer entirely. In January 2025, Zurich North America announced it had integrated Nearmap's AI-enhanced aerial imagery and roof scoring directly into its underwriting systems, calling the result "faster risk assessments and tailored property insurance coverage." EagleView markets virtual inspection tools with 3D roof sketches and historical imagery comparison. Risk analytics firm Verisk argues in a recent roof report that ground-level inspections miss signs of damage because of "limited roof visibility," positioning aerial AI as the more accurate alternative.

Nobody is hiding what this technology does, and the companies building it are proud of the capabilities they have created. What nobody is disclosing is how often it gets things wrong.

What Goes Wrong

Pennsylvania's Insurance Department started receiving complaints from homeowners who had been dropped or non-renewed based on drone and satellite footage. When regulators examined the images insurers were using to justify adverse actions, they found a problem. "Some of the aerial images that we've seen used to take adverse actions against policyholders barely identify the structure of the home, much less the detailed condition of the roof and whether it needs to be repaired or replaced," said Insurance Commissioner Michael Humphreys.

Some images showed nothing more than discoloration or streaking, cosmetic blemishes that a roofer would dismiss on a walk-up inspection but that were being classified as structural damage by software that had never touched the surface.

Pennsylvania issued an official bulletin reminding insurers that aerial imagery alone is insufficient for canceling or non-renewing a policy, that homeowners must be given the opportunity to challenge the findings or fix the identified problems before losing coverage, and that the practice itself was not banned but constrained by a standard the commissioner summarized in two words: be reasonable. Pennsylvania was the eighth state to adopt the National Association of Insurance Commissioners' broader guidance on AI use in insurance, guidance that essentially restates the old standard rather than creating a new one: whatever decisions you make with AI must comply with existing state regulations.

A Patchwork, Not a Framework

California, where wildfire-driven non-renewals have already pushed hundreds of thousands of homeowners into the state's insurer of last resort, is trying to go further. Assembly Bill 75, proposed in March 2026 by Assemblymember Lisa Calderon, would require insurers to notify homeowners before taking or obtaining aerial images, provide copies of those images on request, and prohibit policy decisions based on aerial photos older than 45 days unless the issue is persistent and verified by physical inspection. If an insurer wants to cancel your policy based on what a satellite saw, it would have to send you the photos and an explanation, then give you time to fix whatever the algorithm flagged.

Insurance Commissioner Ricardo Lara supports the bill, and his department has "investigated numerous complaints where flawed aerial imagery led to wrongful cancellations or nonrenewals," he said, describing cases where "insurers used imprecise drone or satellite photos to assess roof conditions, resulting in policies being incorrectly dropped due to erroneous data."

Massachusetts issued guidance in March 2025 outlining consumer rights around aerial imaging. Connecticut warned insurers the same month that cosmetic conditions visible from above, like streaking or discoloration, cannot justify non-renewal. Texas, where Gartenmann's case originated, has investigated individual complaints but has not issued statewide regulation.

Count the states that have acted in any meaningful way and you reach roughly five, which means forty-five states have no specific regulation governing whether an insurer can photograph your roof from orbit, feed the image through a neural network, and cancel your coverage based on the output without ever verifying the result with a human being who has stood on your property.

Original Analysis: Nobody Audits the Algorithm

I went looking for an independent, third-party accuracy audit of any major aerial roof assessment AI. Nearmap, EagleView, Cotality, Verisk. Published false positive rates, confusion matrices, error rates disaggregated by roof type or age or geography. Peer-reviewed validation studies comparing algorithmic assessments against ground-truth professional inspections on the same properties.

I could not find one.

Nearmap publishes marketing materials about AI-enhanced imagery, EagleView describes "accelerated claims processing" and "reduced leakage," and Cotality markets its Age of Roof product with phrases like "precise roof age data." Zurich's press release about the Nearmap integration describes "measurable benefits demonstrated during a pilot rollout" without publishing the measurements. Every company claims accuracy, and none publishes the data that would let an outsider verify it.

Consider what this means in practice for a homeowner whose insurer decides to act on the algorithm's output. An insurer uses an algorithm to determine that your 12-year-old asphalt shingle roof shows signs of deterioration from a photo taken at altitude, and you receive a non-renewal notice. The algorithm's false positive rate on asphalt shingle roofs in humid climates, where algae streaking is cosmetic and common, has never been published, peer-reviewed, or independently tested. You have no way to know whether the assessment reflects actual structural risk or a classification error that nobody has measured because nobody has been required to measure it.

Roof replacement costs range from $13,000 to $24,000 for a standard asphalt roof, according to industry estimates. A homeowner who replaces a roof in response to an AI-flagged non-renewal notice, when the roof had years of serviceable life remaining, has spent tens of thousands of dollars to satisfy an algorithm whose accuracy has never been audited. A homeowner who refuses to replace the roof and loses coverage faces the secondary consequence that a non-renewal can affect eligibility with other insurers, as Insurify insurance agent Katie Walters has noted, making the next policy harder and more expensive to obtain.

What You Should Do Right Now

Request your insurer's underwriting file. Most states require insurers to provide the specific reasons for non-renewal or cancellation and any evidence used. If aerial imagery was a factor, ask for copies of the images. Compare them to a licensed roofer's physical inspection. If the images show cosmetic issues, not structural damage, document the discrepancy and appeal through your insurer's dispute process. File a complaint with your state's department of insurance if the appeal fails.

If you live in California, watch AB 75. If it passes, your insurer will be required to notify you before using aerial imagery and give you a chance to respond before any policy change takes effect. If you live in Pennsylvania, Massachusetts, or Connecticut, your state has issued guidance that constrains how insurers can use aerial photos. If you live anywhere else, you are operating in a regulatory vacuum where the rules governing algorithmic roof assessment are, at best, the pre-AI consumer protection statutes that were written when an inspection meant a person on a ladder.

Photograph your roof annually from ground level and keep dated records. Get a professional inspection every three to five years. If your insurer non-renews and cites roof condition, you will need documentation that predates the dispute. An ounce of timestamped evidence from ground level outweighs a satellite photo taken at a moment the algorithm chose and you did not.

Strongest Counterargument

Aerial assessment at scale catches real problems that ground inspections miss. Verisk's argument has merit: a person standing on a sidewalk cannot see ponding water, flashing separation, or valley deterioration the way a camera directly overhead can. Satellite coverage is universal and consistent in a way that human inspectors, who vary wildly in competence and thoroughness, are not. In catastrophe-prone regions where insurers are hemorrhaging money, better risk assessment protects the solvency of the insurance pool, which ultimately protects every policyholder in it. Forcing insurers to send a human to every roof before making an underwriting decision would add weeks to the process and cost hundreds of dollars per property, costs that would flow directly into premiums. An imperfect algorithm applied universally may produce better aggregate outcomes than a perfect human inspection that covers 5% of the book.

I take this argument seriously, and parts of it are clearly right. Satellite imagery can detect conditions invisible from the ground. Scale matters. But the argument that algorithmic assessment is better than human assessment requires evidence that the algorithm is, in fact, accurate, and that evidence has never been published independently. The insurance industry is asking homeowners to accept coverage decisions made by technology whose error rate is a trade secret. That is not a technology problem but an accountability problem, and it has a straightforward solution: publish the data.

Limitations

This analysis is constrained by the absence of the very data it identifies as missing. Nearmap, EagleView, Cotality, and Verisk do not publish false positive rates, and no regulatory body requires them to. We relied on consumer complaint data from state insurance departments, journalistic investigations, and insurer marketing materials to reconstruct the landscape. Consumer complaints are a biased sample: they capture homeowners who were aware enough and motivated enough to file, not the full population of affected policyholders. We cannot quantify the total number of non-renewals driven by aerial imagery nationally because no state or federal body tracks this specific metric. California's AB 75 has not yet passed and may be amended significantly. Pennsylvania's bulletin is guidance, not binding regulation with enforcement teeth. State-by-state regulatory status was assessed through public records and may be incomplete if a state issued informal guidance not captured in our search. Finally, this article addresses homeowner insurance exclusively and does not examine the separate and potentially larger application of the same aerial AI technology in commercial property insurance, where the regulatory dynamics differ.
