### 1. 🔍 General Editor — 8.5/10

**Strengths:**
- Cold open is excellent. The Washtenaw County homeowner, the kitchen drawer — immediate, concrete, human. Exactly what the STORY_GUIDE demands: "Start with a person."
- Structure flows naturally from anecdote → problem → research → solution → context → actionable → limitations. The eight sections earn their place.
- The "diagnosis is excellent, and the prescription is wrong" thesis lands in paragraph 3 with satisfying compression.
- Closing line ("someone should build it") is opinionated and earned — exactly the editorial voice the guide calls for.

**Issues:**
- **🔴 CRITICAL: Headline says "Seven AIs" but article describes six models.** "Six models were tested: ChatGPT o3, DeepSeek R1, Grok 3, Gemini 2.0, Llama 3.2, and Claude 3.7." That's six, counted three times in the body. The headline says "Seven" ten times across title, OG tags, Twitter cards, structured data, and H1. If the seventh is the fine-tuned model, "None Knew Which One to Do First" is wrong (the fine-tuned model does know — 93.3%). Either way, this is a factual discrepancy that will undermine credibility with any reader who counts.
- **4/8 section headers start with "What"** (What MSU Actually Tested / What the Chatbots Are Missing / What Does Not Exist Yet / What You Should Do Right Now). Noticeable pattern. Vary at least one.
- **Word count: 2,039.** Guide targets 800–1,200 for deep dives. This is 70% over. The content is substantive and dense, but "What the Chatbots Are Missing" partially restates analysis from earlier sections. The medical/exercise analogy is effective but the paragraph could be tighter.

**Mandatory fix:** Resolve Seven/Six discrepancy before ship.

### 6. 🔬 Research Rigor — 8.5/10

**Original Contribution:**
- ✅ The $2,200 sequencing cost calculation is a novel analysis not present in the MSU paper. It takes the study's ranking-failure finding and translates it into a homeowner cost impact. This is the kind of original contribution the guide demands.

**Methodology Transparency:**
- The $2,200 calculation shows inputs (three retrofits, costs, payback periods) and outputs ($4,900 vs $2,700 cumulative savings). The intermediate steps (annual savings derivation, compounding logic) are implied but not shown step-by-step. The Limitations section explicitly disclaims it as "illustrative" and "directional, not universal." Acceptable for a narrative article, but a footnote or sidebar showing the full math would strengthen it.

**Limitations Acknowledgment:**
- ✅ Present and honest. Covers: model aging, self-evaluation bias (fine-tuned model tested against training source), price stability assumptions, illustrative nature of cost estimate, and local-condition dependence. 119 words — concise but thorough.

**Strongest Counterargument:**
- ⚠️ **Not stated at full strength.** The guide requires: "The best case against the article's thesis must be stated at full strength — not strawmanned, not immediately dismissed." The strongest counterargument is probably: *"General-purpose models improve quarterly. GPT-5 may close the ranking gap without specialized fine-tuning, making MSU's approach obsolete before it reaches consumers. Meanwhile, the 93.3% accuracy means 1 in 15 homeowners gets the wrong priority — for a $12,000 decision, that error rate may make professional auditors more necessary, not less."* The Limitations section touches the first point ("Model capabilities improve quarterly; results could differ with newer versions") but states it as a caveat, not as a full-throated counterargument. The 6.7% error rate implication is not explored at all.

**Verifiability:**
- Research claims trace to arXiv paper and ResStock dataset ✅
- Cost claims trace to named sources (Angi, PayScale, ACEEE) but lack hyperlinks (see Legal Accuracy) ⚠️

**Recommendation:** Add 2-3 sentences steelmanning the counterargument, either at the end of "What Does Not Exist Yet" or as a standalone paragraph before Limitations.

- The article avoids data overload — numbers appear when they advance the argument, not as decoration.

**Minor notes:**
- A simple table or sidebar comparing optimal vs AI-recommended ordering (with the three retrofits, their costs, paybacks, and cumulative savings) would make the $2,200 claim more immediately verifiable and more shareable as an image.
- The 98.9% CO₂ accuracy and 93.3% payback accuracy are stated close together but not compared visually. A paired stat presentation would highlight the gap between "identifying the right fix" (easy) and "ranking by payback" (hard) — which is the article's entire thesis.
