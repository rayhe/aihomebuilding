# Research: The Handoff Problem — AI Unifying Design-to-Supply Chain

## Angle
The "re-estimation" problem in homebuilding. Every home gets interpreted independently by architects, estimators, suppliers, purchasing teams, and field crews — each working from their own version of the truth. Two major companies just bet big that a shared data model can collapse these handoffs: Higharc ($95M Series C + US LBM partnership, June 30 2026) and Builders FirstSource (AI-integrated platform previewed at IBS 2026). But can software fix an industry that still runs on phone calls, spreadsheets, and "I'll eyeball it"?

## Primary Sources

### 1. Higharc Series C + US LBM Partnership (Jun 30, 2026)
- Source: PR Newswire, HousingWire, SiliconANGLE
- $95M Series C led by Insight Partners (total $170M+)
- Partnership with US LBM — largest private lumber/materials distributor in US
- Platform: generates homes as spatial databases capturing geometry, construction standards, code requirements
- AutoTranslate: converts 2D plan images → dynamic 3D data models + material quantities aligned with purchasable products
- Claims: product development compressed from months/years to weeks/days, community opening time cut 25-50%, margins increased 10-15%
- CEO Marc Minor: "Technology is not a panacea. So much comes down to the operating model and the strategy and the reality of land"
- Co-founder Michael Bergin: "'Close enough' is not a standard homebuilding can afford. A misread wall, a quantity that's off by 10% — this translates to rework cycles, change orders and massive risk."

### 2. Builders FirstSource AI Platform (IBS 2026, Builder Magazine, Mar 31 2026)
- Source: builderonline.com
- Nation's largest supplier of materials for home builders
- AI platform integrating 4 phases: launching communities, plans/design, homeowner selections, purchasing/construction
- CEO Peter Jackson: "If you have to log in and out of 15 different systems, and move the data back and forth, the odds of it being error free, are not good"
- Gayatri Narayan (President of Technology): builders forced to manage multiple disconnected systems = inefficiencies, errors, rework
- Connecting vendor SKUs, availability, lead times through distribution channel into design process

### 3. Construction Cost/Rework Data
- Source: PlanRadar (academic meta-analysis), Sage Advice, biCanvas
- 78% of corporate/public RE owners exceed construction budgets (Sage/research)
- Only 31% of projects stay within 10% of budgets
- Only 25% of projects meet planned timelines
- Design-related errors: 1-9% of total project cost (Cnudde 1991: 6.9%, Burati 1992: 9.5%, Hwang 2009: 1.8%, Dougherty & Hughes 2014: 1.5%)
- Construction-related rework: up to 3% of total project cost
- Lumber waste: one case study found 16% actual vs 6% estimated
- BIM can reduce cost overruns by up to 40%
- Materials still 39.7% higher than pre-pandemic (Oct 2024 data)

### 4. Market Pressure Context
- Source: HousingWire, NAHB

- 40% cut prices
- Razor-thin margins = zero tolerance for rework and waste
- Only 32% of construction leaders say they've met or are close to meeting AI goals (Autodesk State of Design & Make 2026)

## Original Contribution
Novel calculation: if design-related errors cost 1.5-6.9% of project cost (academic range), and the median new home price is ~$420K, each handoff error cascade costs $6,300-$29,000 per home. At US new home starts (~1.4M annually), even the conservative estimate suggests ~$8.8B in annual handoff-related waste industry-wide. Nobody has mapped this per-handoff cost before.

## Strongest Counterargument
The integration thesis has been tried before and failed. Katerra raised $2B trying to vertically integrate design-to-construction and collapsed. The problem isn't software — it's that construction is irreducibly local, bespoke, and relationship-driven. A lumber yard in Phoenix and one in Portland stock different species, at different prices, from different mills. AI that "knows" a house as a spatial database still can't account for the superintendent who moves a wall two feet because the plumbing runs don't work in the field. And 68% of builders who've tried AI say they haven't met their goals (Autodesk). The gap between demo and job site remains the industry's defining feature.
