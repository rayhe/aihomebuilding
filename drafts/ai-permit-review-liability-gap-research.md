# Research: AI Permit Review Liability Gap

## Angle
Cities are deploying AI to review building permits. Standard construction contracts (AIA, ConsensusDocs) don't address AI. When AI misses a code violation, nobody's contract says who pays. The homeowner probably eats it.

## Journalist
Catherine "Code" Chen — policy, legal, building codes

## Kill Test
✅ If you're building or buying a home in Austin, Honolulu, Lancaster, or San Jose, your permit may have been AI-reviewed. If that AI missed a fire code or setback violation, you need to know who pays for the fix.

## Original Contribution
Three-party liability chain analysis: When AI plan review misses a code violation, trace the liability through:
1. **City → Homeowner**: Municipal sovereign immunity may shield the city
2. **AI Vendor → City**: EULA caps liability at license fee (typically $50K-$200K/year)
3. **Builder → Homeowner**: Standard warranty covers "workmanship" not "plans the city approved"
4. **Result**: Homeowner holds the bag — permitted doesn't mean compliant, and AI just widened that gap

## Counterargument
AI plan review is a pre-check, not a replacement for human review. Cities still have human reviewers as final sign-off. The AI just flags issues earlier. But: if cities use AI accuracy claims to reduce human reviewer headcount (which is the financial incentive), the backstop disappears.

## Limitations
- No published case law yet of AI-caused permit failure → liability dispute (it's coming)
- AI vendor EULA terms aren't publicly available for most municipal contracts (FOIA-able but not yet filed)
- Accuracy claims (90%, 95%) are vendor-reported, not independently audited
