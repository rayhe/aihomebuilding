# Research: The EV-Ready vs. V2H-Ready Wiring Gap in New Homes

## Journalist: Priya Greenwood (Sustainability & Green Building)

## Thesis
California's 2025 Building Code (effective January 1, 2026) mandates 100% EV-ready infrastructure for all new residential construction — one EV receptacle per dwelling unit. But "EV-ready" means a 240V outlet and a dedicated circuit. It does NOT mean "V2H-ready" — the wiring needed to use your electric vehicle as a home backup battery. The gap between what the code requires and what homeowners will want within 5 years is $4,000–$10,000 in retrofit costs, versus $1,000–$2,000 if builders wired for it during construction.

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? YES — tells builders what to wire NOW to avoid costly retrofits, tells buyers what to ask for, and tells everyone why current codes are solving the 2020 problem while missing the 2026 one.

## Primary Sources

### 1. California 2025 Building Code (CALGreen, Title 24)
- Effective January 1, 2026
- Mandates 100% EV-ready infrastructure for new residential, hotel, and commercial projects
- 1 EV receptacle per dwelling unit (residential)
- 40% Level 2 EVSE for hotels
- Stricter load sizing requirements
- Aligns with California's 2035 100% EV sales goal
- Source: California Building Standards Commission (dgs.ca.gov), chargedfuture.com analysis

### 2. NEC 2026 (National Electrical Code)
- Ratified June 2025
- Mandates licensed/qualified installers for permanent EVSE installation (bans DIY)
- Article 625 covers EV charging requirements
- New provisions for bidirectional charging equipment
- Source: NFPA 70, motortrend.com analysis

### 3. DOE Efficient New Homes Multifamily EV Ready Checklist V2
- 20% of units must be EV Ready
- Compliance with NFPA 70
- Three tiers: EV Capable (conduit only), EV Ready (outlet installed), EVSE Installed (charger installed)
- Source: energy.gov

### 4. V2H Market Data (2026)
- Only ~15 of 54 bidirectional EVs support true V2H in the US (most are V2L only)
- V2H market: $93.6M (2026) → $532.6M by 2032 (~5.7x growth)
- Typical output: 7–19.2 kW
- Equipment costs: $3,500–$7,299 for bidirectional charger
- Total installation: $4,000–$10,000 installed
- Key vehicles: Ford F-150 Lightning (80A charger + Home Integration System), GM Silverado EV (6-7 days backup, 21 days with solar), Tesla Cybertruck (Powershare)
- Source: recharged.com, Eos/Medium, insideevs.com

### 5. Enphase IQ Bidirectional EV Charger
- 11.5 kW AC bidirectional
- ISO 15118-2 certified
- IQ Meter Collar for whole-home monitoring
- Black Start backup capability
- 10-year warranty
- Source: enphase.com

### 6. Panel Upgrade Data
- 20% of existing homes need panel upgrades just for basic Level 2 EV charging (EnergySage)
- Panel upgrade cost: $800–$3,000 (100A→200A)
- 50%+ of homebuilders already installing EV chargers (ecmag.com)
- 40% of builders providing EV outlets on more than half their homes
- New construction electrical: ~$3.50/sqft nationally

### 7. DOE/energy.gov Bidirectional Charging Brief
- EVs as mobile battery storage for buildings
- V2B (vehicle-to-building) and V2G (vehicle-to-grid) capabilities
- University of Delaware V2G revenue model
- New York school district BEV grid participation examples
- Source: energy.gov

## Original Contribution (Required)
Calculate the cost delta between "EV-ready" and "V2H-ready" wiring in new construction:

**EV-Ready (what code requires):**
- 240V/50A dedicated circuit to garage: ~$500–$800 incremental in new construction
- NEMA 14-50 receptacle
- Panel capacity for one 40-50A circuit

**V2H-Ready (what buyers will want within 5 years):**
- Everything above PLUS:
- 200A+ main panel (many new homes already have this)
- Transfer switch pre-wire or smart panel (Span, Lumin, etc.): $200–$500 in new construction vs. $2,000–$4,000 retrofit
- Critical load sub-panel if not using smart panel: $300–$600
- 100A circuit capacity reserved for bidirectional charger (vs. 50A for one-way): $100–$200 incremental
- Conduit sized for future bidirectional charger (larger gauge wire): $50–$100 incremental

**Total V2H-ready premium in new construction: ~$650–$1,400 above EV-ready**
**Retrofit cost from EV-ready to V2H-ready: $4,000–$10,000**
**Builder's avoidable cost per home: ~$2,600–$8,600**

## Strongest Counterargument
V2H adoption is still tiny — only 15 vehicles support it, and homeowners may never buy one. Future-proofing for V2H adds cost to every home for a technology most buyers won't use for years, if ever. The code should mandate minimum viable EV support and let the market handle upgrades.

## Limitations
- Cost estimates are California-specific (labor rates vary 2-3x by region)
- V2H technology is evolving fast — requirements may shift as standards mature (ISO 15118-20, SAE J3072)
- No third-party study has calculated the exact new-construction vs. retrofit gap for V2H wiring
- Transfer switch/smart panel costs are manufacturer-quoted, not independently audited
- The 5-year adoption timeline is an estimate based on market growth projections

## Angle
The story through the lens of a builder looking at the 2026 California code, doing exactly what's required (one 240V outlet per home), and setting up every buyer for a $6,000 retrofit the moment they buy a V2H-capable truck. The code solved charging. It didn't solve the grid.
