# Research: AI Home Inspection — 14 States, No License Required

## Thesis
14 US states don't require home inspectors to be licensed. The home inspection industry remains largely manual, inconsistent, and unregulated. AI tools are emerging that could either standardize quality (good) or enable unqualified people to produce professional-looking reports (bad). The regulatory vacuum meets the technology wave.

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? **YES** — directly relevant to every home buyer deciding whether to hire an inspector, and every buyer in an unlicensed state who doesn't know their inspector might have zero formal training.

## Primary Sources

### 1. State Licensing Data (McKissock Learning)
- 14 states don't require a home inspector license: California, Colorado, Georgia, Hawaii, Idaho, Iowa, Kansas, Maine, Michigan, Minnesota, Missouri, Nebraska, Utah, Wyoming
- Licensed states vary wildly: some require 100+ hours training + national exam (NHIE) + insurance; others just a form and a fee
- New York excludes newly constructed homes from inspection licensing requirements entirely
- Source: mckissock.com

### 2. NAR/Clever Real Estate New Home Inspection Study
- 14% of new home buyers (1 in 7) forgo inspection entirely
- 65% of those who DID inspect found issues
- 24% of new homes didn't pass first inspection
- 32% found minor issues; 30% passed first time
- Nearly 9 in 10 newly built homes required premature maintenance
- Source: nar.realtor

### 3. Repair Pricer Analysis of 50,000 Home Inspection Reports
- 1M+ repairs identified costing >$11,000 on average
- 55% of homes: doors needing adjustment (potential foundation issues)
- 54%: lacking exterior caulking/sealant (water damage risk)
- 48%: lacking GFCI protection (electrocution risk)
- Source: gaar.com / Repair Pricer

### 4. BRANZ New House Construction Quality Survey (SR335)
- Defects found in 10-65% of inspected new homes across different inspection types
- Most common compliance defect: window fixing to frame (builders unaware of 2011 amendment)
- Flashing defects: wrong clearances, no slope, inadequate kick-out — any single defect at an opening could cause water damage
- Source: branz.co.nz

### 5. Mordor Intelligence — Building Inspection Services Market
- Home inspection services: 44.12% of building inspection services market (2025)
- Other Specialised Services (energy audits, drone surveys, AI-enabled): growing at 8.74% CAGR
- States revoking waiver clauses, requiring certified reports
- Source: mordorintelligence.com

## Original Contribution
1. Cross-reference: the 14 unlicensed states include California (largest housing market), Colorado, Georgia — collectively representing ~25-30% of US home sales. This means roughly 1 in 4 home inspections in America has zero state quality control.
2. The AI paradox: tools like Binsr and Spectin could standardize reporting quality across all 50 states, functioning as a de facto quality floor. But they could also let anyone with a smartphone produce a professional-looking report — giving the appearance of competence without the underlying knowledge.
3. Cost calculation: at average inspection cost of $400 × ~5M annual inspections = ~$2B industry, but the cost of missed defects far exceeds this (average post-closing repair: $11K+ from Repair Pricer data).
