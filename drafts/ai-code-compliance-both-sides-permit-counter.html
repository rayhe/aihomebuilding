Both Sides of the Permit Counter Now Have AI. They Don't Agree.

By Jake Kowalski · July 18, 2026

← Back to AI Home Building

An architect in Austin runs her residential plans through UpCodes Plan Review, uploading the drawings, setting the jurisdiction, and clicking run. Six minutes later: zero code violations across architecture, structural, and energy disciplines. She submits to the city feeling confident for the first time in her career that nothing will bounce.

Fourteen days later, the city's plan reviewer sends back three corrections, two involving stairway handrail extensions under IRC R311.7.8.2 and one flagging an egress window well depth that her AI never mentioned. She calls the department. "Our system flagged it," the reviewer says.

Their system is CodeComply.Ai, a different AI entirely, running against a different code database with different local amendments loaded. Both AIs analyzed the same drawings and reached different conclusions, and nobody's contract addresses what happens next.

Two AIs, One Counter

Until June 2026, AI code compliance was mostly a municipal tool. Cities like Austin, Honolulu, and Naples deployed platforms such as CivCheck, CodeComply.Ai, and Blitz AI to pre-screen incoming plans and cut review backlogs. Architects and builders were still checking code compliance the old way: a senior associate with a red pen, a copy of the IRC, and a weekend.

Then UpCodes launched Plan Review on June 3, and the dynamic changed overnight. Suddenly, 800,000 architecture, engineering, and construction professionals got access to an AI that checks drawings against 11 million locally adopted code sections across 6,000-plus U.S. jurisdictions, covering architecture, structural, MEP, fire protection, life safety, accessibility, and energy before they submit a single sheet to the building department.

Simultaneously, HUD offered state and local governments up to $3 million in grants to deploy their own AI permitting systems, with a mid-July 2026 application deadline. Federal dollars are about to put AI on the municipal side of counters that don't have it yet.

So now both sides have AI, which sounds like progress until you realize the two systems are built on different foundations.

Why They'll Fight

Building codes are not one document but thousands of documents layered on top of each other, amended locally, and adopted on different timelines by different jurisdictions. A single residential plan in unincorporated Contra Costa County, California, is governed by the 2022 California Residential Code (which is itself the 2021 IRC with California amendments), plus the county's own local amendments, plus Title 24 energy requirements that changed in January 2023 and again in January 2026.

UpCodes claims to track 11 million locally adopted code sections. CodeComply.Ai lists support for IBC, IMC, IPC, IRC, ADA, FHA, UFC, state-amended codes, and five NFPA standards. Neither company publishes how frequently it syncs with individual jurisdictions' amendment packages, how it handles the gap between a jurisdiction adopting an amendment and that amendment appearing in the database, or what happens when a code section contains language like "or equivalent" or "as approved by the building official."

That ambiguity language is everywhere in residential codes, and it matters more than most designers realize. IRC Section R301.1.3 lets a building official accept "alternative materials, design, and methods of construction" if they provide equivalent performance, which means the code explicitly defers to human judgment in exactly the places where AI tools are weakest. No algorithm can predict what a specific building official will accept as "equivalent," because that determination varies by jurisdiction, by official, and frankly by what day of the week you catch them.

So here are three concrete ways two AI systems will disagree on the same residential plan:

Code year mismatch. Your jurisdiction adopted the 2021 IRC with 47 local amendments in March 2024, but the designer's AI defaults to the 2024 IRC base code because the firm hasn't updated their project jurisdiction settings since onboarding. Maximum stair riser height, guard rail spacing, egress window dimensions can all shift between code years. One system says compliant. One says violation. Neither is wrong in the context of the code edition it checked against, which is exactly why the disagreement is so maddening for the builder who just wants to pour a foundation and start framing.

Amendment lag. A county passes a local fire sprinkler amendment in February 2026, and UpCodes might pick it up by March while CodeComply.Ai might not have it until May. During that gap, one AI flags a missing sprinkler branch line; the other doesn't know the requirement exists. For the builder who submitted plans in April, the answer depends on which AI got the memo first.

Interpretation divergence. Code sections that reference "approved by the building official" create a black box that AI cannot penetrate. One system might flag the section as a warning; the other might skip it entirely, treating approval-contingent requirements as outside the scope of automated analysis. Both approaches are defensible, yet neither matches what the human reviewer will actually do when they pick up the drawings Monday morning.

Who Pays

Standard construction contracts were not written for this scenario, and the gap is wider than most architects realize. AIA Document A201, the most widely used general conditions agreement in the country, assigns design professionals the responsibility to "prepare Construction Documents in accordance with applicable laws," which covers code compliance as a professional obligation but says absolutely nothing about what happens when a design professional relies on an AI compliance tool and that tool misses a locally amended requirement that a completely different AI, operated by the municipality, later catches during plan review.

CodeComply.Ai's own terms are explicit: "Automated compliance checks are informational and do not replace professional review or constitute legal advice. Final compliance determinations remain the responsibility of qualified reviewers." In other words, the city's AI doesn't take the fall. And UpCodes, positioned as a QA/QC tool for designers, is almost certainly offering the same carve-out.

Which leaves the architect holding liability for the corrections, the builder absorbing the schedule delay, and the homeowner paying the carrying cost while two machines argue about handrail extensions. Nobody planned for this.

Industry data puts the cost of a single plan rejection at $550 to $8,500, depending on project complexity and how far into the pipeline the error cascades before someone catches it. Across the industry, roughly 30 to 40 percent of submittals get rejected on first pass. If dual-AI pre-checking catches even a fraction of those before submission, wonderful. But if it introduces a new category of AI-versus-AI disagreement corrections that wouldn't have existed under manual review alone, you've traded one bottleneck for a more confusing one with better marketing.

What to Do About It

Run both tools if you can afford to, because redundancy is the point. If your firm uses UpCodes for pre-submission QA, find out what AI system your target jurisdiction uses and test your plans against it before submitting. CodeComply.Ai offers a submitter-side product in addition to its municipal platform, which means you can close the gap between the designer's code check and the city's code check before you walk through the door.

Document everything you can. When your AI says compliant and the city's says otherwise, you need a paper trail showing which code edition you checked against, which amendments were loaded, and when the analysis was run. That documentation won't prevent the correction, but it gives you standing to push back if the city's AI is running against an outdated amendment set, and it will matter enormously if the dispute ever ends up in front of an arbitrator or a judge.

Verify your jurisdiction settings with obsessive care, because UpCodes covers 6,000-plus jurisdictions but that coverage is only as good as the project setup. If your firm set "City of Austin" when the project is actually in Austin's extraterritorial jurisdiction governed by Travis County codes, both the AI's output and every decision you made based on it are worthless.

Keep the human in the loop. ICC's position on its own AI Navigator, which has answered over 140,000 code questions since 2023, is unambiguous: "a human should always remain in the loop, applying knowledge, verifying information, and ensuring decisions prioritize safety and compliance." That's not corporate boilerplate. It is the single best piece of advice for anyone navigating the dual-AI permit landscape.

The Fair Counterargument

None of these AI tools claim to be the final word. UpCodes calls itself a "QA/QC partner." CodeComply.Ai calls its outputs "informational." Both explicitly preserve the human reviewer's authority. If the human reviewer does their job, the dual-AI disagreement is just noise that gets resolved at the counter the way it always has: a conversation between the designer and the plan checker about what the code actually requires in this specific jurisdiction, for this specific building, on this specific lot.

That counterargument holds up exactly as long as building departments are adequately staffed to provide that independent human review. With 83 percent of developers citing permitting delays as a major barrier to housing development, and with HUD now funding AI tools specifically to help understaffed departments move faster, the trend is toward more AI deference, not less. The human reviewer who catches the AI-vs-AI discrepancy and applies professional judgment is exactly the person departments are trying to route around.

What We Don't Know

Nobody has tested UpCodes Plan Review and CodeComply.Ai on the same plan set and published the results, which means we don't know the actual concordance rate between any two AI code compliance tools, we don't know how often they agree on the same violation, and we don't know whether their disagreement patterns are random or systematic. UpCodes hasn't disclosed pricing for Plan Review, and CodeComply.Ai hasn't published independent accuracy data beyond its vendor claims. We reached out to both companies and received no response before publication.

This article maps a collision that the product launches, the federal funding patterns, and the mechanics of code amendment databases tell us is about to happen at scale. It is not a post-mortem of documented disagreements. When those start showing up in plan review correction logs, and they will, we will be back with the numbers.
