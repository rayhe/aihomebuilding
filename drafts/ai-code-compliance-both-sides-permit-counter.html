Both Sides of the Permit Counter Now Have AI. They Don't Agree.

By Jake Kowalski · July 18, 2026

← Back to AI Home Building

An architect in Austin runs her residential plans through UpCodes Plan Review, uploading the drawings, setting the jurisdiction, and clicking run. Six minutes later: zero code violations. She submits to the city confident nothing will bounce.

Fourteen days later, the city sends back three corrections — two involving stairway handrail extensions under IRC R311.7.8.2, one flagging an egress window well depth her AI never mentioned. Not compliant. She calls the department. "Our system flagged it," the reviewer says.

Their system is CodeComply.Ai, a different AI entirely, running against a different code database with different local amendments loaded. Same drawings. Different conclusions. And nobody's contract addresses what happens next.

Two AIs, One Counter

Until June 2026, AI code compliance was mostly a municipal tool. Cities like Austin, Honolulu, and Naples deployed platforms to pre-screen incoming plans. Architects checked code the old way: senior associate, red pen, weekend.

Then UpCodes launched Plan Review on June 3. Overnight, 800,000 AEC professionals got access to an AI that checks drawings against 11 million locally adopted code sections across 6,000-plus U.S. jurisdictions.

Simultaneously, HUD offered state and local governments up to $3 million in grants to deploy their own AI permitting systems, with a mid-July 2026 application deadline. Federal dollars are about to put AI on the municipal side of counters that don't have it yet.

So now both sides have AI, which sounds like progress until you realize the two systems are built on different foundations. They will clash.

Why They'll Fight

Building codes are not one document but thousands layered on top of each other, amended locally, and adopted on different timelines. A residential plan in unincorporated Contra Costa County, California, is governed by the 2022 California Residential Code (itself the 2021 IRC with state amendments), plus county amendments, plus Title 24 energy requirements that changed in January 2023 and again in January 2026.

UpCodes claims to track 11 million locally adopted code sections. CodeComply.Ai lists support for IBC, IMC, IPC, IRC, ADA, FHA, UFC, state-amended codes, and five NFPA standards. Neither company publishes how frequently it syncs with individual jurisdictions' amendment packages.

Code year mismatch: Your jurisdiction adopted the 2021 IRC with 47 local amendments in March 2024, but the designer's AI defaults to the 2024 IRC base code because nobody updated the project settings. Maximum stair riser height, guard rail spacing, and egress window dimensions all shift between code years, and the designer won't know about the discrepancy until the city's AI catches something the designer's AI missed because it was reading a different edition of the same book. One system says compliant. One says violation.

Amendment lag: A county passes a fire sprinkler amendment in February 2026, and UpCodes might pick it up by March while CodeComply.Ai might not have it until May. For the builder who submitted plans in April, the answer depends on which AI got the memo first, and neither vendor will tell you how frequently their amendment database actually syncs with individual jurisdictions.

Interpretation divergence: Code sections that reference "approved by the building official" create a black box that AI cannot penetrate, because one system flags the section as a warning while the other skips it entirely, and both approaches are defensible yet neither matches what the human reviewer will actually do.

Who Pays

Standard construction contracts were not written for this scenario. AIA Document A201 assigns design professionals the responsibility to "prepare Construction Documents in accordance with applicable laws," which covers code compliance as a professional obligation but says absolutely nothing about what happens when the design professional's AI checks against one code database and the municipality's AI checks against a different code database with different local amendments loaded at a different sync frequency, and both tools reach contradictory conclusions about the same drawings.

CodeComply.Ai's own terms are explicit: "Automated compliance checks are informational and do not replace professional review or constitute legal advice." In other words, the city's AI doesn't take the fall, and UpCodes is almost certainly offering the same carve-out.

Which leaves the architect holding liability for the corrections, the builder absorbing the schedule delay, and the homeowner paying the carrying cost while two machines argue about handrail extensions and nobody in the transaction chain planned for this scenario.

Industry data puts the cost of a single plan rejection at $550 to $8,500. Across the industry, roughly 30 to 40 percent of submittals get rejected on first pass. If dual-AI pre-checking catches even a fraction before submission, wonderful. But if it introduces a new category of AI-versus-AI disagreement corrections, you've traded one bottleneck for a more confusing one.

What to Do About It

Run both tools if you can afford to. If your firm uses UpCodes for pre-submission QA, find out what AI system your target jurisdiction uses and test your plans against it before submitting, because CodeComply.Ai offers a submitter-side product in addition to its municipal platform and you want to find the disagreements before the plan reviewer does.

Document everything. When your AI says compliant and the city's says otherwise, you need a paper trail showing which code edition you checked, which amendments were loaded, and when the analysis ran.

Verify your jurisdiction settings with the same obsessive care you'd use on a structural calculation, because UpCodes covers 6,000-plus jurisdictions but that coverage is only as good as the project setup. If your firm set "City of Austin" when the project is actually in Austin's extraterritorial jurisdiction governed by Travis County codes, both the AI's output and every decision you made based on it are worthless.

Above all, keep the human in the loop. ICC's position on its own AI Navigator, which has answered over 140,000 code questions since 2023, is unambiguous: "a human should always remain in the loop." That is the single best piece of advice for anyone navigating the dual-AI permit landscape.

The Fair Counterargument

None of these AI tools claim to be the final word. UpCodes calls itself a "QA/QC partner." CodeComply.Ai calls its outputs "informational." Both explicitly preserve the human reviewer's authority. If the human reviewer does their job, the dual-AI disagreement resolves the way it always has: a conversation between the designer and the plan checker about what the code actually requires.

That counterargument holds up exactly as long as building departments are adequately staffed for independent human review, which is the core assumption that nobody wants to examine. With 83 percent of developers citing permitting delays as a major barrier to housing development, and with HUD now funding AI tools specifically to help understaffed departments move faster, the trend is toward more AI deference, not less.

What We Don't Know

Nobody has tested UpCodes Plan Review and CodeComply.Ai on the same plan set. We don't know the concordance rate between any two AI code compliance tools. UpCodes hasn't disclosed pricing, and CodeComply.Ai hasn't published independent accuracy data. We reached out to both companies and received no response before publication.

This article maps a collision that the product launches, federal funding patterns, and the mechanics of code amendment databases tell us is imminent. When documented disagreements start showing up in plan review correction logs, we will be back with the numbers.
