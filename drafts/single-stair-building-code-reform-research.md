# Research: Single-Stair Building Code Reform

## Topic
19 states have introduced or passed legislation to allow single-staircase apartment buildings above 3 stories. The second staircase — mandated since an 1860 NYC tenement fire — costs ~$200,000 per building, consumes 9% of floor area, and kills projects on small infill lots. With sprinklers now standard, the safety rationale has eroded. This is the biggest building code reform wave in decades.

## Primary Sources

1. **Pew Charitable Trusts** (Oct 2025): "States Advance Single-Stairway Reforms to Expand Housing"
   - 19 states + DC introduced bills since 2022
   - 7 states enacted in 2025: Colorado, Montana, New Hampshire, Texas (allow); Maine (in process); Hawaii, Maryland (study)
   - Montana strongest law: 6 stories, all jurisdictions, fewest restrictions

2. **Pew/Center for Building in North America** (Feb 2025): "Small Single-Stairway Apartment Buildings Have Strong Safety Record"
   - NYC/Seattle fire death rates comparable to other residential buildings (2012-2024)
   - 4 deaths in single-stair buildings over 12 years, none caused by lack of second stairway
   - 99% of fire deaths in buildings WITHOUT sprinklers
   - Second stair + corridor = ~9% of total floor area
   - ~$200,000 to construct second staircase per mid-rise building
   - Efficiency ratios: dual-stair 10 percentage points lower than single-stair (2024 Boston study)

3. **Harvard JCHS / Boston Indicators** (Oct 2024): "Legalizing Mid-Rise Single-Stair Housing in Massachusetts"
   - 4,955 undeveloped parcels within ¾ mile of rapid transit in Greater Boston
   - Up to 130,000 new housing units through urban infill if single-stair allowed to 6 stories / 24 units
   - Very few units created in 9-30 unit buildings due to cost/efficiency gap from two-stair requirement
   - Chris Herbert, JCHS Managing Director: "single-stair designs unlock the potential for new units"

4. **Banker & Tradesman** (Dec 2025):
   - Two-stairwell building: 82% efficiency (housing use)
   - Single-stairway building: 92% efficiency
   - Adding second stairwell: $200,000–$500,000 for Boston projects on 3,000–5,000 SF parcels

5. **Planning Magazine / APA** (Nov 2025):
   - Colorado HB 25-1273: municipalities >100K must adopt code allowing single-stair up to 5 stories
   - "Hundreds of thousands of dollars per building" in savings (Stephen Smith, Center for Building in North America)
   - Denver Single-Stair Housing Challenge winners

6. **HousingWire** (Jan 2026):
   - Minnesota studying single-stair up to 6 stories based on safety evidence
   - Dallas adopted residential IBC for multifamily up to 8 stories
   - Culver City, CA: first CA jurisdiction to legalize 6-story single-stair (Sept 2025)

7. **Fire Engineering / Fast Company** (Mar 2026):
   - ICC considering 4-story single-stair exception in 2027 code
   - 7% of floor space consumed, 6-13% total construction cost increase for second stair (Pew)
   - Fire safety advocates vs. density advocates tension

8. **Reason Foundation** (Nov 2025):
   - Single-stair reduces total construction costs 6-13%
   - Single-stair design: 10 apartments/floor vs 9 for dual-stair; 6 ADA units vs 4
   - George Mason University 2024 study: better ventilation, more daylight with dual-aspect units

9. **Mass.gov** (Feb 2025): Gov. Healey executive order
   - Massachusetts exploring single-stair construction
   - Following NYC, Seattle (decades), Honolulu (2012), Tennessee, Montana, Connecticut

10. **Texas 2036** (Jul 2025): SB 2835
    - Texas allows single-stair up to 6 stories, 24 units
    - Floor plate efficiency up to 95%

## Original Contribution

**Per-unit cost calculation:**
- Typical 5-story, 24-unit building on 5,000 SF lot
- Second staircase cost: ~$200,000
- Per-unit penalty: $200,000 ÷ 24 = $8,333 per unit in pure construction cost
- On smaller lots (3,000 SF) with 12-16 units: $200,000 ÷ 14 = $14,286 per unit
- Efficiency loss (82% → 92% = 10pp): on 25,000 SF total building, that's 2,500 SF of lost rentable space
- At $3/SF/month rent: $7,500/month or $90,000/year in lost revenue per building
- Combined 30-year impact: $200K construction + $2.7M lost rent = ~$2.9M per building

**National housing yield extrapolation:**
- Boston: 4,955 transit-adjacent parcels → 130,000 units (26.2 units/parcel avg)
- If similar parcel density applies to the 7 states that have enacted reforms:
  - Colorado (Denver, Colorado Springs, Aurora, Fort Collins — cities >100K): major metro areas with extensive transit-adjacent infill opportunities
  - Texas (Houston, Dallas, Austin, San Antonio — 4 of top 10 US metros): vast potential
  - Montana, New Hampshire, Tennessee already enacted
- Conservative estimate: if Boston-like analysis applied to top 20 metros in reform states, potential is multiples of 130K

## Strongest Counterargument
Fire safety officials argue single-stair buildings create bottleneck evacuation in 5-6 story buildings. The ICC's own committee has pushed back, and the International Fire Code Council officials point to wheelchair users and elderly residents who cannot descend stairs quickly. Must address head-on.

## Limitations
- The 130,000-unit figure is a theoretical maximum assuming all identified parcels are developed
- Cost savings vary dramatically by market — Boston/NYC numbers don't transfer to rural Montana
- Several states have enacted legislation but implementation is years away (Washington scissor stairs: 2027)
- Single-stair alone doesn't solve the housing crisis — zoning, permitting, labor, and materials costs remain
