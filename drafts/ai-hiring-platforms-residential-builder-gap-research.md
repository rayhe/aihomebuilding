# Research: AI Construction Hiring Platforms — The Residential Builder Gap

## Angle
AI-powered labor marketplace platforms are emerging to digitize construction hiring, backed by major venture money and the industry's biggest contractors. But they're built for ENR Top 400 firms doing data centers and hospitals. The 300,000+ small and custom residential builders — the ones actually building America's houses — are invisible to these platforms. They still hire through group texts, phone calls, and asking around at the supply house.

## Primary Sources (5+)

### 1. Skillit — AI-Powered Craft Hiring Platform
- Source: Business Wire press release (June 9, 2026) + ENR article (July 2, 2026)
- Strategic partnership with DPR Construction (WND Ventures) and Suffolk Technologies (Suffolk Construction's VC arm)
- Two ENR Top 10 firms investing directly
- Claims "nation's largest network of vetted craft workers"
- Founded by Fraser Patterson, journeyman carpenter turned GC in NYC
- Voice-first: workers speak their profile instead of typing resumes
- AI agent builds profiles, dynamically asks questions based on employer demand
- Key quote: Patterson admits "most of the hiring is from these top companies... energy farms and grid infrastructure upgrades and data centers"
- Customers: Swinerton, Mortenson, Brasfield & Gorrie — all mega-contractors, zero custom home builders
- Key admission: "We see the future of construction hiring as more connected, more responsive, and grounded in real jobsite needs" — but those "real" needs are multi-trade, multi-region, multi-union enterprise hiring

### 2. Propel People — SMB-Focused AI Hiring Platform
- Source: Morningstar/PR Newswire (multiple releases, 2025-2026)
- $3M seed round led by 1848 Ventures
- CEO Dexter Bachelder: 25 years construction tech (Aconex → Oracle, Command Alkon → Thoma Bravo, Togal.AI, Slate.AI)
- ProScore: AI candidate ranking engine
- Bilingual English/Spanish by default
- Free tier for SMB contractors
- Mobile-first, two-way texting
- Key difference: explicitly "built for subcontractors and small-to-mid-sized contractors"
- Key concern: $3M seed round against a $12.1M workforce problem — tiny

### 3. Buildout AI (UK)
- Source: Construction Management (June 10, 2026)
- AI-powered recruitment platform for UK construction
- Founded by James O'Donoghue and Jack Hurdley (West Midlands)
- Chairman: Sir Terry Morgan CBE
- UK CITB: sector needs 47,900 new workers/year (240,000 over 5 years)
- Context: same problem in UK — AI tools going to big firms first

### 4. ALT Labour Solutions
- Source: YouTube interview with Alex Thibault (June 2026)
- Online marketplace connecting contractors to share/lend/acquire skilled trades labor
- Serves both union and non-union companies
- Interesting model: not hiring, but labor SHARING between contractors
- Reduces layoffs by letting companies temporarily lend idle workers

### 5. ABC (Associated Builders and Contractors) — 2026 Workforce Analysis
- Source: ABC press release (Jan 15, 2026) + Globe Newswire + IndexBox
- 349,000 new workers needed in 2026, rising to 456,000 in 2027
- "Majority of new worker demand in 2026 will be attributable to retirement rather than increased demand"
- ~1/5 of all electricians over 55
- Nonresidential specialty trade contractors added 95,000 jobs since Aug 2024
- Construction hiring fell to slowest rate on record in Feb 2026 (JOLTS)
- 202,000 job openings on last day of Feb 2026
- Chief Economist Anirban Basu: "demand for electricians capable of precision wiring has surged due to data center construction"

### 6. NAHB — Workforce Composition Shifts
- Source: NAHB/ACS analysis (April-May 2026) + Eye on Housing
- Trade workers: 71% of construction workforce in 2005 → 59% in 2024
- Peak: 8.5M trades workers in 2006 → 7.1M in 2024
- 12.1M total construction workforce in 2024
- Management grew from 1.2M to 2M (10% → 17% of workforce)
- Engineering/science roles more than doubled
- Foreign-born workers: 26.3% of construction workforce (record), 33% of trades
- Drywall/ceiling installers: 57% foreign-born. Roofers: 53%. Painters: 53%.
- 65% of builders report some/serious shortage of finished carpentry (Feb 2024 HMI)

### 7. Fox Business / HBI-NAHB Report (July 12, 2026)
- Source: Fox Business article
- NAHB CEO Jim Tobin: "every month, the construction industry is short by approximately 250,000 workers"
- Has been as high as 400,000 during peak demand
- HBI-NAHB report: 723,000 new workers needed annually
- 1.5 million home housing gap
- HBI President Ed Brady: "shortage adds nearly two extra months to building timelines"
- 33% of residential construction trades workers are foreign-born

### 8. NAHB "AI for Residential Construction" Book
- Source: NAHB BuilderBooks (March 2026)
- Author: Grace Tsao Mase (architect, licensed contractor, AI developer, 20+ years digital innovation)
- Addresses AI's role in estimating, scheduling, workforce management for residential
- Key insight from ProBuilder coverage: AI can "learn each trade partner's actual performance patterns, adjusting for realistic timelines rather than relying on optimistic promises"
- Only 11% of Buildxact users leveraging AI-powered features (Buildxact report)
- Small builders still using Excel/paper: <20% use scheduling software (NAHB 2024)

### 9. Harvard JCHS — Rebuilding Construction Trades Workforce
- Source: Harvard Joint Center for Housing Studies
- 2022 trades workforce 6.2M — 11% lower than 2007 peak, still 2% below pre-pandemic
- New immigrant flow averaged 45,000/yr (2010-2019) vs 88,000/yr (2003-2009)
- Only 3% of young adults interested in construction careers (2017 NAHB survey)
- Share of workforce under 35: 45% in 2007 → 36% in 2012 (stayed low)

### 10. LinkedIn Analysis — The Real Problem
- Source: LinkedIn post (June 2026) 
- Data center projects offering PER DIEM to local electricians to lure them from residential
- "We're not just competing with other construction projects. We're competing with a new wave of infrastructure investment driven by AI, data centers, advanced manufacturing"

## Original Contribution (Required)
**The economics of AI hiring platform ROI for different builder sizes:**
- Skillit/enterprise platforms: designed for contractors hiring 50-500+ workers across regions. Cost structure unknown but built for volume.
- Propel People: free tier available, built for SMB, but $3M seed round = tiny team, likely limited coverage
- For a custom builder doing 5-15 homes/year: they need maybe 3-5 reliable subs per trade, total 20-30 trade relationships. They don't need an AI platform — they need the same 30 guys they've used for 10 years. When one retires, they ask around. That's the hiring process.
- The AI platforms solve the WRONG problem for residential: residential doesn't have a matching problem, it has a SUPPLY problem. There aren't enough workers, period. Matching more efficiently when supply is zero doesn't create workers.
- But: Propel People's bilingual/mobile-first approach might crack open a specific pain point — reaching the 33% foreign-born workforce that doesn't use LinkedIn or Indeed.

## Strongest Counterargument
Skillit claims to serve "enterprise, mid-market AND small contractors." If the platform scales down, it could eventually reach residential builders. Propel People is explicitly built for SMB. And the argument that residential hiring is "relationship-based by nature" might be a rationalization for an industry that's simply been too slow to digitize. After all, ride-sharing was "relationship-based" too — people used the same cab company for decades. Then Uber ate the market.

## Limitations
- No pricing data available for Skillit or Buildout AI
- No user adoption numbers for any platform among residential builders specifically
- Cannot verify Skillit's claim of "nation's largest network" — no network size disclosed
- Fox Business article is politically framed; data points from NAHB/HBI are solid but their projection methodology isn't transparent
- ALT Labour Solutions data limited to YouTube interview

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? YES. If you're a homeowner wondering why your builder can't find a framer, this explains the structural dynamics. If you're a small builder, it tells you what AI hiring tools exist (Propel People's free tier) and why the venture-backed ones aren't built for you. Actionable: try Propel People (free), use AI to write better job descriptions for Indeed/ZipRecruiter, but don't expect technology to conjure workers that don't exist.
