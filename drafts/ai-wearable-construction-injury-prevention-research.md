# Research: AI-Powered Wearable Sensors Predict Construction Injuries Before They Happen

## Journalist: Marcus "Steel" Washington (Workforce & Labor)

## Angle
Your framing crew has a 1-in-4 chance of filing an MSD claim this year. A $22/month clip-on sensor predicts which workers are heading for a back injury — weeks before it happens. The actuarial data is verified: 50-60% fewer injuries. So why do fewer than 1 in 5 US construction sites use them?

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? YES.
- Builders: Dramatically lower workers' comp costs (one prevented claim saves $42K+), reduce project delays from injured workers
- Homebuyers: Fewer construction delays, lower embedded insurance costs in home prices
- Workers: Fewer career-ending injuries

## Novel Contribution
**Breakeven calculation nobody else has done for residential construction specifically:**
- Average residential framing crew: 6 workers
- StrongArm subscription: $22.50/worker/month × 6 workers × 12 months = $1,620/year for the full crew
- Average back injury claim: $42,000 direct + $84,000-$168,000 indirect (2-4x per OSHA)
- One prevented injury in 7.7 years of crew coverage = breakeven on direct costs alone
- With verified 50-60% injury reduction rate and industry baseline of ~5 MSD claims per 100 construction workers/year:
  - 6-worker crew expects ~0.3 MSD claims/year baseline
  - At 55% reduction: ~0.14 claims/year
  - Prevented ~0.17 claims/year × $42,000 = $7,140 saved/year
  - Net savings: $7,140 - $1,620 = $5,520/year for a 6-person crew
  - ROI: 340%+

## Strongest Counterargument
- Workers resist surveillance. Wearables track location, movement, biometrics — labor unions see it as electronic leash
- Privacy concerns are real and documented
- Only works if 40%+ of crew actually wears them consistently (Kinetic data)
- Many small residential builders can't afford the overhead of data platforms
- The 50-60% reduction numbers come from warehouse/manufacturing settings — residential construction verification is thinner

## Limitations
- Actuarial data (Perr&Knight/Kinetic) is from warehouse and manufacturing primarily, not residential construction specifically
- No randomized controlled trial — all observational/actuarial comparisons
- Small builder adoption data is virtually nonexistent
- MDPI 150-site study skews toward large commercial projects
- Cost calculations assume consistent wear compliance, which field data shows drops without management reinforcement
