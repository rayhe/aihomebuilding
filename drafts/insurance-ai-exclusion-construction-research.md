# Research: ISO Generative AI Insurance Exclusion & Construction Industry

## Journalist: Catherine "Code" Chen (Policy & Regulation)

## Core Story
ISO (Insurance Services Office / Verisk) has introduced a new optional endorsement for CGL policies — CG 40 47 01 26 — the "Generative AI Exclusion." It took effect January 2026. ISO forms underpin approximately 82% of U.S. property and casualty insurance policies. Multiple carriers are adopting it. Construction companies using AI tools — even casually — may find themselves uninsured for AI-related defects.

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? YES. If your GC, architect, or engineer uses AI tools and something goes wrong, your insurance coverage may have a gap you don't know about. Directly actionable: check your policy at renewal.

## Primary Sources

### 1. Cohen Seglias (construction law firm) — April 2026
- ISO endorsement: CG 40 47 01 26 ("Generative AI Exclusion")
- Definition of GenAI: "a machine-based learning system or model that is trained on data with the ability to create content or responses, including but not limited to text, images, audio, video, or code"
- Applies to Coverage A (bodily injury and property damage) AND Coverage B (personal and advertising injury)
- "May be broad enough to successfully bar coverage for claims involving any aspect of AI, generative AI, or large language models"
- Construction-specific examples: flawed AI-generated structural calculation, hallucinated building specification, erroneous project schedule
- ISO forms underpin ~82% of US P&C insurance
- Small and mid-sized construction businesses face heightened exposure
Source: https://www.cohenseglias.com/construction-law-now/new-generative-ai-insurance-exclusion-what-the-construction-industry-needs-to-know/

### 2. Business Insurance — April 2026
- ISO exclusions took effect January 2026
- Three endorsements: CG 40 47; CG 40 48; CG 35 08
- "There are a few carriers that are starting to adopt those exclusions" — John Farley, MD, Arthur J. Gallagher & Co. cyber practice
- "Clarity is essential, as relying on legacy wording or exclusions can lead to silent AI exposures" — Shawn Ram, CRO, Coalition
- Market has been "restrained" in taking drastic actions but both affirmative and exclusionary language exists
Source: businessinsurance.com

### 3. The Insurer / TradingView — October-November 2025
- Individual carriers filing own exclusions: WR Berkley, Cincinnati Financial, Frederick Mutual, Philadelphia Insurance
- Verisk's Core Lines Services made multistate filing in July 2025 with optional GenAI exclusion
- At least half a dozen insurers filed to adopt Verisk's wordings
- Berkley's and Cincinnati's exclusions cover: use, deployment, or development of AI; representations/warranties by chatbots
- "Absolute AI Exclusion" endorsements from some carriers — fully exclude any claim arising from AI
- Pushback from brokers led one US carrier to withdraw AI exclusions
Source: tradingview.com (The Insurer syndication)

### 4. Financial Tech Times — May 2026
- "Many organizations do not realize that a single AI-generated output can trigger liability" — Dustin Carlson, SRA 831(b) Admin
- "If many companies depend on the same AI system, a single failure could create losses across an entire portfolio" — aggregation risk
- Silent coverage (where AI risks were not explicitly addressed but could be covered) is disappearing
- 831(b) captive insurance plans gaining interest as alternative risk strategy
Source: financialtechtimes.com

### 5. Lexology / Browne Jacobson — January 2026
- EU AI Act becomes generally applicable August 2, 2026 — high-risk AI system obligations
- Construction firms using AI for safety-critical applications, worker monitoring, or automated decision-making may be "high-risk"
- Fundamental challenge: when AI contributes to defect, delay, or safety incident, liability between contractor, AI provider, and others is complex
Source: lexology.com

### 6. Bernstein Shur (law firm) — March 2026
- Copyright/license violations: uploading architect/engineer plans to AI platforms may exceed permitted use
- AI-facilitated algorithmic bid rigging risk
- "Shadow AI" problem in construction — employees using unapproved AI tools
Source: bernsteinshur.com

### 7. SMACNA — September 2025
- "Bring Your Own AI" dilemma — foremen using free AI apps for materials lists, estimators using chatbots for code interpretations
- Individual actions create liability exposure that compounds over time
- No clear policies at most construction firms
Source: smacna.org

## Original Analysis
Cross-reference the ISO definition ("machine-based learning system or model trained on data with the ability to create content or responses") with common construction AI tools:
- Procore AI features (scheduling, risk prediction)
- ALICE Technologies (schedule optimization)
- Togal.AI (takeoffs)
- OpenAI/ChatGPT (ad hoc code lookups, spec writing)
- Midjourney/DALL-E (rendering — less risk)
- AI plan review tools (Archistar, Blitz AI)

The definition is broad enough to capture ALL of these. Even a contractor who asks ChatGPT "does this wall need a fire rating per IBC 2021?" and acts on the answer is potentially using "generative AI" under ISO's definition. If the answer is wrong and causes a defect, the CGL policy may exclude coverage.

## Actionable Takeaways
1. Check your CGL policy for CG 40 47, CG 40 48, or CG 35 08 endorsements at next renewal
2. Document all AI tool usage in project files — what was asked, what was verified by a human
3. Never rely on AI output for code compliance without licensed professional verification
4. Consider standalone AI liability coverage or captive insurance
5. Establish a formal AI use policy for your firm — no "shadow AI"
6. Review subcontractor agreements for AI use disclosure requirements
