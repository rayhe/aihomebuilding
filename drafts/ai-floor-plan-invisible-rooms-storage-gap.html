Your AI Floor Plan Has Four Bedrooms, Three Baths, and No Place to Store a Broom.

By Elena Vasquez · July 18, 2026

Louis Kahn divided architecture into "served" and "servant" spaces: the living room is served, while the closet that holds your vacuum, your winter coats, your wrapping paper, your toolbox, your beach umbrella, your backup toilet paper, and the sleeping bag your brother-in-law used last Thanksgiving is the servant. Kahn understood that the quality of the first depends entirely on the existence of the second. Remove the servant spaces and the served ones collapse into dysfunction, no matter how photogenic their countertops are.

AI floor plan generators have never heard of Louis Kahn. They have, however, seen approximately fourteen million real estate listing photos, and in those photos the pantry does not exist. The laundry room does not exist. Nor do the linen closet, the mudroom, the coat closet, the broom closet, the mechanical room, or the boot bench by the back door. They have been cropped out of the training data the way a realtor crops the power lines out of a listing's hero shot, and the AI has learned the lesson perfectly. It builds homes with no place to put the laundry.

What the Algorithm Sees

The National Association of Home Builders has surveyed more than 3,200 recent and prospective buyers across 200-plus features, and the results are the same every time. A laundry room ranks first, wanted by 87 percent of buyers. A walk-in pantry sits at 81 percent and has held the top kitchen-feature slot in every iteration of the survey since its inception. A full bath on the main floor: 80 percent. These are not luxury aspirations but the spatial infrastructure of daily life, the rooms where you fold clothes at eleven at night and stack canned tomatoes and hang wet raincoats before they ruin the hardwood you also asked for.

Now open any AI floor plan generator on the market and type: "four-bedroom, three-bath, 2,400 square feet, modern." What you get back is an open-concept kitchen with a waterfall island, a primary suite the size of a studio apartment, a great room with double-height ceilings, and a second-floor layout that maximizes bedroom count while minimizing everything that makes bedrooms tolerable. You will notice several things missing: no mudroom, no pantry, or there is one so shallow it cannot hold a cereal box sideways. There is a laundry "area" — which is to say two hookup icons wedged between a bathroom wall and a stair landing, with no counter to fold on, no cabinet to store detergent in, and no door to close when you have guests. There are no linen closets. A coat closet by the front door, if it exists, is thirty inches deep and eighteen inches wide, which in practical terms means it holds four coats and a grudge.

Researchers at TU Delft's AiDAPT lab describe the underlying problem with typical precision: "AI still struggles to understand space, a fundamental aspect of architecture." Generated floor plans "often show disconnected rooms or ignore the building's surroundings and context." But the observation is broader than spatial reasoning. What matters is what the model has been shown. Real estate photography does not photograph pantries. Pinterest does not pin mudrooms. Instagram does not feature broom closets. Every dataset these tools learn from is a curated catalog of the photographable home, and the AI has faithfully reproduced its omissions.

Technical Blindness

A 2025 study published in MDPI compared AI-generated apartment plans from PlanFinder against human-designed layouts in Polish multi-family housing, scoring both against mandatory legal criteria and ergonomic standards. Results were striking not for what the AI got wrong, but for what it could not even conceive of including. What the researchers termed "zero compliance" with essential service requirements meant the algorithm "did not designate spaces for washing machines and storage closets." It "failed to provide sufficient storage space in the corridor or closets." On mandatory building-code criteria, human-designed plans passed; AI-generated plans failed at nearly every checkpoint.

Those researchers named this gap "technical blindness." It is worth pausing on that phrase, because it captures something important about how generative models treat residential architecture: the algorithm is not forgetting these spaces. It has never seen them annotated, dimensioned, and labeled in its training set with sufficient frequency to understand they are required. A laundry room is not a design flourish but infrastructure. But in the visual grammar of the datasets these tools learn from, it is invisible, and so the model treats it the way you would treat a word you have never encountered and skips right over it.

The Gap Between What Sells and What Works

Market data tells one story with unusual clarity. According to the 2026 International Builders' Show research, 72 percent of buyers now want what the industry has started calling a "super pantry," an oversized storage room capable of absorbing Costco runs, small appliances, and the accumulated kitchenware of a household that actually cooks. Sixty percent are willing to pay more for larger, better-organized laundry and utility spaces. Houzz's 2026 Kitchen Trends Study, drawn from 1,800 respondents, found that 76 percent of renovating homeowners add specialty built-in features, with pantry cabinets leading at 47 percent, because the original kitchen did not have enough storage. Ninety-four percent integrate specialty storage solutions into their cabinetry. They are buying drawer organizers and pull-out spice racks and under-cabinet wine fridges because the plan gave them a beautiful island and no place to keep anything.

A separate Hippo survey of 2,000 homeowners found that 90 percent rated flexible spaces as very or moderately important when choosing a new home. Its most telling finding: the top regret among homeowners was compromising on features they really wanted. Built-in storage ranked second, at 49 percent, among the most valued design features. These are people telling you, after the fact, what they wish their home had, and no algorithm ever asked them.

Gen Z is already noticing the problem. Houzz's 2026 Home Study found that 33 percent of Gen Z homeowners are remodeling laundry rooms and 24 percent are upgrading closets, at higher rates than any older generation. They are not doing this because laundry rooms are glamorous. They are doing it because someone, or something, designed their home without one.

Why This Matters If You Are Building

If you are using an AI tool to generate preliminary floor plans for a new home or major renovation, here is what you need to know: the plan will probably look impressive. Its proportions will feel generous. Its primary suite will be aspirational. And the service infrastructure, the invisible architecture that determines whether you enjoy living in the house or merely tolerate it, will be absent or vestigial. You will need to add it back, and adding rooms after a plan has been set is not a cosmetic edit. It is a structural revision that ripples through every adjacent space, every wall dimension, every plumbing stack and HVAC run.

Before you fall in love with an AI-generated layout, run it against this checklist:

Any space that fails this audit will need to be carved from somewhere. That somewhere is usually a bedroom, a closet, or a chunk of the great room that looked so generous on screen. Any resulting change order will not be trivial. General contractor estimates for plan revisions during design development typically run 15 to 25 percent above the original scope when rooms need to be added rather than resized.

In Defense of the Tools

To be fair to the developers: these tools are not claiming to produce construction-ready documents. Patrick Murphy, CEO of Maket, has stated publicly that AI handles "70 to 75 percent of the work" in floor plan exploration, with the remaining quarter requiring professional review. Maket now offers agentic editing, a conversational refinement where a user can type "add a pantry" or "make the laundry room bigger" and watch the plan adjust in real time. Training data bias is correctable. Augment the dataset with utility-rich residential plans, weight storage and service spaces as required room types, and the omissions begin to close.

That counterargument is real, and it will probably age well. Two years from now, AI floor plan tools will likely produce plans that include laundry rooms and pantries by default, because the market will demand it and the training data will catch up. But we are not two years from now. We are today, and today, the tools produce plans that look beautiful and function like a hotel room. A place designed for a three-night stay, not a thirty-year life.

What Good Architects Have Always Known

There is a reason that the best residential architects spend disproportionate time on the spaces you will never see in a magazine: the hallway closet, the back-of-house circulation, how far you walk between the garage door and the kitchen counter where you set the grocery bags, how the laundry room relates to the bedrooms it serves. These are the spatial decisions that separate a house you show off from a house you love, and they require a kind of intelligence that is not geometric or statistical but deeply human: an understanding of what it feels like to carry a basket of wet towels up a flight of stairs at the end of a long day, and the quiet grace of a home designed so that you never have to.

AI cannot generate that understanding from listing photos. It will need to learn it from the plans of architects who already have it, and those plans include the rooms that never get photographed.

Limitations of This Analysis

NAHB's buyer preference survey draws from self-reported data by recent and prospective buyers, and self-reported preferences do not always predict actual purchasing behavior, particularly when budget constraints force tradeoffs. Because the Polish PlanFinder study evaluates multi-family apartment layouts under Polish building codes, it may not generalize directly to single-family residential AI tools marketed in the United States. Arguments about training data bias are inferred from observed tool outputs and published training methodology descriptions, not from direct examination of proprietary datasets, which are not publicly available. No controlled study has systematically compared all major AI floor plan generators against a standardized storage and utility-space checklist. Cost estimates for plan-revision change orders are drawn from general contractor industry benchmarks and vary significantly by market, project complexity, and stage at which changes are requested.
