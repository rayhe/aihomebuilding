# Research Notes: AI Insurance Exclusion Coverage Gap in Residential Construction

## Core Thesis
Insurers are stripping AI-related coverage from construction E&O and CGL policies at the exact moment builders and architects are adopting AI tools. Nobody's told the residential builder that the tool saving them 30% on design time is creating an uninsured liability exposure.

## Primary Sources

### 1. ISO/Verisk Standardized AI Exclusion Forms (CG 40 47, CG 40 48)
- **Effective:** January 1, 2026
- **Source:** Insurance Services Office (ISO), subsidiary of Verisk
- ISO forms underpin approximately **82% of U.S. property and casualty insurance policies** (Cohen Seglias)
- CG 40 47: "Generative AI" exclusion for CGL policies
- Defines generative AI as "a machine-based learning system or model that is trained on data with the ability to create content or responses, including but not limited to text, images, audio, video, or code"
- Applies to **both** Coverage A (bodily injury and property damage) AND Coverage B (personal and advertising injury)
- Source: Cohen Seglias Pallas Gross & Kaufman LLP, "New Generative AI Insurance Exclusion: What the Construction Industry Needs to Know" (April 2026)

### 2. Carrier Adoption
- **Philadelphia Insurance** and **Hamilton Select**: already excluding AI-related claims from E&O coverage entirely
- **AIG**, **Great American**, and **W.R. Berkley**: filed for regulatory approval to introduce comparable exclusions
- Source: Risk Specialty Group / FinancialContent, January 16, 2026
- Travis Landers, ARM, President and Founder of Risk Specialty Group: "only a handful so far carry absolute AI exclusions, but the rate of adoption is accelerating with each renewal cycle"

### 3. Stanford AI Hallucination Research
- General-purpose AI tools: hallucination rates between **58% and 88%**
- Specialized "legal-grade" AI tools: error rates of **20-33%**
- Source: Stanford Law School research (cited by Risk Specialty Group and Cohen Seglias)

### 4. AIA Contract Gap
- Standard AIA contracts (A201-2017) assume human professionals making decisions, exercising judgment
- **No provisions for AI** — no disclosure requirements, no liability allocation for AI-generated outputs
- 5 specific gaps identified (Frantz Ward LLP, JDSupra):
  1. Standard of care: unclear whether AI reliance satisfies professional obligations
  2. Responsibility for design documents: liability flows to human party regardless
  3. Intellectual property: no framework for AI-generated work
  4. Third-party technology risk: no allocation between project team and software provider
  5. Insurance alignment: professional liability policies structured around human error
- Source: Frantz Ward LLP, "AI Is Already on Your Project. Your Contract Doesn't Know It." (JDSupra, 2026)

### 5. UK/International Perspective (HCR Law / LawExchange)
- JCT and NEC standard forms (UK equivalent of AIA): also make no express provision for AI
- Multiple jurisdictions confirm: AI treated as a tool, professional deploying it remains liable
- Danish law: "AI is treated as a tool, analogous to other technical instruments. The professional deploying the tool remains responsible."
- Mexican law: "AI is treated as a tool rather than an autonomous decision-maker... courts are likely to apply traditional regimes on breach, latent defects and professional negligence"
- Source: HCR Law/LawExchange International joint article (May 2026)

### 6. ServiceTitan 2026 Residential State of the Trades Report
- **74%** of residential contractors view AI as an efficiency engine
- Only **~25%** currently using AI
- 48% of early adopters report increased productivity
- 45% report time savings
- 73% believe starting early creates competitive advantage
- Source: ServiceTitan (Nasdaq: TTAN), survey of 1,000 residential contractors, April 7, 2026

### 7. Homebuilder Warranty Claims Data
- U.S. homebuilders paid **$1.071 billion** in warranty claims in 2024 (27 publicly traded builders)
- Lennar: $290 million in warranty claims (2023)
- D.R. Horton: $127 million (2023)
- Source: Warranty Week, "New Home Builder Warranty Report" (2024 data, published 2025)

### 8. Construction Disputes
- Average cost overrun on US projects: 31.7% of budgeted CapEx due to claims/disputes
- Source: HKA CRUX Insight 2024 report (490 US projects, avg CapEx $583M)

## Original Contribution

### The Residential Builder Squeeze Calculation
A residential builder using AI design tools faces a novel risk:
- They're legally liable for AI-generated errors (standard of care hasn't changed)
- Their insurance may now exclude AI-related claims
- The gap = uninsured professional liability exposure

Back-of-envelope calculation:
- Average residential construction claim: $31,000–$43,000 (NAHB estimates for defect remediation)
- If 25% of builders are using AI (ServiceTitan) and ISO exclusion adoption reaches even 50% of carriers...
- That's potentially hundreds of millions in newly uninsured exposure annually

### Four-Step Policy Audit (Risk Specialty Group)
1. Check declarations page for endorsement schedules
2. Search policy body for any reference to "artificial"
3. Review definitions section for narrowed language around "professional services"
4. Verify retroactive date governing claims-made coverage for past AI-assisted work

## Kill Test
**Does this help someone building or buying a home?**
YES. If you're a builder using AI tools, you need to check your insurance policy NOW. If you're a homeowner, you need to understand that AI-related defects in your home might not be covered by your builder's insurance. This is actionable, urgent, and nobody in the residential space is talking about it.

## Strongest Counterargument
Most in-force E&O policies do NOT yet contain AI exclusions. The exclusion is optional (CG 40 47 is an endorsement, not a mandatory form). And the practical impact on residential is still theoretical — no major claims have been adjudicated under these exclusions yet. The alarm may be premature.

Counter to the counterargument: insurance changes happen at renewal, not at claims time. By the time a builder discovers their policy excludes AI, they've already been using the tool for months. The structural incentive for carriers to adopt the exclusion is overwhelming — it's free risk reduction for them.
