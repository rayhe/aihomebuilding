# Research: Section 232 Full-Value Tariff Impact on New Home Construction

## Angle
On April 6, 2026, Section 232 tariffs on steel, aluminum, and copper derivative articles shifted from metal-content-only to full customs value. A $2,000 imported washer with $50 of steel went from $25 in duty to $500. This directly impacts every new home build. Original contribution: calculate the total tariff impact on a typical new home's appliance + fixture + HVAC package.

## Journalist
Catherine "Code" Chen — Policy & Regulation

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? YES. Directly tells builders/buyers why their appliance package just got more expensive and what to do about it.

## Primary Sources

### 1. White House Proclamation (April 2, 2026)
- "Strengthening Actions Taken to Adjust Imports of Aluminum, Steel, and Copper Into the United States"
- Effective April 6, 2026
- Key clause: Section 232 ad valorem duty on derivatives "shall apply to the full customs value of the imported product, regardless of metal content"
- Primary metal articles: 50% rate continues
- Secondary derivatives (appliances, HVAC, fixtures): 25% on full customs value
- 15% weight cliff: if combined weight of aluminum, steel, copper < 15% of total weight AND not in HTSUS Chapters 72, 73, 74, or 76, exempt
- Source: Multiple law firm analyses (White & Case, Thompson Coburn, Phillips Lytle, Troutman Pepper)

### 2. Appliance Math (TrendyTechTribe, May 2, 2026)
- $2,000 premium imported washer with $50 declared steel content
- Old: 50% × $50 = $25 duty
- New: 25% × $2,000 = $500 duty (20× increase)
- Rate DROPPED from 50% to 25%, but base BALLOONED
- Premium imported appliances with high engineered content hit hardest
- Goods that are mostly metal by value barely move
- Missouri nail plant case: 2018 loophole that let finished imports escape duty, now closed

### 3. NAHB / Builder Confidence (April 2026)
- Builder confidence hit 8-month low: index fell to 34 (from Realtor.com)
- 62% of builders cite rising material costs
- 70% report pricing challenges due to economic uncertainty
- Housing permits fell 10.8% in March 2026 to 1.37M units
- Source: NAHB/Wells Fargo Housing Market Index

### 4. Construction Input Prices (ABC, Jan 2026)
- Construction input prices rose 7.1% annually in January 2026
- Driven by tariffs on copper, iron, steel, and industrial equipment
- Source: Associated Builders and Contractors

### 5. Contractor Impact Survey (BusinessWire, May 4, 2026)
- 72% of U.S. contractors anticipate negative impacts from tariffs, inflation, labor shortages
- 74% remain optimistic about 2026 business performance
- Source: New industry study published May 4, 2026

### 6. Insurance Premium Pipeline (RatesChaser, April 23, 2026)
- 47% of homeowners experienced premium increases in past year (J.D. Power)
- Home insurance now 9% of typical monthly mortgage payment (highest ever)
- National rates projected to increase 4% in 2026 (Insurify)
- California leads at 16% projected increase
- Typical roof replacement: $15,000 in materials → $17,250-$18,750 after tariffs
- Tariffs add $30 billion to US housing costs (DCSC estimate)

### 7. Section 232 Legal Structure (Multiple law firms)
- Three-tiered approach: Annex I-A (50% steel/aluminum articles), Annex I-B (secondary derivatives 25%), Annex III (copper)
- UK-origin products: reduced rates under strict metal origin rules
- 15% de minimis weight threshold exemption in Annex IV
- Transitional rates for industrial equipment through 2027
- Source: White & Case, Thompson Coburn, Phillips Lytle, Troutman Pepper

## Original Contribution: New Home Appliance/Fixture Tariff Math

### Typical new construction home appliance + fixture package (mid-range):
Based on HomeGuide 2026 data and Experian estimates:

| Item | Cost | Steel/Al % of weight | Old Duty (metal only) | New Duty (full value) |
|------|------|----------------------|----------------------|----------------------|
| Refrigerator | $1,800 | ~15-20% | ~$45 | $450 |
| Range/Oven | $1,200 | ~40% | ~$120 | $300 |
| Dishwasher | $800 | ~20% | ~$40 | $200 |
| Washer | $1,000 | ~15% | ~$38 | $250 |
| Dryer | $900 | ~20% | ~$45 | $225 |
| HVAC system (imported components) | $4,000 | ~30% | ~$300 | $1,000 |
| Water heater | $1,500 | ~50% | ~$188 | $375 |
| Electrical panel | $300 | ~70% | ~$53 | $75 |
| Plumbing fixtures (faucets, etc.) | $800 | ~25% | ~$50 | $200 |
| **TOTAL** | **$12,300** | — | **~$879** | **~$3,075** |

**Tariff increase on appliance/fixture package: ~$2,200 per home**

Notes:
- Items over 15% metal by weight are NOT exempt (most appliances are borderline)
- The 15% cliff creates perverse incentives for manufacturers to redesign
- Domestic alternatives partially avoid this but at higher base prices
- Import vs. domestic split matters enormously

### The cascade:
1. Tariff adds ~$2,200 to appliances/fixtures per home
2. Construction input prices already up 7.1% (ABC data)
3. Higher rebuild costs → higher insurance premiums (4-16% increase depending on state)
4. Builder confidence at 34 (8-month low)
5. Permits down 10.8%

## Counterargument (at full strength)
- The tariff was designed to protect domestic manufacturing and close a genuine loophole (Missouri nail factory case)
- For products that are MOSTLY metal, the rate actually dropped from 50% to 25%
- Domestic appliance manufacturers (like Whirlpool, which lobbied for tariffs) benefit from reduced competition
- Long-term, this may incentivize domestic production, which would avoid tariffs entirely
- The 15% weight threshold provides a genuine escape hatch for low-metal-content goods
- Builders can spec domestic appliances to avoid the worst impacts

## Limitations
- Exact metal content percentages in appliances are estimates (manufacturers don't publish BOM data)
- Import share varies by brand and model
- Some appliance components are sourced domestically even for "imported" brands
- HVAC tariff impact depends on how much of the system is imported vs. domestically assembled
- The 15% weight cliff hasn't been adjudicated yet, so boundary cases are uncertain
