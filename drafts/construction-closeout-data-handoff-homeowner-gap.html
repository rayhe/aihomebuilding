Project Management
By Frank DeLuca • July 23, 2026

Your Builder Took 4,200 Photos of Your Home Being Built. You Got None of Them.

The project manager on your $480,000 custom home walked the site 247 times. Each time, he opened his phone, tapped into Procore or Buildertrend or CoConstruct, and photographed what he saw: the rebar layout before the pour, the plumber’s rough-in before the drywall covered it, the framing inspection tags, the insulation brand stamps still visible on the facing. Every subcontractor invoice passed through the system. Every change order. Every daily log entry, from the number of carpenters on site to the weather conditions that pushed the roof dry-in back two days.

By the time you picked up your keys, your home’s project file contained something like 4,200 photographs, 156 daily logs, 23 inspection reports, 47 subcontractor invoices, a complete material selections record, and every RFI and change order from fourteen months of construction.

You received a binder with a warranty card for the dishwasher, the paint colors on a sticky note, and a garage door opener.

What Disappeared

Construction management software generates a volume of structured data that would make an auditor weep with gratitude, and Procore alone reports that its platform hosts more than two billion documents across active and completed projects. A 2025 study by the National Institute of Building Sciences and FMI found that 60% of construction owners now use BIM, GIS, and project management software, and 28% already use artificial intelligence, but only 23% are “highly engaged” in data-centric approaches across the full project lifecycle, which means the data gets created and then most of it dies at closeout.

23% Share of construction owners “highly engaged” in data-centric practices across the full project lifecycle. The other 77% generate data during construction and lose most of it at closeout. (NIBS/FMI, 2025)

Residential is worse, because commercial projects at least have contractual closeout requirements. AIA’s A101-2017 requires as-built drawings and product data submissions, and the UK’s Building Safety Act mandates a permanent digital record of safety-critical information for higher-risk buildings. In American residential construction, the builder’s contractual obligation at closeout is typically limited to delivering the certificate of occupancy, turning over the keys, and starting the warranty clock, while the digital project record that documented every hidden condition behind every wall surface belongs to the builder’s account on whatever platform they use, staying there or not at the builder’s discretion.

Most platforms retain completed project data for one to three years after the project closes and then archive or purge it, so the builder moves on to the next job and the homeowner moves in without knowing what’s inside the walls they just bought.

Where Missing Data Costs Real Money

Insurance is where the gap bleeds first and worst, because the dominant tool insurers use to estimate your home’s replacement cost is Verisk’s 360Value, a software platform that attempts to reverse-engineer rebuilding costs by pulling data from county assessor records and a series of prefilled assumptions about your foundation type, roof materials, and interior finishes. An investigation by the San Francisco Chronicle and United Policyholders found that 360Value’s prefill data for a key home characteristic, foundation shape, was wrong half the time, according to Verisk’s own 2019 internal report, because the algorithm treats your house like an IKEA flatpack, counting nails, doors, and gallons of paint, then multiplying by unit costs that may or may not reflect your market.

What that produces: Amy Bach, co-founder of United Policyholders, told MoneyGeek that homes insured at $400 per square foot faced actual rebuild costs of $850 per square foot, and “that’s on the low end of the gaps people have.” A Philadelphia Federal Reserve analysis of the Marshall Fire found that every ten-percentage-point increase in underinsurance reduced the share of homeowners filing a rebuilding permit within a year by four points, with underinsurance lowering total rebuilding permits by 25% and accounting for more than half of all property sales of destroyed homes within eighteen months.

Your builder’s project file contains the actual cost data, meaning the real subcontractor invoices, the real material quantities, and the change orders that turned your standard kitchen into the upgraded version, all of which would produce a replacement cost estimate accurate to within a few percent rather than the algorithmic guess that is currently wrong by a factor of two in some California markets. Nobody transfers it to you, and nobody tells you to ask.

$400 vs. $850 Per-square-foot gap between what some insurers estimated as rebuild cost and what rebuilding actually cost. The builder’s actual construction records would have closed that gap. (United Policyholders / MoneyGeek)

Warranty claims are the second leak, because most production builders budget $2,000 to $3,000 per home for warranty reserves, and when something fails eighteen months after closing, the useful question is which subcontractor installed it, what product did they use, and is there a manufacturer’s warranty that outlasts the builder’s. Every piece of that information lives in the project file you never received: the subcontractor’s name and contact details, the product model number, the installation date, the inspection result. Without it, you end up arguing over whose fault the leak is while the warranty clock ticks toward expiration.

Renovations represent the third cost, one familiar to anyone who has opened a wall in a twenty-year-old house and discovered the discovery tax: the hours a contractor spends figuring out what’s behind the drywall before actually starting the work you hired them to do. Framing layout, wire gauge, plumbing material and routing, which walls carry loads versus which ones are partitions. Experienced renovators estimate that 15% to 25% of a project’s budget goes to discovery and investigation that would be unnecessary if the original construction documentation existed, and on a $150,000 kitchen renovation, that is $22,500 to $37,500 in costs that fund uncertainty rather than improvement.

Why Nobody Hands It Over

Builders will tell you it’s about liability, and they are not wrong. Every daily log entry that mentions rain during a concrete pour is a potential exhibit in a construction defect case. Every inspection photo that shows a missed fire block is a plaintiff’s lawyer’s discovery dream. Handing over four thousand photographs of the construction process is handing over four thousand pieces of potential evidence, and the incentive structure runs exactly opposite to the homeowner’s interest.

This is the strongest argument against data transfer, and it deserves its full weight, because builders operate in a legal environment where documentation can and does get used against them, and voluntarily expanding the universe of discoverable evidence is not a decision any construction attorney would encourage.

It is also self-defeating, as demonstrated by Oxland Builders in Stratham, New Hampshire, which provides a digital closeout package including paint colors, brands, and sheens, flooring manufacturers, fixture model numbers with brand and finish, appliance serial numbers, warranty documentation, and care instructions for specific materials. They describe the practice as part of building long-term client relationships and earning repeat business, and the detail they provide is exactly the information most builders let evaporate at the handover meeting, and they have not, to anyone’s public knowledge, been sued into oblivion for providing it.

But they remain an exception so rare that their blog post about their closeout process reads like a press release from another industry entirely.

Existing Technology, Missing Will

Every major construction management platform already stores the data in structured digital form. Procore’s API can export project photos, daily logs, submittals, and RFIs in organized packages, while Buildertrend and CoConstruct maintain selection sheets, change orders, and payment records in exportable formats. Autodesk’s Forma platform advertises the ability to “generate accurate, comprehensive turnover documentation with a single click,” and Procore’s integration with Buildr creates structured turnover packages that include warranty management portals.

Every one of these tools was built for commercial construction, where the building owner is sophisticated enough to demand the data and the project value is large enough to justify the administrative cost. In residential, a digital export of the project record, stripped of pricing data the builder legitimately considers proprietary and curated to include photos, material specs, subcontractor contacts, inspection results, and as-built conditions, would cost the builder negligible additional effort because the data is already organized and the export tools already exist. What is missing is not technology but expectation.

An AI tool that automatically generates a homeowner-facing closeout package from existing construction management data, translating builder-facing project records into homeowner-useful documentation and redacting genuinely sensitive pricing and margin information, would be straightforward to build on top of any major platform’s API. Several proptech startups are circling this problem for commercial real estate. None have shipped a residential product.

What You Can Do About It Right Now

Before signing your construction contract, ask for a digital closeout deliverable and put it in the scope of work, specifying what you want: construction photos organized by trade and date, a complete material selections record with manufacturer and model numbers, a subcontractor contact list, all inspection reports, and as-built drawings or sketches showing framing, plumbing, and electrical routing.

Expect pushback, and accept a middle ground if necessary, because a curated package without pricing data is better than a binder with a paint swatch, but get it in writing before construction starts, since asking for it after the certificate of occupancy is issued means asking your builder to do free work on a closed project, and the answer will be no.

If you are buying a production home where the contract is not negotiable, ask the sales office what digital documentation is provided at closing, because the answer will tell you more about the builder’s confidence in their own work than any model home walkthrough could.

And check your insurance by dividing your Coverage A dwelling limit on the declarations page by what it would actually cost to rebuild your home at current local construction costs per square foot, because if the resulting number falls below 0.8, you are in coinsurance penalty territory where a partial loss pays out proportionally less than you expect, a gap that could have been closed if someone had transferred the actual construction cost data to the person writing the policy.

A Digital Birth Certificate That Nobody Issues

Residential construction generates more data about the homes it builds than at any point in history, with Procore, Buildertrend, CoConstruct, and a dozen other platforms capturing the granular detail that would serve homeowners for decades: the framing photo that shows where the pipes run, the daily log that records the pour date, the subcontractor invoice that identifies who installed the windows and which product they used.

None of this data belongs to the person who paid for the house, and no state requires its transfer. No standard format exists for a residential “digital birth certificate,” and an industry that has spent the last decade digitizing every phase of construction has not digitized the one phase that matters most to the person writing the check: the moment the data crosses from the people who built the house to the person who has to live in it, maintain it, insure it, and eventually sell it.

That is not a technology problem. It is an accountability problem dressed up as a closeout meeting with a binder.
