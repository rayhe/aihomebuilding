# Research: SBA Builder Self-Certification vs. Archistar AI PreCheck — Two Philosophies for the Same Disaster

## Core Thesis
The LA wildfire rebuild has spawned two simultaneous, philosophically opposing federal/state responses to the same permitting bottleneck. The Trump administration's SBA self-certification (EO 14377, Jan 23 2026) lets builders bypass permits entirely after 60 days by signing a form promising compliance. Newsom's Archistar AI PreCheck uses computer vision and ML to accelerate permit review from within the system. One says the system is broken, go around it. The other says the system is broken, make it smarter. Neither has cracked the real bottleneck: 18 months after 16,000+ structures burned, fewer than 10 homes have been rebuilt according to the county. The permits are moving — ~6,785 issued — but the houses aren't.

## Primary Sources

### 1. Executive Order 14377 (Jan 23, 2026) — White House
- Title: "Addressing State and Local Failures to Rebuild Los Angeles after Wildfire Disasters"
- Directs FEMA/SBA to promulgate regs preempting state/local permitting found to have "unduly impeded" federal emergency relief use
- Framework: replace permitting with builder self-certification to a federal designee
- FEMA retains authority to review all construction for health/safety compliance
- 30-day proposed reg, 90-day final reg timeline
- Source: Snell & Wilmer analysis, Feb 4, 2026

### 2. SBA Regulatory Guidance (Jan 29, 2026) — SBA.gov
- Implements self-certification under 13 CFR 123.800 et seq.
- Requirements: builder certifies (a) all permit apps submitted 60+ days ago, (b) delay due to government inaction, (c) compliance with all building codes, health/safety, inspections, CO requirements
- Two documents: Disaster Loan Modification + Builder's Certification
- Formally amends borrower's Loan Authorization and Agreement
- Applies only to SBA disaster loan-financed projects
- False statements subject to civil and criminal penalties
- Future applicability: any jurisdiction whose permitting prevents disaster survivors from recovery
- Source: SBA.gov press release, GlobeNewsWire

### 3. SBA Final Disaster Relief Extension (Jul 2026) — SBA.gov
- $3.4B approved for ~13,000 LA borrowers (>50% of all FY25 disaster assistance)
- Less than 25% drawn down due to permitting backlogs
- 12,600 disaster loans originally, total now ~13,000
- Permit approvals nearly doubled to ~6,500 since Feb 2026 visit
- Multiple deadline extensions (Oct 2025, Jan 2026, final extension)
- SBA Administrator Kelly Loeffler, EPA Administrator Lee Zeldin visited LA in Feb 2026
- Source: SBA.gov, GlobeNewsWire, Jul 2026

### 4. Archistar eCheck / AI PreCheck — Governor Newsom
- Launched: July 15, 2025 (beta in City + County of LA)
- Later renamed from "eCheck" to "AI PreCheck"
- Free via partnerships: LA Rises, Steadfast LA, Autodesk, Amazon contributions
- Technology: computer vision, ML, automated rulesets
- Pre-checks building plans against local zoning/building codes before submission
- ICC (International Code Council) partnership ensures up-to-date code content
- Available for City of LA, LA County, City of Malibu
- 30+ municipalities worldwide (Vancouver, Austin, NYC, Colorado, BC)
- Source: Governor's office press releases, Archistar blog, PR Newswire

### 5. Archistar Performance Data — Governor's Office, Archistar
- LA County like-for-like rebuilds: 54% reduction in staff review time vs. apps without Archistar report
- Non like-for-like rebuilds: 36% reduction
- 50% time savings from zoning perspective (quoted testimonial)
- Up to 55% reduction in permit review cycles across municipalities
- First submission quality boosted to 90%
- 6-person tech team in LA office (team manager: Moniba Montazeri)
- Source: Gov's office (ca.gov), Archistar website

### 6. LA Rebuild Dashboard (ca.gov) — Latest Available Data
- **LA County:** 3,770 apps received, 3,078 permits issued, 207 avg calendar days app-to-permit, 43 avg days in agency review
- **City of LA:** 5,724 apps (2,233 for SF/duplexes/ADUs), 3,587 permits issued (1,586 for SF), 66 avg days, 17 avg days review
- **City of Malibu:** 270 apps, 78 permits, 250 avg days, 73 avg days review

- Fewer than 10 homes rebuilt per LA County (as cited by SBA)
- ~900 homes under construction (Grist, Jan 2026 anniversary reporting)

### 8. NACo (National Association of Counties) Analysis
- Counties note permitting processes serve broader purposes: land use, environmental conditions, long-term risk reduction
- SBA rule applies only to procedural requirements (permits/approvals), not substantive codes
- State/local governments may NOT issue stop-work orders for failure to comply with preempted requirement
- SBA accepted public comments through Mar 2, 2026
- Source: naco.org

## Kill Test
**Does this help someone building or buying a home?**
YES. Anyone rebuilding in the LA fire zone has two competing pathways and needs to understand:
- Which pathway applies to their situation (SBA loan vs. not)
- What self-certification actually means for liability
- Whether AI pre-check is worth using even if they go self-cert route
- What the real bottlenecks are (hint: not just permitting)

## Original Contribution
1. **The permit-to-completion gap calculation:** ~6,785 permits issued ÷ 16,000 structures destroyed = 42% permitted. But fewer than 10 completed = 0.06% completion rate. The bottleneck isn't permits anymore — it's everything downstream.
2. **Liability analysis:** Self-certification shifts compliance liability from government reviewer to builder. If a self-certified build fails inspection later, who pays? The builder signed the form. No government plan checker caught it. Homeowner is between two parties pointing fingers.
3. **The philosophical gap:** Archistar checks compliance before submission (prevention). Self-certification checks compliance never — builder promises, FEMA retains review authority, but the house is already being built. One is pre-check, the other is post-hoc.
4. **The Malibu outlier:** 250 average days from application to permit. Even with AI pre-check. Malibu coastal commission complexity, lot-specific conditions, environmental review. AI tools don't solve jurisdictional complexity.

## Strongest Counterargument
Self-certification isn't reckless deregulation — it explicitly requires compliance with ALL substantive building codes, health and safety requirements, and inspections. It only bypasses the procedural permitting delay. The builder is still liable, arguably more so since they signed a federal certification. And when fewer than 10 homes are rebuilt after 18 months, the status quo demonstrably isn't working.
