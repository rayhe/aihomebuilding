# Research: AI Permit Review Tools — Workforce Impact

## Journalist
Marcus "Steel" Washington — workforce & labor beat

## Angle
AI permitting pre-check tools are spreading to dozens of US cities just as building departments are cutting staff. Denver cut 59 plan review positions and signed a $4.6M AI contract in the same quarter. Are these tools replacing workers or making the survivors' impossible workload viable?

## Primary Sources

### 1. NAHB "Government Regulation in the Price of a New Home: 2026" (June 9, 2026)
- Regulations add $131,734 to average new home ($499,500) — 26.4% of price
- 40% increase from $93,870 in 2021
- Building code changes: $40,288/home (single largest item, 8.1%)
- Pure cost of delay: $4,112/home ($2,480 dev + $1,632 construction)
- 94.2% of developers report delays averaging ~7 months from regulatory compliance
- 93.4% of builders report delays averaging ~6 weeks
- 97.4% of builders say code changes added to costs
- US disposable income rose only 18.3% over same period — regulatory costs growing 2x faster

### 2. PermitPlace "2026 State of Building Permits Across America" (March 2026)
- 741 cities across 44 states analyzed
- National average initial review: 22.9 days (median: 14 days)
- Fastest metros: Denver (2 days), Houston (2 days), Miami (2 days)
- Slowest: Chicago (92 days), San Francisco (60 days), Portland (51 days)
- Gap between published and actual timelines: 2-5x
- Most commercial projects need 2-3 correction resubmittals, each adding full review cycle
- Carrying cost estimate: ~$500/day for commercial projects
- Incomplete applications are #1 cause of delays

### 3. Denver CivCheck Deployment (PYMNTS, May 2026)
- $4.6M five-year contract with CivCheck/Clariti
- Denver previously approved only 37% of applications on first try
- Target: 80% first-try approval
- 180-day shot clock with $10,000 refund promise if missed
- 59 budgeted plan review positions cut for 2026 (down to 251)
- 45% reduction in single-family/duplex possession time since 2023
- CivCheck: flags missing docs, incomplete fields, application errors before formal review

### 4. Clariti AI Studio (HousingWire, May 2026)
- Julia Richman, VP of Government Relations: "70% time reduction on average"
- 50% fewer review cycles reported by cities using CivCheck
- Working with ~20 planning departments including Denver, Toronto, Seattle
- AI Studio: diagnostic tool for municipalities to identify process problems
- Tool is pre-submission check — human reviewers still do final evaluation

### 5. Austin/Archistar (Homes.com News, April 2026)
- 3-year contract, ~$1.1M annually
- 3-month pilot in 2023, council approval August 2024
- 190 submissions, zero negative feedback
- 50% review time reduction for staff
- Voluntary pre-check for single-family residential zoning
- PDF upload → run against zoning regulations → feedback in 1 business day
- No cost to applicants (included in fee structure)
- Janet Heit, chief admin officer: "serving as a second set of eyes"
- Expanding to plumbing, electrical, mechanical, commercial

### 6. Additional Cities Deploying AI
- **Honolulu**: CivCheck for residential permits (Dec 2025), commercial by mid-2026
- **Seattle**: Executive order — all development applications through AI pilot (2025), full rollout 2026
- **Naples, FL**: First Florida city to partner with Blitz AI + CityView
- **Pueblo County, CO**: Blitz AI approved Jan 2026, funded by state grant (DOLA)
- **Harris County, TX (Houston)**: Approved Nov 2025, vendor not yet selected
- **LA County**: AI report generation for building plans "in just one business day"

### 7. UCLA Anderson School Study (Ongoing)
- LA average approval time: 652 days (1.8 years)
- Average construction time: 863 days (2.4 years)
- Average total development time: 1,413 days (3.9 years)
- 25th percentile: 946 days (2.6 yrs), 75th: 1,739 days (4.8 yrs)
- Observable characteristics explain only 25.8% of variation in development time

### 8. Independent Institute Analysis
- Different reviewers interpret same code differently (like baseball umpire strike zones)
- Archistar eCheck: upload drawings → computer vision + ML maps setbacks, heights, lot coverage, window placement, energy specs → standardized compliance report in minutes
- Austin adopted Archistar as pre-check for single-family home permits

## Original Contribution

### The Replacement Math
Denver's 59 cut positions at estimated $85K/year (fully loaded government salary for plan reviewer in Colorado):
- Annual cost of cut positions: 59 × $85,000 = $5,015,000/year
- CivCheck contract: $4.6M / 5 years = $920,000/year
- **AI costs 18.3% of the eliminated humans**
- But AI only handles pre-check — the 251 remaining reviewers still do every final review
- Workload per reviewer increased by 23.5% (310 → 251) while the tool handles intake screening
- This is augmentation AND replacement simultaneously: fewer humans, each supported by AI, handling more volume

### The Carrying Cost Calculation
- Average new home: $499,500 (NAHB/Census)
- Typical construction loan: ~80% of value = $399,600 at ~7.5% (current rates)
- Daily carrying cost: $399,600 × 0.075 / 365 = $82.10/day
- National avg review time: 22.9 days (PermitPlace)
- With 2 review cycles (typical residential): 45.8 days in permit review
- AI pre-check reduces to ~1.5 cycles (50% fewer cycles per Clariti): 34.4 days
- Savings: 11.4 days × $82.10 = $936/home
- At 1 million US single-family starts/year: $936M in potential national savings
- Current AI adoption covers ~20 cities: perhaps 10% of starts = ~$93.6M actual savings
- **Nobody has combined PermitPlace cycle data with NAHB carrying cost data and Clariti's cycle-reduction claims to calculate per-home savings**

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? **YES.**
- If you're a builder: tells you which cities have AI pre-check (use it), the carrying cost of correction cycles ($82/day), and the new reality that departments are shrinking while using AI to maintain throughput
- If you're an owner: your permit delays cost real money and the system is changing — faster in some cities, uncertain in others
- If you're a plan reviewer: your profession is being restructured around AI, and the math is not favorable
