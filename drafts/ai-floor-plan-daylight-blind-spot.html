Your AI Floor Plan Has Never Seen the Sun. Researchers Proved It Could in Half a Second.

By Elena Vasquez · July 20, 2026

Open any AI floor plan generator. Type "three-bedroom ranch, 1,800 square feet, open concept kitchen." Watch it produce a layout in seconds, rooms snapped to a grid, hallways connecting bedrooms to bathrooms with algorithmic efficiency. Notice what it never asks you: which direction does your lot face?

That question determines whether your living room will be flooded with afternoon light or sit in permanent shadow. Whether your kitchen catches the morning sun through east-facing glass or relies on overhead fixtures until someone remembers to open the blinds. Whether the master bedroom bakes in western exposure every summer evening or stays cool enough to sleep without blackout curtains. Orientation is not a detail. It is the single largest variable in how a home actually feels to live in, and every consumer AI floor plan tool on the market ignores it completely.

This would matter less if buyers didn't care. They do.

What Buyers Actually Want

A Redfin survey published in May 2026 asked a direct question: would you rather have a smaller home with more natural light, or a larger home with less? Forty-four percent chose smaller and brighter. Only 3% of respondents said sunlight was "not at all important" in evaluating a home. Among Baby Boomers, the preference was even starker: 54% would sacrifice square footage for light.

of U.S. residents would choose a smaller home with more natural light over a larger one (Redfin, May 2026)

When Redfin asked why, 53% cited mood and mental well-being as their primary reason for valuing sunlight. Not energy savings. Not aesthetics. The way the light makes them feel. Sixty-nine percent said the amount of sunlight in their current home directly affects how satisfied they are with their living situation, with nearly a third saying it affects their satisfaction "a lot."

Cassia Bourne, a Redfin agent in Denver, put it in terms that anyone who has shown houses understands: "I have buyers who walk into a home that checks every box online and walk right out after making it past the entryway because of low light."

Redfin took the data seriously enough to build a product around it. In May 2026, the company launched Sunscore, a property-level sunlight rating from 0 to 100, powered by Shadowmap's 3D modeling technology. It accounts for the home's shape, surrounding buildings, terrain, trees, and the sun's position throughout every day of the year. It sits on listing pages alongside price and square footage. A major real estate platform decided that how much light a home receives deserves the same visibility as how much it costs.

What AI Design Tools Actually Do

Now consider what happens when someone uses an AI tool to design a new home. They describe what they want. The model generates a floor plan. It optimizes for room count, adjacency (kitchen near dining, master away from kids' rooms), hallway efficiency, building code minimums for egress and ceiling height. Some tools let you drag walls and resize rooms. A few produce 3D walkthroughs complete with virtual furniture.

None of them simulate daylight.

Not Planner 5D, not RoomGPT, not HomeDesigns AI, not Maket, not Spacely AI. Not a single consumer-facing AI floor plan tool incorporates solar orientation into its generation process. They produce plans that exist in a world without a sun, rooms arranged in abstract space with no compass bearing, no latitude, no seasonal arc. A living room facing north in Minneapolis receives roughly half the annual daylight hours of the same room facing south. The AI that generated both layouts doesn't know the difference.

This isn't because daylighting analysis is inherently complex. Architects have simulated it for decades using tools like Ladybug and Honeybee, open-source plugins for the Grasshopper visual programming environment within Rhino 3D. Sefaira, now owned by Trimble, does it inside SketchUp. Cove.tool integrates it into early-stage commercial design workflows. But these are professional tools, priced and structured for architects working on $5 million projects with dedicated design teams. They require 3D models, material specifications, and environmental data files. They assume you already have a building to analyze, not a prompt.

The 0.5-Second Solution That Nobody Shipped

Researchers have solved the technical problem. Multiple times.

A team at the University of Hong Kong and Zhejiang University, published through ANZAScA in 2025, trained a conditional generative adversarial network on 260 AI-generated floor plans and their corresponding daylight simulation maps. The trained model produces a full daylight distribution prediction from a single 2D floor plan image in 0.5 seconds. It achieves a structural similarity index of 0.93 against ground-truth physics simulations that take minutes to run. A companion CNN classifies the spatial rationality of the layout with 82% accuracy. The entire pipeline runs on a standard GPU.

A separate study by Hu, Zheng, and Lai, published through Springer, went further. Their diffusion model generates residential floor plan variants and a GAN predicts each layout's daylight autonomy within roughly 5% of ground-truth Radiance simulations, running over 200 times faster than the physics engine. Wang et al. achieved a 50-fold speedup with a cGAN that predicts daylight performance from basic room parameters in 0.3 seconds.

Time for a trained GAN to predict daylight distribution from a 2D floor plan, vs. minutes for traditional simulation (Guo et al., ANZAScA 2025)

When researchers at Cambridge tested AI-generated floor plans for daylight performance, the results varied wildly. A ChatGPT-generated plan for Jakarta produced over 900 lux in the living area, enough to cause glare discomfort. A Copilot-generated plan for the same climate achieved only 306 lux in the living room, below the recommended threshold for visual comfort. One kitchen layout hit 1,347 lux from what the researchers described as "exaggerated opening placements." Same climate, same building type, two AIs, three different daylight disasters. Nobody would have known without running the simulation.

What the Code Now Requires

Building codes are catching up to what buyers already demanded. The International Code Council approved Proposal G143-25, which requires habitable spaces intended for living and sleeping to have natural light through exterior glazed openings. The minimum net glazed area must be at least 8% of the floor area of the room served. This closes a loophole in prior codes that allowed builders to substitute artificial lighting for natural light entirely.

Tom Culp of Birch Point Consulting, who tracked the proposal through the ICC process, noted what the old code permitted: "That just makes me shudder to think of my kids living in a space like that. College is stressful enough as it is."

The code change is significant for AI-generated plans because it creates a minimum standard that requires knowledge of the building envelope. An AI tool that generates a floor plan without placing windows of sufficient area on exterior walls now produces a plan that may not meet code. A tool that places windows without knowing which wall faces south, east, north, or west meets the letter of the code but fails its intent. Light is not just about having a hole in the wall. It is about where that hole points.

Why This Gap Persists

Commercial AI design tools are not blind to daylight. TestFit, which works with multifamily and commercial developers, connects site geometry to environmental performance from the earliest design stage. Cove.tool's AI runs energy and daylight analysis simultaneously with massing studies. These tools assume a user who knows what orientation means, who has a site survey, who is paid to care about building performance.

Consumer AI floor plan tools assume something different: a homeowner or small builder who wants a layout, fast, and who will figure out the details later. Orientation is a detail. Windows are a detail. Whether the master bedroom catches four hours of direct western sun in July or none at all is a detail. The tools optimize for what the user typed (three bedrooms, open concept, under 2,000 square feet) and ignore what the user will experience (a kitchen that feels like a cave at breakfast).

This is the commercial incentive problem. Daylight simulation adds computational cost, requires location input, and complicates the user experience. Asking "which direction does your lot face?" before generating a plan adds friction. Not asking produces a plan that looks convincing on screen and fails the moment it meets a compass bearing. The tools that don't ask get more users. The homes those users build get less light.

The Global Wellness Institute estimates wellness real estate at $548 billion globally. Homes designed around natural light, clean air, and occupant health report resale values 10% to 25% higher than comparable properties. The America at Home Study, surveying 6,000 consumers, found wellness to be the number one purchase motivator. Light is not a niche concern. It is the feature most correlated with whether a homeowner is happy in their home, and the feature most absent from the tools that generate homes.

What This Means If You're Designing or Buying

If you're using any AI tool to generate a floor plan, the first thing to establish is your lot's solar orientation. Open Google Maps satellite view, find your parcel, note which property line faces south. A compass app on your phone standing on the lot takes thirty seconds. That single data point tells you more about how your home will feel than anything the AI tool is currently optimizing for.

Once you have the bearing, apply three rules that architects internalize early. In the Northern Hemisphere, south-facing glass receives the most consistent daylight year-round and the most useful solar heat gain in winter, when the sun is low. East-facing rooms get morning light, cool by afternoon. West-facing rooms catch the hottest, harshest light of the day, the light that makes bedrooms uncomfortable in summer without mechanical shading. North-facing rooms receive soft, even, indirect light all day, which is excellent for studios and terrible for living rooms in high latitudes where winter days are already short.

If you're buying rather than building, check the listing on Redfin for a Sunscore. A rating below 50 means the home is in significant shadow for much of the year. Above 80 means consistent daylight. If the home isn't listed on Redfin, walk through it at 10 a.m. and again at 3 p.m. on a weekday. The difference between those two visits tells you more about the home's daylighting than any listing photo, which was taken with every light on and the curtains open.

If you're a builder generating plans for clients, the absence of daylight analysis in AI tools is an opportunity, not a constraint. Run the AI-generated layout through Ladybug, which is free and open-source, or commission a daylight consultant to evaluate the plan for a few hundred dollars. The resulting insight, a quantified prediction of how much light each room receives across every month of the year, is the kind of deliverable that distinguishes a thoughtful builder from a fast one. Your competition is using AI to generate plans faster. You can use AI to generate plans that actually work.

Limitations: Daylight simulation accuracy depends on the fidelity of the 3D model, surrounding context (adjacent buildings, vegetation, terrain), and weather data. The 0.5-second GAN prediction (Guo et al., 2025) was trained on a limited dataset of 260 plans and has not been validated across diverse climates, lot configurations, or multi-story residential buildings. Consumer AI floor plan tools were evaluated based on publicly available product descriptions and interfaces as of July 2026; some tools may have added orientation or daylighting features not reflected in their public documentation. The Redfin Sunscore is proprietary and its methodology has not been independently validated. The ICC Proposal G143-25 is approved but adoption timelines vary by jurisdiction.
