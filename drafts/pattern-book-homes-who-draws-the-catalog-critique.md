## Critic 1 — 🔍 General Editor

**Score: 9.0 / 10**

### What works
- **Cold open is strong.** Amber Long printing thirteen floor plans and posting them on a city website drops the reader immediately into a specific, concrete scene. No throat-clearing, no market-size opener.
- **Headline is excellent.** It's specific, surprising, and reveals the gap in the law without telegraphing the conclusion. Both title sentences earn their place.
- **Structure follows the story, not a formula.** The article moves from scene → scope → legislative gap → technology → cost math → historical precedent → counterargument → limitations → stakes. No section repeats the pattern of the one before it.
- **The stat-callout (~30 designs vs. 4 million deficit) is devastating.** The visual pause forces the reader to sit with the absurdity of the ratio.
- **The Sears section earns its place.** Not a decorative historical aside — it directly sets up the question of whether standardization equals convergence.
- **The closing image (morning light never reaches the kitchen) lands.** It makes the abstract question of "who draws the plans" viscerally concrete.

### What could be better
- The article runs long at ~1,800 words. A few sentences in the "Enter the Algorithm" section repeat the point about AI tools being production-ready when one strong assertion would suffice. The two-paragraph stretch from "None of this work is theoretical" through "a federal law is about to fund the exact deliverable" could be tightened by ~80 words without losing content.
- The last paragraph of the closing section has two independent arguments stacked into a single long sentence (the showroom argument and the neighborhood argument). Splitting them would let the neighborhood question — the real thesis — stand alone as a harder-hitting kicker.

## Critic 3 — ⚖️ Ethics Reviewer

**Score: 9.0 / 10**

### Moral reasoning
- The article frames the AI-vs-architect question as an economic inevitability rather than a moral failure, which is the right call. It doesn't demonize AI tools or lionize architects. It observes a gap in legislation and asks what happens when economics fill it.
- The closing question ("a collection of structures that pass code, or a place where someone decided what the street should look like") is genuine, not leading. It doesn't answer its own question, which respects the reader's capacity to think.

### Self-congratulation
- None. The article does not pat itself on the back for noticing the legislative gap. The first-person in the limitations section is functional ("I counted…"), not performative.

### Positions
- The article takes a clear position — Congress left a significant gap in the pattern book provision — and states it directly. It does not hide behind false balance. But it also gives the counterargument (homes already look alike; speed matters) genuine space and doesn't dismiss it.

### Minor concern
- The "$50,000 in funding vs. $100,000 on architect-drawn plans" comparison in the cost-math section is a powerful but hypothetical framing. The $50,000 grant figure is not cited to a specific provision or funding level in the ROAD Act — it appears to be an illustrative example. Flagging this as an area where clarity on whether it's hypothetical vs. statutory would strengthen the ethical integrity.

## Critic 6 — 🔬 Research Rigor

**Score: 8.5 / 10**

### Original Contribution ✅
- **Cross-referencing the total number of pre-approved designs (~30) across all active programs against the 4-million-home deficit.** This is a novel synthesis that nobody else has done — it reframes pattern books from "exciting new solution" to "rounding error at current scale." The ~30-vs-4M ratio is the article's strongest original finding.
- **Cost-math analysis** comparing architect-drawn plans ($26,000–$130,000 for 13 designs) against AI-generated alternatives is original arithmetic, not a vendor claim.
- The article connects the ROAD Act pattern book provision specifically to the existence of commercial AI design tools — a connection not made in any of the legislative analysis pieces from CNN, USA Today, or Investopedia.

### Limitations Acknowledgment ✅
- The "What This Analysis Cannot Tell You" section is genuinely transparent:
  - No program has operated long enough for meaningful data
  - The ~30 design count is a manual tally, not an official census
  - AI costs are estimated from published pricing, not program invoices
  - No city has disclosed using AI tools for pattern book design
  - "I am describing a trajectory, not documenting a practice that has already arrived"
- This is honest and specific. It names each blind spot individually rather than hiding behind "results may vary."

### Strongest Counterargument ✅
- "Homes already look alike" is stated at full strength and not immediately dismissed. The D.R. Horton data point (unsold down 25%) reinforces that production builders already work from finite plan sets. The "brochure photography" line is a specific, concrete observation, not a straw man.
- The speed argument (removing design-review shortens timelines, reduces carrying costs) is also given genuine weight with Overland Park's honest admission that data is "not available yet."

### Verifiability ✅
- Every factual claim links to a source the reader can check. 14 inline hyperlinks to government websites, academic papers, news outlets, and program pages. No vague attribution ("some experts say"). Strong.

### Methodology Transparency (Partial)
- The cost-math section shows inputs ($2,000–$10,000 per plan × 13 designs) clearly. ✅
- The $50,000 grant vs. $100,000 architect comparison does not show where the $50,000 figure comes from. It appears illustrative. Should be labeled as such.
- The ~30 design count methodology (manual review of city plan libraries) is disclosed. ✅

### Concern
- The article would be strengthened by one more data combination. For example: mapping the ~dozen active pattern book programs against the cities where builder HMI is lowest (Sunbelt oversupply markets), or cross-referencing the ROAD Act's zoning incentive provisions with existing pattern book cities to see how many would qualify for dual benefits. This would push the original-contribution score from strong to exceptional.

1. **Label the $50,000 grant figure as hypothetical.** Change "a city that receives $50,000 in pattern book funding" to something like "a city that receives, say, $50,000 in pattern book funding" to clarify this is an illustrative amount, not a statutory figure.

2. **Attribute the $2,000–$10,000 plan cost range more precisely.** "Standard AIA fee schedules" is vague. Consider "per Angi/HomeAdvisor residential plan cost data" or "per industry-standard residential architect fees."
