# Research: NAHB Regulatory Cost Study — $131,734 Per New Home

## Topic
The NAHB's June 2026 study finding that regulations at all government levels add $131,734 to the average new single-family home (26.4% of the $499,500 sale price). The 40% increase since 2021 outpaced disposable income growth (18.3%) by more than 2:1.

## Journalist
Jake Kowalski — punchy, data-heavy, construction-math oriented

## Original Contribution
The compounding effect of regulatory cost increases combined with higher interest rates. In 2021, a builder's $93,870 regulatory burden carried at ~4.5% construction loan rates cost ~$4,224/year in financing. In 2026, the $131,734 burden at ~8.5% costs ~$11,197/year. The financing cost of regulation nearly TRIPLED — from $4,224 to $11,197 — even though the regulatory cost itself only rose 40%. Builders (and ultimately buyers) are getting hit twice: the regulation costs more AND the money to carry it costs more.

## Key Data Points

### NAHB Study (Published June 9, 2026)
- Source: NAHB press release + full study
- $131,734 regulatory cost per new single-family home
- 26.4% of average sale price ($499,500 as of Jan 2026)
- Breakdown: $84,939 construction phase + $46,795 land development phase
- Construction phase regulatory share rose from 13.3% (2021) to 17.0% (2026) — nearly 4 percentage point increase
- Land development share fell from 10.5% to 9.4%
- Building codes: single largest line item at $40,288 (changes over past decade)
- Permit fees: $20,154 (second largest)
- Architectural design standards beyond ordinary: $16,117 (third)
- Construction phase delays: under $2,000
- Historical trajectory: $65,224 (2011) → $93,870 (2021) → $131,734 (2026)
- 40% increase 2021→2026, fastest growth between consecutive surveys ever
- U.S. disposable income grew just 18.3% over same period — regulatory costs growing 2x faster than ability to pay
- 1.2 million home structural shortage (NAHB estimate)
- Survey: 54 land developers + 337 single-family builders, March 2026
- NAHB Chairman Bill Owens (Worthington, OH)
- NAHB President/CEO Jim Tobin
- Regional variation: Southeast (TX, FL, Carolinas) generally lower cost; CA, NY, NJ, IL much higher
- Bipartisan 21st Century ROAD to Housing Act cited as potential reform

### Propmodo Analysis (June 16, 2026)
- The study provides no detailed explanation for why costs jumped so dramatically
- Timing suggests: codes became more stringent 2020-2025 (energy efficiency, climate resilience), permitting timelines extended due to staffing shortages, environmental review expanded, development fees rose
- California ADU reform example: permitting went from <1,000/yr pre-2016 to >20,000 by 2021
- Texas cities (Austin, Houston) added thousands of units through local reforms

### Insurance Cross-Reference (Realtor.com/Insurify, May 2026)
- New construction insurance premiums 35% lower than 20-year-old homes
- $1,002/year savings (new vs 20-year)
- $7,300 savings over first decade
- $32,000 combined insurance + maintenance savings over 10 years
- New construction avg premium: $1,828/yr vs $2,830 for 20-year home
- Building code compliance is one driver of lower premiums

### Construction Loan Rate Context
- 2021: Prime rate ~3.25%, construction loans ~4.5%
- 2026: Prime rate ~7.5%, construction loans ~8.5%
- Regulatory carrying cost 2021: $93,870 × 4.5% = $4,224/yr
- Regulatory carrying cost 2026: $131,734 × 8.5% = $11,197/yr
- Increase: $6,973/yr — carrying cost of regulation nearly tripled

## Limitations
- NAHB is an industry lobby group; study's purpose is to argue for deregulation
- Survey of 337 builders may not be nationally representative
- "Regulatory cost" includes safety-critical items (fire codes, seismic, energy)
- Cannot separate "good" regulation from "excessive" regulation
- Construction loan rate comparison is approximate (actual rates vary by builder, region, creditworthiness)
- The carrying cost calculation treats regulatory costs as a discrete draw on the construction loan, when in practice they're embedded in the total project cost

## Counterarguments
- Many regulations (fire codes, seismic standards, energy efficiency) have measurable safety and economic benefits
- The 2021-2026 code changes include energy efficiency upgrades that reduce operating costs by $500-1,500/yr
- Without building codes, insurance costs would be significantly higher (the $1,002/yr savings shows codes DO create value)
- The NAHB study doesn't control for inflation — some of the 40% increase is general price inflation, not regulatory burden growth
- States that reformed regulations (CA ADU, TX cities) saw results — the problem may be implementation, not regulation per se

## Kill Test
Helps someone building or buying: YES
- Buyers can understand exactly what share of their home price goes to regulation ($1 in $4)
- Builders can calculate their regulatory carrying cost at current rates
- Both can advocate for specific reforms (code streamlining, permit fee reduction) with data
- The interest rate × regulatory cost interaction gives builders a concrete dollar figure for bids
