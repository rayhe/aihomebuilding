# Research: AI Appraisals vs. Human Appraisers — The AVM Rule and the Bias Nobody Fixed

## Topic
The federal AVM (Automated Valuation Model) rule took effect October 1, 2025. Six agencies (CFPB, OCC, Fed, FDIC, NCUA, FHFA) now require mortgage originators and secondary market issuers using AI/algorithmic appraisals to meet 5 quality control standards. But the data shows human appraisers are MORE biased than the algorithms they're meant to check.

## Journalist
Catherine "Code" Chen — Policy & Regulation

## Primary Sources

### 1. CFPB/Federal Register — Final AVM Rule
- Published: August 7, 2024, Federal Register Vol 89 Issue 152
- Effective: October 1, 2025
- 6 agencies: CFPB, FDIC, FHFA, FRB, NCUA, OCC
- 5 quality control factors:
  1. Ensure high confidence in estimates
  2. Protect against data manipulation
  3. Avoid conflicts of interest
  4. Require random sample testing and reviews
  5. Comply with nondiscrimination laws
- "Principles-based" — no specific policies prescribed
- Applies to mortgage originators and secondary market issuers
- Source: govinfo.gov Federal Register, consumerfinance.gov

### 2. Brookings Institution — Biased Appraisals Research
- Homes in Black neighborhoods valued 21-23% below what they'd be in non-Black neighborhoods
- Appraisal bias explains 9-19% of devaluation
- Cost of devaluation: ~$162 billion across 113 metro areas
- Homes in majority-Black neighborhoods 1.9x more likely to be appraised under contract price
- Source: brookings.edu

### 3. Freddie Mac — 12 Million Appraisals Study (2015-2020)
- 12.5% of homes in Black areas appraised below contract price
- 15.4% in Latino areas below contract price
- 7.4% in white areas below contract price
- Source: Freddie Mac research note

### 4. FHFA/Brookings — Contract Price Confirmation Bias
- 30% of appraisals fall EXACTLY at agreed contract price
- In rural areas, 90% of appraisals confirm or exceed known contract price
- Mortgages with appraisals set at exact contract price are MORE likely to default
- This means appraisers are NOT independently verifying — they're confirming
- Source: Brookings/FHFA data

### 5. AVM Accuracy Data
- Zillow Zestimate: 1.74-1.83% median error (on-market), 6.9-7.01% (off-market)
- Redfin Estimate: 1.92-1.99% (on-market), 7.67% (off-market)
- Fitch Ratings: 7 AVM providers predicted within 10% of sales price for 95%+ of properties
- FHFA: AVMs outperform human appraisals in rural areas
- Source: HomeLight, Inman, Fitch Ratings, FHFA

### 6. Bureau of Labor Statistics — Appraiser Demographics
- 98% of home appraisers are white
- Source: BLS, NBC News reporting

### 7. Dr. Junia Howell — Racial Penalty Research
- "Racial penalty" (appraisal difference not attributable to home/neighborhood characteristics) averaged $408,000 in 2022
- Up from $211,000 in 2013
- White neighborhoods: mean appraisal $817,500 in 2022
- Communities of color: mean appraisal $409,500 in 2022
- Source: Bloomberg CityLab exclusive data

## Original Contribution
Cross-referencing the AVM rule's 5 quality control standards against the documented failure modes of human appraisals:
1. "High confidence in estimates" — 30% of human appraisals match exact contract price (confirmation bias, not independent assessment)
2. "Protect against data manipulation" — appraisers who know the contract price produce inflated values to confirm deals
3. "Avoid conflicts of interest" — appraisers are paid by the party that wants the deal to close
4. "Random sample testing" — no systematic testing of human appraiser accuracy at scale
5. "Nondiscrimination" — 12.5% undervaluation rate in Black vs 7.4% in white neighborhoods

Human appraisers demonstrably fail at least 4 of the 5 standards we just imposed on algorithms.

## Strongest Counterargument
AVMs can't see inside a home. A Zillow Zestimate doesn't know about the water damage in the basement, the custom kitchen, the unpermitted addition, or the structural crack in the foundation. Human appraisers provide information algorithms literally cannot access. Interior condition drives enormous value differences between apparently identical homes. A hybrid model (AI estimate + human inspection) may be better than either alone.

## Limitations
- Most AVM accuracy data is self-reported by providers (Zillow, Redfin)
- The Brookings data measures appraisals below contract price, which may not capture all bias
- The CFPB rule has been in effect less than a year — compliance data is not yet available
- The current CFPB under new administration may not enforce the rule aggressively
- Off-market AVM accuracy (6.9-7.7% error) is significantly worse than on-market

## Kill Test
Does this help someone buying or building a home? YES.
- Homebuyers should know that their appraiser's number is less independent than they think
- The AVM rule creates new rights/protections they should understand
- Knowing AVM accuracy rates helps them evaluate Zestimates/Redfin estimates
- Understanding bias patterns helps minority homeowners challenge undervaluations
