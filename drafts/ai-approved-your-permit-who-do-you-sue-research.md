# Research: AI Approved Your Permit. Who Do You Sue?

## Journalist: Catherine "Code" Chen (Policy & Regulation)

## Angle
Cities across the US are rushing to deploy AI for building permit review — cutting wait times from months to days. But when AI approves a permit that shouldn't have been approved (or rejects one that should have passed), the liability framework is effectively nonexistent. Standard construction contracts (AIA, ConsensusDocs) weren't written for AI. No court has ruled on AI-related construction liability. Your homeowner is building on a foundation of legal quicksand.

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? **YES** — if you're pulling permits in Austin, LA, Honolulu, San Jose, Lancaster, Bellevue, or Louisville (all adopting AI permit tools), you need to understand who's on the hook when the AI gets it wrong. This directly affects your liability exposure and insurance coverage.

### 3. Legal/Liability Analysis
- **Jane Kutepova, Michelman & Robinson LLP (Area Development, Q4 2025):** "Construction contracts, by and large, don't address the use of AI, which is becoming dangerous." Standard AIA/ConsensusDocs agreements fail to account for: who selects/configures AI, who owns AI-generated data, who assumes liability for AI decisions, whether AI failures count as force majeure.
- **Bilzin Sumberg (Law360, Nov 2025):** "An inaccurate cost projection or flawed design generated by AI can cause defects, overruns or delays." Contracts must explicitly allocate liability. Remedies (indemnification, damages, corrective work) must be specified.
- **Hahn Law:** "There is no known litigation related to the use or misuse of AI utilized in a construction project." Outcomes in other sectors (Tesla self-driving) "hinge on whom to attribute liability: the user, the developer, or the AI by and of itself." AI-specific insurance coverage is "limited."
- **Browne Jacobson (2026):** EU AI Act compliance requirements coming. Safety monitoring, data governance frameworks needed.
- **EY Survey (2025):** 78% of municipal leaders concerned about AI regulations.
- **DroneDeploy:** Claims 95% accuracy for OSHA violation detection. What about the 5%?
- **Independent Institute (March 2026):** "The primary barrier [to AI permitting] isn't technical, but political." Cities have structural incentive to maintain slow permitting.

### 4. The Liability Gap (Novel Analysis)
Key unresolved questions for a homeowner/builder:
1. If AI pre-check greenlights your plans and you build, then an inspector finds a code violation — are you liable for relying on city-endorsed AI?
2. If AI rejects your plans incorrectly and delays your build 6 months — who pays carrying costs?
3. The vendor contracts likely have liability caps and indemnification flowing TO the vendor. Cities probably accepted those terms. The homeowner has no privity with the AI vendor.
4. Municipal sovereign immunity may shield the city from AI errors the same way it shields from human reviewer errors.
5. Insurance: E&O policies for architects/engineers don't cover AI tool errors. CGL policies for builders exclude "professional services." There's a coverage gap.

### 5. The Due Process Question
- When a human reviewer rejects your plans, you can appeal to a board of adjustment or zoning board. You can argue your case.
- When AI rejects your plans, what's the appeals process? Can you challenge the algorithm? Request the training data? Demand to know which code section the AI flagged?
- Lancaster Mayor Parris: "Our city staff still sign off on every permit." But if staff are rubber-stamping AI pre-checks, the human review is a fiction.
- None of these cities have published AI error rates, false positive rates, or audit results.

## Strongest Counterargument
Human reviewers are already inconsistent and slow. The Hahn Law article notes "different reviewers may interpret the same code differently" — the umpire analogy. AI at least applies rules consistently. An AI that catches 90% of issues and misses 10% may still outperform a burnt-out reviewer with a 300-case backlog who misses 25%. The legal framework for human reviewer errors (municipal immunity, limited tort liability) already exists and is imperfect. AI doesn't make the liability question worse — it makes it visible.
