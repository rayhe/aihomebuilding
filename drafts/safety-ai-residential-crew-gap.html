A $4 Billion Safety Industry Built Its Tools for the Wrong Jobsite

By Marcus Washington · July 20, 2026
← Back to all articles

Jose Luis Collaguazo Crespo was thirty-two years old when he slipped off a ladder on the second floor of an affordable housing project in Martha's Vineyard last winter and fell to his death in the basement below. He was framing walls, and he was not wearing a harness, and no camera watched the ladder where he stood, and no sensor measured his fatigue, and no algorithm flagged the guardrail that wasn't there.

Three months after Crespo died, DroneDeploy announced that beta users of its new Safety AI tool had seen an 89 percent drop in unsafe conditions within three weeks of turning it on. Safety AI works by scanning 360-degree walkthrough images that construction crews already capture for progress documentation, flagging conditions that violate specific OSHA standards, and routing prioritized reports to site safety managers who review them from centralized dashboards.

Crespo's crew did not have 360-degree cameras or a DroneDeploy subscription or a site safety manager. What they had was a truck, a ladder, nail guns, and a house to frame before the next one started.

Every major AI safety product for construction follows this same structural pattern: technically sound, supported by genuine evidence, and designed for a customer profile that excludes the workers being killed at the highest rates in the industry.

Where Citations Land

Federal OSHA's citation database for the current enforcement year tells a story that the AI safety industry has not yet absorbed, and you can read it by pulling a single standard and sorting by industry. Fall protection is the most-cited regulation in American workplace safety, and roofing contractors lead the enforcement list with 3,687 citations across 3,630 inspections and $27 million in penalties during the period from October 2024 through September 2025. Framing contractors sit second at 1,965 citations and $12 million in penalties, with siding, masonry, and general residential building construction filling out the next three slots in order.

Commercial and institutional building construction, which is the market segment where DroneDeploy, Buildots, OpenSpace, and every other AI-powered site monitoring platform sells its product, generated one hundred and nine citations across the same period and $600,000 in penalties. It barely cracked the top six.

Widen the lens beyond fall protection to all OSHA standards and the concentration only sharpens further. Roofing contractors alone drew 10,619 citations and $46.5 million in penalties across 4,255 inspections in that same enforcement year, and when you add framing, siding, masonry, and residential building together, the residential trades total exceeds 20,400 citations. Not a rounding error, but rather the overwhelming majority of the entire construction enforcement landscape, generated almost entirely by companies with fewer than twenty employees working on houses.

What $4 Billion Buys and Who Gets Left Out

Construction wearable technology reached $4.15 billion in market size in 2024, according to ResearchAndMarkets, and is projected to hit $6.85 billion by 2029 at a 10.8 percent compound annual growth rate. Money flows toward smart PPE, biometric monitoring, fatigue detection, GPS-based hazard zoning, and AI-powered camera systems that analyze body posture for slip, trip, and lifting risk. Academic research matches the trajectory with increasing confidence: a May 2026 paper in MDPI demonstrated an ear-level wearable built around a Raspberry Pi Pico microcontroller that classifies fatigue, drowsiness, and elevated heart rate variability on-device in under half a second, with an F1-score of 97.8 percent on an unseen test subject.

Nearly none of it is reaching the six-person roofing crew in Fresno.

Consider what the entry requirements actually look like for a contractor who does residential work. DroneDeploy's Safety AI is a premium add-on to DroneDeploy Ground, which itself requires 360-degree cameras capturing regular walkthroughs, a project management workflow that stores and processes those images, and a safety team to review flagged hazards from aggregated dashboards. DroneDeploy's own marketing describes the system analyzing "thousands of images already captured every week" and generating reports mapped to specific OSHA 1910/1926 standards. Layton Construction, a $3 billion revenue general contractor, called it "the best" thing DroneDeploy has ever done, which is precisely the kind of endorsement that tells a five-person framing crew in the Central Valley everything they need to know about who this product was built for.

AI camera systems designed for construction equipment run $2,000 to $14,000 per unit, with monthly subscriptions from $100 to $600 per camera for data access, storage, and analytics. A residential roofing crew's primary piece of equipment is a ladder, and no one has figured out how to mount a $2,000 AI camera on a 28-foot extension ladder that gets strapped to a different truck every morning.

Research prototypes fare even worse when measured against this market. An ear-level wearable that shows genuine promise remains in "controlled evaluation" after just three test subjects, and a drone-based PPE detection system that achieves 99.9 percent accuracy in identifying missing helmets and harnesses requires "high-performance hardware" mounted on an autonomous drone that has never left a laboratory. Neither has a price, because neither is yet a product anyone can buy.

Workers' Comp Math That Nobody Is Running

Here is the part that should attract venture capital and keeps not doing so, because the people who write checks in construction tech have not looked at the right spreadsheet. A residential roofing contractor in California with six employees and $300,000 in annual payroll pays between $45,000 and $60,000 a year in workers' compensation insurance, based on a classification rate for roofers (code 5552) that runs $15 to $40 per $100 of payroll depending on carrier and region, and in some California markets the manual rate touches $80 per $100 before adjustments bring it down to something the company can survive.

Experience Modification Rate, or EMR, is the multiplier that adjusts workers' comp premiums based on a company's three-year claims history, and the swing between an excellent rating and a poor one is staggering. At 1.0, the rate is average. At 0.65, a contractor with strong safety performance saves 35 percent on premiums. At 1.5, a contractor whose crew has had injuries pays 50 percent more than the baseline, and on a $100,000 base premium, that swing is $85,000 a year, which for a company whose total revenue might be $800,000 determines whether the owner takes a salary at all.

Each lost-time construction injury costs $42,000 on average, per Bureau of Labor Statistics data from 2022, which remains the most recent comprehensive estimate available and underpins the $7.87 billion aggregate loss that the industry recorded from 167,000 such injuries that year. A residential roofing contractor who avoids one injury every two years recovers $21,000 annually in direct costs alone, before accounting for the EMR improvement that compounds across three years of premium calculations and the indirect costs that typically multiply the direct figure by two to four times in terms of project delays, crew replacement, and management time consumed by the claim.

Twenty-one thousand dollars per year in avoided direct costs would buy a substantial amount of monitoring technology if someone built a product at that price point designed to work on a residential roof. But the cheapest meaningful deployment in the current market, a set of AI cameras at $2,000 per unit plus subscription fees, exceeds $21,000 in the first year, assumes the crew operates heavy equipment to mount cameras on, and still requires someone to review the footage, all of which makes the product useless for the contractor it could help most.

Six Phones, Zero Products

What the six-person roofing crew does have is six phones in six pockets, and every worker on every residential jobsite in America carries a device with a camera, GPS, an accelerometer, a gyroscope, and a cellular connection whose processing power exceeds the Raspberry Pi Pico that powered the ear-level wearable achieving 97.8 percent F1-scores in the lab by a factor of several hundred.

Nobody has built the app that would turn those phones into a safety monitoring system. Nobody with access to OSHA citation data and workers' comp rate tables has looked at the staggering concentration of risk in residential trades and concluded that a $10-per-worker-per-month phone-based monitoring tool aimed at five-person roofing crews would address a market of approximately 86,000 residential roofing firms in the United States, per Census Bureau data, most of which are currently paying workers' comp premiums that would fund the subscription three times over from avoided claims alone.

Incumbent AI safety companies have not built this product because they sell to enterprise procurement departments, not to the owner of a roofing company who answers his own phone and writes checks from a pickup truck. Hardware-based wearable companies have not built it because their unit economics require per-device revenue that is incompatible with a five-person crew generating $800,000 in annual revenue. And the startup ecosystem has not built it because construction tech investors have historically evaluated total addressable market based on commercial construction spending, which is large and visible, rather than residential trade workers' comp spend, which is diffuse and invisible and significantly larger in aggregate.

What Building It Would Require

A product gap this specific can be described with some precision. A phone-based application that uses the onboard camera, accelerometer, and GPS to perform three core functions: detect fall hazard proximity when a worker is near an unguarded edge without a harness, flag missing PPE when no hard hat is detected for longer than a configurable threshold, and monitor for signs of heat stress or fatigue through activity pattern anomalies that deviate from each worker's own baseline. Output would include a daily safety score per worker and per crew, a weekly summary for the contractor, and a quarterly report formatted for the workers' comp carrier's EMR review process, all at a price point below $15 per worker per month to clear the adoption threshold for a company with margins that tight.

Vision models capable of this detection already exist in production-grade form, with YOLOv5 achieving 141 frames per second for PPE and machinery detection in 2024 testing, and a 2026 survey of AI in construction safety documents eight separate working systems for hard hat, harness, and vest detection from camera feeds. Algorithmic capability is not the constraint here. Commercial willingness to sell to a customer who has never bought software, has no IT department, and evaluates technology by asking the guy at the supply house if anyone else is using it: that is the real constraint, and it is significant, but it is also the kind of barrier that a well-designed product eliminates rather than accepts.

Crespo's death was preventable, and OSHA says so, and his enforcement record confirms it. A guardrail or a harness would have been enough, and the citation for the ladder he fell from cost his employer a fraction of what his life was worth. AI safety vendors claim they can see the missing guardrail before anyone falls, and data from DroneDeploy and its peers supports that claim on the commercial jobsites where their technology is deployed.

But this $4 billion industry built its tools for the jobsites where fewer people die, because those are the jobsites where the purchase orders are larger and the procurement departments exist and the sales cycle looks like something a venture-backed SaaS company recognizes. In 2024, 389 construction workers died from falls to a lower level, out of 1,034 construction fatalities total, and the BLS data does not tell us how many were working on houses, but OSHA's citation data tells us where the violations concentrate, and the enforcement record points overwhelmingly at residential trades where workers carry a phone in their pocket and have never heard of the technology that might have saved them, because it was not built for them, and nobody with the capital to build it has looked closely enough at the data to understand that the business case was there all along.

What This Analysis Does Not Prove

  • OSHA citation data reflects enforcement activity, not comprehensive injury rates. Federal OSHA covers approximately 60 percent of private-sector workers; state programs cover the rest, and their data is not included in the tables above. Residential sites may be over-represented in citations precisely because fall protection violations are more visible on houses than inside enclosed commercial structures.
  • Average lost-time injury cost of $42,000 is a 2022 figure, not inflation-adjusted, and aggregates across all construction types rather than isolating residential-specific claims, which may skew higher or lower depending on injury severity mix.
  • No phone-based safety monitoring product has been tested at scale on residential jobsites. Feasibility claims rest on laboratory results from camera-based PPE detection systems and on-device inference from proof-of-concept wearables, neither of which has been validated in conditions specific to residential roofing: steep slopes, variable lighting, small crews, and rapid site changes measured in days rather than months.
  • Workers' comp premium calculations vary significantly by state, carrier, and specific classification code. California roofing examples represent a high-cost market, and in states like Florida or Texas, dollar figures would differ materially, though the structural exclusion from AI safety tools would remain unchanged.
  • We did not interview residential contractors for this analysis, and their perspective on why they have not adopted safety technology, whether they would at lower price points, and what barriers beyond cost prevent adoption is an empirical question this article raises but cannot answer.
