# Research: AI Hiring Screens Are Filtering Out Construction's Second-Chance Workers

## Journalist: Marcus "Steel" Washington (Workforce & Labor)

## Thesis
The construction industry needs 349,000 new workers in 2026 (ABC) and faces a 2.1 million skilled trades gap by 2030 (JLL). Historically, construction has been the ultimate second-chance industry — the top employer of formerly incarcerated people (29.1% of employed ex-prisoners work in construction, per Finnish longitudinal study; similar patterns in US/UK data). Now AI-powered hiring and screening tools (Checkr, HireVue, Workday, Pymetrics) are being adopted by construction firms, and they're systematically filtering out exactly the workers the industry desperately needs: people with criminal records, employment gaps, non-traditional backgrounds, and older workers.

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? YES — if you're a GC or subcontractor struggling to fill positions, this article tells you exactly how your hiring software may be rejecting qualified candidates, what the legal risks are, and what to do about it.

## Primary Sources

### 1. Labor Shortage Data
- ABC estimates 349,000 net new workers needed in 2026, rising to 456,000 by 2027
- JLL: 2.1 million skilled trades positions could go unfilled by 2030, potential $1 trillion annual economic losses
- NAHB: skilled labor gap costs housing sector $10 billion/year in delayed construction
- Ford CEO Jim Farley: 5,000 open mechanic positions unfilled even at $120K/year
- ABC: majority of 2026 worker demand is retirement replacement, not growth
- One-third of construction workforce is foreign-born (ABC); ICE enforcement + TPS rescission shrinking this pool
- Construction employment growth forecast: only 0.2% in 2026 (JLL)

### 2. Criminal Record / Second-Chance Hiring Data
- 70+ million Americans have a criminal record (White House 2022 report)
- Nearly 75% of formerly incarcerated still unemployed 1 year after release
- 29.1% of employed ex-prisoners work in construction — the #1 industry (Finnish longitudinal study, Springer)
- Construction's share of formerly incarcerated workers is nearly 2x the general population rate
- CIOB research: only 25% of construction managers would consider hiring someone with unspent conviction
- 32% would not consider it; 43% said "might"
- 70% of people leaving prison still without work 6 months after release (UK data, CIOB)
- Workers with criminal records perform better on the job, are promoted faster, and have fewer workplace incidents than workers without criminal records (Cornell research)
- More than half of unemployed men in their 30s had a criminal history of arrest (NLSY97 study, PMC)

### 3. AI Hiring Tool Bias Data
- Stanford study: AI resume-screening tools gave older male candidates higher ratings; systematically favored female over Black male applicants with identical qualifications
- Brookings study: LLMs caused significant gender and racial discrimination in resume screening, particularly against Black men
- Pymetrics study (Stanford): AI found ways to distinguish race even without demographic info — "proxy discrimination"
- HireVue used by 60% of Fortune 100 and 8 of 10 largest US federal agencies
- 90% of businesses use AI hiring tools (Inc. report)
- Workday class action (Mobley v. Workday): AI tools discriminate based on race, age, disability; judge ruled AI can be "agent" of employer
- Gartner: 50% of companies that made AI-driven job cuts will rehire by 2027 under different titles
- Goldman Sachs: 20.6% of US companies using AI (June 2026), expected 24% by year-end
- California: prohibits AI from inferring criminal histories through social media activity; requires 4-year AI decision records
- EEOC four-fifths rule: disparate impact when AI selection rates for protected groups fall below 80%

### 4. Legal Framework
- FCRA (Fair Credit Reporting Act): requires consent, disclosure, accuracy for AI screening
- Title VII: employers can't outsource discrimination responsibility to AI
- EEOC + DOJ warnings: employers liable for AI tool discrimination
- Ban-the-Box laws: 37 states + 150+ municipalities
- NYC Local Law 144: requires bias audits for AI hiring tools
- California finalized regulations (Oct 2025) on AI in hiring
- Colorado: allows applicants to appeal adverse AI decisions
- Huskey v. State Farm: AI in claims processing disproportionately harmed Black homeowners

### 5. The Paradox
- Construction is adding jobs while other sectors cut (Goldman Sachs notes construction offset AI-related job losses in other sectors)
- Data center construction alone accounts for 296,700 jobs from $86B in 2026 spending
- Yet AI hiring tools trained on white-collar hiring data are being deployed for blue-collar construction hiring
- Employment gap penalties disproportionately hit formerly incarcerated, caregivers, and seasonal workers — all common in construction
- Construction's own culture of word-of-mouth hiring and foreman references is being replaced by algorithmic gatekeeping

### 6. Pell Grant Expansion (July 2026)
- New federal rule: Pell Grants available for 8-to-15-week vocational programs starting July 1, 2026
- $365M in corporate pledges for skilled trades training
- Creates pipeline of newly credentialed workers who will immediately hit AI screening walls

## Novel Contribution
Original analysis: cross-referencing ABC labor shortage data with AI screening adoption rates and second-chance employment statistics to calculate the "algorithmic exclusion gap" — the estimated number of qualified construction workers being filtered out by AI tools annually. No one has done this calculation.

## Strongest Counterargument
AI screening tools don't create bias — they mirror the bias that already existed in human hiring. Construction foremen were already discriminating based on criminal records, age, and race. At least AI tools can be audited and corrected. Checkr's "Fair Chance" features specifically help employers customize screening to comply with ban-the-box laws. The real problem isn't the tools — it's the policies employers set within them.

## Limitations
- The Finnish study (Springer) is European data; US construction-specific data on formerly incarcerated employment rates is harder to pin down precisely
- The "algorithmic exclusion gap" calculation will use assumptions about adoption rates and screening rejection rates that are estimates, not measured
- Most AI bias studies focus on white-collar hiring; construction-specific AI hiring studies barely exist
- We can't verify individual companies' rejection rates without access to their systems
