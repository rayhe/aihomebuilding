# Research: AI-Altered Real Estate Listing Photos — "Housefishing"

## Slug: ai-listing-photos-housefishing-disclosure

## Journalist: Elena Vasquez (Architecture & Design)

## Angle
When AI rewrites how homes look in listings, the gap between image and reality becomes a legal, financial, and architectural problem. Not the "AI renders are unbuildable" story (we've done that twice). This is about the sales side: agents and landlords using ChatGPT/Midjourney to alter listing photos of *existing* properties, creating a deception problem that has now triggered regulatory intervention in California and New York.

Elena's take: the visual language of real estate has always involved aspiration, but AI crosses a line from staging into fabrication. When a listing photo shows a lawn that doesn't exist, a kitchen renovation that hasn't happened, and a view that's been digitally composited, you're not marketing a home — you're marketing a fiction.

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? YES — directly helps homebuyers identify AI-altered listing photos, understand their legal protections, and know what disclosure requirements exist. Also helps agents/landlords understand new compliance obligations under AB 723.

## Primary Sources

### 1. California AB 723 (effective 2026)
- Requires disclosure when digitally altered images are used in real estate listings/advertising
- Must include: clear statement that image has been digitally altered, link/URL/QR code to original unedited image
- Applies to both homes for sale AND rental listings
- Noncompliance exposes agents/sellers to liability under consumer protection laws and DRE disciplinary action
- "Digitally altered" = modified with photo editing software or AI to add, remove, or change visual elements including fixtures, furniture, appliances, paint colors, landscaping, flooring, facades, exterior views
- Source: Bornstein Law analysis (bornstein.law/ab-723-ai-generated-images-disclosure-requirement/)

### 2. New York Department of State Warning (2026)
- Secretary of State Walter T. Mosley issued formal "trend alert" warning homebuyers about AI-generated listing images
- Cited Real Property Law § 441-c: dishonest and misleading advertisements prohibited
- General Business Law §§349, 350 and 350-a: deceptive acts and false advertising = substantial monetary penalties
- 19 NYCRR § 175.25(c)(9): all advertisements must include honest and accurate depiction
- Referenced International Journal of Information Management study: consumers distrust AI-generated images, perceive them as less authentic/professional/misleading
- Source: dos.ny.gov official press release

### 3. Cotality Consumer Survey (2026)
- 75% of consumers expect AI plays role in homebuying process
- Trust in AI to help find a home DROPPED from 30% (2025) to 16% (2026)
- 70%+ across all age demographics say AI-produced errors are "unacceptable"
- 68% say transparent disclosure of AI involvement is important or essential
- 37% say disclosure should be mandatory (61% among baby boomers)
- 44% willing to PAY someone to verify AI decisions
- 55% prefer working with a human for mortgage (up from 46% last year)
- Younger buyers more likely to use AI BUT also more willing to pay human to confirm
- Source: Cotality/europesays.com coverage

### 4. NAHB Builder AI Survey (July 2025)
- 20% of builders use AI for marketing/advertising materials
- 11% for market analysis/project planning
- <5% for any of 10 other business functions
- Strong interest in expanding to marketing and planning
- Source: NAHB/Wells Fargo HMI survey (nahb.org)

### 5. PetaPixel / Wired Investigation (October 2025)
- DeAnn Wiley viral X post: Detroit house on Zillow — AI image vs Google Maps reality wildly different
- Quote: "landlords are using AI to stage the pictures but the AI is also cleaning up the walls, paint, windows"
- NYC StreetEasy apartment: AI image made bedroom look far bigger than actual tiny loft
- Realtor Jason Haber: "Why would I send my photos... to a virtual stager... at $500 when I can just do it in ChatGPT for free in 45 seconds?"
- National Association of Realtors specifically prohibits misleading images
- AutoReel: company specializing in turning listing photos into AI video
- Source: petapixel.com/2025/10/27

### 6. Inman News — Deepfakes/AI Slop in Real Estate (November 2025)
- Three threat categories: deepfakes, AI slop, fake digital staging
- Impersonation fraud, fabricated property features, misleading photos
- Agents need "digital literacy and verification practices"
- Source: inman.com/2025/11/06

### 7. HousingWire — "When Listings Lie" (2025)
- Virtual staging evolved from simple furniture-adding to AI-powered property alteration
- "Controversial force in real estate marketing"
- Agents using AI staging as cost-saving measure
- Source: housingwire.com

### 8. International Journal of Information Management Study (2025)
- Referenced by NY Department of State
- Finding: consumers distrust AI-generated images in real estate
- AI images perceived as: less authentic, less professional, misleading
- Negatively affects consumer trust and purchase intentions
- Especially for "high involvement decisions" like buying a home
- Source: sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0268401225000866

## Data Points Summary
- Trust in AI for home finding: 30% (2025) → 16% (2026) — nearly halved
- 20% of builders use AI for marketing (NAHB, July 2025)
- 75% of consumers expect AI in homebuying (Cotality 2026)
- 70%+ say AI errors are unacceptable
- 68% want transparent AI disclosure
- 44% would pay extra for human verification
- Virtual staging: $500 traditional vs ~$0 with ChatGPT
- California AB 723: disclosure + original photo link required
- New York: existing deceptive advertising laws apply
- NAR rules prohibit misleading images (but enforcement is MLS-level)

## Structural Insight for Article
The interesting structural argument: the economics of real estate photography just collapsed. Traditional virtual staging cost $500/room. AI staging is essentially free. When the cost of deception drops to zero, the volume of deception explodes. California and New York responded with regulation, but 48 states haven't. The homebuyer navigating listings in most of the country has no regulatory protection from AI-altered photos.

Meanwhile, the data shows trust is collapsing (30% → 16%). The industry is destroying its own credibility faster than it can regulate itself. The NAR prohibition exists but enforcement happens at the MLS level — and MLSs vary wildly.

## Elena's Voice Notes
- She sees this as visual culture degradation — the listing photo was never truthful, but it had physical constraints (you had to photograph the actual space). AI removes that constraint entirely.
- She'd note the architectural irony: the industry that creates physical spaces is now marketing fictional ones
- She's skeptical of the "it's just staging" defense — there's a difference between adding furniture and adding a kitchen renovation
- She'd find the Detroit example viscerally offensive — a dilapidated house presented as move-in ready
- She'd have specific, practical advice for buyers: what to look for in AI-altered images
