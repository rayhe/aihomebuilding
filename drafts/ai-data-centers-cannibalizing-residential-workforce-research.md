# Research: AI Data Centers Are Cannibalizing the Residential Construction Workforce

## Angle
The AI industry that promises to solve the construction labor shortage is actively making it worse. Data center construction is pulling electricians, HVAC technicians, and other skilled trades away from residential projects with higher wages, longer backlogs, and steadier work. The irony: the technology that claims it will replace construction workers needs 20% of the existing workforce just to build its own infrastructure.

## Novel Contribution
Original calculation: If OpenAI's estimate of 20% of the skilled trades workforce is accurate, and the residential sector employs roughly 30% of construction workers, the data center buildout could effectively consume two-thirds of residential construction's labor capacity over 5 years — if no new workers enter the pipeline. Cross-referencing ABC's 349K/year need with the net-negative manufacturing pipeline (Randstad's 102-out-per-100-in ratio), the math doesn't close without immigration or massive training investment.

## Strongest Counterargument
Data center construction is geographically concentrated (Texas, Virginia, Ohio, Wisconsin) — not all residential markets feel it equally. And higher wages attract new entrants over time, expanding the pie. The AI industry argues it's creating jobs, not stealing them.
