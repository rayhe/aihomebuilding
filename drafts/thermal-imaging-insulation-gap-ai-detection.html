Eighty-Nine Percent of American Homes Are Under-Insulated. The Tool That Catches It Costs $300 and Takes Twelve Minutes.

By Priya Greenwood · July 21, 2026
← Back to AI Home Building
89% Percentage of U.S. single-family homes that fail to meet even the 2012 energy code for insulation, according to an ICF Consulting study of 1 million homes using the NREL ResStock database.

The HERS rater pulled up to a new-construction home in Charlotte last February and did what HERS raters do: graded the insulation. The builder had specified R-15 fiberglass batts in every exterior wall cavity, exactly meeting North Carolina's code requirement. The installer had stapled them in. The drywall crew was scheduled for Monday.

She pulled out a FLIR E8-XT and pointed it at the north-facing wall. The screen bloomed blue and orange. Three stud bays near the kitchen showed temperatures 11°F cooler than the adjacent cavities. She pulled the batt in one of them. It had been cut four inches short at the bottom, folded over a horizontal wire run, and compressed against a junction box until it bunched like a crumpled paper bag. Two of the three had gaps visible only from the attic side, where nobody had checked.

Grade III. Five percent of the north wall performing as if it had no insulation at all.

The builder was standing right there. He said what builders often say: That's how they always do it.

The Number That Should Keep You Up at Night

In 2024, the Insulation Institute commissioned ICF Consulting to answer a question most homeowners never think to ask: how many American homes are actually insulated to a reasonable standard? ICF used the National Renewable Energy Laboratory's ResStock database, which models a million representative homes across every climate zone, vintage, foundation type, and geometry in the country. The baseline wasn't aggressive. It was the 2012 International Energy Conservation Code, a standard so modest that 22 states had already adopted it or something stricter.

Eighty-nine percent of single-family homes failed to meet it.

That number needs to sink in. It does not mean 89% of homes built before modern codes. It means 89% of the entire single-family housing stock, including homes built this decade, fail a standard that the industry itself calls "easily achievable."

The North American Insulation Manufacturers Association puts a fine point on it: insulation has three times the impact on a home's energy performance and comfort as windows or doors. Homeowners will comparison-shop window brands for weeks. Very few have ever looked at their wall insulation.

They have a reason for that. You can't see insulation. Not after the drywall goes up. Not during a standard home inspection. Not ever, unless someone tears a wall open or points a thermal camera at it.

What Your Inspector Doesn't Do

A standard home inspection, the one you pay $400 to $600 for before closing, covers structure, roofing, plumbing, electrical, HVAC, and a visual check of accessible insulation, typically whatever's visible in the attic hatch. What the inspector cannot see includes everything behind drywall, under siding, and between floors. That is where most insulation lives and where most insulation fails.

The American Society of Home Inspectors published an article in 2025 making the case that thermal imaging should be standard, not optional. The equipment cost has cratered. Infrared cameras that ran $5,000 to $10,000 a decade ago now cost $1,500 to $3,000 for professional-grade units. Consumer models from FLIR and Seek Thermal start at $300. A smartphone attachment clips on in seconds.

Despite that collapse in price, most inspection firms offer thermal imaging as a $150 add-on. An ASHI-certified inspector in Central Texas explained the distinction bluntly: "There's a difference between 'add thermal imaging for $150 extra' and 'thermal imaging is included in your broad inspection.'" When it's optional, buyers skip it. When buyers skip it, the insulation gap goes undetected. When the gap goes undetected, the homeowner pays for it every month in utility bills and never knows why the bedroom above the garage is always cold.

What the Camera Sees

A thermal camera doesn't see through walls. It reads surface temperatures. But surface temperatures tell a story. Missing insulation in a wall cavity makes the drywall cooler in winter and warmer in summer than the adjacent cavity where the batt was installed correctly. The temperature differential is often 6 to 11 degrees Fahrenheit. Thermal bridges, the stud-framing paths where heat conducts straight through the wall assembly, show as vertical lines of warmer surface temperature in winter. Moisture intrusion behind the wall makes a distinct cold spot where evaporation is pulling heat.

Building Science Corporation ran one of the most comprehensive insulation studies in the field's history, the Thermal Metric Project, with a consortium of six manufacturers. The headline finding was reassuring on its face: all insulation types perform equally when properly installed and air-sealed. R-value is R-value. But the second finding was the one that mattered more. Thermal bridging through standard wood framing reduced the thermal performance of every tested wall assembly by roughly 15%, regardless of what insulation filled the cavities. The studs themselves are the weak link, and no amount of careful batt installation eliminates them.

That 15% penalty is baked into every stick-framed home in America. The question is whether the other 85% of the wall is actually performing. In half the homes being built right now, it's not.

The DOE's Uncomfortable Data

RESNET's grading system for insulation installation is simple. Grade I means the installer followed manufacturer instructions, filled every cavity, cut around every wire and pipe, and encapsulated the insulation on all six sides. HERS modeling software credits 100% of the installed R-value. Grade II means minor gaps and compression, up to 2% of the area. Grade III means 2% to 5% of the insulation is missing or so compressed it might as well be. Anything beyond 5% and the HERS software stops pretending: it models the uninsulated area separately, as if the wall has a hole in it.

In a field survey, the Department of Energy found that only about half of new homes achieved Grade I installation quality. Half. Not homes built in 1975. Homes built under current code, inspected by current building departments, insulated by current contractors. The Insulation Institute cited this finding in a 2017 webinar and urged builders to treat it as a cost problem: on a $2,600 batt insulation job, a whole-house Grade III rating means $130 of insulation the homeowner paid for that the energy model simply ignores.

But the homeowner's cost is worse than $130 in wasted material. The EPA estimates that properly air-sealing and insulating a home saves 15% on heating and cooling, which translates to 11% of total utility costs nationally. For a household spending $2,000 a year on energy, that's $220 a year. For a home in Climate Zone 5 or above, the winter-dominated zones that stretch from Iowa to Maine, the savings from correct insulation run higher: 16% to 18% of heating and cooling costs alone.

15% Reduction in heating and cooling costs from proper insulation and air sealing, per EPA estimates. National average: $220 per year on a $2,000 energy bill. Northern climates save more.

Over a 30-year mortgage, $220 a year is $6,600 in undiscounted energy waste. In Minneapolis, where heating costs dominate and the savings percentage is higher, the number climbs past $10,000. And that's the gap between under-insulated and code-minimum. The gap between code-minimum and well-insulated is another conversation entirely.

A Neural Network That Sees What Inspectors Miss

Researchers have been feeding thermal images of building envelopes to neural networks for several years now, and the results have quietly reached a point that the inspection industry hasn't caught up with. A study published in the MDPI journal Applied Sciences used Gaussian mixture model clustering to segment thermal images by temperature, DBSCAN to isolate individual defect regions, and an artificial neural network trained on morphological features to classify which anomalies were actually thermal bridges versus ambient noise, reflections, or equipment artifacts.

The system achieved 89.29% precision, 87.29% recall, and an 87.63% F-score on field-validated buildings with known thermal bridges. It doesn't hallucinate defects. It identifies them with a consistency that human inspectors, whose judgments are inherently subjective and vary with experience, cannot match across a hundred inspections.

A separate team at Umeå University in Sweden developed a label-free approach using generative adversarial networks. Their method predicts what the thermal distribution of a wall should look like based on a standard color photograph, then flags regions where the actual thermal scan diverges from the prediction. No training labels. No manual defect catalog. The system learns what "normal" means and points at what isn't. It can be mounted on drones for automated large-scale scanning of entire neighborhoods.

These are not commercial products yet. But they point to a near future where a drone flies your street on a January morning, feeds thermal images through a classification model, and sends you a report that says: your north wall has a 4-square-foot insulation void at the second-floor band joist. The technology to do this exists today. The business model to deliver it is maybe 18 months from someone packaging it into a subscription.

Why Nobody Flies the Camera

The barrier isn't cost. A $300 FLIR ONE Gen 3 snaps onto an iPhone and produces thermal images good enough to identify missing insulation cavities, duct leaks, and moisture intrusion. A professional FLIR E8-XT runs about $2,500 and produces images detailed enough for AI classification. The hardware is there.

The barrier is the same one that keeps construction the least digitized major industry on Earth: habit, liability, and misaligned incentives. The home inspector works for the buyer, but only for a few hours, and their liability exposure makes them conservative about new tools. The builder who installed the insulation has no interest in someone checking it with infrared after drywall. The building department inspector checks for the presence of insulation, not the quality of its installation. Nobody in the transaction is paid to look harder.

ServiceTitan's 2026 State of the Trades report surveyed a thousand residential contractors and found that 74% view AI as an efficiency tool. Only 25% are actually using it. That gap, between knowing a tool exists and putting it in someone's hands, is the entire story of construction technology adoption. The camera exists. The AI exists. The proof that 89% of homes are under-insulated exists. Nobody has stitched them together into a standard workflow.

What You Can Do About It

If You're Buying a Home

  • Request thermal imaging as part of your home inspection, not as an add-on. If your inspector doesn't offer it, find one who does. Budget $150 to $300 for the service. It will tell you more about the home's actual energy performance than any disclosure form.
  • Schedule the thermal scan during heating or cooling season when there's at least a 15°F difference between indoor and outdoor temperature. A scan in September in Atlanta won't show you much.
  • Ask the inspector to photograph all exterior walls, the attic floor, and any ceilings below unconditioned space. Those are where insulation defects hide.

If You're Building New

  • Require a pre-drywall thermal scan in your contract. This is the one moment when insulation defects are both detectable and fixable. After drywall, the wall is sealed for 50 years.
  • Insist on RESNET Grade I installation and hire an independent HERS rater to verify. Energy Star certification, DOE Zero Energy Ready Homes, and most utility rebate programs already require Grade I. Your builder should be hitting it anyway.
  • If your builder resists, ask why. The answer usually involves the insulation subcontractor, who is often the lowest bidder and the least supervised trade on the job.

If You Already Own

  • Buy a FLIR ONE ($300) or rent a thermal camera from a tool-rental outlet. On a cold morning, 30°F or below, walk the interior of your home with the camera pointed at exterior walls. Cold spots against warm walls are insulation voids. Consistent cold stripes at 16-inch intervals are stud-framing thermal bridges, which are normal. Random cold patches between studs are not.
  • Check the attic. Bare spots, compressed batts, and gaps around penetrations are the most common and most fixable insulation problems in existing homes.
  • Claim the 25C tax credit: up to $1,200 per year through 2032 for insulation and air-sealing improvements. The Inflation Reduction Act made this available every year, not once per lifetime.

The Scan That Should Be Standard

The economics of thermal imaging in residential inspection stopped making sense as an upsell about five years ago. The camera is cheaper than the inspection itself. The information it produces is more consequential than half the items on a standard checklist. Outlet covers, dripping faucets, and cracked grout are visible to the naked eye. The insulation that constitutes 89% of your home's energy envelope is not.

When the AI catches up, and it's close, the argument becomes even harder to dodge. An automated classification model running on a $300 camera attachment could scan every wall in a home during a walkthrough, flag defect zones with GPS-tagged images, and produce a report that says exactly where the insulation failed and what it's costing the homeowner per year. The precision is already at 89%. The recall is at 87%. The cost curve is pointing at zero marginal cost per scan.

The question for the inspection industry isn't whether to adopt this. It's whether to adopt it before someone outside the industry does it for them. A startup with a fleet of thermal-equipped drones and a trained classification model could offer neighborhood-scale insulation audits for less than the cost of a single traditional inspection. The data is already worth something to utilities running weatherization programs, to insurers pricing policies against moisture damage, and to cities trying to meet emissions targets.

Eighty-nine percent of American homes have insulation that doesn't meet a standard the industry calls easy. A camera that costs less than a decent dinner for two can prove it in twelve minutes. An AI that nobody has productized yet can do it without a human in the loop. The question is not whether this information exists. It's how long we keep choosing not to look at it.
