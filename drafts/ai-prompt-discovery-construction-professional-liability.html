A Federal Judge Just Made Your Architect's AI Prompts Discoverable. Nobody in Construction Saved Theirs.

By Catherine Chen

On May 18, 2026, Magistrate Judge Thomas Farrish of the U.S. District Court for the District of Connecticut ordered a plaintiff to hand over every generative-AI prompt its expert witness used while preparing a report. Case involved a fuel terminal, not a house. Expert was a climate historian, not a structural engineer. Prompts were used to sort documents, not to design a foundation.

Doesn't matter.

Under the ruling's logic is straightforward: AI prompts are discoverable methodology under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 26, no different from formulas, code, or statistical model parameters. That logic applies with equal force to any professional whose opinions could one day be tested in court. Architects. Structural engineers. Home inspectors. Energy consultants. Every professional who designs, verifies, and signs off on the home you're building.

And here is the problem that nobody in residential construction has begun to solve: according to a Bluebeam survey of 1,000 architecture, engineering, and construction professionals, 42% now use AI for planning and design work. A separate Arup survey of engineers, architects, and city planners across ten countries found 36% use AI tools daily, with more than 80% using them at least weekly. Yet a Builder Online industry survey found that 94% of designers and 92% of contractors using AI report having received no formal training in how to apply it. No training means no protocols, no protocols means no documentation, and no documentation means that when a project goes sideways, the AI audit trail that opposing counsel will now demand does not exist.

What the Court Actually Said

In Conservation Law Foundation, Inc. v. Shell Oil Co. (Case No. 3:21-cv-00933, D. Conn., ECF No. 970), the expert, Dr. Naomi Oreskes, used a commercially available generative-AI tool to filter and identify potentially relevant documents from the defendants' productions. She disclosed her AI use in the report and identified certain search terms and frequencies. Defendants wanted the prompts themselves, and the court agreed.

His reasoning was deceptively simple.

An expert's methodology is discoverable, and using AI to narrow a document set is part of that methodology, so the prompts that shaped which documents the expert saw, and which she never saw, are within scope. He rejected the argument that a protective stipulation covering "notes, drafts, or communications" shielded the prompts. As Mayer Brown's analysis of the ruling observed, the court placed AI prompts "in the same category as dataset selection criteria or statistical model parameters, aspects of methodology that have long been fair game."

The order is currently stayed pending the district court's resolution of the plaintiff's objection. It is a single magistrate-judge ruling arising from a narrow set of facts, but as a Baker Botts analysis published June 8, 2026, noted, courts have not treated AI as conferring new protections or automatically destroying existing ones, and they have applied "the same questions they have always asked: whether there was a qualifying relationship, whether confidentiality was maintained, and whether the materials were prepared in anticipation of litigation." Settled framework. New facts.

Translate This to Your Home Project

Now picture residential construction.

An architect uses an AI tool to generate three preliminary floor plan options for a custom home, then refines one through iterative prompting. A structural engineer pastes a load calculation into an AI assistant and asks it to verify the result. A home inspector dictates field notes into an AI tool that formats his report in twelve minutes instead of four hours. An energy consultant uses AI to model HVAC loads from a floor plan image.

Each of these professionals has just created a chain of AI interactions that, under the logic of the Connecticut ruling, could be demanded in discovery if the project becomes disputed. Iterative design prompts shaped which floor plan you saw and which you didn't. Precisely the kind of document-filtering methodology the court treated as discoverable. A structural engineer's prompt history reveals whether AI was asked to verify a conclusion or generate one. A distinction going directly to opinion reliability. An inspector's AI-formatted report raises questions about what got added, omitted, or reframed from raw field notes.

Nobody in this industry is ready for any of this. RICS surveyed 2,200 construction professionals in 2025 and found that 45% have implemented no AI at all, 34% are in early pilot phases, and less than 12% report regular use. Adoption is growing, with 61% of firms now using or planning to increase AI investments according to AGC's annual survey, up from 44% in 2024, but it is growing without guardrails. No published AIA or ASCE practice standard addresses AI prompt documentation requirements. No standard professional services agreement includes an AI disclosure clause. No errors-and-omissions insurance policy that I have found requires prompt preservation as a condition of coverage.

Consider the insurance dimension: E&O carriers are already adding AI exclusions to professional liability policies, a trend this publication has covered extensively. Judge Farrish's ruling creates a new exposure that sits in a gap between what insurers are excluding and what courts are requiring. An architect whose insurer excludes AI-related claims, and who failed to preserve the prompts that a court might demand, has managed to be uninsured on one side and undocumented on the other.

The Residential Amplifier

Large commercial firms have compliance teams, risk officers, and IT departments that can implement prompt-logging protocols. A 200-person architecture firm with a general counsel can respond to a discovery demand with a binder of preserved AI interaction logs.

A sole-proprietor architect designing custom homes in Bend, Oregon, cannot do any of that, and neither can a two-person structural engineering consultancy reviewing residential additions in suburban Houston, or a home inspector running a one-truck operation with an AI report-writing subscription. These are the professionals who design, verify, and certify the vast majority of residential construction in the United States, and they are the ones least equipped to comply with a discovery regime that treats their AI prompts as methodology.

Compliance costs nearly nothing: export a chat log, screenshot a session, save prompt history to a project file. Every AI tool already supports preservation; what is missing is awareness that it matters. And under the Connecticut ruling's logic, the absence of a record can be as damaging as an unfavorable one. Reuters, analyzing the ruling, noted that "an expert who treated an AI session as a disposable scratchpad may find that the lack of a record is as damaging as an unfavorable record would have been." In litigation, a gap in documentation invites a spoliation argument: whatever was deleted must have been harmful.

The Strongest Case Against Panic

Judge Farrish's ruling is narrow. It addressed prompts used to filter documents, a research-methodology function, not prompts used to generate designs or perform engineering calculations. A structural engineer's use of AI to verify a beam sizing is arguably closer to using a calculator than to culling a document production, and courts do not require disclosure of every keystroke on a TI-84. Design-professional AI use is categorically different from expert-witness AI use, and extending this ruling to cover every interaction in every construction project would be an aggressive judicial expansion that no court has endorsed.

Worth noting: the order is stayed, and a district court may reverse the magistrate judge. Even if upheld, it would carry no binding authority outside the District of Connecticut. Other circuits may draw the line differently, perhaps treating prompts used for document filtering as discoverable while protecting prompts used for substantive professional analysis under work-product doctrine.

All of that is true, and none of it is comforting. As the court itself observed, the reasoning, that methodology is discoverable and AI prompts are methodology, is "intuitive enough that other courts are likely to find it persuasive." And the Multi Housing News, reporting on AI risk in construction just six days ago, warned that "in the event of litigation, companies will be required as part of the discovery process to show the AI inputs and responses, including what documents or data the AI tool was trained on or used as inputs." The direction of travel is clear even if the final destination isn't.

What This Means for Your Project

If you are building a custom home and your architect or engineer uses AI, and odds are increasingly good that they do, you should ask three questions before signing a contract:

First: does the professional disclose which AI tools they use, and for what tasks? Not "we use AI sometimes." Specify which tools, which tasks, which stages of the design process.

Second: does this professional preserve AI interaction logs for the duration of the project plus a reasonable post-completion period? Construction defect statutes of limitations in most states run six to ten years. A typical AI chat session vanishes when a browser tab closes.

Third: does the professional's errors-and-omissions insurance cover AI-assisted work, or has the carrier carved it out? If the insurer has excluded AI-related claims, and the professional used AI to generate or verify a design element that later fails, the insurance that you assumed stood behind the professional's work may not exist.

Uncomfortable questions, but also free to ask, and the answers will tell you more about a professional's risk management than any portfolio or testimonial page ever could.

Limitations of This Analysis

Judge Farrish's order is a single magistrate-judge ruling in a climate litigation case, not a construction defect case. No court has ordered AI prompt disclosure in a residential construction dispute. Extrapolating from expert-witness methodology to design-professional methodology is reasonable but untested. AI adoption survey data varies widely, from 27% (Bluebeam) to 75% (APM/Censuswide), depending on how "AI use" is defined and who is surveyed. No data isolates AI adoption rates specifically among residential-focused architects or engineers. This article assumes the ruling's logic will extend to construction professionals; that assumption could prove wrong.
