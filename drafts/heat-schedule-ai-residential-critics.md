## 6. 🔬 Research Rigor — Score: 8.0 ❌ FAIL

### Blocking issue: Math error in the 31 labor-hours calculation

The article's centerpiece original calculation is wrong:

> "on days above 90°F, high-risk outdoor laborers worked 2.6 fewer minutes for every degree above that mark, which sounds small until you run it across a 12-person crew over a two-week stretch of 100°F days in Phoenix, and you are looking at roughly 31 lost labor-hours, nearly four full workdays"

**Correct calculation:**
- 2.6 min/degree × 10 degrees (100°F − 90°F) = **26 min lost per worker per day**
- 26 min × 12 workers = **312 min = 5.2 labor-hours lost per crew per day**
- 5.2 hours × 10 working days (two weeks) = **52 labor-hours = 6.5 full workdays**

**Article claims:** 31 labor-hours ≈ 4 workdays
**Correct figure:** 52 labor-hours ≈ 6.5 workdays

**Root cause:** 2.6 × 12 = 31.2 — the author appears to have multiplied minutes-per-degree × crew-size and called the result "labor-hours," omitting the multiplication by degrees-above-90 (10) and the division by minutes-per-hour (60), or omitting the days multiplier entirely. The number 31 only matches if you assume ~6 working days, not "a two-week stretch."

**Fix options:**
1. **Correct the math:** Change "roughly 31 lost labor-hours, nearly four full workdays" to "roughly 52 lost labor-hours, more than six full workdays" (keeps two-week framing)
2. **Adjust the scenario:** Change "two-week stretch" to "one-week stretch" and recalculate: 5.2 × 5 = 26 labor-hours ≈ 3.25 workdays. "roughly 26 lost labor-hours, more than three full workdays"
3. **Show the math inline** (preferred for methodology transparency): Add a parenthetical: "(2.6 minutes × 10 degrees × 12 workers × 10 days ÷ 60)"

**Recommendation:** Option 2 is editorially cleaner — a one-week stretch of 100°F days is a more common real-world scenario than two consecutive weeks, and the number is still striking.
