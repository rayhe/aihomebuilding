A Surveyor Charges $600 to Measure How High Your Front Door Sits. An Algorithm Did It for 12,241 Homes From Photos Google Already Had.

By Catherine Chen · July 24, 2026
← Back to all articles

When a lender wants to know whether your home will flood, they check a map. Not a satellite image. Not a terrain model. Not even a recent survey. They check a map that FEMA drew, in many cases, before your neighborhood existed.

Eighty-four percent of those maps are outdated. Six percent date to the 1970s and 1980s. Forty percent of the continental United States has never been mapped at all. These are not obscure academic findings. They come from Neptune Flood's research division, published December 2025, cross-referenced against First Street Foundation data that identified nearly 10 million high-risk properties sitting outside any FEMA flood zone.

30% of National Flood Insurance Program claims over the past decade came from properties outside FEMA-designated high-risk zones. Source: Neptune Flood Research Group, December 2025.

That number deserves a moment. Three in ten homeowners who filed federal flood claims lived in places the government told them were safe.

What the Surveyor Actually Measures

Flood damage is not a neighborhood-level event. Two houses on the same block can have radically different outcomes. One sits fourteen inches above the base flood elevation. Water reaches the foundation, pools against the garage door, and recedes. The other sits six inches below. Water enters through the front door, saturates the drywall to three feet, destroys the HVAC system, and triggers a $90,000 remediation.

The variable that separates those two outcomes is the lowest floor elevation relative to grade. How high does your house sit? It sounds simple. Measuring it is not.

A licensed surveyor visits the property with a rod and level, measures the elevation of the lowest floor, compares it to the FEMA base flood elevation for the zone, and produces a document called an Elevation Certificate. That document costs between $170 and $2,000, depending on terrain and location, with the national average running about $600 according to Angi and HomeAdvisor data. It takes roughly five business days. It requires the surveyor to physically stand on your lot.

Until October 2021, FEMA required an Elevation Certificate to price every National Flood Insurance Program policy. Risk Rating 2.0 dropped that requirement, substituting third-party data sources and catastrophe models. An EC can still lower your premium, but few homebuyers obtain one voluntarily. It is friction and cost at exactly the moment when closing deadlines compress every decision.

What the Algorithm Measured Instead

In April 2026, four researchers at Texas A&M published a pipeline that does something no commercial product currently offers. Xiangpeng Li, Yu-Hsuan Ho, Sam D. Brody, and Ali Mostafavi built a three-stage system that extracts the lowest floor elevation of a residential structure from Google Street View imagery, then uses that measurement to estimate property-specific interior flood depth and dollar loss.

They called the extraction framework Elev-Vision. It processes the Street View photograph of a home, identifies the lowest visible floor relative to grade, and outputs a height measurement. No surveyor. No site visit. No $600.

12,241 residential structures across 18 areas of interest in Texas, assessed for property-level flood risk using Google Street View imagery. Source: Li et al., arXiv:2604.01153, April 2026.

Across 12,241 residential structures in 18 areas of Texas, Street View imagery existed for 73.4 percent of parcels. Direct elevation extraction succeeded for 49 percent of them, producing usable measurements for 5,992 homes. For the remaining properties, the team trained Random Forest and Gradient Boosting models on 16 terrain, hydrologic, and geographic features to impute the missing values. They kept predictions only where cross-validated performance was defensible and explicitly excluded five areas where the models fell short.

That kind of honesty is worth noting. They threw out their own data when it did not meet their standard.

In the final stage, the extracted elevations were combined with Fathom 100-year inundation surfaces and U.S. Army Corps of Engineers depth-damage functions. The output was not a color-coded risk zone. It was a dollar figure: how deep the water enters this specific house, and how much damage it causes.

The Information Nobody Packages

First Street Foundation already provides property-level flood risk scores through its Flood Factor tool, freely available online. Realtor.com integrates those scores into some listings. FEMA's Risk Rating 2.0 uses third-party elevation data from multiple sources.

None of them do what the Texas A&M pipeline does.

First Street tells you the probability your property floods. It does not tell you the interior depth or the repair cost. Risk Rating 2.0 prices your insurance premium but does not give you the underlying elevation analysis in a form you can act on. Neither reads a photograph of your house and extracts the physical measurement that determines whether water stops at the foundation or enters the living room.

That measurement, the height difference between street grade and the lowest floor, is the single variable that separates a $3,000 claim from a $90,000 one. A surveyor charges $600 to produce it. An algorithm produced it for nearly six thousand homes from images that were already sitting on Google's servers.

The research paper is public. The framework is described in enough detail to replicate. The code and data exist. And yet there is no product. No app that lets you paste a Zillow listing URL and receive an AI-generated elevation analysis before you make an offer. No title company that includes computer-vision-derived flood depth in the closing disclosure. No insurer that runs Elev-Vision against every new policy application and tells the homeowner what it found.

Why the Gap Persists

Commercial incentives explain part of it. Insurers benefit from information asymmetry. A policyholder who does not know their property sits below the base flood elevation pays a premium calibrated to the insurer's proprietary model, not to the policyholder's informed choice. Giving homebuyers a transparent, property-specific analysis would sharpen their negotiating position on both purchase price and insurance rates. That is not obviously in the insurer's interest.

Regulatory gaps explain the rest. Flood disclosure requirements vary wildly by state. Twenty-nine states require some form of flood disclosure during a real estate transaction. Many of them only require the seller to disclose what they know, not to conduct an independent assessment. A seller who has never ordered a flood analysis has nothing to disclose.

FEMA itself has said its maps are "snapshots in time" used for insurance determinations and development management. They were never intended to be comprehensive risk assessments. But comprehensive risk assessments now exist. They run on publicly available data. They cost essentially nothing per property at scale.

The regulatory framework has not absorbed that fact.

What a Homebuyer Should Do Right Now

Check your Flood Factor score at floodfactor.com. It is free, property-specific, and incorporates climate projections that FEMA maps ignore. If the score shows moderate or major risk for a property you are considering, pay for an Elevation Certificate before you close. Six hundred dollars is less than one percent of a typical closing cost package, and the measurement directly affects both your insurance pricing and your long-term financial exposure.

Ask your insurer whether they accept third-party elevation data. Under Risk Rating 2.0, submitting an EC that shows your lowest floor sits above the base flood elevation can reduce your NFIP premium by hundreds of dollars annually. Some private flood insurers, including Neptune and Aon Edge, can issue policies without one, but the premium may be higher without it.

Do not rely on a FEMA zone designation as your sole source of flood risk information. In Harris County, Texas, 47 percent of flood claims over three decades came from outside the 100-year flood zone. Some inland and fast-growing regions have five to eight times more high-risk properties than FEMA identifies. A Zone X designation does not mean you will not flood. It means FEMA has not determined that you will.

The Policy Lag

Real estate disclosure reform moves slowly. Building code adoption moves slower. FEMA's Risk Rating 2.0 was the most significant structural change to flood insurance pricing in decades, and it arrived in October 2021 after years of development. Congress has reauthorized the NFIP on short-term extensions twenty-nine times since 2017 without passing long-term reform.

Meanwhile, the technology moved. A computer science lab in College Station, Texas, demonstrated that you can assess property-level flood risk for thousands of homes in a single pipeline run using photographs that Google captured for an entirely different purpose. The per-unit cost is computational, not logistical. It scales to millions of properties without deploying a single surveyor.

Somewhere between the research paper and the closing table, that capability disappeared. Not because it failed. Not because it was impractical. It disappeared because nobody built the product, and no regulation requires anyone to try.

That gap costs money. Not to the insurers, who price risk on proprietary models with access to data the homeowner never sees. Not to the researchers, who have published their methodology and moved on to the next paper. It costs money to the buyer who closes on a home in a Zone X area, skips the $600 elevation survey because nobody suggested it, and discovers three years later that six inches of floodwater enters their living room during a storm the maps said would not reach them.

The data was there. Google took the photo. The algorithm worked. Nobody connected the pipeline to the transaction.
