# Research: OSHA Heat Rule — Five Years, No Standard

## Angle
The federal government has spent five years trying to write a heat safety standard for construction workers. It still doesn't exist. Five states have their own rules; 45 don't. Meanwhile, AI wearable companies are building the heat monitoring infrastructure that OSHA can't legislate — privatizing a baseline safety function at $25-40/worker/month. The result: a two-tier system where workers on big commercial projects get sensor-based heat alerts while residential crews get "drink water" and a prayer.

## Primary Sources

### 1. OSHA Rulemaking Timeline (OSHA.gov)
- October 2021: OSHA publishes Advance Notice of Proposed Rulemaking (ANPRM) for Heat Injury and Illness Prevention
- August 30, 2024: Biden admin publishes proposed rule (89 Fed. Reg. 70698)
- June 16–July 2, 2025: Informal public hearing held
- October 30, 2025: Post-hearing comment period closes
- As of July 2026: Trump administration has NOT finalized, killed, or withdrawn the rule — regulatory limbo
- OSHA also removed heat inspection targets from its enforcement directive (Civil Eats, April 2026)

### 2. Death/Injury Data
- BLS Census of Fatal Occupational Injuries: 999 workers killed by environmental heat 1992-2021, ~33 fatalities/year average (OSHA SBREFA document)
- BLS: 33,890 heat injuries/illnesses involving days away from work, 2011-2020 (~3,389/year)
- Harvard T.H. Chan / Environmental Health (2025): Nationwide analysis estimates ~28,000 workplace injuries attributable to heat annually (heat index ≥70°F)
- Construction workers account for >1/3 of all occupational heat deaths (CDC NIOSH)
- 285 construction workers died from heat 1992-2016, 75% during June-July-August (CDC)
- ILO: ~19,000 heat-related occupational deaths globally per year, 22.85M occupational injuries
- National Council for Occupational Safety & Health 2026 Dirty Dozen: D.R. Horton named; ~28,000 heat-related workplace injuries/year

### 3. State-Level Heat Standards
- **California §3395** (effective 2006, amended 2015): "Maria Isabel Vasquez Jimenez heat illness standard." 80°F: shade required. 95°F: high-heat procedures (buddy system, observation, 10-min cool-down breaks). Applies to construction, agriculture, landscaping, oil & gas. FREE compliance: water, shade, breaks, training.
- **Colorado** (2024): Heat standards for outdoor workers
- **Oregon** (2022): Heat illness prevention rules adopted after 2021 heat dome killed 100+ Oregonians
- **Washington** (2008, expanded 2023): Outdoor heat exposure rules
- **Minnesota** (2024): Indoor and outdoor heat standard
- **45 states**: NO specific heat standard. General duty clause only.

### 4. Harvard Study — States With vs Without Heat Rules (Alahmad et al., Environmental Health, 2025)
- On 105°F days, injury odds increase 16% (OR 1.16, 95% CI 1.11-1.21) in states WITHOUT heat rules
- On same days, only 8% increase (OR 1.08, 95% CI 0.88-1.32) in states WITH heat rules
- At 110°F: 22% (no rules) vs 9% (rules)
- Population attributable fraction: 1.18% of ALL injuries attributable to heat ≥70°F
- Applied to BLS national total (2,368,900 injuries in 2023): ~27,953 heat-attributable injuries

### 5. Industry Opposition
- Associated General Contractors of America (AGC): testified that 90°F is "a vacation" compared to south Texas summer (Stephen Kinn, June 2025 hearing)
- American Petroleum Institute (API): lobbied to kill rule, called rest break requirements "unreasonable" and said they would "stifle creativity, innovation"
- SBA Office of Advocacy: pushed for "performance-oriented approach" — translation: let employers decide
- California §3395 compliance cost: effectively $0 technology — water jugs, shade structures, training, breaks
- OSHA proposed: 80°F initial trigger, 90°F high-heat trigger, 15 min rest per 2 hours above 90°F

### 6. AI Wearable Heat Monitoring (The Privatized Alternative)
- **Kenzen**: arm-worn sensor, monitors heart rate, activity, skin/ambient temp, predicts core body temperature. Subscription per-worker per-month. DHS S&T awarded $161,600 for development. Deployed with Garney Construction (pipeline).
- **WakeCap**: $28M Series A (May 2025), solar-powered helmet sensor, <$1/worker/day. Real-time location + fall detection + heat stress alerts.
- **Soter Analytics**: $12M raised, spinal motion tracking + ergonomic coaching
- **Triax SitePulse**: real-time location + fall detection + heat stress alerts
- Smart PPE market: $5.39B in 2026, 16.2% CAGR (The Business Research Company)
- Residential adoption: virtually zero. All deployments are commercial/infrastructure.

### 7. Reuters (July 2026): "Companies 'flying blind' on heat stress risk"
- Heat stress causes ~19,000 deaths, 22.85M occupational injuries annually (ILO)
- Productivity falls 2-3% per degree above 20°C (WHO/WMO)
- ILO: lost work hours from heat equivalent to 80 million full-time jobs by 2030
- UAE, Saudi Arabia, Oman: outdoor work banned during hottest 3 hours of summer days
- Most jurisdictions: "employers lack clear benchmarks for action"

## Original Contribution
1. **Cost-of-inaction calculation**: California's §3395 costs ~$0 in technology per worker. Five years of OSHA rulemaking = zero federal protection. Meanwhile, privatized heat safety (Kenzen/WakeCap/Triax) costs $300-$480/worker/year. A residential crew of 10 workers: $3,000-$4,800/year for technology that replaces a $0 regulation.
2. **Two-tier safety system**: Large commercial builders (Suffolk, DPR, Garney) deploy sensor-based heat monitoring. Small residential builders (<20 employees, 85% of NAHB membership) have no technology AND no federal standard. The gap is structural.
3. **The Harvard evidence gap**: States with heat rules show HALF the injury increase on extreme heat days. The data exists. The rule doesn't.
4. **Named after a dead worker**: California's standard is named after María Isabel Vásquez Jiménez, a 17-year-old farmworker who died of heat stroke in 2008. Federal OSHA started rulemaking in 2021. Five years later, the rule that could have been named after any of the ~165 workers who died of heat in that span doesn't exist.

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? YES. If you're a residential builder in any of the 45 states without a heat standard, you have no clear legal obligation beyond the vague general duty clause. Your workers are unprotected. If a worker dies of heat on your job site, you face an OSHA citation under the general duty clause (average $21,000 fine) — but no specific, enforceable standard told you what to do. The article tells builders what California's $0-technology standard actually requires, and why the privatized wearable alternative creates a liability gap.
