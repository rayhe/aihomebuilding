Frank DeLuca July 22, 2026

Your Builder Cut His Price $26,000 to Close the Sale. The Home Already Saved You $32,000. Nobody Mentioned It.

America's median new home now sells for $440,600. In July, 37 percent of builders dropped that number, slashing an average of six percent, roughly $26,400, to keep buyers walking through the door. Two-thirds piled on additional incentives: rate buydowns, closing cost credits, appliance upgrades they would never have offered two years ago. Builder confidence hit 34 on the NAHB/Wells Fargo Housing Market Index, extending the longest sustained streak below 40 since 2012, when the last housing crisis was still unwinding and subdivisions across Nevada sat half-built and overgrown.

Price cuts are the only lever they know how to pull.

But here is what nobody puts on the brochure. Nobody. A newly built home costs 38 percent less to insure than the average existing home in America, according to a 2026 Insurify analysis conducted for Realtor.com and drawn from millions of actual insurance policy records. That gap is worth $1,120 a year, and over a decade, when you fold in reduced maintenance and fewer catastrophic system failures, cumulative savings run to $32,000, which is six thousand dollars more than the price cut that builder just gave away to close the deal.

Not a single builder in the NAHB's July survey cited insurance savings as a primary sales tool. None.

How Fast a New Home's Insurance Advantage Disappears

Insurify's data plots an age curve that is remarkably clean. A brand-new home carries an average annual premium of $1,828. At ten years old, that number climbs to $2,476, a 35 percent jump driven mostly by roof wear and system aging that begins triggering claims adjusters' attention. At twenty years that number reaches $2,830, and by thirty it sits at $2,889, a gentler slope than the first decade but still climbing. By forty, which happens to be the median age of America's housing stock, homeowners pay $2,948 per year, a figure that will almost certainly cross $3,000 by the time the next age study is published.

Julia Taliesin, an economic analyst and licensed insurance agent at Insurify, laid out the actuarial logic: "Newly built homes are generally less expensive to insure because they have newer roofs, plumbing, and electrical systems and are built to newer safety standards, making them less likely to generate claims."

That single sentence contains a financial argument worth more than most rate buydowns, and it costs the builder nothing to deliver. Zero.

Run the mortgage math. Take a median-priced home with twenty percent down. Monthly principal and interest on a $352,480 loan at 6.55 percent comes to $2,237. Insurance savings of $1,120 per year translate to $93 per month, a figure that sounds modest until you realize what it represents in mortgage-rate terms. Ninety-three dollars a month, which is what it would cost to buy down your mortgage rate from 6.55 to approximately 6.29 percent, except this buydown requires no lump-sum payment, no lender negotiation, and no hit to the builder's margin, because it flows directly from the physics of a newer building envelope that has never weathered a hailstorm and plumbing that has never had the chance to corrode.

A builder who cuts price by six percent hands the buyer $26,400 once, takes the hit on this quarter's earnings, and trains every future prospect to wait for a similar discount before making an offer. Insurance and maintenance savings, by contrast, hand the buyer $32,000 over a decade without costing the builder a cent. One number appears on the listing sheet and the other is entirely invisible. Buyers shop on sticker price because nobody showed them anything else, and builders hemorrhage margin competing on the only metric anyone can see.

Premiums Keep Climbing, and New Construction Keeps Pulling Away

A separate Newrez study of 1.2 million mortgage loans found that average annual homeowners insurance premiums rose 64 percent between year-end 2021 and year-end 2025, climbing from $1,597 to $2,625. In 2025 the rate of increase slowed to ten percent, the most moderate year since 2021, but cumulative damage was already baked into household budgets. Tariffs on imported building materials are pushing reconstruction costs higher still, and in a market where materials tariffs have raised the price of Canadian lumber, Mexican gypsum, and Chinese steel, every dollar of reconstruction cost increase flows directly into the premium the homeowner pays. Insurify projects an additional $106 per year in premium pressure from materials tariffs alone, with states like Nebraska facing 13 percent increases and California staring down a 16 percent jump that would add $388 to the average annual policy cost.

Every increase widens the gap between new and old, starting with roofs: newer roofs produce fewer hail claims. Newer plumbing produces fewer water damage payouts, which remain the single largest category of homeowners insurance claims filed in America. Newer wiring produces fewer electrical fires, fewer smoke damage claims, and fewer total losses that force carriers to write six-figure checks. Each year that passes makes new construction's insurance advantage a little more valuable, precisely because existing homes are aging into more expensive risk profiles at the same time.

Climate risk accelerates the effect even further, and the numbers are staggering. Nationally, 18.3 percent of homes face severe or extreme risk of hurricane-grade wind damage, representing nearly eight trillion dollars in property value, and severe convective storms have caused more than $51 billion in insured losses in each of the past three years. That damage footprint has migrated inland far beyond the coastal zones that once defined what insurance companies meant by "high-risk." In that environment, older homes with twenty-year-old roof assemblies and aging fastener patterns absorb the worst of the losses while newer homes built to current wind resistance codes and impact-rated glazing standards generate claims at a fraction of the rate.

In Florida, where average annual premiums hit $8,292 in 2025 with projections reaching $8,458 by year-end 2026, the new-construction discount could exceed $3,000 per year, or $250 a month in real savings that shows up on every utility bill cycle. Florida State University research found that a ten percent rise in insurance premiums triggers a 4.6 percent decline in housing prices in the affected area. For builders selling in those markets, an insurance-forward sales strategy is not supplemental; it is the difference between holding price and joining the race to the bottom.

A $5,000 Tool That Replaces a $26,000 Price Cut

Every piece of technology needed to surface these numbers in real time already exists. None of it has been pointed at a builder's sales office, which is precisely the kind of absurdity that makes you wonder whether the people building the software have ever set foot inside a model home.

Cape Analytics uses geospatial imagery and machine learning to assess roof condition, vegetation proximity, and structural attributes for insurance underwriting, processing millions of properties annually without a single physical inspection. Zesty AI builds climate risk models at the individual parcel level, scoring wildfire, flood, wind, and hail exposure with enough granularity to differentiate between two homes on the same block. Verisk's 360Value platform generates reconstruction cost estimates that drive dwelling coverage limits for carriers across the country. Kin Insurance and Hippo have built AI-first underwriting engines that price policies based on property-specific risk factors rather than the blunt-instrument zip-code averages that dominated underwriting for decades.

Not one of these platforms integrates with a builder's CRM or sales workflow today, which means the most sophisticated AI insurance underwriting capability on the planet sits on one side of a wall while the people who could use it to sell homes sit on the other. A builder's system can track which lot a prospect visited, which floor plan they preferred, which countertop finish they selected at the design center. It cannot tell that prospect what their homeowners insurance will cost compared to the twenty-year-old Colonial they toured last Saturday. Generating that number would require data that already sits in public records, including tax assessments, satellite imagery, building code vintage, and local claims history, all of which insurance companies already aggregate at scale and most of which is available through APIs that any competent software developer could integrate in a matter of weeks. Displaying it on a tablet at the model home before the buyer finishes their coffee would require integration work that is, by modern software engineering standards, routine.

Annual cost for an AI-powered insurance comparison tool, even at enterprise SaaS pricing, would run $5,000 to $15,000. One single foregone price cut pays for three to five years of the service. Let that sink in.

Realtor.com senior economist Joel Berner stated the implication without hedging: "The real story here is that choosing new construction expands a buyer's budget. What buyers thought they could afford in an existing home when they accounted for operating costs is less than what they can afford in a new home because of the savings we highlight here."

That argument deserves the first page of every builder's sales presentation. Today it occupies none. Literally none.

What This Means If You Are Buying or Building

If you are buying a new-construction home, pull an insurance quote before you negotiate, because that number belongs in your affordability calculation alongside mortgage payment, property tax, and HOA. If your builder cannot generate one, do it yourself through Insurify, Jerry, or Policygenius, all of which return quotes in under five minutes. Compare the result to quotes on the resale properties you were considering, because in most markets the difference will be large enough to shift your affordability calculation by several hundred dollars a month, and larger still in states where insurance premiums have doubled in the past four years. Expect the gap to be wider than any discount the builder offered, and bring it to the negotiation table armed with the specific dollar figure rather than a vague sense that new homes cost less to own.

If you are a builder sitting on unsold inventory in July 2026, stop leading with price cuts and start leading with total cost of ownership. Insurance savings are not a marketing claim; they are actuarial fact underwritten by millions of policy records from an industry that stakes its solvency on getting these numbers right. A $93-per-month advantage in operating costs is a stronger closing argument than a $26,000 price reduction that craters your margin and teaches every buyer in your market to wait for a better deal.

If you build in Florida, Texas, Oklahoma, Nebraska, or Colorado, where insurance costs have become a genuine barrier to homeownership, the new-construction insurance advantage is the difference between a buyer who qualifies and one who walks away. Lenders increasingly incorporate insurance costs into debt-to-income calculations, and a $250-per-month insurance savings could push a marginal buyer across the qualification threshold without a single dollar of price reduction and without a single concession that hits the builder's bottom line.

Where This Argument Breaks

Insurance savings are national averages, and averages conceal as much as they reveal. A buyer in Vermont, where premiums average $1,374 per year, sees a smaller absolute gap than a buyer in Nebraska, where they average $4,028. Some carriers offer meaningful discounts for older homes with documented roof replacements or recent electrical upgrades, and those discounts can narrow new construction's advantage in specific cases. Realtor.com's $32,000 ten-year savings figure assumes premium differentials remain stable over the coming decade, which is a significant assumption in a market that has seen 64 percent cumulative increases since 2021 and shows no structural reason for those increases to reverse.

But notice what every one of those limitations actually argues for: more granular, property-specific data, not less. A builder who generates individualized insurance estimates for each lot, each floor plan, and each specification level eliminates the averaging problem entirely. A buyer who sees what their actual savings would be, calculated from their actual carrier options in their actual market, does not need to trust a national average at all.

Here is what we could not verify: whether any production builder in America currently provides real-time insurance cost comparisons as part of the new-home sales process. We found no evidence of it in NAHB marketing guidance, in builder conference programming, or in any of the major builder CRM platforms' feature sets. AI insurance underwriting exists. Builder sales technology exists. Nobody has connected them. Until someone does, builders will keep cutting prices by $26,000 to sell homes that already save the buyer $32,000, because they never figured out how to make the invisible number visible.
