You Skipped the Home Inspection to Win the Bid. An AI Would Have Found the $63,000 Problem.

By Catherine Chen · May 12, 2026

A couple in Portland waives their inspection contingency because three other offers are coming in clean, and the listing agent made it quietly clear that contingencies kill deals. They close in eighteen days. Nine months later, the sewer lateral collapses and the remediation bill is $47,000, which is more than their entire emergency fund. A camera scope two years earlier had shown root intrusion at 60 percent of the pipe diameter. Nobody asked, because asking would have cost them the house.

This is not an edge case. This is the market.

The Numbers Behind the Waiver Epidemic

In 2022, at peak market heat, 52 percent of buyers in bidding wars waived their inspection contingency according to Gitnux’s aggregation of industry data. Among first-time buyers competing in hot markets, the rate climbed to 62 percent. That single statistic should alarm anyone who works in housing policy, because first-time buyers are precisely the population least able to absorb a five-figure surprise.

Financially, it is brutal. Eighty-eight percent of buyers who did get inspections found issues, thirty-four percent found problems significant enough to renegotiate the deal, twenty-three percent negotiated the price down by $5,000 or more, and fifty-five percent used inspection findings to walk away entirely, which means that more than half the time, the inspection revealed something bad enough that the buyer decided losing the house was better than owning it. Those protections evaporate the moment you check the waiver box.

Forty-five percent of buyers who waived cited cost as the reason. A standard home inspection runs $300 to $500 for a property under 2,000 square feet. Larger homes cost more. Those buyers calculated that $450 was not worth the competitive disadvantage, but what they actually calculated, whether they realized it or not, was that the risk of a $63,000 problem was worth less than the certainty of losing the bid, a calculation that is rational in the moment and catastrophic in the aggregate across 2.3 million annual transactions where nobody looked before they signed.

Massachusetts Said No

On October 15, 2025, Massachusetts became the first state to legislate against the waiver pressure directly. Under 760 CMR 74.00, enacted as part of the Affordable Homes Act, sellers cannot condition the acceptance of an offer on the buyer waiving their inspection right. It requires written agreements stating that buyers retain the right to inspect, and agents face heightened liability if they steer buyers toward waivers.

New York is considering similar legislation. State governments are increasingly scrutinizing the practice, though industry groups argue that restricting waiver flexibility limits the tools buyers have to make competitive offers. Fair point. Massachusetts exempts intra-family transactions and new construction with builder warranties, a reasonable carve-out because new construction has a builder on the hook and grandma selling to her nephew has other protections. Everyone else needs someone with a flashlight in their crawlspace.

Seven months in, the Massachusetts law has produced no enforcement actions or case law. Its practical impact on agent behavior remains theoretical. Legislation that prohibits a practice but provides no enforcement mechanism is a policy preference. Not a policy.

AI Inspection Tools Are Getting Faster Than the Problem

While states debate legislation, a cluster of AI startups is approaching the problem from the other direction: make the inspection fast enough and cheap enough that the competitive disadvantage disappears.

OfferWise has processed more than 15,000 home inspection reports through AI analysis, generating risk-adjusted scores that help buyers prioritize which defects are urgent, which are cosmetic, and which justify walking away. Their data reveals a transparency score of 71.5 percent across reports, meaning nearly 30 percent of the information a buyer needs is either missing or unclear in the document they paid for. Where the report buries information, the AI surfaces it.

RealReports partnered with Clear Estimates to build an AI cost estimator backed by a $25 billion database of contractor bids. It generates hyper-local repair cost breakdowns in seconds at one-tenth the cost of a traditional estimator. If the inspection report says "evidence of moisture intrusion at the foundation wall," the AI returns the actual remediation cost in your zip code, broken down by materials, labor, and permits, before your agent finishes reading the sentence.

Binsr Inspect, a Phoenix startup with $1.1 million in seed funding, cuts inspector workflow time by a factor of three to five through AI photo translation and automated comment generation. Your inspector still walks the property, but the report that used to take four hours to write now takes 45 minutes. That compression matters in competitive markets where every day of delay weakens your position.

HomeScore offers AI property risk assessments for $29 per month. At that price, a buyer could run preliminary analysis on every property they tour before deciding which ones justify spending $450 on a human inspector. Triage, not replacement.

A Cost Nobody Has Calculated

Here is a calculation that, as far as we can find, nobody has published.

U.S. inspectors conducted 4.5 million inspections in 2022. If 52 percent of bidding-war buyers waived inspections, and bidding wars accounted for roughly half of transactions in that peak market, approximately 2.3 million home purchases proceeded without professional inspection. Apply OfferWise’s finding that 34 percent of inspected homes had major issues. If the same rate holds for uninspected homes, roughly 780,000 buyers closed on properties with significant undiscovered problems, representing an estimated $50 billion in hidden defect liability in a single year.

An AI pre-screen at $29 to $100 per report could have covered those 2.3 million skipped transactions for $67 million to $230 million. That is 0.1 to 0.5 percent of the risk exposure. Even catching half the issues a human inspector would find, the expected value of the screen dwarfs its cost by two orders of magnitude.

The Workforce Problem AI Cannot Solve

Every AI inspection tool on the market has the same dependency. OfferWise analyzes reports that human inspectors wrote. RealReports costs come from bids that human contractors submitted. Binsr compresses workflows for inspectors who still physically walk the property. Not one of these tools replaces the person with the thermal camera, the moisture meter, and the willingness to army-crawl under a 24-inch clearance foundation to check for termite damage. No algorithm does that.

That person is 52 years old, on average. The U.S. employs 52,000 home inspectors, an aging workforce losing members faster than it replaces them: only 3,200 new licenses were issued in 2023 against an 8 percent annual turnover that removes roughly 4,160 inspectors per year, producing a net annual deficit of about 960 professionals in a field where the median salary of $65,000 is not enough to attract younger workers willing to spend August afternoons in un-air-conditioned attics photographing mold colonies. That pipeline is not keeping up. Oregon alone has a 1,500-inspector workforce gap with only 50 graduates per year from its training programs. Housing production is stalling statewide because there are not enough people licensed to tell you whether the house is safe to live in.

If AI tools make inspection reports so fast and cheap that inspection fees drop, fewer people will enter a profession that already struggles to recruit. The AI could accelerate the workforce decline it depends on. That feedback loop has no obvious circuit breaker.

The Strongest Case Against This Article’s Thesis

AI inspection advocates will argue, with justification, that the comparison is wrong. It is not AI versus human inspectors. It is AI-assisted inspection versus nothing at all, because 52 percent of competitive buyers are currently choosing nothing, and any tool that shifts even a fraction of those buyers from zero information to partial information reduces aggregate damage by billions of dollars per year in undiscovered defect liability, a number so large that even a mediocre AI screen paying for itself requires catching only one major defect per hundred reports. Buyers who waive inspections are not weighing AI against a human crawling their foundation. They are weighing silence against the risk of losing the house. That is a different calculation entirely.

ServiceTitan’s 2026 Residential State of the Trades Report supports this: 74 percent of residential contractors view AI as critical for efficiency, and 48 percent of early adopters report measurable productivity gains. If that productivity extends to inspection workflows, throughput increases could partially offset workforce decline. Forty-one percent of buyers under 35 already prioritize tech-enhanced inspections. The market is pulling in the direction of AI augmentation regardless of what the policy debate concludes.

What You Should Do

If you are buying a home in a competitive market and your agent suggests waiving the inspection, run a $29 AI pre-screen on the property before you make that decision. HomeScore, OfferWise, and similar tools can flag high-risk properties in minutes. If the AI flags nothing alarming, your waiver is at least an informed gamble rather than a blind one. If it flags foundation concerns, roof age, or water intrusion risk, that is $29 worth of information that might save you $63,000.

If you do get a full inspection, feed the report through an AI cost estimator like the RealReports and Clear Estimates tool. Your inspector identifies the problems. AI prices the repairs in your local market. Together, they arm you with a negotiating position backed by data instead of guesswork, and the seller’s agent cannot dismiss contractor-level cost breakdowns as speculation when the numbers come from a $25 billion database of actual bids.

If you are in Massachusetts, understand that the law protects your right to inspect but does not protect you from market pressure to close fast. Use AI tools to compress the inspection timeline rather than waiving it. Binsr-equipped inspectors can deliver reports in one to two days instead of four to five. Speed is the asset you trade against, not thoroughness.

Limitations

OfferWise’s $63,688 average repair figure is drawn from reports that were actually commissioned, which introduces selection bias: buyers who pay for inspections may be targeting older or riskier properties, inflating the average relative to the broader market. The 52 percent waiver statistic comes from the 2022 peak market, and rates in 2026’s cooler conditions are likely lower, though no comprehensive updated survey exists. AI defect detection claims, including the 23 percent improvement cited by ZipDo, lack independent third-party verification. RealReports and Clear Estimates’ cost accuracy has not been audited against actual completed repair invoices in published research. The Massachusetts law is too new for outcome data on whether it has changed agent or buyer behavior. We did not interview inspectors, buyers, or agents for this article; all claims are sourced from published reports, industry surveys, and regulatory filings.
