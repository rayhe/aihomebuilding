# Research: Tile-Laying Robots and the Residential Toilet Problem

## Angle
Tile-laying robots (Partner Robotics P900, semi-automated systems) claim 5-6x speed over manual labor. They've covered ~100,000 sqm. The $1.96B robotic tile market grows at 17.4% CAGR. But residential tile work is mostly bathrooms, showers, backsplashes — small, awkward spaces full of cuts around toilets, vanities, and thresholds. The robots do the easy flat-floor part and leave humans the hard 60%.

## The Residential Math Problem

### Typical Residential Tile Work
- Kitchen floor: 100-200 sqft (relatively flat, open — robot-friendly)
- Master bathroom floor: 50-100 sqft (toilet cutout, vanity, curved shower pan)
- Guest bathroom: 30-60 sqft (even more cramped)
- Shower/tub surround: 60-100 sqft (vertical, curved, lots of cuts)
- Backsplash: 20-40 sqft (behind outlets, switches, under cabinets)
- Total per house: 260-500 sqft of tile work
- Robot-eligible flat floor: maybe 40-50% of total area

### Break-Even Calculation
- Semi-automated system cost: $80K-$220K
- Manual tile setter labor: ~$5/sqft average
- Residential tile subcontractor doing 150 jobs/year × 200 sqft avg = 30,000 sqft/year
- If robot handles 40% = 12,000 sqft of robot-eligible work
- Labor savings: 12,000 × $5 = $60,000/year
- Payback on $80K system: ~16 months (optimistic — doesn't include transport, setup, maintenance)
- Payback on $220K system: ~44 months
- Problem: Still need a human tile setter for the other 60% (cuts, walls, showers, thresholds)
- You haven't eliminated a worker — you've given them an expensive assistant for the easy parts

### The "Toilet Gap"
- Robots work on open, flat, rectangular surfaces
- Residential bathrooms have: toilets, vanities, tub/shower flanges, door thresholds, floor drains
- Every one of these requires custom cuts, scribing, hand-fitting
- The robot can't navigate around a toilet flange or scribe to a curved shower pan
- Backsplashes require vertical work around outlets, switches, windows
- The hard part of residential tile isn't laying tile — it's cutting tile
