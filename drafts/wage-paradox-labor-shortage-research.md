# Research Notes: Construction Wage Paradox — "Shortage" vs. Declining Real Wages

## Thread: #507
## Journalist: Marcus "Steel" Washington
## Beat: Workforce & Labor

## Core Thesis
The construction industry's "labor shortage" narrative conflicts with BLS wage data showing residential building worker real wages declining. In a genuine shortage, prices (wages) should rise. They're falling. The gap between ABC's projected labor need and actual market behavior suggests the "shortage" is partially a demand slowdown dressed up as a supply problem.

## Key Data Points

### ABC Labor Gap Projections (shrinking)
- 2023-2024: 500,000+ net new workers needed annually
- 2025: 439,000 needed
- 2026: 349,000 needed (down 20% from prior year)
- Source: Associated Builders and Contractors (ABC), Jan 2026

### BLS Wage Data (softening)
- Residential building worker nominal AHE: +2.1% YoY (March 2026)
- Residential building worker REAL wages: -1.2% YoY (March 2026)
- Nominal wage growth PEAK: 9.4% in mid-2024
- Real wage growth PEAK: 6.2% in parts of 2024
- Source: BLS via NAHB Eye on Housing, May 2026

### Hiring Rate (record low)
- February 2026: Construction hiring rate 3.3% (lowest on record since Dec 2000)
- February 2026 had "the least construction labor force churn since the BLS began this survey in December 2000" — Anirban Basu, ABC chief economist
- Source: BLS JOLTS via Construction Dive

### Employment (declining)
- Residential construction employment: DOWN 3,100 jobs in December 2025
- Residential building specifically: DOWN 4,200 jobs (partially offset by +1,100 specialty trades)
- Source: BLS

### Sectoral Wage Comparisons
- All construction AHE: $38.08/hr (4.5% YoY)
- Residential specifically is lower — BLS breaks out residential building companies
- Residential vs manufacturing: 8.4% premium ($36.54/hr)
- Residential vs transportation/warehousing: 22.4% premium ($32.34/hr)
- Residential vs mining/logging: 3.6% discount ($41.10/hr)
- Source: BLS via NAHB

### Contractor Sentiment
- 63% expect to expand headcount 2026 (DOWN from 69% in 2025)
- 15% expect to shrink headcount (UP from 10%)
- Source: AGC/Sage Construction Hiring & Business Outlook

### Composition of Demand
- "A majority of new worker demand in 2026 will be attributable to retirement rather than increased demand for construction services, despite the ongoing boom in artificial intelligence infrastructure buildout" — Anirban Basu, ABC
- Source: ABC, Jan 2026

## Original Analysis
Cross-referencing ABC gap projections with BLS wage data reveals opposing trajectories:
- If the shortage were genuine and worsening, wages would rise to clear the market
- Instead: gap is shrinking (500K → 349K) AND wages are falling in real terms (-1.2%)
- Both movements point the same direction: softening demand, not persistent shortage
- The gap shrinks because builders need fewer workers, not because more showed up
- This is Economics 101: in a shortage, prices rise. In a demand contraction, prices fall. Residential construction wages are falling.

## AI Angle
- AI/automation pitched as labor shortage solution (Buildots, OpenSpace, scheduling AI, prefab)
- But if the problem is demand softening, AI doesn't solve a shortage — it accelerates the demand decline's labor impact
- Retirement losses (experienced trades aging out) represent tacit knowledge AI cannot replace
- AI might help with productivity per remaining worker, but won't train a new plumber or replace an electrician's 30-year intuition
- Risk: AI becomes the justification layer for wage stagnation — "we invested in technology" substituting for "we raised pay"

## Counterarguments
1. Skills mismatch is real even if aggregate numbers are soft — licensed electricians and experienced plumbers are genuinely scarce
2. Regional variation is enormous — Bay Area, NYC union markets very different from right-to-work states
3. The BLS data covers residential building companies, not specialty trade contractors — trades may show different patterns
4. ABC's 349K is a projection model, not a count

## Limitations
- NAHB/BLS wage data = residential building companies (NAICS 2361: new construction + remodelers), NOT specialty trade contractors (NAICS 238x)
- Specialty trades might show stronger wage growth — data not directly comparable
- ABC's labor gap projection is model-based, not census-based
- December 2025 employment loss could be seasonal — winter always brings construction layoffs
- No data on informal/undocumented labor, which affects the supply side in ways BLS doesn't capture
