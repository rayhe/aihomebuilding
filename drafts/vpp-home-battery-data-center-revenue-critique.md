# Critique: Article #415 — VPP Home Battery Data Center Revenue
## Round 1 | 7-Critic Pipeline | June 28, 2026

**Headline:** Your Home Battery Is Worth More to a Data Center Than It Is to You. The Builder Who Installed It Didn't Mention That.  
**Byline:** Priya Greenwood  
**Word count:** 2,028

---

## 1. 🔍 General Editor — Overall Quality, Structure, Engagement

**Score: 8.5 / 10**

### What's Weak
The article is 2,028 words — nearly double the 800–1,200 word deep-dive guideline. The excess is concentrated in two places: the Base Power section spends two dense paragraphs on the company's pricing model (upfront cost, electricity rate, installation cost, tax credit, payback period, revenue projections), and the "Why Builders Are Missing This" section re-states the $500 vs. $5,000 comparison and the PJM 76% figure, both already established in earlier sections. The article could lose 400–500 words without sacrificing substance. The "What happened next should terrify anyone who pays an electricity bill in the mid-Atlantic and excite anyone who owns a home battery" line in "The Grid That Broke First" is a tease-then-payoff construction that reads slightly formulaic.

### What Works
The cold open is strong — it drops you into a breaking news event (the Sunrun/Tesla/Renew Home announcement), immediately contextualizes the scale (14 nuclear reactors), and lands the thesis in the second paragraph. Structure flows logically: news event → market context → builder opportunity → earnings potential → builder psychology → skepticism → action items → limitations. The "16 Gigawatt Asterisk" section arriving *after* the bull case is excellent sequencing — the reader is invested before the skepticism hits. The two-question closer ("Is the panel rated for bidirectional interconnection? Is there a dedicated circuit and conduit run?") is concrete and actionable.

### Fixes Needed
- Consider trimming 400–500 words, primarily from the Base Power detailed pricing (paragraph 2 of "What a Home Battery Actually Earns") and the "Why Builders Are Missing This" section, which re-treads established ground.
- Rewrite the "terrify/excite" line — it's a dual-emotion tease that reads slightly synthetic.

### What's Weak
**"Which" density is elevated.** 15 instances in 2,028 words (0.74%) versus typical human journalism at 0.3–0.5%. Two sentences contain double-"which" constructions (line 72: "which now includes...which in PJM has already been" and line 80: "which is 6.25%...which has historically been wide enough"). It reads more like a financial analyst's note (capacity prices, stock moves, arbitrage models) than a sustainability journalist connecting energy data to real people's lives. The second paragraph — "the kind of move that happens when institutional investors suddenly realize an entire asset class has been mispriced" — is pure Wall Street vocabulary, not Priya's voice.

**"500" appears 11 times** across the article. Even accounting for different contexts ($500 pre-wire, 500 MW, 500 megawatts projected by 2030), this creates a subliminal repetition that a copy editor would catch.

### What Works

A battery in someone's garage goes in before lunch." — this four-sentence staccato sequence is outstanding. "That will never happen" lands as a punch. The degradation paragraph builds a single long argument through subclauses without losing the reader. No banned phrases anywhere.

### Fixes Needed
- Break the two double-"which" sentences (lines 72 and 80) into shorter constructions.
- Add at least one homeowner-level detail or anecdote — even a hypothetical scenario grounded in a specific city, bill amount, or builder conversation — to restore Priya's voice.
- Vary the "500" number by occasionally using "five hundred" in prose or restructuring to avoid the numeral.

## 3. ⚖️ Ethics Reviewer — Moral Reasoning, Self-Congratulation, Positions

**Score: 9.0 / 10**

### What's Weak
The headline — "The Builder Who Installed It Didn't Mention That" — implies builders are deceptive by omission. Most builders genuinely don't know about VPP revenue potential; the omission is ignorance, not concealment. The headline creates a slightly adversarial framing that the article body doesn't support. Additionally, the Base Power section emphasizes revenue projections ($70M for 2026) without noting that the company's economics depend on customers being locked into 3-year electricity purchase agreements — a constraint with consumer protection implications that goes unremarked.

### What Works
The article is fair to all stakeholders. Builders get their incentive structure explained respectfully ("optimize relentlessly for first cost" is descriptive, not judgmental). The skepticism section doesn't vilify data center operators — they're presented as a market force, not a villain. Financial returns are consistently hedged with "theoretically," "could," and "depending on." The counterargument is given full force, including the devastating historical parallel to solar net metering cuts. No self-congratulation, no preachiness.

### Fixes Needed
- None blocking. Consider softening the headline's second sentence to imply ignorance rather than concealment, or acknowledge in the body text that most builders aren't aware of VPP economics.

## 5. ⚖️ Legal Accuracy — Citations, Statutory References

**Score: 8.5 / 10**

### What's Weak
**The federal bill is not named.** The article says "a bipartisan federal bill introduced this month" without identifying it as H.R. 9430, the Ratepayer Protection Act. For a trade publication read by professionals who may need to track the legislation, this is a verifiability gap. The bill's sponsors (Rep. Gabe Evans, R-CO and Rep. Kathy Castor, D-FL) are also omitted.

**The Sunrun stock move is imprecise.** "Sunrun's stock jumped 25% in a single trading session" uses the intraday high, not the closing price. ainvest reports a 12.57% close; Barron's reports 30% intraday early trading. "Jumped 25%" without specifying intraday vs. close is imprecise for a data-driven article.

**PJM queue timeline note:** The article states PJM "paused applications for new generating sources from 2022 to April 2026." The TechCrunch source (June 2026) says "starting in 2022, only reopening the queue in April" without specifying the year. PJM's interconnection queue reforms actually began in phases during 2023–2024. The "April 2026" interpretation is sourced but may be imprecise — the reopening was a staged process, not a single event.

### What Works
All four hyperlinked sources are confirmed real and active (MarketWatch, ainvest, TechCrunch, People.com). The IRA 30% tax credit claim is accurate — Section 25D covers residential energy storage at 30% through 2032. PJM capacity prices ($28.92 to $329.17/MW-day) are correctly sourced and the 11x math checks out ($329.17/$28.92 = 11.37x). Goldman Sachs projections (41 GW in 2026, 66 GW in 2027) and DOE 12% estimate are consistent with multiple independent sources.

### Fixes Needed
- Name the bill: "H.R. 9430, the Ratepayer Protection Act" instead of "a bipartisan federal bill."
- Specify the stock move: "jumped as much as 25% intraday" or use the closing figure (12.57%).

## 6. 🔬 Research Rigor — Novel Contribution, Limitations, Counterarguments, Verifiability

**Score: 8.5 / 10**

### What's Weak
**The article's central original claim — $500 for VPP-ready pre-wiring — lacks methodology transparency.** The Limitations section says this estimate "is based on typical residential electrical rough-in costs" but doesn't show the math. What does a 240V circuit run cost? What does 50 feet of conduit cost? What does a CT clamp hookup add? The STORY_GUIDE requires "show the math with inputs, assumptions, and calculation" for numerical claims. The $500 figure is the article's thesis, and it's presented as an unsourced assertion.

**The "10:1 cost penalty" is cherry-picked.** The article presents ranges of $500–$1,000 (new construction) and $2,000–$5,000 (retrofit), then takes the lowest new-build number against the highest retrofit number to produce "a 10:1 cost penalty." The mean-to-mean comparison is ~$750 vs. ~$3,500 ≈ 4.7:1, which is still dramatic but less so. The full ranges are disclosed in the preceding paragraph, but the 10:1 framing is the one readers will remember and share.

**The 80% market share claim** ("Production homebuilders, the companies building 80% of America's new single-family homes") is unsourced.

### What Works
The original contribution is genuinely novel: nobody has calculated the marginal cost of making a new home VPP-ready during construction vs. retrofit. This isn't vendor claim synthesis — it's an independent cost analysis applied to a breaking news event. The $1,200/year capacity payment calculation is verifiable: $329.17/MW-day × 365 × 0.01 MW = $1,201.47. The Limitations section is honest, specific, and comprehensive — covering labor rate variation, aggregation requirements, audit gaps, legislative risk, and field-vs-lab degradation data. The counterargument is stated at full strength with a devastating historical parallel (solar net metering cuts of 30–60%). Not strawmanned. Not dismissed.

### Fixes Needed
- Add a brief itemized breakdown for the $500 pre-wire cost: circuit ($X), conduit ($X), mounting prep ($X), CT clamp hookup ($X).
- Soften the "10:1 cost penalty" to "4:1 to 10:1 cost penalty, depending on the specific scope" or similar.
- Source the 80% production homebuilder market share claim (NAHB publishes this data).

## 7. 📊 Data Presentation — Accuracy, Math, Comparisons, Units

**Score: 8.5 / 10**

### What's Weak
**The 10:1 ratio framing** (see Research Rigor above) selects the extremes of two ranges to produce the most dramatic number. This is a presentation choice that borders on cherry-picking. A reader who only remembers "10:1" will have an inflated sense of the differential.

**Two Base Power pricing scenarios are adjacent and potentially confusing.** Paragraph 1 of "What a Home Battery Actually Earns" describes the Base Power model ($695–$995 upfront, Base Power owns the battery, 3-year commitment). Paragraph 2 discusses installation costs if you buy the battery yourself ($10,000 before tax credit). A builder reading quickly could conflate the two, thinking the $10,000 cost applies to Base Power's model. Consider adding a clearer transition: "If you buy the battery yourself instead..."

**"Sunrun's stock jumped 25%"** — as noted under Legal Accuracy, this was the intraday move. The closing price change was 12.57%. For a data-driven article, this is imprecise.

### What Works
The nuclear reactor comparison (16 GW ≈ 14 reactors) immediately makes an abstract gigawatt figure tangible. The 11x capacity price increase is correctly calculated and properly contextualized with the delivery year comparison. Units are consistent throughout — GW for grid-scale capacity, MW for company deployments, kW for home systems, kWh for battery capacity, $/MW-day for capacity prices, ¢/kWh for retail rates. The 1.9% to 3.1% deflation of the 16 GW headline figure is an excellent piece of context that most articles about this announcement missed entirely. The $1,200/year capacity payment math is transparent and verifiable.

### Fixes Needed
- Adjust the "10:1" framing (see above).
- Add a clearer transition between the Base Power model and the self-purchase scenario.
- Specify "intraday" for the 25% stock move, or use closing price.
