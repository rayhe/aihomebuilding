# Research: E&O Insurance AI Exclusions and the Design Firm Coverage Gap

## Thesis
Insurance carriers are stripping AI-assisted design work from architects' and engineers' professional liability (E&O) policies — right as the majority of firms adopt AI tools. Homeowners building or renovating may have no idea their design team's insurance won't cover AI-generated errors. This is a concrete, immediate liability gap that affects anyone commissioning professional design work.

## Key Data Points

### Verisk AI Exclusion Forms (Jan 1, 2026)
- **CG 40 47 and CG 40 48**: Standardized endorsement language to exclude losses from generative AI
- Primarily written for CGL policies, but language being adapted across professional liability lines
- Source: Risk Specialty Group analysis, May 2026; FinancialContent/MarktersMedia

### Carriers Already Excluding AI
- **Berkley Insurance Company**: "Absolute" AI exclusion across D&O, E&O, and fiduciary liability; specifically names ChatGPT, Bard, Midjourney, DALL-E
- **Hamilton Insurance Group**: Own generative AI exclusion
- **Philadelphia Insurance / Hamilton Select**: Excluded AI-related claims from E&O entirely
- **AIG, Great American, W.R. Berkley**: Filed for regulatory approval for comparable exclusions
- Source: Risk Specialty Group, FinancialContent May 7 2026

### AI Hallucination Risk in Design
- Stanford Law School research: **58–88% hallucination rates** on general-purpose AI tools
- **20–33%** on specialized "legal-grade" systems
- For design firms: a hallucinated beam specification or nonexistent building code citation = a claim waiting to happen
- Source: Stanford Law School (cited by Risk Specialty Group)

### Architecture/Engineering AI Adoption Rates
- **73% of U.S. architecture firms** adopting AI-driven tools (Research.com, 2026)
- **59% of architects** using AI per RIBA AI Report 2025
- **41% of firms** use AI daily (WiFiTalents/industry aggregate)
- **Only 27% of AEC professionals** currently use AI, but **94% plan to increase** in 2026 (ASCE survey, Dec 2025)
- **95% of engineering leaders** say teams must adopt AI within 2 years (CoLab survey of 250 leaders)
- Source: RIBA, ASCE, Research.com, CoLab

### Contract Gaps
- AIA and ConsensusDocs standard contract templates: **ZERO mention of AI** (Area Development, Q4 2025)
- Contracts need AI-specific clauses: liability allocation, verification standards, disclosure requirements, IP ownership
- Without express language, outcomes hinge on "unpredictable litigation"
- Source: Jane Kutepova, Michelman & Robinson LLP, Area Development

### Legal Risk Categories (Bilzin Sumberg)
- Contractual liability (who pays when AI design errors cause defects?)
- IP infringement (AI training data may contain copyrighted works)
- Data privacy (project data, financial info, geolocation)
- Employment/labor law (workforce displacement, algorithmic hiring bias)
- Regulatory compliance (outdated AI datasets vs current building codes)
- Vendor agreements (indemnification for AI errors)
- Consumer protection
- Source: Bilzin Sumberg, Nov 2025, Law360

### What Firms Can Do (4-Step Audit)
1. Check declarations page for endorsement schedules
2. Search policy body for any reference to "artificial"
3. Review definitions section for narrowed "professional services" language
4. Check retroactive date for claims-made coverage on past AI work
- Some carriers will negotiate carve-backs for supervised AI use with documented governance
- Source: Travis Landers, ARM, Risk Specialty Group

## Kill Test
**Does this help someone building or buying a home?**
YES. If you're hiring an architect or engineer for a $500K+ project, you need to know:
1. Whether they're using AI (most are, or will be)
2. Whether their E&O policy still covers AI-assisted work (increasingly, no)
3. What happens if an AI-generated spec is wrong and causes a defect (you may be the one suing into an uncovered claim)
4. What to ask before signing a contract

## Original Contribution
Cross-referencing the adoption rates (73% of firms using AI) with the exclusion timeline (Jan 2026 Verisk forms) reveals a coverage gap that's wider than either datapoint alone suggests. Most firms adopted AI before their carriers started excluding it. The coverage they had when they started using AI is not the coverage they'll have at renewal. Nobody is tracking this gap systematically for residential clients.
