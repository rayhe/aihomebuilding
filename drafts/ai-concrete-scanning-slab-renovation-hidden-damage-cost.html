Your Plumber Is About to Saw Through a Cable Holding 33,000 Pounds. A $500 Scan Would Stop Him.

By Jake Kowalski • April 7, 2026

A half-inch post-tension cable carries about 31,000 pounds of force. It sits inside your slab, under tension, invisible, holding the concrete together like a clenched fist. When a saw blade catches one, the cable does not politely yield. It snaps. Sometimes violently enough to crack the slab around it. Always expensively enough to turn a $15,000 bathroom remodel into a $22,000 problem.

This happens more often than it should.

According to the NAHB/Census Bureau Survey of Construction, 72.4% of new single-family homes started in 2023 were built on slab foundations, up from 45.8% in 2000. In Texas, Oklahoma, Arkansas, and Louisiana, 96.9%. Most of these slabs contain post-tension cables spaced 24 to 48 inches on center.

Every time a plumber needs a new drain line, somebody has to cut into that slab. The industry standard for avoiding embedded hazards: construction drawings (if the homeowner still has them) and the contractor's gut feeling.

Ground-Penetrating Radar Gets Smart

Ground-penetrating radar has existed since the 1970s. It sends radio pulses into concrete and reads reflections from embedded objects. Rebar, conduit, PT cables, and voids all bounce the signal differently. A skilled technician reads a GPR B-scan like a sonogram.

The problem: skilled.

Reading GPR data is genuinely difficult. Hyperbolic signatures overlap in congested areas where rebar and cables run close together. Interpreting depth, diameter, and material type takes years of training. Most plumbing contractors do not have it, and hiring a GPR specialist for a bathroom remodel felt like overkill.

That is changing. Devices like the Screening Eagle Proceq GP8000 now include AI auto-tagging that identifies and labels hyperbolas in scan data automatically. The software runs on an iPad, uses stepped-frequency continuous-wave technology across 0.2 to 4.0 GHz, and produces real-time 3D maps of what sits inside the concrete. Augmented reality overlays project cable locations directly onto the slab through the tablet camera.

GSSI offers the StructureScan Mini XT as a handheld unit that fits in a tool bag. According to GPRS, one of the largest scanning service providers in the U.S., their equipment achieves accuracy within plus or minus a quarter inch to the center of an object and plus or minus half an inch to its depth.

A quarter inch. On a cable that is half an inch in diameter. That is the difference between a clean core and a $5,000 repair bill.

The Math Nobody Does

I ran the numbers for a typical bathroom remodel on a post-tension slab. A plumber relocating a shower drain, adding a new toilet flange, and rerouting a supply line needs three to five slab penetrations. Post-tension cables run on 24-to-48-inch centers. Each penetration point has a meaningful chance of intersecting a cable if the plumber is working blind.

Here is the expected-value calculation:

Inputs: 4 slab penetrations. PT cables on 36-inch centers. Each core cut is 4 inches in diameter. The probability of a single blind cut intersecting a cable: approximately 11% (4-inch cut diameter divided by 36-inch cable spacing). This is a simplified geometric estimate; real risk varies with cable profile and slab geometry.

Cumulative probability of at least one strike in 4 blind cuts: 1 minus (0.89)^4 = 37%.

Average repair cost per cable strike: $3,000 to $8,000, depending on access and structural engineering requirements. Call it $5,000 as a midpoint, based on Post-Tensioning Institute repair guidance and contractor estimates from Sun Belt markets.

Expected loss from skipping the scan: 37% times $5,000 = $1,850.

Cost of a professional GPR scan: $300 to $800 for a residential visit, depending on the market. Call it $500.

Net expected savings: $1,350 per bathroom remodel. If you are doing a kitchen and a bathroom, the expected savings roughly double because you are adding penetrations without adding scan cost.

That is a 3.7-to-1 return on expected value alone. But expected value flattens the reality. You either hit a cable or you do not. If you do, the repair cost is not $1,850. It is $5,000 to $8,000, plus the delay, plus the finished plumbing work that now needs to be redone around the repair access point, plus the structural engineer's assessment fee. Total damage can exceed $12,000 on a bad day.

What the AI Actually Does

Traditional GPR requires a trained operator to manually identify each hyperbola in the B-scan and classify it as rebar, cable, conduit, or void. New AI systems use convolutional neural networks trained on thousands of annotated scans to automate that classification. A 2025 study in MDPI's NDT journal demonstrated a dual-channel CNN framework achieving reliable automated rebar detection even in congested areas where manual interpretation struggles.

Scan time for a residential bathroom floor: 5 to 15 minutes. The scanner marks locations with paint or chalk directly on the concrete. The plumber sees exactly where not to cut.

Not perfect. AI auto-tagging works best in the shallow layers where residential PT cables sit (2 to 4 inches deep). Performance degrades with closely spaced objects, heavy rebar mats, and wet concrete. Manufacturer accuracy claims come from controlled testing, not field conditions. But "varies" is dramatically better than "guessing."

What You Should Do

If you own a slab-on-grade home built after 1980 in TX, FL, AZ, CA, or any expansive-soil market: assume your slab has post-tension cables until proven otherwise. Check your original construction documents. If you cannot find a PT layout drawing, a GPR scan can tell you whether cables are present and where they run.

Before any slab penetration: hire a GPR scanning service. GPRS operates in all 50 states. Local concrete cutting companies often offer scanning as an add-on. Cost: $300 to $800, typically billed as a flat fee per visit, not per square foot. On a $15,000 or more renovation that involves any slab cuts, this is less than 5% of the project cost.

Ask your plumber directly: "How do you verify there are no PT cables before cutting?" If the answer is "we look at the plans" or "we go slow and listen," that is not good enough. Cables do not always follow the original drawings. Field conditions change. Tolerances slip. Plans get lost.

If you are a plumbing or remodeling contractor: the Proceq GP8000 runs approximately $15,000 to $25,000. Four slab-penetration jobs per month in a PT-heavy market, and it pays for itself in avoided liability within a year. Alternatively, subcontract scanning at $500 per visit and pass the cost through. Most homeowners will pay it once you explain what a cable repair costs.

The Strongest Case Against

Experienced plumbing contractors in post-tension markets have been cutting slabs for decades without GPR. They use probing techniques, test cuts, and visual inspection of the slab edges where cable anchors are sometimes visible. Many have never hit a cable. Their argument: the tools work, the risk is overstated, and adding a $500 scan to every bathroom remodel inflates costs for the majority of jobs where nothing would have gone wrong anyway.

This is fair. Most blind slab cuts do not hit cables. A 37% cumulative probability across four cuts means 63% of the time, you are fine. And the $5,000 midpoint repair estimate assumes a competent PT repair contractor is available quickly. In reality, the range is wide: a single easily accessible cable repair in Houston might run $1,500, while a cable in a finished room in the Bay Area could top $10,000.

The counterargument also has a selection bias problem. Contractors who have been doing this for 20 years without hitting a cable may also be working in markets or slab types with wider cable spacing or no PT at all. Their track record does not transfer to every slab.

Limitations

No centralized database tracks post-tension cable strikes during residential renovations. The 11% per-cut probability used in this analysis is a simplified geometric model based on typical cable spacing. Real-world risk depends on cable profile (draped vs. straight), slab geometry, and how much the as-built conditions deviate from the design drawings.

Repair cost estimates ($3,000 to $8,000) come from contractor quotes in Texas and Florida markets. Costs in higher-labor regions could exceed $10,000. In some cases, a snapped cable does not require immediate structural repair if the structural engineer determines the remaining cables can carry the load. That is a best case that reduces the financial impact but still requires professional evaluation.

AI auto-tagging accuracy claims from Screening Eagle and GSSI have not been independently validated in peer-reviewed residential studies. The academic research on CNN-based GPR interpretation uses bridge deck and commercial slab data. Residential slabs are typically simpler (thinner, less congested), which should favor the AI, but the specific performance gap has not been quantified.

And GPR has a fundamental limitation: it cannot identify cable tension. It can show you where a cable is. It cannot tell you whether that cable is still carrying 31,000 pounds or whether it has already been compromised by corrosion. For that, you still need a structural engineer.
