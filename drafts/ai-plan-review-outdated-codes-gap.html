FEMA Gave 39 States Its Lowest Building Code Score. AI Plan Review Will Help You Build to Those Bad Codes Faster.

By Catherine Chen · April 7, 2026

Nineteen states scored zero.

Not a low score. Not "needs improvement." Zero out of 100 on FEMA's Building Code Adoption Tracking assessment, which rates how well states adopt and enforce modern building codes for hazard resistance. Louisiana, North Carolina, Pennsylvania, and 16 other states received that mark. Another 20 landed in FEMA's lowest category overall. That is 39 states where FEMA considers the building code framework inadequate to protect residents from windstorms, flooding, seismic events, and wildfire.

Now picture an AI plan review tool deployed in one of those states. It scans your blueprints against the locally adopted code, checks 400 provisions in three minutes, and returns a compliance report. Everything passes.

What passed? A code written in 2009.

What 17 Years of Code Updates Actually Changed

Building codes are not cosmetic. Each three-year update cycle from the International Code Council incorporates research, disaster data, and engineering advances that directly affect whether a house survives what nature throws at it. A jurisdiction still on the 2009 IRC misses the following:

A home built to the 2009 code in a tornado-prone county has no storm shelter requirement. No wildfire-resistant exterior mandate. Energy performance roughly 30% worse than current standards. And an AI tool checking that home against the local code will flag none of this, because none of it violates the 2009 rules.

Compliance is not the same as safety. The algorithm doesn't know the difference.

How Many Homes This Affects

I cross-referenced FEMA's BCAT data with Census Bureau building permit figures to estimate the scope. Roughly 1.4 million residential building permits are issued annually in the United States. Major construction states with FEMA's lowest building code rating include North Carolina, Pennsylvania, Tennessee, and Missouri. Texas has no statewide mandatory residential code at all, leaving adoption to individual jurisdictions.

At the DOE's figure of $162 per year in energy savings from modern codes, applied over a 30-year mortgage, each of those homes forgoes roughly $4,860 in lifetime energy savings. Aggregate that across 560,000 homes and the annual cohort alone represents about $2.7 billion in missed energy savings over their lifetimes. Factor in avoided disaster damage, and the number climbs higher.

FEMA's own Building Codes Save study found that thousands of localities operated under "archaic building codes" that exposed people to "a dangerous, costly and unnecessarily high level of risk." FEMA's 2023 National Preparedness Report called the failure to adopt new codes "one of the most significant factors that compounds risk and increases costs from natural hazards."

Where AI Plan Review Works Brilliantly

California scored 99. After the 2025 LA fires, Governor Newsom deployed Archistar's AI PreCheck tool to help fire survivors pre-check their building plans against local zoning and building codes before submission. Early results showed permits moving at roughly three times the pre-fire pace, with local review averaging under 30 days.

In Surrey, British Columbia, which launched AI PreCheck in March 2026, city staff discovered that 80% of residential permit applications contained significant zoning deficiencies, requiring an average of 1.6 resubmissions. Jerome Thibaudeau, Surrey's business transformation manager, called the AI "spell-check for zoning." Each avoided resubmission saves the applicant weeks. A UCLA study found that cutting approval times by just 25% could boost housing production by 33%.

These results are real. Nobody disputes that AI plan review can accelerate permitting in jurisdictions that have strong, modern codes. California's building code is among the most robust in the country. Speeding up compliance with good standards is good policy.

Speeding up compliance with 2009 standards is a different proposition entirely.

The Entrenchment Problem

Code adoption is political. Every update cycle triggers opposition from builders and legislators who argue that stricter codes raise construction costs and price out homebuyers. That argument has real backing: a 2021 NAHB study found that regulatory costs account for 24% of a new home's final price, or $93,870 on a median-priced home. For every $1,000 added, roughly 2,200 families lose their ability to buy.

In North Carolina, the Republican-controlled legislature blocked Governor Cooper's plan to update the state's energy codes. In Congress, House Republicans attempted to defund the DOE's new energy code grants. Political resistance to code modernization is a feature of the system, not a bug.

AI plan review could inadvertently remove one of the few pressures that drives code updates: friction. When permit review is slow and painful, jurisdictions have an incentive to modernize. Updated codes can actually reduce review workload by clarifying ambiguities and standardizing requirements. A 2014 ICC survey found that 80% of code professionals expected to retire by 2029, with median salaries of $50,000 to $74,999, well below private sector equivalents. When those officials leave, the understaffed departments they leave behind become the exact customers for AI plan review.

If AI makes a 2009 code fast and painless to enforce, where is the institutional pressure to update it?

What You Can Do

1. Check your state's FEMA building code rating. Visit FEMA's BCAT fact sheets and find your state. If it's in the "Lower Resistance" category, your code likely lags the current model code by 6 to 15 years.

2. Ask your builder which code edition your plans comply with. Not "is it code-compliant?" but "which edition of the IRC and IECC are you building to?" If the answer is anything before 2018, ask what it would cost to upgrade to current model code provisions for wind, energy, and fire. Budget $3,000 to $8,000 for a typical single-family home, depending on scope.

3. Request an energy code gap analysis. Hire a HERS rater ($300 to $500) to evaluate your plans against the 2021 IECC regardless of what your jurisdiction requires. You will get a specific list of upgrades with estimated energy savings. If the $162/yr DOE average applies to your home, upgraded insulation and air sealing pays for itself in under 20 years through utility savings alone.

4. Build beyond code in hazard zones. If you are in a wildfire-prone area and your code lacks WUI provisions, specify Class A roofing, ignition-resistant siding, and enclosed eaves anyway. If you are in Tornado Alley and your code predates 2024 IRC storm shelter provisions, a safe room costs $3,000 to $8,000 according to FEMA.

5. Ask your city council why they haven't adopted the current code. Code adoption is a local government decision. Building departments rarely lobby for their own updates because they lack the political capital. Homeowners and builders who show up at council meetings and ask why their jurisdiction is on a 2009 code create the only kind of pressure that works.

In Defense of the Lag

Code adoption delay is not always irresponsible. New code editions impose training costs on inspectors, plan reviewers, builders, and designers. A jurisdiction with three plan reviewers and 2,000 permit applications per year cannot afford six months of staff retraining every three years. Phased adoption, where states adopt critical safety provisions immediately and phase in energy and resilience upgrades over two to four years, is a defensible strategy.

More directly: NAHB's cost data is real. A $5,000 code compliance increase on a $400,000 home prices out 11,000 families nationally. Housing affordability is not a trivial concern, and legislators who resist code updates are often responding to constituents who cannot absorb higher costs. The fact that modern codes produce long-term savings does not erase the upfront barrier for a family trying to close on their first home.

AI plan review, deployed thoughtfully, could actually help. An AI tool that checks plans against both the locally adopted code and the current model code, flagging the gap with estimated costs, would give homeowners real information instead of a binary pass/fail against standards they never chose. No commercially available tool does this today. It should.

What This Analysis Doesn't Cover

My estimate of 560,000 homes per year in weak-code states relies on Census Bureau state-level permit totals cross-referenced with FEMA's BCAT categories. BCAT categorizes states by "resistant jurisdictions," a metric that does not perfectly map to building code edition. Some states with low FEMA ratings have strong local codes in major metros. Dallas, Houston, and Austin all enforce relatively recent codes despite Texas lacking a statewide mandate.

Energy savings of $162 per year are a DOE national average. Actual savings depend on climate zone, house size, and local energy prices. Homes in mild climates see less benefit from envelope upgrades. My $2.7 billion aggregate uses a uniform multiplier that likely overstates savings in the South and understates them in the Midwest.

I also cannot verify which code edition each of the 20,000+ permit-issuing jurisdictions currently enforces. FEMA's BCAT is the best available dataset, but it assesses states, not individual cities and counties.
