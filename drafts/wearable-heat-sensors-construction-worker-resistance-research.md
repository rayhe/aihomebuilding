# Research: Wearable Heat Sensors on Construction Sites — The Technology Works, the Workers Don't Want It

## Journalist: Marcus "Steel" Washington (Workforce & Labor)

## Angle
BLS officially reports ~39 heat deaths per year from occupational exposure. A January 2026 University of Michigan study (Shkembi et al., medRxiv) estimates ~9,800 annual excess deaths — a 250x discrepancy. Construction workers are 4x more likely to die from heat than workers in other industries (CPWR, August 2025). Wearable heat monitoring devices (SlateSafety BAND V2, Kenzen, Triax Spot-r) exist, can detect dangerous core body temp rises in real time, and cost ~$160/month per worker. But adoption on residential construction sites is negligible. Why? Worker resistance: surveillance fears, alert fatigue, macho job-site culture ("I know when I'm hot"), and foremen who view wearables as productivity-killers. The technology isn't the bottleneck — trust is.

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? Yes — homebuilders need to understand that heat illness kills more workers than official stats suggest, costs them $30K-$50K per workers' comp claim, and that wearable solutions exist but require trust-building before deployment. For homebuyers: your home was built by someone working in 105°F heat. That matters.

## Primary Sources

### 1. Shkembi et al. (2026) — University of Michigan / medRxiv
- **Title:** "Mortality burden of outdoor occupational heat exposure in the United States"
- **Key finding:** ~9,800 (95% CI: 3,100–17,000) annual excess deaths from outdoor occupational heat, vs. 39 officially reported by BLS
- **Method:** Panel data for 3,108 US counties (2010–2019), linking CDC WONDER mortality with WBGT heat exposure prevalence
- **Equity finding:** 62% of excess deaths in most socially vulnerable counties (only 25% of workers)
- **Funded by:** CDC/NIOSH (Grant T42 OH008455)
- **DOI:** 10.64898/2026.01.29.26345131
- **URL:** https://www.medrxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.01.29.26345131v1

### 2. CPWR — Center for Construction Research & Training (August 2025)
- **Title:** "Heat-Related Worker Deaths Surge as Construction Industry Faces Mounting Crisis"
- **Key findings:**
  - Construction workers 4x more likely to die from heat than other industries (0.15 vs 0.04 per 100,000 FTW)
  - Fatal heat injuries up 77.4% from 2012 to 2023
  - 18 heat deaths in construction in 2023 = more than 1/3 of all occupational heat fatalities (construction = 7% of workforce)
  - Summer (Jun/Jul/Aug) = 71.4% of construction heat deaths
  - Geographic: Texas 25 deaths, California 13, Florida 9 (2011–2023)
  - Nonfatal injuries dropped 57.2% (1,500 → 620) — likely underreporting, not improvement
  - 5%+ of severe heat injuries found only in narrative descriptions, not coded properly
- **URL:** https://www.cpwr.com/wp-content/uploads/DataBulletin-August2025.pdf
- **Via:** Risk & Insurance, August 20, 2025

### 3. OHS Magazine — "How Technology is Transforming Heat Stress Prevention" (Feb 2026)
- Traditional monitoring (WBGT, admin controls, buddy systems) is reactive and based on averages
- PPG wearables monitor heart rate, skin temp, sweat rate in real time
- OSHA NEP (National Emphasis Program) + proposed heat rulemaking pushing regulatory change
- Key problem: traditional methods "aren't holding up" per OHS
- URL: https://ohsonline.com/articles/2026/03/print/how-technology-is-transforming-heat-stress-prevention-in-the-workplace.aspx

### 4. SlateSafety BAND V2 — Pricing & Specs
- Wearable armband: heart rate, core body temperature, exertion level, location, fall detection
- Automated work/rest cycle guidance
- Rental: $80/week, $160/month per unit (via RAECO Rents)
- Web portal for real-time supervisor visibility across whole team
- URL: https://raecorents.com/products/bandv2-by-slate-safety-personal-heat-stress-monitor

### 5. McCarthy Building Companies / Kenzen Pilot
- McCarthy partnered with Kenzen for wearable heat monitoring pilot on sites in Arizona, Georgia, and Texas
- Kenzen sensor worn on upper arm, monitors core body temp, sweat rate, hydration status
- Proprietary algorithm calculates individual sweat rate in liters/hour
- Alerts workers via smartphone when dangerous heat stress detected
- Workers "appreciate receiving notifications" (per McCarthy press release)
- URL: https://solarbuildermag.com/news/mccarthy-building-co-to-pilot-wearable-heat-monitoring-tech-for-worker-safety/

### 6. Regulatory Landscape
- 7 states with heat protection mandates: California, Colorado, Maryland, Minnesota, Nevada, Oregon, Washington
- ANSI/ASSP A10.50-2024: construction-specific heat stress management guidance
- Federal OSHA heat rule still in proposed/rulemaking stage — not finalized
- OSHA-NIOSH Heat Safety Tool App available

## Original Contribution: Cost-Benefit Analysis Per Crew
**Math:**
- 20-person residential framing crew in Phoenix, AZ
- SlateSafety BAND V2 at $160/month × 20 workers = $3,200/month, ~$16,000 for a 5-month hot season (May–Sep)
- Average heat-related workers' comp claim: ~$30,000–$50,000 (includes medical, lost work time, legal)
- OSHA heat-related penalty (serious violation): average $16,131 as of 2024
- Additional hidden costs: crew productivity loss on hot days (studies estimate 10–25% productivity decline above 95°F WBGT), schedule delays, turnover/replacement costs for injured workers
- **Break-even:** Prevent ONE moderate heat injury per 20-person crew per summer → the wearables pay for themselves 2–3x over
- But: residential construction adoption is near-zero. Why? Commercial GCs (McCarthy, Skanska) pilot these. Residential builders don't.

## Strongest Counterargument
Workers legitimately worry these are surveillance devices. Core body temp, location, heart rate — that's intimate biometric data. Who sees it? Can a foreman fire someone for taking too many breaks? Can insurance companies access the data? Workers' comp insurers already incentivize wearable adoption, which means the data could eventually work against workers. Alert fatigue is real — false positives erode trust fast. And the "tough it out" culture on residential sites isn't irrational — it's how these workers were trained and selected for decades.

## Limitations
- Shkembi et al. study is a medRxiv preprint (not yet peer-reviewed)
- CPWR data is through 2023; 2024 data not yet available
- SlateSafety rental pricing may not reflect bulk/enterprise rates
- No published randomized controlled trial of wearable heat monitoring on residential construction outcomes
- Workers' comp claim cost estimates vary widely by state and severity
- Residential construction adoption data is essentially non-existent (anecdotal)
