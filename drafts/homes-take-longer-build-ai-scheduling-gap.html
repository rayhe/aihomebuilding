Your House Takes 36% Longer to Build Than It Did in 1993. The Homes Got Smaller.

By Frank DeLuca · June 7, 2026

In 1993, the average single-family home went from breaking ground to handing over keys in 5.6 months. A crew showed up, framed the house, roughed in the mechanicals, closed it up, finished it out, and moved on in five and a half months, a number nobody celebrated because nobody thought it needed celebrating. It was simply how long it took to build a house.

By 2024, that number had climbed to 7.6 months of construction time, according to the Census Bureau's Survey of Construction. Add another 1.4 months waiting for permits, and the total from authorization to completion sits at 9.1 months. That is a 36% increase in construction time over three decades, and it would be tempting to blame bigger, more complicated homes, except for one problem: homes are getting smaller.

2,367 ft²
Average single-family home size in 2024, down from a peak of roughly 2,520 ft² around 2015. Census Bureau Survey of Construction, quarterly starts data.

Average single-family floor area dropped from about 2,520 square feet in 2015 to 2,367 square feet in 2024. Yet construction time rose from 6.3 months to 7.6 months over the same period. We are building smaller houses more slowly, and every additional month costs real money that nobody in the industry seems interested in quantifying, so I quantified it.

Two Extra Months, $5.4 Billion

A production builder carrying a $400,000 construction loan at 8% interest pays roughly $2,667 per month in interest alone. Two additional months of construction time adds $5,333 to the carrying cost of each home. Multiply that across the 1.013 million single-family units started in 2024, and the industry-wide cost of those extra two months lands at approximately $5.4 billion per year in additional financing charges, and even that figure is conservative. It does not include extended general conditions, which is the industry's polite term for the monthly bill that keeps accumulating for the job trailer, the dumpster, the portable toilet, the superintendent's truck, and the builder's risk insurance that ticks along whether anyone is working or not. It does not include the opportunity cost of capital sitting in a foundation for seven and a half months when it used to sit there for five and a half. It does not account for the compounding effect on a builder running twenty concurrent projects where a two-month slowdown per unit means four fewer closings per year.

A reasonable estimate of total waste from construction timeline inflation, including general conditions and overhead allocation, sits closer to $7 billion annually. Seven billion dollars, invisible because it accumulated at a rate of about three weeks per decade, slow enough that nobody drew a line on a whiteboard and said this needs to stop.

Notice 2022 at 8.3 months. Pandemic supply chain chaos pushed construction time to its highest level in the dataset's 50-year history. By 2024, things improved, lumber was available again, the backlog cleared, and construction time dropped back to 7.6 months. But it did not drop back to pre-pandemic levels. It did not drop back to 2015 levels. It settled into a new, higher plateau, and the NAHB attributed the gap to three factors: a more stringent regulatory environment, elevated mortgage rates suppressing volume, and a shortage of skilled labor.

Those factors deserve different weights, and the industry does a poor job of separating them.

Regulatory complexity is the biggest contributor. Between 1993 and 2024, the International Energy Conservation Code went through six revision cycles, each one adding insulation requirements, air sealing standards, and mandatory testing like blower-door tests that did not exist in 1993. Stacking additional inspection points onto a residential schedule does not add one day each. It adds the day of the inspection plus every day the work sat idle waiting for the inspector to show up, which in some jurisdictions means five to seven business days of dead time per inspection. A modern home requires roughly twice the number of inspections as a 1993 home, and each one creates a scheduling dependency that ripples through every downstream trade.

Labor scarcity comes second. NAHB estimates a deficit of 439,000 construction workers as of 2025. But the impact on timelines is not uniform, because framers are relatively available while electricians, plumbers, and HVAC techs are the bottleneck trades, and their scarcity creates what schedulers call "resource leveling" problems: you cannot start drywall until rough-in is complete, and rough-in cannot start until all three mechanical trades have finished, and if the electrician cannot get to your job for three weeks because he is booked on four other projects, then drywall waits, and taping waits, and trim waits, and paint waits, and nobody touches the house for 21 days while the carrying costs accumulate silently.

Subcontractor fragmentation is the factor nobody measures. A 1993 home might have needed eight to ten subcontractors. A 2024 home with a heat pump system, spray-foam insulation, an EV charger rough-in, low-voltage wiring for structured data, and a solar-ready roof needs fourteen to eighteen. Every additional sub is another scheduling dependency, another phone call, another three-day gap waiting for someone to show up for four hours of work, and this is not a technology problem so much as a coordination problem being solved, across most of the residential industry, with text messages and memory.

Where Geography Stacks the Deck

Regional variation is staggering. According to the Census Bureau's 2024 regional data, a home in the South Atlantic takes 7.8 months from authorization to completion. A home in the Middle Atlantic takes 13.7 months. Same country. Same building codes, mostly. Almost double the construction time.

5.9 months
Gap between the fastest region (South Atlantic, 7.8 months) and the slowest (Middle Atlantic, 13.7 months). Same country. Same general building practices. Different permitting cultures.

What separates them is not weather, though weather contributes, but rather the density of the permitting bureaucracy, the availability of inspection staff, and local zoning overlay requirements that can add months of review before a shovel touches dirt. Pacific region homes average 10.8 months while New England clocks 13 months. If you are building in New Jersey or Connecticut, your construction timeline is nearly double what it would be in Georgia or the Carolinas, and no scheduling tool is going to close that gap because the delay is not in your schedule but in someone else's inbox.

AI Scheduling Tools Exist, but They Are Not for You

In April 2026, construction scheduling startup Planera launched an AI assistant called Manny that lets project teams query their schedules using natural language and run what-if simulations in minutes rather than hours. In early 2024, Buildots released a Delay Forecast tool that predicts delays before they happen, built on their finding that 62% of construction activities run slower than planned and 25% crawl at half their intended pace. Outbuild now connects field teams directly to the schedule through a mobile app, creating what they call a "continuous feedback loop between planning and execution."

All of these tools were designed for projects with full-time schedulers, CPM networks with thousands of activities, and budgets measured in tens of millions of dollars: commercial construction, infrastructure, data centers.

A residential GC running five custom homes does not have a CPM scheduler. He has a whiteboard in his truck and a sense, built over two decades, of roughly when the electrician should show up relative to when the framer finishes. His scheduling tool is his phone, and his what-if simulation is calling the HVAC sub and asking if he can move the rough-in up a week. His delay forecast is the weather app on his phone and a gut feeling about whether the inspector will actually show up on Thursday.

Buildots requires 360-degree cameras mounted on hardhats that capture progress as workers walk the site. On a 400-unit apartment complex, the per-unit cost of those cameras is noise. On a single-family custom home, it is absurd. Planera's natural-language schedule queries are powerful if you have a schedule detailed enough to query. Most residential projects do not have a schedule that exists in any software at all. Outbuild's mobile app assumes that the superintendent and the subcontractors will log updates digitally, which requires buy-in from a workforce that, on custom residential projects, communicates primarily through text messages that read "running late Tuesday, maybe Wednesday."

What Would Actually Help

If you are a residential builder or GC losing two months per project to timeline inflation, the leverage is not in AI scheduling software, at least not yet, but in three unglamorous operational changes that most builders resist because they require discipline, not technology.

Inspection batching. Instead of scheduling inspections sequentially, group them by phase. Some jurisdictions allow a combined rough-in inspection covering electrical, plumbing, and mechanical in a single visit, and builders who have negotiated this with their local building department report saving five to eight business days per home. Not every jurisdiction offers it, but ask anyway.

Subcontractor scheduling agreements. Write specific start dates and completion windows into your subcontract agreements, with per-day liquidated damages for no-shows and bonuses for early completion. This is standard in commercial construction and almost unheard of in residential, and while subs resist it, a $200/day penalty for a no-show is less than the $89/day carrying cost of a $400,000 loan at 8%.

Trade stacking with buffer tasks. Instead of rigid sequential scheduling, identify non-critical-path tasks that can absorb idle days when a trade does not show. Punch list work, cabinet hardware, closet shelving, landscape grading. Keep a running list of tasks that require no trade coordination and can be assigned to your own crew on dead days. This does not shorten the critical path, but it prevents the project from losing productive days entirely when the inevitable gap appears.

Limitations

My $5.4 billion carrying-cost estimate uses a $400,000 average construction loan at 8% interest, which reflects current market conditions but may overstate the cost for production builders who negotiate lower rates on volume. It also assumes the 1993 baseline of 5.6 months represents "normal" construction time, which it may not. Homes in 1993 had fewer code requirements, fewer inspections, less complex mechanical systems, and less rigorous quality expectations. Some of the extra time is genuinely producing a better product. How much of the 2.0-month increase represents waste versus value is a question I cannot answer with the Census data alone.

Additionally, the Census Bureau's Survey of Construction samples permitted housing starts and tracks them to completion. It does not break down construction time by phase (foundation, framing, rough-in, finish), which means I cannot isolate where the additional time actually accumulates. Regional variations suggest permitting and inspection delays are the primary culprit, but without phase-level data, this remains an informed inference rather than a proven conclusion.

Strongest Counterargument

A builder who has been in the industry since the 1990s would reasonably argue that the extra two months produce a meaningfully better house. A 2024 home is tighter, more energy-efficient, and built to a structural standard that would not have been required or even understood thirty years ago. Blower-door testing catches air leakage that 1993 homes simply lived with. Manual J load calculations, when actually performed, size HVAC systems more accurately than the rule-of-thumb methods that dominated the previous era. Modern electrical panels handle loads that a 1993 panel was never designed for.

Fair enough, because some of the additional time is genuinely adding value. But the NAHB's own analysis credits "a more stringent regulatory environment" as the leading cause, and regulatory complexity is not the same thing as quality. An inspection that makes the house safer takes one day. Waiting nine days for the inspector to schedule that one-day visit does not make the house any safer at all. The wait is pure waste, and it is the dominant contributor to the timeline increase, particularly in high-regulation regions where the gap between South Atlantic construction time and Middle Atlantic construction time is nearly six months. That six-month gap is not producing a house that is 75% better. It is producing a house that waited 75% longer for someone to look at it.

When AI finally does arrive in residential scheduling, the tool that matters will not be a natural-language interface for querying a CPM network but something much simpler: an AI that texts your inspector, confirms the date, alerts you when the slot is at risk, and automatically reschedules your electrician if the rough-in inspection gets pushed, because what residential builders actually need is not project intelligence but the basic coordination work that nobody has time to do and everybody pays for when it does not get done.

Until then, the whiteboard and the phone will have to do. They have been getting slower for thirty years, and nobody drew a line and said stop.
