# Research: AI-Powered Land Acquisition & Site Feasibility for Residential Builders

## Thesis
The US is short 4.7 million homes, and 64% of builders can't find enough lots — yet land acquisition still runs on county records, PDF zoning codes, and gut instinct. AI platforms now compress weeks of parcel evaluation into minutes, and the largest homebuilder in America just went all-in.

## Strongest Counterargument
"Local knowledge can't be replaced by an algorithm. Real land deals depend on relationships — who owns the parcel, what the planning commission is like, which city council member supports density. An AI can tell you a lot is zoned R-3, but it can't tell you the neighbor will file 14 objections."

This is fair. AI doesn't replace boots-on-the-ground dealmaking. But it compresses the pre-qualification funnel — instead of a land team spending 3 weeks evaluating 5 parcels, they can screen 500 in a morning and hand-pick the 5 worth a site visit. The relationship skills still matter; they just get applied to better targets.

## Cost Data
- Average finished lot: ~$91,100 (NAHB 2024, 13.7% of $665,298)
- Land acquisition due diligence: typically 2-6 weeks per site for zoning analysis, environmental review, utility verification
- Professional land feasibility study: $5,000-$25,000 depending on complexity and market
- Prophetic/Acres-type platforms: enterprise SaaS pricing, likely $50K-$200K+/year for large builders
- D.R. Horton annual revenue: $36.8B (FY2024) — even small lot acquisition efficiency gains = massive dollar impact
- 4.7M housing deficit × $665K avg = $3.1 trillion in unbuilt housing stock

Something that captures the manual absurdity of current land acquisition + the scale of the problem. Examples:
- "Your Land Team Spent Three Weeks Evaluating Five Lots. The AI Screened 183,000."
- "64% of Builders Can't Find Enough Lots. The Algorithm Found 3,000."
- "D.R. Horton Builds 90,000 Homes a Year. It Just Handed Land Acquisition to an Algorithm."

## Article Angle
Focus on the specific bottleneck: it's not that land doesn't exist, it's that finding *buildable, zoning-compliant, infrastructure-accessible* land is a manual nightmare. Traditional process: drive neighborhoods, check county GIS, call zoning office, read 300-page zoning codes, hire consultants, wait weeks. AI process: describe criteria in plain language, screen 150M+ parcels, get zoning-aware feasibility in minutes. But be honest: AI finds opportunities faster, it doesn't close deals or navigate politics.
