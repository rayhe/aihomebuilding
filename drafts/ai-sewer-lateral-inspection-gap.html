Your City Grades Every Sewer Pipe With AI. The One Under Your House Still Gets a Plumber's Opinion.

By Jake Kowalski · June 21, 2026
← All Stories

Macomb County, Michigan, has a problem most counties share: aging underground pipe that nobody can see until it collapses. What Macomb County doesn't share is the response. They fed their CCTV inspection footage into SewerAI's AutoCode platform, which ran machine learning models against every frame, classified each defect using NASSCO's Pipeline Assessment Certification Program grading standards, and returned coded reports at six times the speed of a human reviewer. "Using SewerAI software enables us to perform that task much quicker, more effectively and more efficiently while saving ratepayers millions of dollars," said Candice S. Miller, Macomb County's public works commissioner.

SewerAI has now processed 3.4 million inspections across 578 million feet of pipe in more than 2,000 cities, claiming 99% accuracy, and last year it raised $15 million in Series B funding from Emerald Technology Ventures and Innovius Capital to push the platform into even more municipalities.

Meanwhile, you bought a house last year. Your real estate agent recommended a sewer scope. A plumber showed up with a camera on a reel, pushed it into your cleanout, watched the feed on a screen the size of a paperback novel, and narrated what he saw: "Looks like some roots there. Little bit of scale. Overall not bad." He charged you $275 and drove away.

That was your report.

Two Pipes, Two Standards

The pipe running under the street in front of your house belongs to the city. A NASSCO-certified technician grades defects on a 1-to-5 scale using specific codes for each type of failure: BO for broken pipe, DF for deformation, FL for fracture longitudinal, IR for infiltration runner, RB for root ball. Each observation gets a severity grade, a precise distance measurement from the upstream manhole, a photo, and a timestamp. WinCan or GraniteNet software compiles everything into a structured database that integrates with the city's GIS maps. If an AI module is running, the coding happens in real time as the crawler moves, with the software flagging defects the operator might miss.

What runs from the city main to your foundation belongs to you, and it's called a lateral. In most jurisdictions, you own it all the way from the tap on the city main, under the street, under your yard, to where it enters your basement or crawlspace wall. Maintaining it is your responsibility, and when it fails, replacing it is too.

Your lateral gets none of that rigor.

A standard residential sewer scope costs $200 to $500, and what you get for that money is a technician pushing a camera through a 4-inch pipe, watching live video, and talking. Some record the footage and hand you a USB drive; some take screenshots at notable points. A few write a one-page summary peppered with terms like "satisfactory," "fair condition," or "recommend further evaluation." Almost none apply NASSCO PACP codes, almost none produce structured data, and almost none use any form of automated defect detection.

What the AI Actually Does

Understanding the gap requires understanding what the municipal AI tools are doing, because it isn't magic. It's pattern recognition at scale, trained on enormous datasets of pipe failures.

SewerAI's AutoCode watches CCTV footage and assigns NASSCO observation codes to each defect it detects. Root intrusion at 47 feet from the upstream manhole, severity 3. Circumferential crack at 112 feet, severity 2. Grease deposit at 83 feet, severity 1. Every observation is coded, graded, georeferenced, and stored, and then the platform feeds them into its Risk & Rehab module, which generates prioritized capital plans telling the utility which pipes need replacement now, which can wait five years, how much each project will cost, and where to sequence the work to minimize the disruption that blooms across the neighborhood above.

Burgess & Niple's PipeAId tool takes a similar approach but adds a temporal dimension that municipal spreadsheets never captured. For the Town of Newburgh, Indiana, the system processed 40,000 linear feet of municipal sewer and gave the town something they'd never had before: a comparison against their own historical CCTV data from five to ten years earlier, showing the actual rate of deterioration over time. Newburgh's proactive point-repair program was working, and the pipes weren't degrading fast enough to warrant wholesale replacement, a finding that saved the town millions in capital spending they would have authorized on instinct alone.

WinCan's Sewermatics module has transformed the labor economics of municipal inspection so thoroughly that the before-and-after numbers are hard to believe. One contracting firm told Cleaner Magazine that its crews went from 35 to 40 manhole scans a day to more than 100 after adopting AI-assisted coding, while the evaluator headcount dropped from five to one and total capacity doubled without hiring a single additional person.

And in the academic pipeline, the models keep getting smaller and faster. A 2024 study proposed Sewer-YOLO-Slim, a defect detection model compressed to 4.9 megabytes that achieved 93.5% mean average precision with 15.3-millisecond inference on an edge device, which means you could run it on the processor inside a smoke detector. A team at another institution built a battery-powered sensor integrating a camera, a microcontroller, and a LoRa radio that performs on-device defect classification using a TensorFlow Lite model quantized to 8-bit integers, transmitting only the results wirelessly to save power while the hardware cost stays trivial and the accuracy stays competitive with full-scale municipal systems.

None of this is a technology problem. It works, it's proven, it's getting cheaper by the quarter, and it runs on hardware that fits in your hand.

Why Residential Got Left Behind

Municipal sewer mains are 8-inch, 12-inch, 24-inch concrete and vitrified clay pipes running under streets, accessible through manholes, and a robotic crawler with a pan-and-tilt camera rolls through them producing video that is clean, well-lit, and consistent enough for machine learning models to train on reliably.

Your lateral is a 4-inch pipe, often running 50 to 100 feet from the house to the main, laid in a trench that was backfilled 30, 50, or 80 years ago, and the only access point is a cleanout in your basement floor or on the exterior wall. Instead of a crawler, a push camera bumps along, sometimes rotating, sometimes obscured by standing water, sometimes stopped cold by a belly or an offset joint where the pipe settled unevenly.

Interpreting that video is harder in every dimension: smaller pipe diameter, more obstructions, water levels that obscure the bottom third of the pipe, variable lighting that shifts with every inch of camera travel. Nobody has assembled a million-image training dataset of residential lateral defects the way the Sewer-ML dataset exists for municipal mains, and the economic incentives haven't been there, either, because SewerAI charges municipalities that manage thousands of miles of pipe while a homebuyer needs one 100-foot inspection every decade or two.

But the economics of getting it wrong are savage.

A missed root ball in a clay lateral doesn't announce itself politely. It announces itself at 3 AM when sewage backs up through your basement floor drain and saturates the carpet, the drywall, and the insulation in a finished basement you spent $40,000 building out. Cleanup runs $5,000 to $15,000, and the pipe repair behind it is $4,000 to $15,000 for trenchless lining, or $15,000 to $30,000 or more if the pipe has collapsed and requires excavation through your driveway. Your homeowner's insurance, in most standard policies, does not cover sewer line backups unless you purchased a separate endorsement.

What You Should Demand From a Sewer Scope

Until AI-assisted residential sewer inspection becomes standard, and it will, though nobody can promise when, here is what you should insist on when paying for a scope:

Get the video, not a summary, not a verbal walkthrough on the phone, but the full recorded footage with a counter showing distance from the access point. If the technician won't provide a recording, hire a different technician, because the video is evidence and the narration is just opinion.

Ask what pipe material is in the ground. Orangeburg pipe, a bituminized fiber product manufactured from the 1940s through the 1970s, is a deal-breaker. It has a 30-to-50-year lifespan, which means every foot of Orangeburg still in service is past its design life. Cast iron in laterals older than 60 years is nearly as risky. PVC, installed correctly, can last 100 years. Knowing the material alone changes the risk calculation.

Ask for a condition assessment that goes beyond "looks okay." Demand specifics: are there offset joints, and how severe? Root intrusion, and at what footage mark? Is the pipe bellied (sagging), and if so, does standing water accumulate in the low point? Are there cracks, and are they circumferential (less urgent) or longitudinal (more urgent, because longitudinal cracks indicate the pipe is under external load it can't handle)? How many defects total, where are they, and what is the severity of each?

If your inspector uses NASSCO LACP codes, that's excellent. LACP, the Lateral Assessment Certification Program, is the residential equivalent of PACP, though vanishingly few residential plumbers hold the certification. A LACP-coded report is structured, standardized, and comparable. A verbal "it looked pretty good" is not.

Get a second opinion. A $275 sewer scope is trivial next to a $13,000 lateral replacement that a different technician might tell you isn't necessary yet. If one plumber says "replace the whole thing" and another says "monitor and re-scope in two years," you need to understand why their assessments diverge. Often it's because the first plumber also does the replacement work.

Where This Goes Next

Sewer-YOLO-Slim runs on 4.9 megabytes, and a residential push camera with a decent sensor and an onboard processor could run defect classification in real time, overlaying severity codes on the live video as the camera travels through the pipe. A technician would still push the camera, but the AI would handle the grading, and suddenly consistency, objectivity, and a structured digital record replace what has always been narrated opinion.

A company that packages that capability into a residential-grade push camera at the $3,000 to $5,000 price point, affordable for any plumbing firm that does sewer scopes, would transform an industry that has not meaningfully changed since the technology shifted from analog to digital video in the early 2000s. SewerAI's platform already processes lateral inspections for utilities that have taken ownership of their residents' service lines, and the technical barrier to running those same models on residential-grade equipment shrinks every quarter as edge computing gets cheaper and the training datasets get richer.

For builders, the immediate lesson is simpler: install accessible cleanouts. A two-way cleanout at the foundation wall and another at or near the property line gives future inspection cameras a clear shot through the entire lateral, and the cost during construction is under $200 in materials and 30 minutes of labor. Retrofitting a cleanout into an existing home runs $1,000 to $2,500, sometimes requiring concrete cutting. Most jurisdictions require at least one cleanout, but code minimums are designed for maintenance access, not for inspection quality, so two cleanouts placed for camera access is the right call even when code doesn't demand the second one.

What This Article Did Not Prove

SewerAI's 99% accuracy claim is the company's own figure, sourced from their marketing materials, and it has not been independently audited in a peer-reviewed publication that I can find. Accuracy metrics in AI defect detection are also context-dependent: 99% accuracy on a well-lit 12-inch municipal main with a $50,000 crawler camera is a different achievement than 99% accuracy on a water-logged 4-inch residential lateral filmed with a $2,000 push camera. Whether the municipal models transfer directly to residential conditions without retraining is an open and significant question.

Minneapolis city data published in January 2026 puts the average lateral replacement at $13,000, but costs vary dramatically by geography, pipe material, depth, length, surface conditions (under a driveway vs. under a lawn), and local labor rates. In high-cost metro areas, a lateral replacement through a concrete driveway can exceed $40,000.

No residential-grade push camera with onboard AI defect classification currently exists as a commercial product, to my knowledge, and the technology described here (models like Sewer-YOLO-Slim and embedded TinyML systems) exists in research papers and lab prototypes. Bridging the gap between a working prototype and a commercial product that a plumber buys from a supply house takes years and venture capital, not just technical feasibility.

I have no data on how often residential sewer scope reports produce inconsistent diagnoses between different technicians inspecting the same pipe. Subjectivity in pipe assessment is real and widely acknowledged in industry literature, but quantifying it would require a controlled study where multiple inspectors independently scope the same laterals and their reports are compared. That study, as far as I can tell, hasn't been done for residential laterals, though it has been done for municipal mains, and the results were bad enough to drive the adoption of AI coding in the first place.
