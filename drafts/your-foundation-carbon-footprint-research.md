# Research: Your Foundation's Carbon Footprint — The Tool Your Builder Doesn't Know Exists

## Angle
The EC3 tool (Embodied Carbon in Construction Calculator) by Building Transparency is free, open-source, and contains 300,000+ verified Environmental Product Declarations (EPDs) for concrete and other building materials. It lets anyone — including residential home builders and homeowners — compare the carbon footprint of ready-mix concrete suppliers in their area. The problem: almost nobody in residential construction uses it. The commercial sector has adopted it for Buy Clean compliance, but the residential market is still ordering concrete by strength class alone, blind to the 2-3x variation in embodied carbon between suppliers for the same 4,000 PSI mix.

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? YES — a homebuyer or builder could use EC3 to choose a lower-carbon concrete supplier for their foundation at zero cost premium (or sometimes cost savings), cutting 30-50% of their foundation's embodied carbon. Concrete is typically 30-40% of a new home's embodied carbon.

## Primary Sources

### 1. RMI — "The Hidden Climate Impact of Residential Construction" (2023)
- New US home construction: 50+ million tons of embodied carbon annually
- Equivalent to emissions from 138 natural gas power plants
- Average CtG embodied carbon: ~184 kg CO2e/m² of conditioned floor area
- 30-50% reductions achievable with commercially available, affordable, code-compliant materials
- Concrete, insulation, cladding, and interior surfaces = 70%+ of total embodied emissions
- Source: https://rmi.org/insight/hidden-climate-impact-of-residential-construction/

### 2. NRMCA Industry Average EPD & Regional Benchmarks
- National average: ~288 kg CO2e per cubic yard for 5,000 PSI ready-mix concrete
- Regional variation is massive — Pacific Southwest vs. Northeast can differ 20-30%
- Source: NRMCA EPD v3.2 (2023), referenced by build-news.com
- Key insight: within the SAME region, supplier variation for same strength class can be 2-3x

### 3. EC3 Tool — Building Transparency (free, open-source)
- 300,000+ verified carbon factor datapoints
- Enables zip-code-level comparison of concrete suppliers by GWP (global warming potential)
- Free, cloud-based, no certification required
- Integrates with BIM but also works standalone for simple material lookups
- Originally built for commercial/infrastructure but works for residential
- Source: https://www.buildingtransparency.org/tools/ec3/

### 4. Buildcheck.ai — AI-Driven Carbon Tracking (2026)
- Embodied carbon can represent ~50% of whole lifecycle emissions in new energy-efficient buildings
- One Click LCA: 300,000+ verified construction LCA datapoints, used in 170+ countries
- 81% of manufacturers cite EPD complexity as barrier
- AI tools now achieving 10x better data mapping for EPDs
- Gensler's gBlox.CO2 tool enables real-time carbon assessment during early design
- Source: https://buildcheck.ai/insights-case-studies/ai-driven-carbon-tracking-2026s-construction-inflection-point

### 5. Low-Carbon Concrete Strategies
- GGBS (ground granulated blast-furnace slag) can replace up to 80% of Portland cement → 70% CO2 reduction
- Fly ash replacement: 20-35% typical range
- CarbonCure CO2 injection: 5-25% carbon reduction
- Low-carbon concrete often costs the same or less than conventional (SCMs like fly ash and slag are cheaper than Portland cement)
- Source: build-news.com analysis, lowercarbonconcrete.org task force resources

### 6. Buy Clean California Act (AB 262) & CALGreen 2026
- State procurement of concrete now requires EPDs and GWP limits
- CALGreen 2026 updates focus on embodied carbon reporting
- Currently targets public projects, but residential spillover is beginning
- Colorado, New York, Oregon also have Buy Clean policies
- Source: Various state policy documents

## Novel Contribution
A cost analysis nobody's done: comparing the actual per-yard cost of low-GWP concrete vs. conventional for a typical residential foundation (~50-70 cubic yards), using EC3 data for a specific metro area. The insight is that in many regions, the lowest-carbon option is ALSO the cheapest because fly ash and slag are cheaper than Portland cement — you're not paying a premium, you're potentially saving money while cutting 30-50% of embodied carbon.

## Strongest Counterargument
Residential builders don't specify concrete by EPD because: (1) their structural engineers spec by strength class (4,000 PSI), (2) ready-mix dispatch sends whoever is closest, (3) fly ash and slag slow early strength gain (14-day vs 7-day), which can slow formwork stripping schedules, (4) Buy Clean doesn't apply to residential, and (5) homebuyers don't ask.
