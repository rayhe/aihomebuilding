# Research: AI Permit Review — Denver's 37% First-Try Approval Rate

## Topic
AI-powered building permit pre-review tools are being deployed in major U.S. cities to fix the permit bottleneck. Denver's data is the headline: only 37% of applications were approved on first try. An AI tool is targeting 80%. But adoption remains limited to a few dozen cities, and the barriers are political, not technical.

## Primary Sources

### 1. NAHB Government Regulation Study (2026)
- Source: NAHB, "Government Regulation in the Price of a New Home: 2026"
- Cited by: Realtor.com (Jun 2026), MPA Magazine (Jun 2026), White House ERP (Apr 2026)
- Key data:
  - **$131,734** in regulatory costs per new home (26.4% of $499,500 avg sale price)
  - Building code changes alone: $40,288/home (8.1%)
  - Permit, inspection, hook-up fees: $20,154/home (4.0%)
  - **94.2%** of developers reported regulatory delays averaging ~7 months
  - **93.4%** of builders reported delays averaging ~6 weeks
  - Regulatory costs jumped ~40% in 5 years
  - Pure cost of delay (dev + construction): $4,112/home
- White House ERP cited: "29.5% of new home cost is regulatory burden"
- NAHB + NMHC joint study: 41% for multifamily

### 2. Denver / CivCheck (2026)
- Source: PYMNTS (May 2026), Denver7, Route Fifty, Denver Gazette
- Key data:
  - Denver approved only **37%** of permit applications on first try
  - AI target: **80%** first-try approval
  - **$4.6M**, 5-year contract with CivCheck (Clariti)
  - CivCheck flags missing documents, incomplete fields, application errors BEFORE plans reach human reviewers
  - Denver cut Community Planning & Development dept by **59 positions** (now 251 total)
  - Already cut single-family/duplex processing time by **45%** since 2023
  - **180-day shot clock** for permit decisions; $10,000 refund if missed
  - Quote from Julia Richman (Clariti VP): "Most plan review delays start upstream, when submissions enter the queue incomplete or inconsistent"

### 3. Honolulu / CivCheck
- Source: Spectrum News (Dec 2025)
- Launched CivCheck for residential permits Dec 2025
- Commercial projects follow by mid-2026
- Checks code compliance and document completeness at submission

### 4. Seattle
- Source: GeekWire (Jun 2026)
- Mayor Bruce Harrell signed executive order directing ALL development applications through AI pilot
- Dedicated "Permitting and Customer Trust" team
- Full public rollout expected 2026

### 5. Austin / Archistar
- Source: HousingWire
- Archistar "eCheck" for single-family home zoning review
- Instant parsing by computer vision and machine learning
- Maps setbacks, heights, lot coverage, window placement, energy specs against city zoning/building regs
- Produces compliance report in minutes

### 6. Spacial ($10M raise)
- Source: Diginomica (Jun 2026)
- Raised $10M for AI code compliance
- 7-10 days from design to stamped plans (vs weeks/months)
- ~25% reduction in approval time
- Engineers-in-the-loop approach — AI proposes, engineer verifies, customer gets stamped result
- CEO Maor Greenberg: "Building codes aren't digital-friendly. They are legal documents written in ambiguous, jurisdiction-specific language."
- Hybrid approach: NLP + classification + human QA. Automate high-confidence rules, flag rest for judgment

### 7. UpCodes AI Plan Review
- Source: PRNewswire (Jun 2026)
- 11 million code sections across 6,000+ jurisdictions
- Discipline-specific analysis: architecture, structural, MEP, fire protection, life safety, accessibility, energy
- Returns issues by severity/category, linked to drawing page and code section
- Export reports for sharing with consultants/clients/officials

### 8. AutoReview.AI (University of Florida)
- Source: Warrington UF (Mar 2026)
- Patent-pending, decade of research
- Based on full Florida Building Code (800+ pages)
- First US system to use AI for automated site/building plan code checking
- Landscape plan processing with annotated non-conforming items

### 9. Jeff Bezos advocacy
- Source: Independent Institute (Mar 2026), Scott Beyer
- Bezos at business forum: cities should use AI to review permits and deliver yes/no in seconds
- If rejected, system should identify exact changes needed

### 10. Political barriers
- Source: Independent Institute analysis
  - Faster approvals could reduce staffing needs, budgets, institutional influence
  - Existing homeowners prefer slower growth — lengthy permitting stifles production "discreetly"
  - NIMBYism without having to publicly voice opposition
  - "The gap between what is possible and what actually gets implemented is driven by incentives, not capability"

## Original Contribution
Cross-referencing Denver's 37% first-try approval rate against the NAHB delay cost data: if AI can get first-try approval from 37% to 80%, that eliminates 43 percentage points of applications that currently cycle through resubmission. Each resubmission cycle in Denver adds an estimated 2-4 weeks. For a city processing ~15,000 permit applications annually, that's potentially hundreds of thousands of aggregate reviewer-hours redirected. But Denver simultaneously cut 59 positions — raising the question: is this efficiency gain being passed to builders, or used to shrink departments?

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? YES — directly. If you're building a home or addition, your permit timeline is the single biggest variable outside construction itself. Knowing which cities have AI pre-review, what it catches, and what it doesn't tells you whether to expect weeks or months in the queue.
