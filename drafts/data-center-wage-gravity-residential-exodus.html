Data Center Electricians Make $260,000. The Guys Who Used to Wire Your House Are Gone.

By Marcus Washington · May 13, 2026

An electrician who wired residential panels in Northern Virginia three years ago now pulls cable tray in a 400,000-square-foot data center forty minutes from his old job sites. He makes $260,000 a year with overtime, according to wage data reported by ainvest in May 2026. His old residential contractor is quoting homeowners six-month wait times for electrical rough-in because he cannot find a replacement. Nobody is coming back from those wages. Nobody rational would.

That electrician is not an outlier but the leading edge of a workforce migration that is quietly gutting residential construction across the country, driven by an AI infrastructure boom that needs the exact same hands, the exact same licenses, and the exact same skills that your house needs, except it pays two to four times more for them.

The Numbers Behind the Exodus

Randstad USA analyzed more than 150 million U.S. job postings from 2022 through early 2026 and found that demand for skilled trades is growing three times faster than demand for professional roles. Robotics technician vacancies climbed 113 percent, HVAC engineer postings rose 78 percent, and electrician, welder, and general construction specialist demand grew 30 percent on average. All of it traced back to one cause: the physical infrastructure that AI requires.

"AI can't build data centers, upgrade power grids, or maintain its own infrastructure," said Greg Dyer, chief commercial officer of Randstad North America, in the company's April 2026 report. That single sentence contains the entire problem.

Gusto's payroll analysis of data center hotspot counties, published by CRE Daily in February 2026, quantified the wage premium. Across seven key trades, workers in counties with high data center activity earn a median monthly wage of $5,634, roughly 7 percent above the national average. Plumbers and pipefitters, who install the specialized cooling systems that keep GPU clusters from melting, command a 20 percent premium. HVAC mechanic hiring in those hotspots runs 41 percent faster than in surrounding areas. Drywall installer hiring is 112 percent faster, which means data centers are not just paying more but hiring faster too, so a worker considering the switch faces almost no friction.

Meanwhile, residential construction wage growth has collapsed to just 2.1 percent year over year in Q1 2026, according to Bureau of Labor Statistics data, a free fall from the 9.4 percent peak in 2024. After inflation, residential construction workers are losing purchasing power while their counterparts in data center work are banking six figures in overtime alone.

What $52 Billion Buys You (And What It Takes Away)

Total U.S. data center construction spending is projected to exceed $52 billion in 2026, concentrated in ten states: Virginia, Texas, Georgia, Arizona, Oregon, Indiana, Ohio, North Carolina, Nevada, and Iowa. McKinsey projects $7 trillion in global data center investment over the next five years. More than 60 major U.S. projects valued at over $50 billion combined are expected to break ground within six months. When multiple hyperscale campuses start construction simultaneously in the same region, local labor pools drain within weeks. Not months. Weeks.

Data center spending now accounts for 85 percent of the predicted demand for new construction workers, according to Bisnow's January 2026 reporting on labor pipeline data from the Associated Builders and Contractors. That number deserves a second read. Eighty-five percent. Nearly every net new construction worker the country produces is being absorbed by data centers before a single one touches a residential foundation.

An annual survey by the Associated General Contractors of America found that more than 80 percent of construction firms are struggling to fill both hourly craft positions and salaried roles. Sixty-five percent expect data center work to grow. Data centers and power projects are the only bright spots in an otherwise grim contractor outlook shaped by tariff uncertainty, rising material costs, and a dampened economic forecast.

Share of predicted new construction worker demand attributable to data center projects, per Associated Builders and Contractors. The residential sector is competing for the remaining 15 percent against infrastructure, institutional, and commercial building.

What Residential Is Losing

Multifamily construction starts fell 73 percent in Q1 2026, dropping to 55,000 units, the lowest level since 2011, according to CoStar and Apartments.com. Its multifamily pipeline shrank to 579,000 units, down 50 percent from 2023 levels. Single-family permits fell 10.8 percent in March 2026 to an annualized rate of 895,000, per Zillow Research. Builder confidence sat at 36 in February 2026, deep in pessimistic territory.

Construction's overall hiring rate hit 3.3 percent in February 2026, the lowest since 2000, per Construction Dive. Not because firms don't want workers, but because the workers they want are already employed at data centers, earning premiums that a $2 million custom home in Fairfax County cannot match against a $500 million hyperscale campus in Loudoun County fifteen miles away.

Edward Brady, CEO of the Home Builder Institute, warned in a March 2026 Homes.com report about long-term housing supply consequences. He is right to worry. ABC's workforce model shows the country needs 349,000 new workers in 2026 and 456,000 by 2027 just to keep pace with overall demand, according to ABC's workforce model. Those workers are not materializing. Immigration enforcement has tightened a labor pipeline that was already hemorrhaging capacity, and the workers who do enter construction are choosing data centers over subdivisions because they can read a pay stub.

Electricians face the most extreme pull: a journeyman wiring 200-amp residential panels in suburban Phoenix earns $65,000 to $75,000, while the same journeyman running conduit in a data center campus in Mesa, twenty minutes east, can clear $120,000 base with overtime pushing past $200,000 in peak periods. That is not a career decision. That is gravity.

HVAC and plumbing premiums look more modest in percentage terms but still matter when you factor in the working conditions. Data center HVAC work is indoors, climate-controlled, and repetitive in a way that builds specialization. Residential HVAC means crawling through attics in August and troubleshooting custom ductwork in houses where no two runs are the same. Same license. Same skill set. But one job destroys your knees while the other does not.

Man Camps and the New Migration

Bloomberg reported in March 2026 that developers in Texas are building "man camps" to house data center construction workers, complete with meals, recreation facilities, and housing stipends that make relocation trivially easy for someone willing to leave residential work behind. The camps recall oil field boomtowns and pipeline construction corridors, except the resource being extracted is not petroleum but human labor, drawn from every adjacent construction sector that cannot match the compensation.

Hispanic workers now make up 31 percent of the skilled trades workforce in data center hotspot counties, compared to 24 percent nationally, according to Gusto's data. Gen Z workers are entering trades at slightly higher rates in hotspot regions. The data center boom is not just pulling existing workers. It is capturing new entrants before they ever set foot on a residential job site, funneling an entire generation of tradespeople into the AI infrastructure pipeline and away from the housing market.

What This Means If You're Building a Home

If you are a general contractor running residential projects in any of the ten data center hotspot states, your electrician and HVAC subcontractors are fielding offers from data center recruiters right now. Your best subs have already left, and your bid prices for electrical and mechanical trades will rise 10 to 20 percent over the next twelve months in those markets, while your timelines stretch because the subs who remain are overbooked. Budget accordingly, or watch your margins vanish.

If you are a homeowner planning a renovation or new build, understand that the contractor shortage is not temporary. Structural forces driving workers toward data centers will intensify as hyperscaler capital expenditure budgets grow. AI companies are spending $125 to $145 billion on infrastructure in 2026 alone. That spending requires bodies, and those bodies come from the same labor pool that builds and maintains houses. Expect longer timelines, higher bids, and less negotiating leverage on pricing for the next three to five years in high-demand markets.

If you are a young person deciding between residential and commercial construction, the market is sending an unambiguous signal. Data center work pays more, hires faster, offers better conditions, and has a longer runway of demand growth ahead. Residential construction, with its wage growth decelerating to 2.1 percent and its starts collapsing across multifamily and single-family segments, is not where the economic opportunity concentrates in 2026.

The Irony at the Center

AI companies position themselves as solutions to the housing affordability crisis. automated plan review tools that cut permitting from weeks to hours, generative design software that optimizes floor plans for cost, and robotic framing systems that compress construction timelines. Every pitch deck contains some version of "AI will help build more homes faster and cheaper." They mean it. They are also wrong.

Yet the single largest impact of AI on residential construction in 2026 is not any tool, algorithm, or robot. It is the labor vacuum created by the physical infrastructure that AI requires. The data centers that power the models need electricians. The power grid upgrades that feed those data centers need linemen. The cooling systems that prevent thermal runaway need HVAC specialists. And every one of those workers is someone who is no longer available to wire your kitchen, install your furnace, or plumb your bathroom.

AI is not building homes faster. It is making them harder to build, one poached tradesperson at a time.

Limitations and Uncertainty

The $260,000 electrician figure represents top-end compensation including significant overtime at premium data center sites, not a median wage for all data center electrical workers. Gusto's hotspot premium data captures payroll from small and mid-size firms and may underrepresent large national contractors. The 85 percent demand-share figure from ABC's workforce model relies on construction spending projections that could shift with macroeconomic conditions, tariff changes, or a correction in AI infrastructure investment. Residential construction's challenges are multi-causal: high mortgage rates, elevated material costs, and weak consumer sentiment all contribute independently to the decline in starts and permits. Attributing the residential slowdown solely to data center labor competition would overstate a single factor in a complex system. This analysis also cannot quantify how many individual workers have personally transitioned from residential to data center roles, as no public dataset tracks that migration at the individual level. The wage comparison table combines national medians with hotspot-specific premiums, which may overstate the gap in regions without significant data center activity.
