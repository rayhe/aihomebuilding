# Research Notes: AI Aerial Roof Scoring and Insurance Non-Renewals

## Angle
Insurance companies are using AI-analyzed aerial and satellite imagery to grade homeowner roofs and cancel policies — often without the homeowner knowing they were evaluated, seeing the images, or being given a chance to contest. This is a consumer rights and regulatory gap story.

## Journalist
Catherine "Code" Chen — Policy & Regulation

## Key Sources

### 1. Cape Analytics (now a Moody's company)
- Database: 110 million+ residential buildings in the US (now 98% US coverage)
- Product: Roof Condition Rating (RCR), now v5 (launched Feb 2025)
- Adopted by 100+ carriers, 300+ approved rating/underwriting filings across 40+ states
- Uses geospatial imagery, computer vision, deep learning
- Detects: missing shingles, tarps, discoloration, material degradation, ponding, patching
- RCR v5 adds "reason codes" and "confidence scores" for transparency
- Founded 2016, pioneered aerial roof assessment in insurance
- Source: capeanalytics.com press releases, case studies

### 2. State Farm / Accenture FME Program (Oklahoma Lawsuit)
- Hursh v. State Farm (Oklahoma County, Judge Amy Palumbo)
- Plaintiff: Tulsa police officer, denied roof hail damage claim
- Allegation: State Farm hired Accenture to create "Fire Model Enhancement" (FME) program
- FME allegedly created artificial industry standards to justify denying full roof replacement claims
- Impact: Reduced wind/hail claim payments by ~$1.4 billion in 2021 (first year of FME)
- Oklahoma Supreme Court denied State Farm's writ, discovery proceeding
- Source: The Oklahoman, July 8, 2025

### 3. Homeowner Cases
- **Cindy Picos (California):** Dropped by CSAA Insurance based on aerial photo showing "roof on last legs." Independent in-person inspection found 10+ years of life remaining. CSAA didn't change its mind. (Source: WSJ, The Register)
- **Linda Bennett (California):** Lived in home since 1993. State Farm demanded $28,290 roof replacement based on aerial imagery. No inspector ever visited or climbed the roof. (Source: realestate.com.au, ABC7)
- **Alaina Callahan (Houston area):** Insurer demanded new roof — roof was only 5 years old and in good condition. Asked to see aerial photos; never got to see them. "I had no recourse as a homeowner. None whatsoever." (Source: NPR)
- **12+ homeowners** complained to Texas DOI since 2023

### 4. Industry Infrastructure
- **Geospatial Insurance Consortium (GIC):** Provides aerial imagery to insurers, brags about "stunningly clear imagery" and post-catastrophe coverage
- **Vexcel:** Partner of GIC, provides AI-based analysis of aerial imagery
- **Nearmap, Verisk, Betterview:** Other major players in geospatial property intelligence

### 5. Regulatory Landscape
- Five Texas insurers confirmed using third-party aerial photos + AI to state filings
- No federal regulation specifically governing AI-based property assessment in insurance
- Some states considering transparency requirements
- Consumer Federation of America (Doug Heller): "Using images gathered without consumer awareness, or let alone consent, is really problematic"

### 6. Due Process Gap
- Homeowners often can't see the images used against them
- No standardized appeal process
- AI models have known issues: tree cover, shadow, wrong property identification
- Cape Analytics acknowledges need for "confidence scores" and "reason codes" — implying prior versions lacked them
- One Texas case: insurer looked at the wrong image entirely

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home?
YES — absolutely. If you're buying a home, an AI may have already scored the roof before you even know about it, affecting your insurance options and costs. If you own a home, you may be getting non-renewed based on data you've never seen. This article tells you what's happening, what your rights are, and what to do about it.

## Original Contribution
Cross-reference analysis: Cape Analytics' own transparency improvements (confidence scores, reason codes in v5) implicitly acknowledge the accuracy problems homeowners have experienced. The fact that it took 8 years (2016-2025) to add explainability features to a tool being used to cancel policies is itself a finding worth highlighting.

## Strongest Counterargument
AI roof assessment genuinely prevents losses. Bad roofs cause real claims. In-person inspections are expensive, slow, and sometimes dangerous (climbing roofs). If AI can identify genuinely dangerous conditions faster and cheaper, the technology isn't the problem — the lack of transparency and due process is.
