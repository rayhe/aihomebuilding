# Research: Water & Sewer Capacity Gaps in New Subdivisions + AI Modeling

## Journalist: Priya Greenwood (Sustainability & Green Building)

## Angle
Your new subdivision was approved by a city that can't tell you whether the sewer pipes under your street have enough capacity for 200 more homes. AI hydraulic modeling tools exist — most municipalities don't use them for residential approvals. Cities are issuing building permits into infrastructure they haven't modeled.

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? **YES** — homebuyers need to know if their new subdivision's water/sewer infrastructure can handle the load. Builders face moratorium risk and capacity surcharges. This is money-on-the-line actionable.

- Causes: blockages, line breaks, sewer defects allowing stormwater/groundwater overload, power failures, **improper sewer design**
- Source: https://www.epa.gov/npdes/sanitary-sewer-overflows-ssos

### 6. AI Hydraulic Modeling Tools
- **Autodesk Innovyze InfoWorks ICM**: Sewer/stormwater modeling, capacity analysis, what-if scenarios for new development
- **Autodesk InfoWater Pro**: Water distribution system modeling

## Novel Analysis
**The capacity-blind permit problem**: Cross-reference the pattern — cities issuing the most residential building permits (Houston, DFW, Phoenix, Atlanta) have infrastructure grades of C- to D+ from ASCE. Most residential building permits are approved with a simple "will-serve" letter from the utility, which confirms the utility *intends* to provide service, not that it has *modeled whether the system can handle it*. A will-serve letter is a promise, not an engineering analysis.

**Original calculation**: If EPA estimates $625.5B in drinking water infrastructure needs across ~52,000 community water systems, that's ~$12M per system over 20 years, or ~$600K/year. The average residential water impact fee is $3,000-$8,000 per connection. For a 200-home subdivision, that's $600K-$1.6M in impact fees — but if the downstream trunk sewer needs a $5M upgrade, the fees don't cover it. The gap gets pushed to existing ratepayers or deferred entirely.

**The AI fix that exists but isn't used**: Innovyze/Autodesk can model the impact of a proposed 200-home subdivision on an existing sewer shed in under an hour. The software costs $10,000-$30,000/year. For a city issuing 1,000+ residential permits annually, that's $10-$30 per permit. Almost no city requires this for residential approvals.

## Strongest Counterargument
Municipal engineers argue that will-serve letters are backed by capital improvement plans (CIPs) that account for future growth. Many cities do update comprehensive plans with projected water/sewer demand. The question is whether those projections use actual hydraulic modeling (rare for residential) or population-growth extrapolations (common but less accurate). AI modeling is overkill for a 5-lot cul-de-sac; the question is where the threshold should be.

## Limitations
- Infrastructure grades are aggregate (state/national level); individual utility systems vary enormously
- Will-serve letter practices vary by jurisdiction — some cities DO require hydraulic analysis for large subdivisions
- EPA's SSO estimate range (23,000-75,000) is extremely wide, reflecting reporting gaps
- We don't have per-metro data linking permit volume to infrastructure capacity directly — the connection is inferred from aggregate grade data
- AI modeling tools are designed for trained engineers; they're not turnkey for permit reviewers

## Actionable Takeaways
1. **Homebuyers**: Before buying in a new subdivision, request the utility's will-serve letter AND ask what capital improvement projects are planned for your area. Check if your city has a published CIP online.
2. **Builders**: Ask the municipality whether they've done hydraulic modeling for the sewer shed your subdivision feeds into. If they haven't, consider commissioning your own — it's cheaper than a moratorium stopping your project.
3. **If your city has a published infrastructure grade from ASCE state reports**: Check it. C- or below for water/wastewater means the system is already strained.
4. **Watch for moratorium patterns**: If neighboring jurisdictions have imposed moratoriums, yours may be next.
