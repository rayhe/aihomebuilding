BlackRock Will Spend $100 Million Training Electricians. None of Them Are Wiring Your Kitchen.

By Marcus Washington • June 4, 2026

On June 1, BlackRock opened a $25 million request for proposals to fund skilled trades training across the United States. It is part of a larger commitment called Future Builders, a $100 million philanthropic initiative announced in March that aims to train 50,000 electricians, plumbers, HVAC technicians, and ironworkers over five years. The grants, administered through Jobs for the Future, range from $500,000 to $1 million per nonprofit recipient, with applications open through July 10.

That is a lot of money pointed at a real problem. Read the press release and it sounds like corporate altruism on a grand scale, the kind of thing that draws bipartisan applause and gets Larry Fink invited to workforce summits.

Read the balance sheet and the picture shifts.

$2,000
Approximate per-worker spend if BlackRock's $100 million reaches all 50,000 targeted trainees. A union electrical apprenticeship costs $40,000 to $80,000 over four to five years. BlackRock's money supplements existing programs; it doesn't fund standalone training.

BlackRock manages more than $11 trillion in assets. Its infrastructure funds have become, in the words of Engineering News-Record, "a key financial engine" for the sector, backing projects like Meta's estimated $27 billion Hyperion data center in Louisiana, a 2,250-acre campus targeted to begin operations in 2030. That single project will consume thousands of skilled tradespeople for years. Fink has publicly stated that America needs $10 trillion in infrastructure investment by 2033 to modernize aging systems and build the energy, digital, and AI backbone the economy increasingly depends on.

Capital alone cannot meet that demand. You need hands.

Follow the Money Backward

Here is what $100 million means to BlackRock. Assume the firm's infrastructure-focused funds hold roughly $40 billion in assets generating returns in the neighborhood of 8 to 12 percent annually. That translates to $3.2 billion to $4.8 billion in yearly earnings attributable to infrastructure alone. The Future Builders spend, spread over five years, works out to about $20 million a year. That is less than half a percent of the annual returns the training program exists to protect.

I have covered workforce development in construction for a long time, and the programs that work share a common trait: they are funded by the people who need the workers, not by the people who need the tax write-off. BlackRock is not confused about this distinction. Ruth Porat, the president and chief investment officer of Alphabet, endorsed Future Builders by noting that "building the infrastructure to secure America's continued leadership in AI is creating thousands of high skilled trade jobs." Google, which has its own electrical worker training program, benefits from the same pipeline BlackRock is funding. Alphabet is also a BlackRock client. The incentives nest inside each other like matryoshka dolls.

The Numbers That Matter for Your House

The construction industry needs roughly 500,000 additional workers by 2030, according to a Reuters analysis of Bureau of Labor Statistics projections. Forty-one percent of the current workforce is eligible for retirement within the next five years. There are 400,000 open trade jobs right now, per Home Depot's Path to Pro workforce data. Utilities alone expect to spend more than a trillion dollars on grid upgrades in the near term, each project drawing from the same labor pool that residential builders depend on.

Those numbers look abstract until you are the homeowner who called three electricians in April and got one callback in June, with a quote 40 percent higher than the same work cost two years ago. The skilled trades shortage is not new, but the demand profile has fundamentally changed. Data centers and grid infrastructure are sucking tradespeople out of the residential market at a pace that philanthropic training programs, however large, are not designed to offset. BlackRock's Future Builders explicitly targets "critical infrastructure." The Texas pilot committed $30 million to train 12,000 workers for electrical careers, but the state's booming data center corridor stretching from Dallas through San Antonio is the gravitational center of that investment, not the kitchen remodel in a subdivision outside Austin.

5%+
Projected annual employment growth in infrastructure-related skilled trades over the next decade, per Engineering News-Record. The national average across all occupations is 3%. The differential pulls workers toward the bigger paychecks.

The AI Literacy Layer

Future Builders is not the only program targeting tradespeople. Microsoft and North America's Building Trades Unions announced an expanded partnership in April that has already trained 1,500 instructors and is now rolling out free AI literacy courses through LinkedIn Learning, with an industry-recognized credential attached. The program reaches into 34 states through TradesFutures apprenticeship readiness programs. Microsoft's framing is explicit: "Community-First AI Infrastructure commitments," designed to ensure that communities where new infrastructure is built benefit from the jobs created.

The word "residential" does not appear in any of these announcements. Not once.

I want to be precise about what I am saying and what I am not. Trained workers are fungible. An electrician who learns to pull wire through conduit at a data center training site can absolutely go wire your kitchen. Nothing in BlackRock's grant terms requires trainees to work on specific project types after they get licensed, and more workers entering the trades is better than fewer workers entering the trades, full stop. If Future Builders succeeds at its 50,000-worker target and even a quarter of those workers drift into residential, that is 12,500 new tradespeople in a market that desperately needs them.

But the Drift Goes the Other Way

Residential construction has always lost tradespeople to commercial and industrial work when those sectors boom. The pay differential is structural. A journeyman electrician on a data center project in northern Virginia or central Texas can earn $38 to $45 an hour with overtime guarantees and multi-year project stability, according to trade union rate sheets and job postings reviewed for this article. The same electrician doing residential service calls and panel upgrades in a midsize metro area earns $28 to $35 an hour with inconsistent scheduling and windshield time between jobs. That gap does not require BlackRock to issue a directive for workers to avoid residential. It just requires gravity.

BlackRock's program also embeds "financial education and digital savings tools" into its training pipeline. This is BlackRock selling financial products, specifically its own savings and retirement platforms, to a captive audience of newly employed tradespeople. It is not hidden; it is stated in the press materials. The philanthropic arm of the world's largest asset manager is simultaneously creating workers for its portfolio companies and customers for its financial products. The efficiency is remarkable. Whether you call it synergy or vertical integration depends on how much credit you extend to stated intentions.

What This Means If You Are Building or Remodeling

Plan for longer timelines and higher labor costs, especially for electrical and HVAC work. The tradespeople you need are not vanishing. They are being recruited, trained, and compensated by entities with vastly more purchasing power than a residential general contractor can offer. If your project requires specialized electrical work, such as a 200-amp panel upgrade for an EV charger or a full rewire of a pre-1970s home, get quotes early and be prepared to schedule months out. If you can bundle electrical, HVAC, and plumbing work into a single subcontract, you become a more attractive job for a small crew that would otherwise chase individual service calls between larger projects.

The irony is that programs like Future Builders might eventually ease the shortage enough to help residential, but the time horizon is measured in years, not the months between now and your remodel start date. Fifty thousand workers over five years, assuming full success, adds roughly 10,000 new tradespeople per year to a market that is losing 40,000 or more annually to retirement. The math does not close. It narrows the gap, which is meaningful, but it does not solve it, and most of the narrowing benefits the sectors that funded the training.

Limitations

I should note what this analysis does not have. We do not have data on where Future Builders graduates will actually work after completing their training. The residential-versus-infrastructure split is inferred from compensation data and demand patterns, not measured directly. The $2,000-per-worker figure is approximate because some portion of the $100 million goes to JFF administration and program design rather than direct training costs. BlackRock's infrastructure AUM varies by reporting period; the $40 billion figure used in the return calculation is an approximation based on publicly available fund data and may understate or overstate the firm's total infrastructure exposure.

The strongest case against this framing is straightforward: more workers is better for everyone, and calling BlackRock's training spend "portfolio insurance" does not change the fact that 50,000 people who did not have skilled trades careers will now have access to one. That is true. It is also true that the entities writing the checks are not doing it for the sake of the homeowner who cannot get a plumber to return a call. Both things can coexist. The question is which one you build your remodel timeline around.
