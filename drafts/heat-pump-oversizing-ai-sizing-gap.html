Your Heat Pump Contractor Sized It With a Rule of Thumb. A Machine Gets It Right in Fifteen Minutes.

The average U.S. heating system is oversized by 2.3 times. Automated load calculation tools exist. Your contractor doesn't use one.

By Priya Greenwood July 23, 2026

Mike Simons has a number that keeps him up at night. As training and development manager at Abode Energy Management, a home-efficiency consultancy, he has watched HVAC contractors walk into houses and propose heat pump systems two, sometimes three times larger than the home requires. He's seen what that does to the quote. "What should cost $15,000 is now $40,000," he told Canary Media.

Standing in their kitchen, the homeowner has no way to know, because the contractor arrived with experience and a tape measure and the quiet authority of someone who does this every day. So they sign.

That signature locks in two losses, one upfront and one that compounds for years: thousands of dollars for capacity the home will never use, and an energy bill inflated by a system fighting its own physics, cycling on and off because it can't run slowly enough to match a house that doesn't need that much heat.

Machinery to prevent both losses exists, and it's neither exotic nor expensive: an HVAC contractor with a phone, a LiDAR sensor, and fifteen minutes can generate an ACCA-certified Manual J load calculation accounting for actual insulation, window area, orientation, local climate, and infiltration rate. Almost nobody uses it for residential work.

Too big by a factor of two

A study published in Nature Communications in 2025 put real numbers on the problem for the first time at scale. Researchers analyzed operational data from 637 residential heat pumps and found that between 7 and 11 percent showed clear signs of oversizing, a rate substantially higher than the 5 percent reported in prior inspection-based studies that relied on site visits rather than continuous monitoring data, with around 1 percent undersized.

Even that 7-to-11 range is conservative, because researchers used a threshold criterion based on utilization at the bivalent temperature, the outdoor temperature below which the heat pump needs backup heating. A heat pump running at only 50 percent utilization at that critical point is burning money. One running at 30 percent is an engineering embarrassment.

Across the broader HVAC industry, the numbers are worse. Energy management consultant Rick Karg, writing in the Journal of Light Construction, put the average oversizing factor for combustion heating systems in the United States at 2.3 times actual load, which is not 2.3 percent over or even 23 percent over but more than double what the home needs.

A 5 percent annual energy penalty is the conservative estimate for oversized gas systems, climbing to 10 percent for oil-fired equipment. Heat pumps suffer differently but no less: an oversized unit short-cycles, kicking on and off because it cannot modulate below its minimum output, burning a slug of electricity with every restart while the compressor wears at an accelerated pace. Humidity control collapses because the system never runs long enough for the evaporator coil to pull moisture from the air, which in a humid climate means a cold, clammy house and a homeowner who cranks the thermostat lower, driving the bill higher.

Follow the incentives

Ask why contractors oversize, and the answer lives in the economics of callbacks. An undersized system fails visibly: the house doesn't reach temperature on the coldest day, the homeowner calls, the contractor eats the cost of a return visit and sometimes a replacement. An oversized system fails invisibly, in the form of bills that run higher than they should, compressors that die two years early, and basements that feel damp. Nobody calls about damp, and nobody calls about an extra $40 a month, because nobody knows what the bill could have been.

Asymmetric risk explains the default: oversizing costs the homeowner while undersizing costs the contractor, so the industry sizes up every time.

A calculation exists that would eliminate the guesswork: ACCA Manual J, the Air Conditioning Contractors of America's residential load calculation methodology. It accounts for climate zone, building envelope, window characteristics, orientation, occupancy, infiltration rate, and duct losses. Performed correctly, it produces a heating and cooling load in BTU per hour that maps directly to equipment selection.

Most jurisdictions reference Manual J in their building codes, but most contractors do not perform one, with a significant portion still relying on square-footage rules of thumb that typically assign 20 BTU per square foot for cooling and some multiple for heating while ignoring every variable that actually determines load. Two identical 2,000-square-foot houses on the same street, one with R-38 attic insulation and double-pane low-E windows, the other with R-19 and single-pane, would get the same system under that rule.

Fifteen minutes with a phone

Conduit Tech, acquired by ServiceTitan, sells a product that turns a smartphone into a load calculation instrument. A contractor walks the home with LiDAR scanning, using the same depth-sensing technology built into an iPhone Pro, and builds a three-dimensional model of the space in minutes. Software automatically extracts room dimensions, window locations, wall areas, and ceiling heights, then generates an ACCA-certified Manual J report in about fifteen minutes complete with equipment recommendations cross-referenced against manufacturer databases.

Output includes augmented reality visualization: the homeowner can see the proposed outdoor unit sitting on the pad where it will go, scaled to actual size, before a single piece of equipment is ordered. Hard to propose a unit that dwarfs the pad when the customer is watching it materialize in their yard.

IES Virtual Environment goes deeper, running full ASHRAE Heat Balance Method calculations with sub-hourly time steps and modeling solar gain across all surfaces alongside thermal mass and construction materials. Over 100 pre-configured airside HVAC systems come built in. Designed for commercial buildings with 500-room floor plans, but the physics are identical. A competitor called cove.tool automates load modeling with rapid prototyping algorithms validated against ASHRAE Standard 140.

None of these are prototypes, but commercial products with paying customers, standards compliance, and years of field data behind them. Whether the technology works is not in question; why residential HVAC hasn't adopted it is.

Commercial got it right. Residential didn't notice.

Nobody builds a 200,000-square-foot office tower and guesses the HVAC tonnage. An engineer runs a detailed energy model, a mechanical designer sizes each zone, and a commissioning agent verifies that installed equipment matches the spec after construction. Layers of accountability exist because a million-dollar system that can't be swapped out demands them.

Residential construction has none of those layers, because one contractor serves as designer, installer, and commissioning agent while the homeowner serves as the quality assurance department, armed with no data and a thermostat.

A European research team published a climate-data-based sizing methodology for residential air-source heat pumps in Applied Thermal Engineering in 2024. It uses long-term local weather data alongside ambient air temperatures, peak heat demand, and seasonal heat demand to select the right unit without manual input most contractors skip. "By taking into account local weather variations and long-term trends, the tool ensures the economic viability of HP systems," the researchers wrote.

Meanwhile, the DOE's Building America program at Pacific Northwest National Laboratory has developed housing-characteristic-based design guidance accounting for a factor most sizing tools ignore entirely: existing ductwork capacity. ResStock modeling showed that both ACCA Manual S and Maximum Load approaches "neglect the airflow capacity of the existing ductwork." Install a properly sized heat pump into undersized ducts and the system can't deliver its rated output. Install an oversized one and the ducts can't handle the airflow, creating noise, turbulence, and comfort problems.

Neither approach has penetrated the average residential HVAC sales call.

What right-sizing actually looks like

A UK-based installer, Lloyd's Gas & Renewables, publishes real monitoring data from every heat pump it installs. In 2025, the firm released a full year of data from five residential properties.

A 94-square-meter detached home with a 4.8-kW heat loss drew 3,240 kWh of electricity for heating and hot water across the year, achieving a seasonal coefficient of performance (SCOP) of 4.5, meaning each unit of electricity produced 4.5 units of heat, at an annual cost of £590. A larger 167-square-meter detached home with 7.6-kW heat loss hit SCOP 5.3, highest in the group, at £1,002 per year.

Worst performer was a 150-square-meter home with 8.14-kW heat loss, managing SCOP 3.9 and costing £1,127 per year, and between SCOP 5.3 and SCOP 3.9 on similar-size homes the gap runs hundreds of pounds annually, compounding over a fifteen-to-twenty-year equipment life.

"A badly performing heat pump is almost always an installer problem, not a heat pump problem," the firm concluded. Sizing is the first installer decision and the hardest to undo.

Variable speed doesn't fix this

Industry's strongest defense against oversizing is the inverter-driven, variable-speed compressor, which allows modern heat pumps to modulate their output across a wide range so that a unit rated at 36,000 BTU/hr might throttle down to 6,550 BTU/hr when the load is light, eliminating short-cycling in theory. In practice, every variable-speed system has a minimum output floor that an egregiously oversized installation will still hit.

Put a 5-ton system in a house that needs 2 tons and it will short-cycle even with inverter technology, because its minimum output exceeds the home's load for most of the year. GreenBuildingAdvisor forums are full of homeowners doing the math themselves, checking Manual J results against manufacturer capacity tables at specific outdoor temperatures, because they've learned not to trust the contractor's sizing.

Variable speed also does nothing about the upfront cost penalty. A 5-ton variable-speed unit costs more than a 3-ton one, regardless of modulation range. You pay the premium for capacity you won't use, and the system occupies more physical space, requires a larger electrical circuit, and may need a different refrigerant line set.

What we don't know

That Nature Communications study analyzed 637 heat pumps, a substantial sample, but drawn primarily from European installations with different climate profiles, building codes, and equipment than what you'd find in American suburbs, and whether the 7-to-11 percent oversizing rate translates to American homes with American contractors is an open question that could easily tilt higher given more diverse building envelopes, weaker Manual J enforcement, and a deeper "bigger is safer" culture.

Karg's 2.3x average oversizing figure for combustion systems is widely cited but predates the heat pump transition, and because heat pumps have different failure modes than furnaces, short-cycling versus standby losses, the dollar impact of oversizing may be larger or smaller depending on technology and climate.

Nobody has published a controlled study comparing energy consumption in homes where the heat pump was sized by Manual J calculation versus rule of thumb versus AI-assisted load modeling, with the same home, the same climate, and randomized contractor assignment, and that study would settle the argument but nobody has funded it.

Conduit Tech and cove.tool adoption rates in residential HVAC are not publicly reported, and Manual J calculations performed per heat pump installation in the U.S. are not tracked by any trade organization, which means precisely the numbers that would quantify the gap don't exist.

What this means for the person writing the check

If you're about to buy a heat pump, one question separates the contractors who will cost you thousands from the ones who won't: "Show me the Manual J calculation for my house."

A contractor who has performed one can show you the inputs, from your home's insulation values and window U-factors to its infiltration rate and climate zone design temperature, along with the output in BTU per hour that maps to a specific equipment tonnage, and if the proposed system exceeds that number by more than 25 percent, you should ask why.

A contractor who hasn't performed one is sizing your system by feel, and the feel runs in one direction.

AI-augmented versions of this calculation already exist, pulling climate data automatically, estimating missing inputs via predictive models, and cross-referencing manufacturer performance curves against your actual load profile, and they have been available for years at modest cost, but they remain optional in an industry where the penalty for oversizing lands on the homeowner and the penalty for undersizing lands on the contractor, so optional means unused.

Fifteen minutes with a phone. That's all it takes. Your contractor chose not to spend them.
