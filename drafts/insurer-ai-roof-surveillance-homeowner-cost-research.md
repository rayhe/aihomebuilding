# Research: AI Aerial Surveillance in Homeowner Insurance

## Angle
Your insurer flew a drone over your house, scored your roof with an AI, and decided to drop you — you never consented, never saw the photos, and the score might be wrong. This is now standard practice for every major U.S. insurer, and the Geospatial Insurance Consortium has aerial imagery of 99% of American homes.

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? **YES.**
- If you're building: your roofing material and color choice now directly affect an AI's future assessment of your property.
- If you're buying: understanding aerial surveillance changes your insurance planning, inspection strategy, and negotiation leverage.
- If you own: actionable steps to preempt or challenge AI-based non-renewals.

### 2. Geospatial Insurance Consortium / United Policyholders
- GIC (funded by major insurers) + Vexcel have captured aerial imagery of 99% of U.S. buildings.
- Amy Bach (United Policyholders): AI "often misinterprets images, leading to overreactions like dropping long-term policies despite no actual changes."
- Source: United Policyholders (uphelp.org), GadgetReview

### 3. California Assembly Bill 75 (March 2025)
- Assemblymember Lisa Calderon proposed requiring insurers to: notify homeowners before aerial photo capture, supply photos upon request, limit how images can be used in policy decisions.
- Aerial images older than 45 days can't be used unless problem is persistent and verified by in-person inspection.
- Must send images + explanation if canceling/non-renewing based on aerial photos.
- Commissioner Ricardo Lara: "The California Department of Insurance has investigated numerous complaints where flawed aerial imagery led to wrongful cancellations or nonrenewals."
- Currently in Assembly Committee on Privacy and Consumer Protection.
- California already has 30-day notice requirement. Pennsylvania mandates 60-day notice.
- Source: Insurify, AB 75 text

### 4. State Farm $20K Case (Linda Bennett, Santa Ana, CA)
- 20-year State Farm customer. Received $20K repair demand based on aerial images she never knew were taken. No inspector visited. No visible damage.
- State Farm confirmed it uses "a mix of aerial images" from drones, satellites, and manned aircraft, fed through AI systems.
- Source: GadgetReview

## Original Contribution (Novel Analysis)
**The hidden cost cascade of a false positive AI roof score:**
- Average roof replacement: $10,000-$20,000 (legitimate damage)
- But for false positive: homeowner pays either unnecessary replacement OR faces non-renewal
- Non-renewal penalty: shopping for new insurance (time + higher premiums, because non-renewal history follows you)
- FAIR plan premium: typically 2-3x market rate
- If CAPE claims 95% accuracy on roof age, that's a 5% error rate across ~140 million housing units = 7 million homes with potentially wrong AI roof assessments
- Each false positive costs the homeowner $5K-$25K in unnecessary repairs, premium increases, or coverage gaps
- Scale: 7M × average $10K impact = $70B in potential homeowner costs from AI scoring errors
- This calculation is novel — nobody has quantified the aggregate false positive burden

## Strongest Counterargument
Insurance companies argue aerial AI inspection is safer (no one climbs roofs), cheaper, and catches genuine deferred maintenance that protects the insurance pool. They're not wrong — damaged roofs DO cause the most expensive claims, and in-person inspections are expensive and infrequent. The question is whether the efficiency gains justify the opacity and error rate, especially when homeowners can't see or challenge the AI's assessment before losing coverage.

## Limitations
- CAPE's 95% accuracy claim is self-reported; no independent audit exists
- The $70B aggregate cost estimate uses assumptions about average error cost that vary wildly by market
- Non-renewal data is state-level; county-level variation is enormous
- The NPR investigation found 12 Texas complaints, but the true complaint rate is unknown (most homeowners don't file with the state)
- We don't know how many non-renewals are specifically driven by AI aerial assessment vs. other factors
