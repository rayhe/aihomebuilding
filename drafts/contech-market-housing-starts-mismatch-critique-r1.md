## 1. 🔍 General Editor — 9.0/10

The cold open is textbook: a specific builder, a specific dollar amount, a specific day of the week, and a punchline that lands three paragraphs later when "every feature worked exactly as advertised" becomes the quietly devastating kicker. The article never telegraphs its conclusion early — the reader follows the anecdote assuming it's a success story before the rug pull. Structure moves cleanly from anecdote → national data → where ConTech money actually flows → the strongest counterargument → legislation → actionable guidance → limitations. The "What This Means for You" section directly addresses two readers (builders evaluating tools, homebuyers wondering about prices) with specific numbers, which satisfies the actionable-insights gate. One minor note: "Here is where I'm obligated to make the strongest case against my own thesis, because the counter-argument is legitimate" is a touch of meta-commentary that slightly breaks the fourth wall — Frank would more likely just *make* the case without announcing it — but it's a single sentence and the section that follows is substantively strong.

## 2. 🗣️ Voice Coach (Frank DeLuca) — 9.0/10

This reads like Frank throughout. "He signed the contract on a Tuesday" is the kind of irrelevant-yet-telling detail a project manager remembers because he thinks in calendars. "Every feature worked exactly as advertised" is classic Frank — stating a fact that sounds like praise until you realize it's an indictment. "I've watched three housing bills pass in my career, and none of them shortened a single permit timeline in the first year" is world-weary experience distilled into one sentence. The ROI math ($54K return on 30 homes vs. $32.4K on 18 homes → "Net loss.") is Frank thinking in critical paths and cost codes. Sentence rhythm passes all gates with comfortable margin on variance (201.0) and long-sentence percentage (61.2%). No banned phrases. No AI tells detected. The voice is consistent from open to close without breaking character once.

## 3. ⚖️ Ethics Reviewer — 9.0/10

The article avoids the two most common ethical traps in construction-tech journalism: cheerleading for technology and vilifying specific companies. It makes no claim that AI tools are useless — "the tools work" is stated as fact and supported — but contextualizes their value honestly within a demand-constrained market. The strongest counterargument (efficiency as survival mechanism for builders still breaking ground) is presented at full strength with real math before being challenged, satisfying the STORY_GUIDE requirement. The anonymized Dallas builder anecdote protects the source while providing enough specificity (DFW, 23 lots, 9 unsold homes, $15K credits) to feel real. The limitations section is honest about aggregate-data blind spots and unverifiable licensing costs. No self-congratulatory framing, no moral posturing, no taking sides beyond what the evidence supports.

## 4. 📱 Social/Shareability Strategist — 9.0/10

The headline structure — two sentences, one up, one down — is engineered for sharing because it creates cognitive tension that demands a click. The OG/Twitter meta descriptions ("ConTech is booming. Housing starts aren't. The tools work. The market doesn't care.") are punchy four-beat rhythms that fit perfectly in a share preview. The stat callouts (10.3 months of unsold inventory, 1% AI adoption) are visual anchors that work as standalone social cards. Multiple pull quotes are ready-made for sharing: "Every feature worked exactly as advertised," "AI can't fix interest rates," "nobody's coming through the model home door." The ROI flip ($54K win at 30 homes → $32.4K loss at 18 homes) is the kind of concrete comparison that gets screenshot-shared in builder forums. The contrarian thesis (ConTech growth is actually an infrastructure story wearing residential's hard hat) challenges an industry narrative, which drives engagement from both agreement and pushback.

## 5. ⚖️ Legal Accuracy — 8.5/10

The major data-bearing claims are sourced and hyperlinked: Census Bureau housing starts via Reuters, NAHB HMI via nahb.org, Mordor Intelligence market report, Business Research Company market report, WSJ data-center construction piece. Bill Owens is correctly identified as NAHB chairman, Anirban Basu as ABC chief economist, and Stephen Stanley as Santander Capital Markets chief US economist — all verifiable. The carrying-cost math transparently shows its inputs ($300K loan, 9.5% rate, 10-day delay → ~$780 interest + overhead = ~$1,800). The $38,000 license cost is flagged as unverifiable in the limitations section, which is the correct editorial treatment. Remaining gaps from Round 0 persist as minor items: Freddie Mac PMMS data is named but not hyperlinked, the NAHB 2025 AI adoption survey is named but not hyperlinked, and the bipartisan housing bill is referenced without a bill number or name. None of these gaps threaten factual accuracy — the sources are named and traceable — but adding hyperlinks for Freddie Mac and the NAHB survey would strengthen the citation chain for readers who want to verify.

## 6. 🔬 Research Rigor — 9.0/10

**Original contribution:** The article's central insight — that ConTech's 16% growth rate is driven by infrastructure and data-center customers, not the residential builders whose market the industry narrative implies — is a novel synthesis combining NAHB adoption data (1% of SF builders using AI for equipment), Mordor Intelligence market segmentation (29% infrastructure, highest-growth segment), and Census Bureau construction spending (DC spending +23% y/y). The ROI analysis comparing $38K license economics at 30 vs. 18 annual starts is original math that no vendor sales deck includes. **Limitations:** Explicitly stated — aggregate national data, regional divergence, likely higher adoption among production builders, unverifiable license cost. **Strongest counterargument:** Given full treatment with specific dollar figures (the $1,800/home savings calculation) before being challenged on volume-dependency grounds. **Verifiability:** All major claims trace to named sources. **Methodology transparency:** Carrying-cost inputs and ROI assumptions are shown, not hidden.

## 7. 📊 Data Presentation — 8.5/10

The two stat callouts are well-chosen: 10.3 months of unsold inventory (the demand problem) and 1% AI adoption (the adoption gap) — each one anchors a major section of the argument. The data layering is effective: housing starts (895K SAAR, -3.2% y/y) → HMI (34, 15th month below 40) → mortgage rates (6.55%, 11-month high) → market size ($6.9B, 16.3% CAGR) → segmentation (29% infrastructure, 40% execution stage) → DC spending (+23% y/y). Numbers are specific throughout — no vague ranges. The ROI math is transparently constructed and allows the reader to plug in their own volume. One minor loss from the word-count trimming: the single-family permits figure (871K SAAR, lowest since August 2025) was cut from the body text. Permits are a forward indicator that would have strengthened the "this isn't improving" argument, but the article is at the word-count ceiling and the remaining data set is more than sufficient to carry the thesis.
