# Research: AI-Screened Office-to-Residential Conversions

## Thesis
Gensler built an algorithm to screen office buildings for residential conversion. It screens buildings against a "Goldilocks" ideal — and it rejects 65-70% of them. The 30-35% that pass could add 171,000 apartments to the US housing stock. But only 5% of possible conversions are underway. The algorithm's biggest finding isn't which buildings work — it's the size of the gap between what's possible and what's happening.

## Journalist
Elena Vasquez — Architecture & Design. She cares about whether converted spaces actually feel like homes, not just whether the numbers pencil out.

## Primary Sources

### 1. Gensler Algorithm & Screening Criteria
- Source: The Hustle interview with Steven Paynter (Gensler's global building transformation & adaptive reuse leader)
- Algorithm compares building data against a "Goldilocks building" — a perfect residential building in the same neighborhood
- Key screening criterion: elevator-to-window distance (ideally ~35 feet for 30-foot-deep units)
- Most approached with "Class B" buildings (neither high quality nor cheap, never bouncing back)
- 30-35% of office buildings technically convertible; only 10% conversion would improve remaining office market viability
- Only 5% of identified conversions underway — incentive gap is the bottleneck

### 2. White House/CEA Analysis
- Source: Biden White House Council of Economic Advisors report on commercial-to-residential conversion
- 15% of commercial district office buildings in 105 largest US cities suitable for conversion
- Potential: 171,470 new residential units (nearly half of 2022's multifamily building yield)
- Conversions can be 20% cheaper than demolish-and-rebuild
- COVID induced "flight to quality": 10% of US office buildings account for 80% of occupancy losses
- High-vacancy buildings tend to be older, in downtown submarkets

### 3. Pew Charitable Trusts / Gensler Co-Living Study (March 2026)
- Source: Pew Charitable Trusts research article
- Studied 10 US cities: Denver, Minneapolis, Seattle, LA, Houston, Chicago, DC, Albuquerque, Santa Fe, Phoenix
- Worked with Turner Construction Company on cost analysis
- Co-living microapartment costs: $123,300-$238,700 each (one-third to half of new studio apartment costs)
- Conversion reduces construction costs 25-35% per sq ft vs conventional office-to-apt conversion
- Key design innovation: furnished rooms on building perimeter (windows), shared kitchens/bathrooms near building core (where office plumbing already exists)
- Subsidy efficiency: 3.9× multiplier — same public investment produces 3.9× more affordable homes vs studio apartments
- Phoenix example: $25M subsidy → 294 co-living apartments vs 116 studio apartments (2.5× more)
- Seattle multiplier: 4.2×; Chicago: 3.6×
- Target rents: $700/mo (Houston/Albuquerque) to $1,000/mo (LA/Seattle/DC)
- Building with 40% renter households that are single persons; 56-58% in DC/Seattle
- "Problem buildings" with large floor plates and deep cores actually BETTER for co-living

### 4. City/State Incentive Programs
- Source: American Bar Association article (December 2024)
- DC "Office to Anything Program": 20-year tax abatement with 15-year freeze
- Boston "Downtown Residential Conversion Incentive Program": up to 75% abatement for 29 years, "As of Right" zoning
- LA updated Citywide Adaptive Reuse Ordinance (January 2025): any building 15+ years old
- San Francisco: transfer tax waiver on first sale of converted properties
- Houston: waived parking requirements in CBD
- Denver: Adaptive Reuse Pilot Program with dedicated project management
- Also: Phoenix, Pittsburgh, Seattle, Portland, Minneapolis

### 5. Historical Precedent
- LA's 1999 Adaptive Reuse Ordinance: ~20,000 units in downtown LA in ~15 years (RAND Center)
- Calgary: 5 office-residential conversion projects creating 1,200+ new housing units; vacancy trending down
- Hotel/motel conversions easiest (plumbing in every room); office more variable
- Biggest design challenge: deep floor plates with no natural light at core

### 6. Environmental Data
- Conversions retain ~90% of existing concrete
- Concrete: 8% of global carbon emissions, 4× airline industry
- Embodied carbon savings significant vs new construction

## Market Context
- US office vacancy rate: ~20% (2025/2026)
- 24% projected vacancy by end of 2026 (Bloomberg estimate)
- Commercial property values cut by up to $250B
- US housing shortage: 4-7 million homes (Harvard Joint Center for Housing Studies)
- Record 771,000 people experiencing homelessness (HUD 2024), up 18% YoY

## Original Contribution
Calculate the "conversion gap": 171,470 potential units at ~5% conversion rate = ~162,000 units sitting in buildings an algorithm says should be homes but aren't. At median US apartment construction cost (~$350,000/unit for new construction), that's ~$56.7B in potential housing stock idling as empty cubicle farms. The algorithm isn't the bottleneck. The regulatory and financial infrastructure is.

Also: the Gensler algorithm's 35-foot elevator-to-window "Goldilocks" criterion effectively discriminates against post-1980 office buildings (which tend to have deeper floor plates, 50-75+ feet from core to window). This means the easiest buildings to convert are also the oldest — exactly the ones cities are most eager to preserve historically. There's a tension between adaptive reuse as preservation strategy and adaptive reuse as housing strategy.

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home?
YES — helps homebuyers understand what to look for when buying a converted apartment (natural light depth, unit layout relative to core), helps developers screen buildings, helps city planners evaluate their conversion incentive programs against what's actually working, and helps residents of converted buildings understand why their apartment might feel different from purpose-built housing.

## Skepticism
- Only 5% of possible conversions underway despite years of analysis
- Unproven co-living model has financing and insurance challenges
- Plumbing is the killer: office buildings have centralized plumbing, not distributed; running new pipes to every unit is where costs explode
- Acoustics: office buildings weren't designed for residential STC requirements
- Mechanical ventilation: many offices have sealed windows, non-operable; residential codes require openable windows or mechanical ventilation
- Historic preservation constraints can ADD cost, not reduce it
- The "flight to quality" means high-quality office buildings (which are staying occupied) are the ones most suitable for nice apartments; the empty ones are Class B — by definition, not great
