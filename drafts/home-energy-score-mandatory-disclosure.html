Seven Cities Now Require an Energy Score Before You Can List Your Home. The Market Is Already Punishing Low Numbers.

By Catherine Chen · June 22, 2026

In Portland, Oregon, you cannot advertise a home for sale without first disclosing its energy score. Not at closing. Not buried in an inspection report. In the listing itself, right next to the bedroom count and the square footage, sits a number on a 1-to-10 scale that tells every buyer scrolling Zillow exactly how much it will cost to heat and cool that house.

Portland adopted the rule in 2018. Seven other jurisdictions have followed: Minneapolis, Berkeley, Austin, Ann Arbor, Hillsboro, Milwaukie, and Bend. Eugene is actively drafting its own ordinance. The idea is simple. Homes already get a miles-per-gallon sticker when they sit in a dealership. Why not when they sit on a cul-de-sac?

The sticker, it turns out, changes behavior.

The Price Signal Nobody Expected

Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory studied 26,291 home sales in markets with mandatory Home Energy Score disclosure. Their finding was precise: each additional point on the DOE's 10-point scale corresponded to a 0.5% increase in final sale price. On a $400,000 home, that is $2,000 per point. A house scoring 3 versus a comparable house scoring 8 carries a $10,000 implicit penalty, and nobody had to argue about it at the negotiating table. The number spoke for itself.

The effect vanished in voluntary markets. Where scoring was optional, no statistically significant price premium appeared. The mechanism is intuitive: voluntary programs attract the homes that already score well. Mandatory programs force the full distribution into daylight, and buyers can finally distinguish a well-insulated house from one bleeding BTUs through every wall.

Freddie Mac's larger national dataset confirmed the direction. Rated energy-efficient homes sold for a 2.7% average premium between 2013 and 2017. In Austin, ENERGY STAR-certified homes sold for 6% more. In Oregon, Idaho, and Washington, premiums reached 8% for certain green certifications. The literature is not ambiguous. Efficiency is being capitalized into home prices wherever buyers have the data to see it.

Something else showed up in Berkeley Lab's data that real estate agents do not typically discuss. A one-point increase in Home Energy Score was associated with a 5.5% reduction in the odds of a mortgage going 30 days delinquent. Lower energy bills leave more cash for the mortgage payment. Lenders should care about this. Some are beginning to.

Portland's 10x Effect

The American Council for an Energy-Efficient Economy published an evaluation of the three oldest mandatory programs in July 2025. Portland's result was the headline: homes that received an energy score in 2023 were ten times more likely to undergo energy efficiency upgrades than the general housing stock. Not twice as likely. Ten times.

The mechanism is not complicated. A seller gets the score. It comes back as a 4. The assessor's report lists specific upgrades: attic insulation, air sealing, a heat pump water heater. The seller does the math. Spend $3,000 on attic insulation, gain two points, add $4,000 to the listing price. The report did the ROI calculation for them.

Berkeley made a revealing mistake before Portland got it right. The original Berkeley ordinance required disclosure at closing, when the buyer has already committed emotionally and financially. The city amended the law to require disclosure at listing. That one procedural change transformed the score from a bureaucratic formality into a market signal that actually influenced purchase decisions. Minneapolis adopted the listing-stage requirement from the start.

What the Score Actually Measures

The DOE Home Energy Score, administered by Pacific Northwest National Laboratory since 2012, evaluates a home's physical assets: insulation levels, window performance, HVAC equipment efficiency, air leakage, duct condition. An assessor inspects the home, enters roughly 60 data points into the DOE's modeling software, and receives a score and estimated annual energy cost.

Cost to the seller: $150 to $300. Time: about two hours for the inspection, with the report generated automatically.

The system has real quality assurance. Portland requires Earth Advantage, a local nonprofit, to re-score a minimum of 5% of all assessed homes each quarter. Assessors whose scores diverge from QA results get flagged and retrained. Oregon's Department of Energy serves as a statewide proxy partner with DOE, standardizing assessor certification across every participating city in the state.

The score does not measure occupant behavior. A family running their AC at 65 degrees in August will spend more than the estimate. But the score captures the building envelope and equipment, the things a buyer cannot easily evaluate from a Redfin photo gallery, the things that determine whether monthly energy costs land at $120 or $380.

AI Is Compressing the Assessment

The two-hour, $200 human inspection is the bottleneck. Several research teams and startups are working to automate parts of it.

Researchers at the University of Dayton demonstrated in 2021 that machine learning models trained on smart thermostat data could predict a home's attic and wall R-values, furnace efficiency, and AC SEER rating with R-squared values above 0.90. The input was not a physical inspection. It was archived temperature data from a WiFi thermostat, combined with weather records and basic building geometry. The model learned to reverse-engineer the home's thermal properties from how quickly it heated and cooled.

If 60 million American homes have smart thermostats, that is 60 million potential virtual energy audits waiting to be run without anyone climbing into an attic.

Lamarr.AI, an MIT spinout, is approaching the problem from the air. Their platform processes drone-captured thermal imagery through computer vision models that identify insulation gaps, air leaks, and moisture intrusion. In a City of Detroit pilot, the system flagged more than 460 envelope problems across three municipal buildings and estimated that targeted upgrades could reduce HVAC energy consumption by 22%. The technology works for residential inspections at a fraction of the time and cost.

A 2025 study published in IEEE Access combined drone thermal imaging with a YOLOv4 deep learning model for indoor and outdoor heat envelope detection. The approach is scalable and requires no physical entry into the home, which matters when you are trying to score thousands of homes before they hit the market in a city with 200,000 housing units.

The Equity Problem Nobody Solved

Mandatory disclosure creates a two-tier market. Homes with high scores advertise it proudly. Homes with low scores face a quantifiable price penalty. The owners of those low-scoring homes are, disproportionately, the ones who cannot afford to fix them.

A furnace replacement runs $4,000 to $8,000. A full air-sealing and insulation package costs $2,000 to $5,000. Window replacements for a typical home: $8,000 to $15,000. The ACEEE report recommended that municipalities provide free energy scores for low-income homeowners, but a free score without subsidized upgrades just tells a seller their home is worth less without giving them a way to close the gap.

Portland and Minneapolis both connect sellers to utility rebate programs and federal tax credits in the assessment report. The Inflation Reduction Act's residential energy efficiency credits (25C) cover 30% of insulation, heat pumps, and efficient HVAC equipment, up to annual caps. But navigating the rebate landscape requires time and literacy that not every homeowner has, and the credits themselves are under legislative pressure in 2026.

This is the hard part that the policy community has not resolved. Transparency is good. Price signals are efficient. But efficient price signals applied to an inequitable housing stock do not automatically produce equitable outcomes. They can amplify existing disparities if the upgrade pathway is not funded alongside the disclosure mandate.

Where This Is Going

Eugene, Oregon, is running public engagement sessions for a mandatory disclosure ordinance now. Oregon's statewide ODOE partnership makes adoption nearly turnkey for any Oregon city: DOE provides the scoring tool, ODOE handles the partnership requirements, Earth Advantage manages assessor training and QA. The administrative barriers that stopped other states are largely absent in Oregon, which is why five of the eight adopting jurisdictions are there.

Outside Oregon, the path is slower. No state has passed a statewide mandatory residential energy disclosure law. Commercial buildings have building performance standards in over 40 cities, but residential mandates remain rare. The political obstacles are familiar: real estate industry opposition, cost concerns, and the persistent American discomfort with telling a homeowner what they must disclose about their own property.

But the data is doing its own advocacy. An analysis of 143 million home listings from 1995 to 2025, conducted by the residential energy data platform 257, found that the share of listings mentioning energy efficiency features nearly tripled over that period. Listings that mentioned solar panels sold for a 2% premium. Listings that mentioned heat pumps sold for 0.6% to 1% more. Only 8.3% of listings mention energy features at all. The signal is there. It is just buried.

Mandatory energy scoring does not bury the signal. It makes the signal the first thing a buyer sees.

What This Means for You

If you are buying in a mandatory disclosure city: The energy score is now a negotiating tool. A home scoring 4/10 with $300 monthly energy costs is not the same financial proposition as a home scoring 8/10 with $140 monthly costs. Over 10 years of ownership, that difference is $19,200 in energy costs alone, before accounting for the resale premium.

If you are selling: Get the assessment before you list, even if your city does not require it. If the score is low, the $2,000 to $5,000 you spend on insulation and air sealing may return $4,000 to $10,000 in sale price. If the score is high, advertise it. Only 8.3% of listings mention energy features. You will stand out.

If you are building: Design for a high energy score from the start. The incremental cost of better insulation, tighter air sealing, and efficient HVAC during construction is a fraction of retrofit pricing. In markets trending toward mandatory disclosure, that score will be printed next to your house on every listing for as long as the building stands.

Limitations

The Berkeley Lab price premium data comes primarily from Portland and Minneapolis, both progressive cities with environmentally conscious buyer pools. Whether the same premiums would materialize in Houston or Phoenix remains unproven. The sample size, while large for energy policy research at 26,291 homes, represents a fraction of national transactions. Hedonic regression controls for observable home characteristics but cannot fully eliminate selection effects. Assessor variability is real: the 5% QA re-scoring requirement exists because human assessments are imprecise. And the AI-assisted assessment tools described here are mostly in research or early commercial stages. None have been approved as substitutes for the DOE's official scoring methodology.

The trend direction, however, is not ambiguous. Every data source points the same way: buyers pay more for efficient homes when they can see the efficiency. Mandatory disclosure makes it visible. AI is making the measurement cheaper. The remaining question is whether the policy spreads fast enough to matter, and whether it brings the equity infrastructure to match.
