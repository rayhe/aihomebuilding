# Research: AI-Generated Building Plans and the Professional Stamp Liability Gap

## Journalist: Catherine "Code" Chen (Policy & Regulation)
## Slug: ai-generated-plans-professional-stamp-liability

## Angle
Every jurisdiction requires a licensed architect or PE to stamp building plans before permitting. AI tools are now generating floor plans, structural details, and construction documents. NCARB says the architect remains in "responsible control" — but what does that mean when the architect didn't draw a single line? The emerging liability gap between AI outputs and professional accountability.

## Kill Test
✅ Helps builders: Know what contract language to demand around AI-assisted design
✅ Helps homeowners: Understand who's actually liable if AI-generated plans contain errors
✅ Helps architects: Understand their exposure when using AI tools
✅ Specific & actionable: E&O insurance implications, contract clauses, state-level requirements

## Primary Sources (3+ required)

### 1. NCARB Position Statement on AI (Oct 2024)
- Source: https://www.ncarb.org/press/ncarb-position-on-ai
- 150 licensing board members workshopped AI at June 2024 Annual Business Meeting
- Key positions:
  - "Any proposed regulation that addresses AI usage in practice must ensure the licensed practitioner remains in responsible control and continues to be accountable for all technical submissions under their seal"
  - "AI is a tool — it is not a replacement for professional judgment"
  - Planning to reassess "responsible control parameters" for AI tools
  - No precedent for enforcing limitations on a tool's use

### 2. NSPE Position Statement 03-1774 on AI (adopted Sep 2023, revised Feb 2025)
- Source: https://nspe.org/nspe-advocacy/explore-issues/professional-policies-and-position-statements/artificial-intelligence
- "Individuals who design, develop, or oversee AI systems that have a direct impact on public safety should be held to the same standards as professional engineering licensure"
- Calls for: rigorous V&V testing, safety standards, transparency, accountability
- Software upgrades should meet same standard of care as initial launch

### 3. AIA Study: "AI Adoption in Architecture Firms" (March 2025)
- Source: https://www.aia.org/about-aia/press/new-research-explores-perceptions-and-opportunities-artificial-intelligence
- Published collaboration with Deltek and ConstructConnect
- Key data:
  - Only **6% of architects regularly use AI** for their jobs
  - Only **8% of firms** have implemented AI solutions; 20% working on it
  - Driven by large firms (50+ employees) as early adopters
  - **90% of architects concerned** about: inaccuracies, unintended consequences, security, authenticity, transparency
  - Most common uses: chatbots, image generators, grammar/text analytics
  - Adoption driven by architects aged ≤50

### 4. Bilzin Sumberg: "The Legal Risks of AI in Homebuilding" (Aug 2025)
- Source: https://www.bilzin.com/insights/publications/2025/08/the-legal-risks-of-ai-in-homebuilding
- Author: Philip R. Stein
- Key risks identified:
  - Contractual liability: who bears responsibility for AI cost projection errors or flawed designs?
  - IP: generative AI may draw on copyrighted/proprietary training data
  - Contracts must explicitly allocate AI liability, specify remedies (indemnification, damages)
  - Need AI vendor warranties confirming lawful data sourcing

### 5. Proving Ground: "Code and Conduct: Five Areas Where AI Confronts the Architect's Ethics" (Oct 2025)
- Source: https://provingground.io/2025/10/22/code-and-conduct-five-areas-where-ai-confronts-the-architects-ethics/
- AIA Code of Conduct Rule 1.101: "Members shall demonstrate a consistent pattern of reasonable care and competence"
- AIA Code of Conduct Rule 4.102: "Members shall not sign or seal drawings, specifications, reports, or other professional work for which they do not have responsible control"
- Precedents from legal field: US lawyer sanctioned for ChatGPT fake cases (2025), California issued $10K fine for AI-faked court quotations (Sep 2025)

### 6. NSPE/AIA/ACEC Professional Liability Insurance Carriers Survey 2025
- Source: https://www.nspe.org/career-growth/pe-magazine/issue-1-2025/professional-liability-trends-every-design-professional
- Residential/condo projects RED-FLAGGED by insurers
- AI flagged as emerging concern for claims
- Technical errors are the #1 cause of claims
- Nuclear verdicts ($100M+) increasingly common
- Key states for high-value claims: NJ, TX, CA, PA, NY, GA, FL
- Average project size increasing → higher exposure per claim
- Carriers treat AI as "support tool — not a replacement" for professional judgment

## Original Contribution (required)
Cross-reference analysis: NCARB's "responsible control" doctrine was designed for a world where architects supervised human draftspeople. Now AI generates plans at 100x speed. If 6% of architects use AI today (AIA 2025) and firms report 90% concern about inaccuracies, what happens as adoption scales? The math:
- Average residential E&O claim involves $150K-$500K in defense + settlement costs
- If AI-generated plans increase technical error rate by even 5%, the exposure for a 50-person firm could double
- No E&O carrier has explicitly excluded AI-generated design errors — yet. That policy language gap is a ticking clock.

## Limitations to Acknowledge
- No court has yet ruled on an AI-specific construction defect claim (precedent gap)
- AIA's 6% figure is from March 2025 — adoption likely higher now but no updated survey
- E&O claim cost estimates are industry averages, not AI-specific
- "Responsible control" hasn't been formally redefined by any state board for AI contexts

## Strongest Counterargument
AI tools may actually REDUCE errors compared to human drafting. AutoCAD reduced drafting errors vs hand-drawing. AI plan-check tools catch code violations that human reviewers miss. The problem might not be AI errors — it might be that the liability framework hasn't caught up with a tool that, on average, produces fewer mistakes than the humans it assists.
