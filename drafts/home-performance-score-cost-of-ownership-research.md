# Research: Home Performance Scores and Total Cost of Ownership

## Topic
Every single-family home in America now has an algorithmically derived performance score. What happens when buyers can finally see what a home *costs* to own, not just to buy?

## Primary Sources

### 1. Pearl Home Performance Registry (Nov 2025)
- Pearl completed the first comprehensive performance database of America's single-family housing stock
- 92 million single-family homes modeled and scored on Pearl SCORE™ (1-1,000 scale)
- Five pillars: Safety, Comfort, Operations, Resilience, Energy (SCORE acronym)
- Free at pearlscore.com
- Data sources: utility costs, climate data, maintenance projections, homeowner input, home performance benchmarks
- Founded 2013, Certified B Corporation
- Source: Globe Newswire, Nov 12, 2025

### 2. Realtor.com + Pearl Total Cost of Ownership Study (June 2026)
- New home buyer saves $25,335 over 10 years vs buyer of 20-year-old home
- Reference: 1,750 sq ft home, ZIP-code level modeling, aggregated to state
- Two savings categories: (1) utility cost difference, (2) major replacement costs (HVAC, roofs, water heaters)
- Methodology: Pearl SCORE models for energy consumption × state retail gas/electric prices × EIA escalation factors
- Realtor.com now integrating interactive total cost-of-ownership experience on new construction listings

**Additional savings not modeled:**
- Builder warranties (commonly 2-year systems) = $0 HVAC repairs early on

- National new-construction price gap: ~$60,000 ($450K new vs $390K existing)

## Novel Contribution
Cross-reference IECC code adoption year against per-state savings to estimate the dollar value of each code cycle upgrade. The data shows a clear relationship:
- States on 2021+ IECC average ~$35,500 in 10-year savings (top 4: MA, ME, RI, VT)
- States on 2015 or earlier average ~$17,300 (bottom 3: KY, TX, SC)
- Delta: ~$18,200 per home over 10 years attributable to 6 years of code advancement
- Per IECC cycle (3 years): ~$9,100 per home in incremental savings
- At scale: if 1.4M new single-family homes are built per year (Census Bureau 2025 estimate), that's ~$12.7 billion in cumulative homeowner savings per code cycle nationally

## Kill Test
**Does this help someone building or buying a home?**
YES — buyers can now look up any home's performance score for free, compare homes on operating costs, and identify where stricter codes translate to real savings. Builders can use this data to justify code compliance as a selling point.

## Skepticism
- Pearl is industry-funded; their data advantages benefit their business model
- Models, not actual utility bills — modeled estimates have systematic biases
- 1,750 sq ft reference: doesn't represent McMansions or starter homes equally
- Replacement cost modeling assumes standard maintenance schedules; deferred maintenance isn't captured
- Regional energy price volatility could invalidate escalation factors
- Score based on physical attributes, not occupant behavior (wasteful occupant in a high-score home still pays more)

## Strongest Counterargument
Performance scores could create a discriminatory pricing layer in real estate. Older homes in lower-income neighborhoods — which disproportionately received weaker code enforcement — would score lower, potentially accelerating value decline and gentrification pressure. The scoring itself is blind to improvements that don't appear in public records.
