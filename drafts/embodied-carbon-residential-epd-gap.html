Half Your Home’s Carbon Footprint Was Locked In Before the Drywall Went Up. California Requires the Math for Office Buildings. Not for Yours.

By Priya Greenwood · July 23, 2026
← Back to AI Home Building

Pour the foundation, frame the walls, sheet the roof. Before your builder connects a single wire or hangs a single fixture, the concrete beneath your feet has already released roughly 410 kilograms of CO2 per cubic meter into the atmosphere. The steel reinforcement bar inside it added another 1.85 tons per ton produced. Your lumber sequestered some carbon while it grew, then spent some of it getting milled, kiln-dried, treated, and trucked to your lot.

All of that is embodied carbon, the climate cost of building materials from extraction through manufacturing, transportation, and installation, every gram of CO2 equivalent released before anyone flips a light switch, and for your home it is invisible, absent from the bid, absent from the contract, absent from any measurement anyone has ever been required to perform.

The Bigger Half Nobody Counts

A KPMG analysis of building lifecycle emissions found that embodied carbon now accounts for roughly 51% of a building’s total climate impact over its lifespan, with operational energy, the heating and cooling and lighting and appliances that run every day, responsible for the remaining 49%. That split is moving in a direction few homebuyers expect, because as the electrical grid gets cleaner and heat pumps replace furnaces and insulation standards tighten and code-minimum homes become more efficient by default, the carbon emitted from day-to-day living in a building shrinks while the carbon locked into the materials holds steady at the same tonnage it carried on the day the foundation was poured.

51% Share of a building’s lifetime carbon emissions attributable to embodied carbon in materials, transportation, and construction. Operational carbon accounts for the other 49%, and that share is falling as grids and equipment get cleaner.

Buildings and infrastructure account for more than a third of carbon emissions from industrialized nations, and we have spent decades chipping away at the operational portion with better insulation, smarter thermostats, rooftop solar, and increasingly efficient HVAC systems while the embodied portion has received a fraction of the attention, primarily because measuring it is difficult, the supply chains are fragmented, and the data infrastructure barely exists.

For residential construction, it does not exist at all.

California Drew a Line. Your House Is on the Wrong Side.

On January 1, 2026, California’s CalGreen code expansion took effect, requiring commercial buildings of 50,000 square feet or more to comply with mandatory embodied carbon reduction requirements by choosing one of three pathways: reuse 45% of the primary structure and enclosure, conduct a whole-building lifecycle assessment demonstrating a 10% reduction in global warming potential from a reference baseline, or follow a prescriptive approach using materials with Environmental Product Declarations showing carbon intensity at or below 175% of industry averages.

Covered materials include structural steel, concrete, reinforcing steel, flat glass, and mineral wool insulation. Environmental Product Declarations, or EPDs, are standardized documents verified by third parties that disclose the lifecycle environmental impact of a specific product from a specific facility, essentially nutrition labels for building materials, and for commercial construction the supply chain increasingly provides them, but asking your lumber yard for an EPD on the 2x10s going into your floor joists will earn you a silence that stretches well past uncomfortable.

AB 2446 extends the framework further, requiring the California Air Resources Board to build a measurement and reduction framework for embodied carbon in all new non-residential buildings greater than 10,000 square feet and residential projects of five or more units, which means a single-family home, with its average 2,300 square feet, sits 22 times below the commercial threshold and falls entirely outside the regulatory perimeter.

Not an oversight but a deliberate scope decision that leaves more than a million homes per year unmeasured.

The Math Nobody Has Done

Researchers have tried. A lifecycle analysis in Chongqing found that material production accounts for 90.92% of a residential building’s embodied carbon, with concrete contributing 36.3%, steel adding 24.1%, brick accounting for 16.6%, cement at 13.4%, and everything else combining for 9.6%, while the structural system alone carries 78.4% of a building’s embodied carbon load.

Useful numbers, and imprecise ones, because they come from a single building in one Chinese city, using Chinese emission factors and a Chinese concrete mix, and a wood-framed home in Oregon with no brick and minimal concrete behaves differently than a slab-on-grade masonry house in Texas, which is why published estimates for a typical American single-family home span from 30 to 80 tons of CO2 equivalent, a spread so wide it is practically useless for builders making actual material choices on actual projects with actual deadlines.

30–80 tons CO2e Published estimates for the embodied carbon of a typical American single-family home. A spread wide enough to be functionally useless for builders making material choices.

Straightforward reason for the spread: almost nobody measures individual homes. Commercial developers conduct lifecycle assessments because regulation requires it, clients demand it, and green building certifications like LEED and BREEAM reward it, but residential builders operate in a market where nobody asks, nobody requires, and nobody rewards, so the data that would narrow that 30-to-80-ton range simply has not been collected.

An AI Trained on Buildings That Don’t Exist

Researchers at the University of Bath published the first AI tool capable of predicting a building’s embodied carbon from a simple text description, a system where you describe the building, its materials, its dimensions, its intended use, and the model returns an estimate of lifecycle carbon emissions that refines itself as the description gets richer and more detailed, requiring no BIM model, no quantity takeoff, just language.

Sounds like it could close the residential gap. Describe a 2,300-square-foot wood-frame home with a concrete slab foundation, asphalt shingle roof, and vinyl siding, and get a carbon estimate before anyone draws a plan. Designers who tested it on 43 real projects called the feedback “overwhelmingly positive,” which makes the next fact all the more striking.

Training data: 150,000 buildings, every one of them synthetic.

“To overcome the lack of publicly available real-world embodied carbon data, the team trained the AI using a synthetically generated dataset,” the University of Bath disclosed in their announcement, meaning they built 150,000 fake buildings, complete with imagined material quantities and calculated carbon footprints, because they could not find enough real ones with measured embodied carbon data to train on. Promising accuracy, genuine utility for early-stage design decisions, but a foundation made entirely of buildings that were never poured, framed, or occupied.

Professor David Coley, a co-author, noted that “traditional tools often address embodied carbon via material mass accounting methods,” which require detailed bills of materials that exist only in later design stages, and his tool deliberately targets the earliest stage, the cocktail-napkin sketch phase where carbon reduction potential is greatest and available information is thinnest, which is exactly why it works from text descriptions rather than engineering drawings but also why its predictions rest on patterns extracted from buildings that exist only as rows in a training dataset.

Why This Matters for Your Concrete Bill

Concrete is the single largest contributor to residential embodied carbon, and also the one where substitution makes the most measurable difference, because replacing 30% of Portland cement with supplementary cementitious materials like fly ash or slag can reduce the embodied carbon of a residential foundation by 20% to 40% depending on the mix design and local sourcing. Some ready-mix suppliers now offer low-carbon mixes. Most residential contractors have never heard the term “supplementary cementitious material” and would not know how to specify one if it appeared on a bid sheet.

Wood framing is more complicated because lumber stores carbon as it grows, then releases it when the tree is harvested and the wood is processed, and the net balance depends on forestry practices, transportation distances, kiln-drying energy sources, and treatment chemicals, creating a carbon math for a 2x4 that is not simple and that the industry has not made accessible. An EPD for a specific dimensional lumber product from a specific mill would clarify the picture, but fewer than a dozen residential-grade lumber suppliers in North America publish them.

Roger Heeringa, a principal at DCI Engineers, put the broader gap plainly: “For years, our industry has been placing much of the responsibility of sustainability on the operations, the end users of a building. We have an untapped resource of designers, subcontractors and suppliers that can push sustainable practices even further by focusing on the embodied carbon in our buildings.”

The Honest Counterargument

Rational, on its own terms. A whole-building lifecycle assessment costs between $5,000 and $15,000 for a commercial project, and applied to a $400,000 single-family home that would mean a 1% to 4% cost increase for a measurement that does not change the materials already specified, does not trigger any regulatory consequence, and produces a number the buyer has no framework to evaluate. Administrative burden matters too: requiring EPDs from the fragmented residential supply chain, the thousands of local lumber yards, concrete batch plants, and hardware distributors that serve homebuilders, is an infrastructure project in itself. Wood-frame residential construction already carries lower embodied carbon per square foot than steel-and-concrete commercial buildings, so targeting commercial first and letting residential benefit from cleaner supply chains as they develop is pragmatically defensible.

All true. All incomplete. That pragmatic case assumes residential will benefit passively from commercial supply chain improvements, but the materials are not identical: residential concrete mixes differ from commercial ones, residential-grade windows and siding and roofing and insulation follow separate product lines with separate manufacturing processes, and a cleaner steel mill does not automatically produce a lower-carbon asphalt shingle.

What Would Close the Gap

Three things would need to happen, and the technology for at least two of them exists right now.

First, someone needs to measure real homes, not ten, not a hundred, but thousands of actual single-family homes across climate zones, construction types, and material palettes, each receiving a full lifecycle assessment with published data that would replace the synthetic buildings the Bath team had to invent and give AI tools a foundation of real measurements to learn from. Significant cost, but the alternative is that every AI prediction of residential embodied carbon rests on buildings that were never built.

Second, EPD availability for residential-grade materials needs to reach a critical mass. Washington State’s Buy Clean and Buy Fair legislation recognizes EPDs as “the best available tool for reporting product-specific environmental impacts,” but the current EPD ecosystem was built for commercial procurement, and extending it to residential means convincing manufacturers of roofing, siding, windows, insulation, and dimensional lumber to invest in third-party verified declarations, something a handful have done and the vast majority have not.

Third, AI tools need to become builder-facing rather than researcher-facing. Bath’s tool demonstrates that accurate embodied carbon prediction is possible from minimal inputs, and translating that into a field-usable calculator, something a framing contractor or homeowner could run from a phone by entering foundation type and wall assembly and roof material and square footage and getting a credible carbon estimate in return, is an engineering challenge that several groups are working toward but nobody has shipped for residential.

Fifty Million Tons a Year, Uncounted

Roughly one million new single-family homes go up in the United States every year, and if the midpoint of published estimates is even approximately right, somewhere around 50 tons of CO2 equivalent per home, that is 50 million tons of embodied carbon entering the atmosphere annually from residential construction alone, more than the annual emissions of 125 natural gas power plants.

Nobody counts it, nobody is required to reduce it, and an AI that could measure it at scale was trained on buildings that do not exist, while California, which leads the nation on embodied carbon regulation, drew its mandatory threshold at a size that excludes every detached home in the state.

Commercial buildings are solving this problem because regulation forced them to. Residential will not solve it until someone, a state legislature, a green building program, an insurance carrier, or a buyer who asks the right question during a walk-through, forces the same conversation onto a project that is 22 times smaller than the current threshold and matters just as much.

Limitations: Embodied carbon estimates for American single-family homes vary widely (30–80+ tons CO2e) depending on construction type, geographic location, foundation system, and assessment methodology. The University of Bath AI tool has not been independently validated against measured residential buildings in the United States. EPD availability for residential-grade materials is changing rapidly, and specific product categories may have better coverage than this analysis suggests. This article does not account for biogenic carbon sequestration in wood-frame construction, which could partially offset embodied emissions depending on forestry and end-of-life assumptions.
