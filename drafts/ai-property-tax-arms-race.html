Your County Used AI to Find $35 Million in Missing Tax Revenue. A Startup Raised $50 Million to Help You Appeal.

By Catherine Chen · June 6, 2026

Medina County, Ohio, population 183,000, ran every residential property sketch through EagleView's AI last year. It cross-referenced aerial photographs against official assessment records for 145,467 parcels and flagged 70,198 discrepancies: decks that didn't appear on file, room additions never assessed, garages that had quietly doubled in size between revaluations. County officials estimate the corrections added $35 to $40 million to the tax rolls and $12 to $14 million in new annual revenue, without hiring a single additional appraiser.

Nobody knocked on those doors, and nobody drove by with a clipboard. An algorithm scanned photographs taken from an airplane and generated a discrepancy report that a handful of staffers processed at their desks.

That is the offense.

On defense: a startup called Ownwell raised $50 million in February 2026, bringing its total funding to roughly $74 million, to do the opposite. Ownwell's AI pulls comparable sales, generates a ready-to-file appeal packet, and submits it on the homeowner's behalf, charging nothing upfront and taking 25 percent of whatever reduction it wins, zero if it wins nothing. Ownwell reports an 86 percent success rate across more than one million appeals, with average annual savings of $774 per customer.

The Expected Value of Not Sitting This Out

Run the numbers on Ownwell's published statistics and the expected value of filing an appeal comes to approximately $500 per year: 86 percent success rate, multiplied by $774 average savings, multiplied by the 75 percent the homeowner keeps after the contingency fee, which yields $499.59 before rounding.

Five hundred dollars. Zero downside risk. Zero upfront cost.

Yet only an estimated 5 to 10 percent of American homeowners appeal in any given year. In Texas, Ownwell processed appeals for over 200,000 properties whose owners had never protested at all. Two hundred thousand households leaving $500 on the table every year because the process felt too opaque, because they assumed the county was probably right, or because nobody had run the expected-value math for them.

What the County's AI Sees From the Sky

EagleView maintains over three billion high-resolution aerial photographs covering 94 percent of the U.S. population. Its 3D property intelligence technology, announced in February 2026, measures walls, windows, and doors with 98.77 percent accuracy. That is not a typo. It resolves the dimensions of your home's exterior features from photographs taken at altitude with sub-two-percent error.

Riverside County, California, signed a five-year contract with C3 AI in 2023, consolidated from 30 appraisal models down to four unified AI models, and cut processing time by 40 percent across 800,000+ assessable parcels. Cook County, Illinois, released PTAXSIM, a public simulator that lets residents model how assessment changes flow through to their tax bills. Wake County, North Carolina, deployed SAS Viya's machine learning platform using hundreds of property variables and daily sales data. These are not pilot programs; they are the new infrastructure of property taxation.

The Equity Problem Nobody Wants to Quantify

An algorithmic arms race in property taxation sounds like an efficiency story until you look at who wins and who loses when one side gets better tools faster than the other.

Research from the Minneapolis Federal Reserve found that Black and Hispanic homeowners face assessment rates 10 to 13 percent higher than white homeowners within the same jurisdiction, an excess burden of $300 to $390 per year. Separately, the Brookings Institution estimated that homes in majority-Black neighborhoods are undervalued by 21 to 23 percent, representing $162 billion in aggregate lost property value across 113 metropolitan areas.

Those findings appear to contradict each other, but they do not. Homes in predominantly Black neighborhoods carry depressed market values (Brookings) while simultaneously being over-assessed relative to those depressed values (Minneapolis Fed). Owners pay more tax as a percentage of what their home is actually worth, and the disparity compounds every year.

Minority homeowners are less likely to appeal, and when they do they win less often and receive less relief, a pattern that Northwestern University field experiments found persisted even after providing step-by-step procedural guides that successfully increased minority appeal participation rates. An AI appeal tool that charges nothing upfront could theoretically close that gap, or it could widen it if adoption patterns mirror every other fintech product released in the past decade, where early adopters skew wealthier, whiter, and more digitally connected, and the population that would benefit most discovers the tool last.

The Strongest Case for the County's Side

Counties are not villains in this story. Accurate assessments fund schools, fire departments, and the basic municipal infrastructure that keeps neighborhoods functional, and when a homeowner builds a $40,000 addition without pulling a permit, every other taxpayer in the jurisdiction absorbs the cost of the services that addition now consumes. Accurate AI assessment corrects a free-rider problem that manual inspection never could at scale.

EagleView's defenders argue, with justification, that algorithmic assessment reduces human bias. A human assessor driving through neighborhoods makes subjective judgments influenced by curb appeal and street condition, both of which correlate with the racial and economic demographics of the block. A satellite measures the addition at 200 square feet regardless of who lives in the house.

Then there is the collective-action problem: if algorithmic appeals become universal, the tax base contracts, the mill rate increases, every homeowner's effective rate climbs, and the primary beneficiaries turn out to be appeal-service companies capturing 25 percent of the savings on the way through. Counties in Texas have already begun deploying their own AI defense against appeal algorithms, and at least one competitor, TTP, filed suit against Ownwell in the Northern District of Texas in June 2025, alleging that Ownwell's quoted 2.5 percent tax rate differed from its actual algorithmic rate of 2.25 percent.

What This Means If You Are Building or Buying

If you are buying new construction, your home will be assessed for the first time using whatever AI tools your county has deployed. Your assessment will not be a rough estimate derived from neighborhood comparables and a four-minute site visit but a precise, algorithmically generated valuation incorporating every visible feature of your property cross-referenced against every recorded sale in the jurisdiction.

If you are building an addition, assume the county's AI will detect the change within one to two assessment cycles, because the days of finishing a basement and hoping nobody notices are functionally over in any county contracting with EagleView or a similar provider. Pull the permit. What used to be "maybe someone drives by" has become "an algorithm flags the discrepancy in the next flyover, and the correction includes back-assessed taxes plus penalties."

If your current assessment looks high, file an appeal, either through one of the AI services or by pulling your own comparable sales data from county records, because in contingency-fee jurisdictions the expected-value math strongly favors appealing: $500 in expected annual savings against zero downside, compounding every year you own the home.

Limitations

This analysis relies on Ownwell's self-reported success rates and savings figures; no independent audit exists, and the company's incentive to report favorable statistics is obvious. Ownwell's $774 figure is a national average masking significant regional variation in effective tax rates and appeal outcomes. EagleView's Medina County case study is a single-county result, and the $35 to $40 million figure represents gross additions to the tax rolls before appeals reduced the net impact. We could not determine how many of those 70,198 flagged discrepancies were contested or overturned. Racial disparity data from the Minneapolis Fed and Brookings uses methodologies that may not fully control for property condition differences within assessment districts, and the extent to which AI tools exacerbate or reduce those disparities remains an open empirical question.
