Eighty Percent of Building Plans Fail Their First Review. An AI Catches the Errors Before the City Opens the File.

By Catherine Chen · July 21, 2026

Julia Richman wanted to expand her house. When structural problems surfaced during her Denver renovation in 2022, she needed new permits, and she submitted her revised application just as the city rewrote its zoning regulations. Every builder in town rushed to get approvals under the old rules. Her family moved out. They paid a mortgage and rent simultaneously for seven months while the permits languished in a review queue that nobody at city hall could shorten. By the time they cleared, material costs had climbed 20%. Richman estimates the total premium for waiting at roughly $100,000.

She is now vice president of government affairs at Clariti, which makes AI-powered plan review software. She has spent the years since that experience studying exactly where the permitting machine jams, and what she keeps finding is not what most people expect.

It is not the code review itself that stalls everything. It is what happens because the plans weren't right when they showed up.

What Resubmissions Actually Cost

When the City of Surrey, British Columbia, audited its residential permit intake, the numbers were blunt. Eighty percent of applications arrived with significant zoning deficiencies, and each one averaged 1.6 resubmissions before it cleared review. Jerome Thibaudeau, Surrey's business transformation manager for planning and development, described the problem in March 2026: most plans simply weren't ready for the code they were supposed to satisfy.

Surrey isn't an outlier, and the pattern repeats across the country. In Honolulu, Dawn Takeuchi Apuna, director of the Department of Planning and Permitting, put it more plainly to GovTech in June: "I think that for more than 50 percent of the building permit plans that come in, the quality is not good." San Diego County's building division notes that about half of all submitted projects require more than two plan check cycles, stretching total permit timelines for a new single-family home to six months or longer. Washington County, Oregon, warns applicants on its website that "applications rarely meet all code requirements or standards in the first round of reviews."

Every resubmission restarts the review clock. According to PermitPlace's 2026 analysis of 741 US cities, the national median initial review takes 14 days, and the mean sits at 22.9. That figure covers just the first pass. When 80% of applications bounce back 1.6 times on average, each restart adding another two-to-three-week cycle, the actual timeline balloons far beyond what most homeowners budget for in time or patience.

I ran the numbers nobody publishes. If 80% of the roughly 1.5 million annual US housing starts trigger resubmissions averaging 1.6 rounds, and each round restarts a 14-day median review clock, the resubmission penalty alone adds 22.4 days per deficient application. At a conservative $200 to $300 per day in residential carrying costs, including construction loan interest, dual housing payments, and material price exposure, the annual nationwide cost of resubmission-driven delays falls between $5.4 billion and $8.1 billion. None of that money builds a single wall, pours a single foundation, or houses a single family.

Spell-Check for Zoning

Surrey's fix wasn't a faster reviewer. It was a smarter intake. In March 2026, the city launched Archistar's AI PreCheck, a tool that scans uploaded plan sets against local zoning and building codes in minutes, whether applicants submit PDFs, CAD files, or BIM models. Designers upload their drawings, the system flags deficiencies, they fix the plans, and they resubmit before the city clerk touches the file.

Thibaudeau calls it "spell-check for zoning." His analogy understates the complexity involved, but it captures the core function well enough: catch obvious errors while they're still cheap to fix, before they clog the review queue and restart the clock for everyone behind you in line.

Archistar isn't the only platform working this problem. Clariti's CivCheck, which Takeuchi Apuna in Honolulu describes as "TurboTax for permitting," launched there in December 2025 for residential applications, and Denver selected it in March 2026 after evaluating it against the city's own responsible technology framework. Both cities configured CivCheck as a decision-support system rather than an autonomous approval engine, checking completeness and flagging code issues at intake while a human reviewer still makes every final call.

Honolulu's Department of Planning and Permitting reports a 60 to 70 percent reduction in residential permit review times since integrating Clariti with CivCheck on the front end. Edmonton, Alberta, rolled out its own auto-review system and now issues same-day permits for single and semi-detached homes in certain zones, down from a 20-day wait. These are not incremental improvements. They are structural changes to how applications move through the system, and the gains come not from replacing human judgment but from reducing how much of it gets wasted on applications that weren't ready.

Washington Notices

Federal attention arrived in May 2026, when HUD announced grants of up to $3 million per jurisdiction for deploying automated building code permitting systems. HUD named specific platforms in the grant notice, including PermitFlow, Blitz Permits, CivCheck, and Permitify, and framed AI permitting as a housing supply intervention rather than a mere efficiency play, with applications closing July 13.

In California, Governor Newsom went further. After the Palisades and Eaton fires destroyed thousands of homes in early 2025, he partnered with Archistar, Autodesk, Amazon, and philanthropic organizations including Steadfast LA and LA Rises to provide AI plan review to LA County at no cost. Fire survivors could pre-check their rebuilding plans before submission, avoiding correction cycles that would otherwise strand families in temporary housing for months longer than necessary.

Seattle's then-mayor Bruce Harrell signed an executive order directing all development applications through an AI pilot led by a dedicated Permitting and Customer Trust team, with full public rollout expected this year. Austin deployed Archistar for residential zoning review. And the International Code Council partnered with Archistar to integrate dynamically updated ICC code content into automated checks, a collaboration that matters more than it sounds.

Mark Childs, ICC's strategic enterprise lead, pointed to a risk that should keep any city attorney awake: AI tools that scrape codes from municipal websites instead of licensing them from ICC can be 8 to 12 weeks out of date when local laws change. "If you're not dealing with fresh code content," Childs said, "you risk disseminating incorrect information. It's actually worse than doing nothing because you are providing bad information to users. You are likely increasing your risk for lawsuits and constituent dissatisfaction."

Where Pre-Check Falls Short

AI pre-check tools are not permit approval engines. They don't interpret ambiguous code, evaluate structural engineering, weigh neighborhood objections, or navigate environmental review requirements. Permitting decisions that involve judgment, context, and competing interests remain human work, and should remain so.

Denver was explicit about this boundary when selecting CivCheck: the tool "operates within parameters established by city departments, applies consistent rule-based checks tied to local codes, and generates traceable outputs that reviewers can evaluate." Its role is limited to intake support and issue identification at the pre-review stage. Removing human oversight from code compliance decisions would create liability exposure that no municipality's risk office would sign off on, and both Archistar and Clariti position their tools accordingly.

A coverage gap also limits where these tools can reach. They work best where codes are digitized, structured, and maintained by organizations like ICC. Municipalities with outdated code publications, non-standard local amendments, or handwritten variance records won't benefit from AI pre-check without first doing the unglamorous work of digitizing their regulatory landscape, and for many smaller jurisdictions that prerequisite is itself a multi-year project.

One more uncomfortable question deserves airtime: why are 80% of applications arriving broken in the first place? Some of it reflects genuine complexity, since residential codes have grown longer in every cycle and a 40-page plan set has dozens of opportunities to miss a setback requirement or a north arrow on page 23. But some of it reflects a design profession that has learned to use the correction cycle as a free consultation with the building department, treating reviewers as a backstop rather than a final gate. If AI pre-check makes the intake filter rigorous, it may shift workload from city reviewers to architects and designers, who will need to meet a standard they've been trained to treat as negotiable.

Following the Money

For a homeowner financing a $500,000 new build at current construction loan rates, every additional month of permitting delay costs roughly $3,000 to $4,500 in interest alone, before accounting for rent, storage, or material price changes. A resubmission bottleneck that adds an average of 22.4 days to the timeline, nearly three-quarters of a month, translates to $2,200 to $3,400 that never shows up on a line item, never gets explained by the builder, and never gets refunded when the permit finally comes through.

Multiply that across 1.2 million deficient applications per year. Available tools to prevent most of that waste are deployed, tested, and in several cases free. What's missing isn't the technology. It's the adoption.

New York City processes 275,000 building permit applications annually through what its own Commission on Government Efficiency described in July 2026 as a "borough-based structure that has changed little in more than a century." If the nation's largest permitting operation still runs on a system designed before electricity was standard in homes, a three-minute AI pre-check isn't going to disrupt anyone's authority. It's going to save everyone's time.

Limitations

Several caveats apply to the analysis above. Surrey's 80% deficiency rate comes from one municipality; it aligns with Honolulu's 50%+ estimate and San Diego's "about half" figure, but a rigorous national survey of application deficiency rates doesn't exist. Residential carrying cost estimates of $200 to $300 per day derive from Richman's documented $100,000 over seven months (approximately $476/day) on the high end and more typical dual-housing scenarios on the low end; actual costs vary by market, loan terms, and housing situation. My resubmission cost calculation assumes each resubmission fully restarts the review clock, which varies by jurisdiction, as some allow expedited re-review while others do not. Housing starts of 1.5 million are a Census Bureau baseline, and actual 2026 starts may differ. Archistar's and CivCheck's effectiveness data comes from municipal partners, not independent evaluation, and no randomized study of AI pre-check tools has been published to date.
