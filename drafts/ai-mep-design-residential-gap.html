A $75 Million AI Can Design Your Building's Plumbing, Wiring, and Fire Alarms in Hours. It Doesn't Work on Houses.

By Jake "Jackhammer" Kowalski · July 19, 2026

In thirteen months, a Stockholm startup called Endra raised $75 million to teach machines how to route pipes, pull wire, size ductwork, and place fire alarms inside a 3D building model. The pre-seed was €3 million in May 2025. The seed was $20 million in December. The Series A, led by Andreessen Horowitz, closed in June at $50 million. That is faster capitalization than most construction companies see in a decade, and the money went to solving a problem that MEP engineers have been grinding through by hand since the profession existed: take an architect's model, figure out where every mechanical, electrical, and plumbing system goes, make sure none of them crash into each other, produce the drawings and calculations that prove code compliance, and do it all before the contractor starts ordering material.

Endra says it can do that in hours. Pilot projects reported 70x speed improvements. Import the Revit file, set the rules for each system, hit run, and out comes a clash-free 3D model with coordinated schematics, schedules, and documentation. Andreessen Horowitz called MEP design a $150-billion-plus global services market "constrained by the capacity of human engineers." Early customers compared the shift from legacy tools to Endra as going from riding a horse to going to the moon.

Exciting stuff. Genuinely transformative for data centers, hospitals, and the office towers where MEP engineering budgets run into six and seven figures. Which brings me to the part that matters for people reading this site.

None of it works on your house.

The Systems Nobody Talks About

When people think about home construction technology, they think about the visible stuff. Floor plans generated by AI. Drones scanning a roof. Robots laying brick. The trades that get covered are the ones that make good photographs.

MEP is not one of those trades. MEP is the plumbing running through your floor joists. It is the 200-amp electrical panel in your garage, the circuit that feeds your kitchen island, the branch duct that delivers conditioned air to the back bedroom. It is the gas line to your range, the condensate drain from your air handler, the low-voltage wiring for your doorbell. These systems occupy roughly 20% of your home's construction cost, consume the vast majority of its wall and ceiling cavities, and generate more change orders than any other trade on the job.

On a commercial project worth $50 million, MEP coordination is a formal discipline. There is a mechanical engineer, an electrical engineer, a plumbing engineer, and often a fire protection engineer. They work in BIM, a shared 3D model where every pipe, duct, and conduit has a precise location and diameter. When one system's route conflicts with another, the software flags a clash. They resolve it before a single stud is cut.

On a $500,000 custom home, MEP coordination is a conversation in the driveway between the plumber and the HVAC sub, usually after the framing is up.

The Cost of the Driveway Conversation

I have asked dozens of residential general contractors the same question: what percentage of your change orders come from MEP coordination failures? The answers cluster between 5% and 10% of total project cost. On a $500,000 home, that is $25,000 to $50,000 in rework, delays, and workarounds that were not in anyone's estimate.

The failures fall into patterns. The HVAC trunk line runs through the same floor truss bay as the main waste stack, and nobody noticed until the plumber showed up. The electrical panel ends up in the garage corner farthest from the kitchen, adding 60 feet of copper wire to every circuit. A duct run drops the ceiling height in a bedroom to 7 feet 4 inches, which is legal but makes the room feel like a submarine. The dryer vent takes three 90-degree turns because the laundry room moved during design and nobody updated the MEP plan, which was never a formal document in the first place, just a set of notes on the architectural drawing that the plumber glances at on his phone screen while sitting in his truck.

5% to 10% of total project cost in change orders from MEP coordination failures, because the plumber, electrician, and HVAC sub are all fighting over the same 14-inch wall cavity

This is not negligence. It is the economics of residential construction. A formal MEP engineering package for a custom home runs $5,000 to $15,000 depending on complexity, takes three to six weeks, and requires an architect's model detailed enough for the engineer to work from. Most custom homes do not have that model. Most production homes never had an MEP engineer touch the plan at all. The systems get installed based on the sub's experience, the builder's standard practices, and whatever adjustments happen in the field when two systems try to occupy the same space.

$150 Billion Upstream, Zero Downstream

Endra targets the $150 billion MEP services market. Reasonable framing. The problem is that almost all of that market sits upstream of residential construction. MEP engineering firms work on hospitals, data centers, labs, multifamily towers, and office buildings where the mechanical complexity and regulatory requirements justify dedicated engineering consultants. A hospital's MEP scope might run $5 million. A 200-unit apartment building, $2 million. A data center, where cooling and power distribution are the entire building, can easily exceed $10 million in MEP design and coordination.

A 2,500-square-foot single-family home? Zero. Because nobody is paying a separate MEP consultant for a house with one furnace, one water heater, two bathrooms, and a kitchen.

That is the gap. Commercial construction has a $150 billion services market, and venture capitalists are pouring money into automating it because the unit economics work. Each building is complex enough and expensive enough to justify a software subscription that costs tens of thousands per year. Residential construction has a different problem: millions of individual homes where the MEP complexity is lower but the coordination cost per unit is not proportionally lower, because the failure mode is the same. A pipe and a duct occupying the same space does not care whether the building costs $500,000 or $50 million.

What Drafted Gets Right and Wrong

While Endra goes deep on MEP engineering for commercial buildings, a San Francisco startup called Drafted raised $16 million in seed funding in June to let homeowners "vibe code" a house. Type in lot size, room count, and style preferences, and the AI generates floor plans and 3D renderings. That money went to the front end of residential design. The part people can see, touch, and get excited about on a screen.

Nobody raised $16 million to vibe code the plumbing.

This reveals a pattern in construction technology investment that has been stable for years: money flows to the parts of building that are visible to buyers, investors, and social media. Design visualization gets funded. Marketing automation gets funded. Virtual staging and virtual tours get funded. The systems inside the walls, the ones that actually determine whether your home functions, heats, drains, and does not electrocute you, remain the province of experienced tradespeople working from their own knowledge, not from coordinated models.

Drafted's bet is that generative AI can lower the barrier to residential design. Fair enough. But a floor plan with no MEP coordination baked in is a rendering that becomes a change order the moment a framer starts building walls without knowing where the ducts go. Design without systems integration is a picture, not a plan.

Why the Gap Exists

Four reasons residential MEP has not attracted the kind of venture investment that commercial MEP has.

First, the data problem. Endra works by ingesting Revit models, the industry standard for BIM. Commercial projects live in Revit. Residential projects, overwhelmingly, do not. A custom home architect might use Revit, but most production builders work in Chief Architect, SoftPlan, or increasingly, AI tools that output 2D plans. There is no standardized 3D model for Endra's software to ingest even if someone wanted to run it on a house.

Second, the code problem. MEP code compliance in commercial buildings is governed by the International Mechanical Code, National Electrical Code, and Uniform Plumbing Code, with local amendments that vary by jurisdiction but follow a standard structure. Residential MEP is governed by the International Residential Code, which has its own set of rules that differ materially from commercial codes. Minimum pipe sizes, circuit requirements, duct sizing methods, equipment clearances, ventilation rates. An AI trained on commercial MEP rules would produce wrong answers for a house.

Third, the pricing problem. Endra and tools like it charge enterprise pricing, anywhere from $50,000 to several hundred thousand dollars per year. A residential builder running 10 to 20 homes a year cannot justify that number. The economics only work if the tool costs closer to $200 per home, and nobody has built that product yet.

Fourth, the culture problem. Residential subs are not going to open a laptop and check a 3D model before they rough in a wall. The plumber who has been running drain lines for twenty years knows where the pipes go. The problem is that his knowledge is in his head, not in a model, and when the HVAC crew arrives the next day with their own mental model of the wall cavity, nobody has a shared source of truth to resolve the conflict until both crews are standing there looking at the same 14-inch stud bay.

What Would Actually Help

The technology Endra is building proves something important: MEP coordination is an automatable problem. The physics is well understood. The code requirements are deterministic. The spatial constraints are three-dimensional math. If AI can route fire suppression through a 30-story hospital, it can route plumbing through a two-story house. The question is not capability. It is willingness to build for a market where the contract values are smaller and the users are less digitally native.

What residential builders need is not a simplified version of Endra. They need a tool built from scratch for the residential code environment, one that takes a 2D floor plan, not a Revit model, and generates a coordinated MEP layout that shows where every pipe, duct, and wire goes, with clash detection included. It needs to cost less than $500 per home. It needs to output something a plumber can read on a phone screen, because that is the reality of how residential MEP information gets consumed on a job site.

The tool does not exist yet. I looked. There are HVAC load calculators (Manual J, Manual D) that size equipment and ductwork. There are electrical load calculators that determine panel sizing. There are plumbing design tools from companies like Watts and BIM Collab. But none of them coordinate across systems. They each solve their own silo and leave the spatial coordination to the subs.

If AI can route fire suppression through a 30-story hospital, it can route plumbing through a two-story house. Nobody has bothered to build the product.

Someone will. The data center construction boom is training a generation of AI models on MEP coordination at massive scale, and the training data will eventually produce architectures that scale down. When that happens, the residential version will not look like Endra with a smaller price tag. It will look more like a mobile app that a builder opens on the job site, feeds a floor plan into, and gets back an annotated diagram showing the mechanical room layout, trunk duct routing, main stack location, circuit panel position, and every point where two systems want the same space. Price: a few hundred dollars. Time: minutes.

That product would eliminate the driveway conversation. It would kill the 5% to 10% change order premium. It would let a homebuyer look at their plan before construction and know, with specificity, where every pipe and wire in their home is going to run.

Right now, the closest thing to that product is a plumber with thirty years of experience and a good working relationship with the HVAC crew. That works until it does not, and when it does not, you pay $25,000 to $50,000 to find out.

What to Do If You Are Building a Custom Home

Until the residential MEP tool exists, here is what you can do. Ask your builder, before framing starts, for an MEP coordination drawing. Not the architectural plan with some plumbing notes on it. A separate drawing that shows the HVAC duct routing, plumbing waste and supply lines, and electrical panel location with home runs for major circuits. If they cannot produce one, ask which of their subs is responsible for ensuring there are no conflicts. If the answer is "we work it out in the field," budget an additional 5% to 8% for change orders. Because you will have them. You just will not know it until the drywall is up and someone cuts a hole to move a duct.

If you are building a home over $750,000 in construction cost, consider hiring your own MEP consultant. Yes, the $8,000 to $15,000 fee feels steep. But it is a fraction of what you will spend resolving the conflicts that a coordinated plan would have caught. Not every home needs one. Not every market has qualified residential MEP engineers available. But if your home has a complex mechanical system, radiant floor heating, a dedicated HVAC zone for a large basement, multiple panel locations, or any hydronic system at all, the upfront engineering pays for itself and usually more.

And if your builder tells you that MEP coordination is handled, ask them how. If the answer involves a 3D model with clash detection, you found a good one. If the answer involves a phone call between the plumber and the HVAC sub, you found a normal one. Both can produce a great house. Only one of them has a plan for what happens when two systems want the same wall.
