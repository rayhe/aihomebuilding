## Critic Scores

### 1. 🔍 General Editor — 8.5/10

**Strengths:**
- Cold open is textbook trade journalism: a specific builder scenario with real numbers ($3,200 upgrade, $5,000 credit, $1,800 net gain, $360,000 across a subdivision). Reader knows exactly what's at stake before the second paragraph.
- "On July 1, the credit vanished." — One of the best punch lines in the article. Five words, maximum impact.
- Structure flows cleanly: what happened → builder math → buyer math → vacuum → actionable advice → limitations. Each section earns its place.

**Weaknesses:**
- The "What Fills the Vacuum" section tries to cover state programs, code advancement, and market demand in three paragraphs. Each gets slightly cursory treatment as a result, though this is a reasonable trade-off against word count.
- The hyperlink text "an estimated $1.7 billion over seven years according to the most recent Joint Committee on Taxation score" is long and awkward as a single clickable phrase — readers expect link text to be a noun or short descriptor, not an entire clause.

**Blocking issues:** None.

### 3. ⚖️ Ethics Reviewer — 9.0/10

**Strengths:**
- Politically neutral framing: mentions "signed by President Trump" as a factual matter without editorializing. Does not characterize the OBBB as good or bad — just reports what it did and calculates the consequences.
- The counterargument (market demand, resale premiums, code advancement) is given genuine weight, not strawmanned. "A builder who drops Energy Star to save $3,000 in construction cost and loses $10,000 in resale value is not saving money" — this is actually the strongest argument against the article's implied thesis.
- No self-congratulation, no moral preening, no "we must act now" urgency signaling.

**Weaknesses:**
- Minor tension: the policy paragraph claims "Homes that will not be built to Energy Star as a result will consume the budget savings in excess energy costs within a few years" — this assumes the credit termination directly causes builders to stop building to Energy Star, which the article's own market-demand section partially undermines. The causal link is plausible but not airtight. Not an ethical failure — more a logical one.

**Blocking issues:** None.

### 4. 📱 Social/Shareability — 8.5/10

**Strengths:**
- Headline is outstanding for social sharing: "$945 Million a Year" is concrete and shocking; "Eleven Days Ago" creates urgency; "Doing the Math" implies practical, not political.
- The $15–$20/mo stat is extremely shareable — it reframes a complex federal policy change as a monthly dollar amount any homebuyer can understand.
- OG/Twitter descriptions are tight: "The 45L credit made Energy Star homes pencil out. It expired June 30. Builders are recalculating." — three sentences, complete story arc.

**Weaknesses:**

- Could benefit from one more quotable moment in the mid-article — something between the credit table and the buyer math.

**Blocking issues:** None.

### 5. ⚖️ Legal Accuracy — 8.5/10

**Strengths:**
- Credit expirations are accurately stated per IRS Form 8908 instructions (updated 12/2025) and NAHB guidance.
- The "acquired" interpretation (legal title transfer, not contract date) is correctly attributed to both NAHB and ASE's analysis of IRS guidance.
- 45L credit amounts ($2,500 base / $5,000 with prevailing wage for ZERH) are consistent with Energy Star's FAQ and IRS documentation.
- Links now point to specific NAHB blog, ACEEE fact sheet, and ASE analysis rather than generic homepages.

**Weaknesses:**
- The Executive Order link text says "executive order dated July 7, 2025" but the href goes to the NAHB blog post summarizing it (`nahb.org/blog/2025/07/expiring-energy-tax-credits`), not the actual Executive Order. Clicking the link expecting EO text delivers NAHB commentary. Not legally wrong — NAHB does quote the EO — but slightly misleading as a user experience.
- "The OBBB clawed six of those years back" — technically closer to 6.5 years (June 30, 2026 vs December 31, 2032). Defensible rounding in trade writing, but imprecise.

**Blocking issues:** None.

### 6. 🔬 Research Rigor — 8.5/10

**Strengths:**
- **Original contribution present:** The builder break-even synthesis — combining ACEEE credit data ($2,700 avg credit, $2,000-$4,000 incremental cost) with NAHB regulatory burden ($131,734, 26.4% of sale price) to argue the voluntary green premium becomes untenable — is a novel analysis. Nobody else published this specific cross-dataset comparison.
- **Methodology transparency:** The $240M/year figure is now derived transparently ($1.7B ÷ 7 years), attributed to JCT scoring cited by ACEEE, and linked to the specific fact sheet.
- **Limitations section** is honest and specific: data vintage (2024), cost variability by climate zone, state program flux, and the important caveat that NAHB's $131,734 includes all regulation, not just energy mandates.
- **Counterargument** (market demand, resale premiums 2-8%, rising codes) is stated at full strength without immediate dismissal.

**Weaknesses:**
- "Green-certified homes sell for 2 to 8 percent more than comparable non-certified homes" — this claim has no source link. The Round 0 draft linked to NAR's homepage (generic); the revision removed the generic link but didn't replace it with a specific study. This is a verifiability gap per STORY_GUIDE rules.
- The diesel +105.9% YoY figure appears without source attribution. The research notes indicate it's from BLS PPI data via NAHB, but the article doesn't say so. Minor but notable given the "every claim gets a source" standard.
- The "$3,300 monthly PITI payment" in the stat callout is uncalculated — plausible for a $499,500 home at 6.5% with taxes/insurance, but not shown.

**Blocking issues:** None. The unsourced resale premium claim is the most significant gap but appears in the counterargument section, not as a central claim of the article. The diesel figure and PITI number are defensible contextual data points.

### 7. 📊 Data Presentation — 8.5/10

**Strengths:**
- The credit expiration table is clean, scannable, and efficiently delivers five data points that would take three paragraphs of prose.
- Both stat callouts (350,000 homes; $15–$20/mo net benefit) effectively anchor the article's key numbers — one macro, one micro.
- Numbers are consistently specific throughout: $131,734, 26.4%, 105.9%, $2,700, $3,200, $5,000. No vague "thousands" or "significant percentage" language.
- The $20/mo mortgage calculation is transparent: the reader can verify it ($3,000 addition, 6.5% rate, 30-year term).

**Weaknesses:**
- The 350,000 stat callout label is dense — it packs three data points (the ACEEE source, $2,700 average credit, $945M total) into one block of text. Splitting the $945M into its own element or simplifying the label would improve scannability.
- No visual representation of the before/after economics (credit → no credit). A simple two-column comparison could make the builder math section more immediately digestible, though word count constraints make this difficult.

**Blocking issues:** None.
