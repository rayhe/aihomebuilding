# Research: Smart Panel New Construction Code Gap

## Angle
The 2026 NEC now formally recognizes Power Control Systems (PCS) that let smart electrical panels reduce calculated loads — meaning a 200A panel can handle what previously required 400A service. But the vast majority of new homes are still being built with the same dumb $50 load centers that haven't fundamentally changed since the 1950s. The code caught up. The builders didn't.

## Journalist
Catherine Chen — NEC 2026 code changes, UL 3141 certification, regulatory/builder gap

## Kill Test
✅ If you're building or buying a new home: should you demand a smart panel? What's the marginal cost? What does it save in the first 5 years when you inevitably add an EV charger, heat pump, or battery system?

## Primary Sources

### 1. NEC 2026 — Article 130 & Section 120.7
- New Article 130 covers Power Control Systems (PCS), relocated and expanded from Article 750
- Section 120.7: PCS can now be used for branch circuit, feeder, and service load calculations
- PCS control setting must be ≤80% of overcurrent protective device rating (120.7(B))
- Complex load calc provisions for controlled vs. noncontrolled loads (120.7(C))
- Example in NEC Informative Annex D.15: dwelling with 29,040 VA total service load (121A at 240V)
- Source: ecmag.com (Mark C. Ode analysis), Eaton blog, pv-magazine-usa.com

### 2. UL 3141 — First PCS Safety Standard
- Published by Underwriters Laboratories in response to growing PCS complexity
- Builds on and incorporates prior UL standards for energy management devices
- Required for PCS applications in 2026 NEC
- Span: first smart panel manufacturer certified (all 4 newest models), Oct 2025
- Source: solarpowerworldonline.com, utilitydive.com

### 3. Builder Economics (Span/PulteGroup)
- Span panels can save builders $3,000-$10,000 per home by avoiding 400A utility service
- PulteGroup (top 3 US builder) deploying Span panels: "Our program with SPAN has been very successful"
- Kirk Hammersmith, National Director of Procurement at Pulte: "We are considering smart electric panels with advanced load management in many of our communities"
- Source: businesswire.com (Feb 2025)

### 4. Eaton $75M Investment in Span (March 2026)
- Strategic partnership: Span's smart energy tech in Eaton's forthcoming smart panel
- Eaton's circuit and surge protection integrated into Span panels
- Product updates expected Q2 2026
- Eaton expects residential energy consumption to increase 65% by 2030
- EV charging: 40-60% of home energy usage
- Heath Monesmith, Eaton COO Electrical: "Energy affordability and electrification require smart energy management"
- Source: pv-magazine-usa.com

### 5. Panel Upgrade Costs (Existing Homes)
- Panel-only upgrade: $3,000-$5,000 (NYSERDA)
- Service upgrade (incl. utility work): $4,000-$6,000+ (Lumin Smart)
- Major infrastructure (transformer replacement): $10,000-$100,000+ (Lumin/Las Vegas case)
- Wait times: weeks to 9 months depending on utility
- Span PowerUp: avoids $3,000-$20,000 service upgrades (NW Electric & Solar)

### 6. Market Data
- US smart panel market: $2.4B in 2024 (Research and Markets)
- Key players: Siemens, Schneider, Eaton, Span, Lumin, Leviton, EcoFlow
- "Standard electrical panel has not seen major innovation for nearly a century" — Arch Rao, Span CEO

### 7. DOE/LBNL Research
- DOE-funded project: "Affordable and Equitable Residential Electrification Under Electrical Panel and Service Constraints"
- NLR and LBNL characterizing the population of homes requiring panel upgrades
- Developing low-cost digital capacity management solution
- Proposing changes to NEC to advance affordable electrification
- Source: energy.gov

### 8. NEC 220.82 Optional Method
- ChargeRight (Jason Walls, Master Electrician, IBEW Local 369): 70% of homes already have capacity for L2 EV charger using NEC 220.82
- Standard Method vs. Optional Method: same 2,400 sqft home shows 149A vs 204A — a 23-amp gap
- That gap = $3,000-$5,000 unnecessary panel upgrade
- Source: YouTube (ChargeRight)

### 9. PG&E PanelBoost Program
- Deploying Span Edge for existing homes
- Dynamic Service Rating™ shapes home energy demand during peak events
- "PG&E just made it possible to effectively have a 200-amp panel by throttling the loads" — installer
- Source: morningstar.com/Span blog (Feb 2026)

## Original Contribution
Cross-reference NEC 2026 PCS provisions with actual builder adoption. Calculate the marginal cost differential: smart panel (~$3,500-$5,000 installed) vs. traditional panel (~$500-$1,000) in new construction = ~$3,000-$4,000 delta. Compare to the near-certain cost of a service upgrade within 5 years (EV + heat pump): $5,000-$15,000+. The math doesn't just favor smart panels — it makes the traditional panel a deferred liability hiding in your spec sheet.

## Strongest Counterargument
Smart panels add software dependency and cloud connectivity to safety-critical infrastructure. If Span goes under, your panel's "smart" features become dead weight. The panel still works as a basic breaker box, but the load management that justified the smaller service dies with the company. Internet-required operation (Span requires internet for control) means a connectivity outage could affect your ability to manage your own electrical system. Traditional panels are dumb and reliable — they'll outlive any startup.

## Limitations
- No public data on what percentage of new construction homes are being spec'd with smart panels
- Builder cost savings figures ($3K-$10K) come from Span's own press materials
- The $75M Eaton investment doesn't guarantee market success or product availability
- NEC 2026 adoption varies by state — some jurisdictions still on 2020 or 2023 NEC
- Long-term reliability data for smart panels in residential applications is thin (product category is <5 years old)
