Your Builder Installed Seven Smoke Detectors and Zero Leak Sensors. Water Causes More Claims.

By Frank DeLuca July 22, 2026

I have watched a plumber rough in 200 feet of copper and PEX through a two-story home in about six hours. He connects the main line, runs branches to every fixture, pressure-tests the system at 80 psi, and waits fifteen minutes to confirm nothing leaks. Then the drywall crew buries all of it behind gypsum board, and for the next thirty years the homeowner has exactly zero visibility into what that plumbing is doing unless a stain blooms on the ceiling or the water bill triples in August.

Meanwhile, down the hall, an electrician installs seven smoke detectors, two carbon monoxide alarms, and wires them into a monitored circuit, because the building code says he must. Those detectors cost the builder roughly $250 total in materials and about an hour of labor. Fire and lightning account for 21.9 percent of all homeowner insurance claims in the United States, according to Insurance Information Institute data for 2019 through 2023.

Water damage and freezing account for 22.6 percent. More.

Your builder installed nothing to detect it.

What a Stain on the Ceiling Actually Costs

Five years of claims data ending in 2023, compiled by the Insurance Information Institute and ISO, tell a story that every builder should know by heart. Water damage and freezing ranked as the second most common cause of homeowner insurance claims in America, trailing only wind and hail. An average water damage claim ran $15,400, a figure that climbed 10 percent from the prior five-year window of $13,954. Across the entire insurance industry, water damage costs carriers approximately $13 billion per year, according to National Association of Insurance Commissioners data.

About 14,000 Americans experience a water damage emergency at home every single day. One in sixty insured homes files a water damage or freezing claim each year. CoreLogic extends the window further: one in fifteen insured homes will face a non-weather-related water damage claim within a typical five-year policy period. An average leak goes undetected for more than 75 days, according to EMC Security's claims analysis, silently rotting subfloor, feeding mold colonies, and soaking insulation that the homeowner paid $4 a square foot to have blown into the walls six months earlier.

Hidden leaks waste roughly 10,000 gallons of water per household per year, according to EPA estimates. FEMA calculates that a single inch of standing water in a typical home causes up to $25,000 in damage. And here is the number that should embarrass every general contractor in the country: fewer than 20 percent of homeowners take any precautionary steps to protect their homes against water damage, according to a ConsumerAffairs analysis of industry data.

Nobody told them to, because every builder who handed over the keys walked the homeowner through the thermostat programming, the smoke detector test button, the garage door opener frequency, the sprinkler controller zones, and the warranty binder full of appliance manuals, and never once mentioned that a $350 device existed that could detect the single most common non-weather cause of property damage before it ate through the subfloor and into the joists.

A Device Your Plumber Already Knows How to Install

Smart water monitoring is not experimental technology. Moen's Flo Smart Water Shutoff installs on the main water line and uses machine learning to monitor pressure, flow rate, and temperature continuously. It detects leaks as small as one drop per minute through what Moen calls Microleak Technology, and it can automatically shut off the home's water supply when it identifies a catastrophic failure, and it has been commercially available since 2019.

Phyn Plus works differently, measuring 240 pressure and flow readings per second to build a hydraulic fingerprint of every fixture in the home. Flume clamps non-invasively onto the water meter and reads ultrasonic flow data without cutting a single pipe. Bluebot offers whole-house monitoring for $200 to $600 with no subscription fees. LeakBot clips onto the main water pipe in under two minutes using thermal sensing technology that detects flow anomalies as small as three milliliters per minute.

Moen even built a feature called Local Mode, specifically designed for construction sites. It works without WiFi, requiring only pressurized pipes and AC power. "Many job sites don't have Wi-Fi up and running during the construction or renovation process," explained Suzy Street, a product manager at Moen, in a press release. "With Local Mode, a Wi-Fi connection isn't needed for the Smart Water Shutoff to be effective in helping prevent water damage." Moen also sells Spacer Kits that let plumbers pre-plumb a home for future device installation during rough-in, when the pipes are exposed, the access is trivial, and the incremental labor cost is measured in minutes.

Technology, installation pathways, and construction-phase integration all exist, and almost nobody uses any of it.

Running Your Builder's Missing Calculation

Here is the math that should appear in every builder's pro forma and does not.

A Moen Flo or Phyn Plus device costs $250 to $400 at retail. During construction, when the main water line is exposed and the plumber is already on site, installation takes five to ten minutes and costs roughly $50 in labor. Total investment: $300 to $450, call it $375 at the midpoint.

Retrofit that same device after the homeowner moves in, and the price balloons to $400 to $900. A plumber has to schedule a service call, locate the main shutoff, sometimes cut into finished drywall to access the pipe, and charge $150 to $500 for labor alone.

Running the expected value: one in sixty insured homes files a water damage claim each year, and the average claim costs $15,400. That is an expected annual loss of $257 per home, before you even consider insurance premium discounts. Carriers offering 5 to 10 percent reductions for homes with certified leak detection, applied to the national average homeowner premium of approximately $2,200, produce $110 to $220 in annual savings. A June 2026 partnership between PEMCO Mutual Insurance and Moen goes further, offering members up to $1,000 annually toward covered plumbing repairs on top of the device discount.

Combined, the expected annual benefit of a $375 sensor installed during construction is roughly $422: $257 in avoided expected loss plus $165 at the midpoint insurance discount. That puts the payback period at 10.7 months. Over a 30-year mortgage, discounted at 3 percent, the present value of that benefit stream exceeds $8,200 against a $375 initial investment.

For a production builder constructing 1,000 homes a year, this scales linearly. A $375,000 investment in smart water shutoffs across the portfolio Claims exposure reduced: $257,000 annually in expected losses across the portfolio, plus the marketing value of offering a differentiated product in a market where every subdivision looks identical. Warranty callbacks drop, insurance partnerships generate referral revenue, and all of it costs less than the landscaping budget for the model home.

One Builder Did It. Harder Than Expected.

Lennar, the largest homebuilder in the United States by volume, partnered with Moen to install Flo Smart Water Shutoff devices in newly built homes. Gabriel Halimi, CEO of Flo Technologies, framed the partnership bluntly: "Forty percent of homeowners will suffer from some type of water damage. Forty percent."

But that partnership revealed a problem nobody in the builder-tech pipeline had anticipated. According to reporting by Green Building & Design magazine, "because the devices were installed long ahead of move-in, the homeowners didn't always understand the smart system or utilize it fully. To the unknowing user, it's a copper pipe with a retrofitted doodad in the basement utility closet." Mark Rippee, a Moen executive, acknowledged the gap: "It's a critical piece for us as we continue on in years to come, to engage with builders and find the right ways to partner with them and give them enough product knowledge to show homeowners how to provision the device and get in control of their water usage."

In other words, the technology worked exactly as designed, monitoring flow rates, catching pressure anomalies, and standing ready to shut off the water supply before a failed fitting could soak the first-floor ceiling insulation and migrate through the drywall into the framing. Onboarding did not, and so devices sat installed but unactivated, walkthroughs skipped them, sales teams never featured them, and closing documents buried them. A $350 investment that could prevent a $15,400 claim sat dormant in a mechanical closet because the construction process treats plumbing as infrastructure and infrastructure as invisible.

Why No Code Requires This

Smoke detectors were first required by building code in the late 1970s, after decades of fire-death data made the case unassailable. Carbon monoxide detectors followed, mandated in most states by the 2010s after a slower but equally data-driven push. In January 2023, Connecticut passed HB 6848, mandating combustible gas detectors in all new construction. Each of these mandates followed exactly the same arc that water leak detection is tracing now: a hazard was documented over decades of accumulating mortality and property-loss data, a detection technology matured from expensive laboratory equipment into a mass-market consumer product, the per-unit cost dropped below a threshold that made builder and code-body opposition untenable, and the building code eventually caught up to what the actuarial tables had been screaming for a generation.

Water leak detection is sitting at step three. Hazard documented: 22.6 percent of all claims, $13 billion a year, 14,000 incidents daily. Technology matured: multiple commercially proven devices from Fortune 500 manufacturers with seven years of field deployment. Cost dropped: $200 to $400 for a device that addresses the most common cause of non-weather property damage. But no code has caught up.

Not a single state in the United States requires a water leak detection sensor in new residential construction. Not California, which mandates water-conserving fixtures and submetering in multifamily buildings under Health and Safety Code Section 17922.14. Not Florida, which requires water-level detection devices for water heaters in multifamily buildings but exempts single-family homes. Not Connecticut, which just mandated gas detectors but said nothing about water sensors.

Neither the International Residential Code nor any state amendment mentions smart water monitoring. Uniform Plumbing Code requires a pressure test at rough-in, the same fifteen-minute static test that confirms the system does not leak on the day it was installed, and considers the plumbing fully commissioned when it passes. What happens on day two, or day two hundred, or day two thousand, is the homeowner's problem.

What To Do With This Information

If you are building a home: ask your general contractor to install a Moen Flo, Phyn Plus, or equivalent smart water shutoff on the main line during rough-in. Expect to pay $300 to $450 for the device and installation combined. Verify that the device is included in the homeowner walkthrough and that someone shows you how to connect it to WiFi and activate monitoring before you sign the certificate of occupancy. Call your insurance carrier before closing and ask whether a leak detection system qualifies for a premium discount. Get it in writing.

If you are buying a production home: ask the sales office whether smart water monitoring is included. If it is not, ask why. You are buying a house with seven smoke detectors, two CO alarms, a GFCI outlet in every bathroom, and an arc-fault breaker on every bedroom circuit, all mandated by code, and zero protection against the second most common insurance claim in the country.

If you are a builder: run the math on your next community. A hundred homes at $375 per unit is $37,500, roughly the cost of the stone veneer on the clubhouse. Insurance partnership revenue, warranty callback reduction, and marketing differentiation will cover it in the first year. Moen's Insurance Partner Program is open to any size carrier, and the company claims a 96 percent reduction in water damage claim events for homes equipped with the device.

Limitations

Moen's 96 percent claim reduction figure comes from marketing materials and has not been independently verified in a peer-reviewed study. Expected-value calculations here use national averages for claim frequency and severity; actual risk varies significantly by climate, construction type, plumbing material, and soil conditions. Premium discount ranges of 5 to 10 percent are reported by device manufacturers and industry publications, but individual carriers set their own discount schedules, and not all carriers offer one. This analysis does not account for potential failure modes of the sensors themselves, including false-positive shutoffs that could interrupt water service, sensor drift over time, or WiFi-dependent features that fail during internet outages. Moen's Local Mode addresses WiFi issues during construction but not during occupancy. Lennar's experience suggests that installation without education produces underutilized systems, which reduces effective benefit below the theoretical maximum.

Against mandating leak sensors, the strongest counterargument is this: they add complexity and potential failure points to a plumbing system designed to be passive and maintenance-free. A stuck shutoff valve activated by a false positive at 2 AM while the homeowner is traveling creates its own damage scenario. Unlike a smoke detector, which has a single binary output, alarm or no alarm, a smart water valve makes a physical intervention in the plumbing system that can itself cause harm if the algorithm is wrong. Industry reluctance to mandate these devices is not entirely irrational. It is, however, increasingly expensive.
