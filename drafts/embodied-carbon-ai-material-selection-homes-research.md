# Research: AI-Powered Embodied Carbon Tracking in Residential Construction

## Angle
Your new home dumped 55 tons of CO2 into the atmosphere before you moved in. Nobody counted it. Now AI tools connected to EPD databases are finally making embodied carbon visible — and they're revealing that the biggest carbon villain in your house isn't your HVAC system. It's your foundation.

## Journalist: Priya Greenwood (Sustainability & Green Building)

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? YES — directly tells builders which materials drive embodied carbon and what substitutions save the most, with specific cost/carbon tradeoffs.

## Primary Sources

### 1. Massachusetts 100-Home Embodied Carbon Study (December 2025)
- Source: NEHERS Alliance, Stephens & Company, Ekotrope, Builders for Climate Action, NMR Group
- Funded by: MassCEC, National Grid, Eversource
- First study to measure both operational and embodied carbon at meaningful scale (100 newly built single-family homes)
- **Key findings:**
  - Average 55.5 tons CO2e per home (embodied, cradle-to-gate A1-A3)
  - 100 homes = 5,555 total tons CO2e = equivalent of driving a gas car 14 million miles
  - Over 25 years, embodied carbon = up to 32% of total lifetime emissions
  - Operational emissions average 5.5 tons CO2e annually per home
  - Massachusetts built 11,390 homes in 2024 → 632,000+ tons CO2e in embodied carbon that year alone
  - 60-70% of data needed for embodied carbon assessments already exists in HERS energy modeling files
  - Concrete and foundations are the dominant source of embodied carbon
- Test-drive for RESNET/ICC 1550 Standard (first embodied carbon standard for low-rise residential)
- Cited: JLC Staff, Journal of Light Construction, March 5, 2026

### 2. BEAM Estimator Tool (Builders for Climate Action)
- Building Emissions Accounting for Materials
- Used in the Massachusetts 100-Home study
- Contains comprehensive list of materials/products with carbon footprints
- Can model assemblies or entire buildings
- v1.1 released
- Described as "the right-sized tool for most small to mid-scale projects" by David Arkin AIA
- Integration worksheet transfers data from Ekotrope (HERS rating software) into BEAM
- Source: buildersforclimateaction.org

### 3. EC3 (Embodied Carbon in Construction Calculator) — Building Transparency
- Free, open-access tool
- Integrated with Autodesk Construction Cloud
- Over 200,000 EPDs (Environmental Product Declarations) in database
- Allows builders to compare carbon footprint of functionally equivalent materials
- Specific products, not just generic categories — e.g., comparing concrete from Plant A vs Plant B
- Source: Autodesk blog, Building Transparency

### 4. RMI Embodied Carbon 101
- Building materials responsible for 11% of global GHG emissions (AIA cite)
- Construction industry: 11% of total global carbon emissions from manufacture of materials
- Annual building operations: additional 28% of global emissions
- As operational efficiency improves, embodied carbon's proportional share grows
- Carbon-storing materials: wood, hemp, straw, bamboo, algae
- Source: RMI (Rocky Mountain Institute), Audrey Rempher et al.

### 5. UK Embodied Carbon of Concrete Study
- Led by Alice Moncaster (Open University) and Jane Anderson
- Reviewed hundreds of verified EPDs for cements, aggregates, ready-mix concretes
- Found significant inconsistencies and errors within EPDs
- Infinite formulations for concrete → no single carbon footprint value
- Need for detailed rules for consistency across analyses
- Source: American Ceramic Society, July 2024 (citing papers in Buildings and Cities journal, 2020-2022)

### 6. RESNET/ICC Standard 1550
- First embodied carbon standard developed for low-rise residential sector
- Draft PDS-02 opened September 2025
- Would enable HERS Raters (who already visit ~88% of new MA homes) to add embodied carbon to their ratings
- Leverages existing data collection workflows (60-70% overlap with energy modeling data)
- Source: resnet.us

### 7. ELI/AIA New York — Driving Digital Decarbonization (Feb 2026)
- Autodesk's BIM + lifecycle thinking for embodied carbon
- 22 nations adopted BIM policies
- EU evaluating whole-life emissions policy
- US government Buy Clean task force for federal projects
- Marta Bouchard (Autodesk sustainability lead) emphasized "cradle-to-gate" embodied carbon locked in once building erected
- Source: Environmental Law Institute, GreenTech webinar series

## Novel Contribution
Original calculation: Using the MA 100-Home study's 55.5 tons average, extrapolated to national new home construction (~1.4M starts in 2025). That's roughly 77.7 million tons CO2e in embodied carbon from new residential construction annually — more than the entire airline domestic passenger flights in the US (~67M tons). Nobody's published this national extrapolation from the MA data.

Also: The MA study found 60-70% of embodied carbon data already sits in HERS energy modeling files. This means the data infrastructure for carbon accounting is ALREADY in place for the majority of new homes in states with high HERS adoption. The bottleneck isn't data collection — it's that nobody's connecting the dots.

## Strongest Counterargument
The MA study covers only cradle-to-gate (A1-A3) emissions. End-of-life stages (demolition, disposal, recycling) are excluded. Timber framing looks great on embodied carbon, but if a wood-frame house is demolished and landfilled in 60 years, the stored carbon re-enters the atmosphere. The full lifecycle story could look very different from the cradle-to-gate numbers these tools report.

## Limitations
- MA study is geographically limited (New England climate, construction practices)
- Only 100 homes — statistically meaningful but not a national sample
- National extrapolation assumes MA homes are representative (they may not be — more masonry basements, different insulation levels)
- EPD databases still have inconsistencies and errors (per the UK concrete study)
- No cost data for low-carbon material substitutions in the MA study
- RESNET 1550 still in draft — not yet adopted as a standard
