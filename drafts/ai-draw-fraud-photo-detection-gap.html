Your Contractor Submitted Progress Photos for a $40,000 Draw. An AI Made Them in Ten Minutes.

By Jake Kowalski · July 23, 2026

A private lender in Dallas wired $38,000 to a contractor last March after reviewing six progress photos showing finished drywall, installed cabinets, and painted trim. ChatGPT generated the photos. That house still had exposed framing.

That lender discovered the fraud 47 days later, when a routine site visit revealed the truth. By then, the contractor had filed for another draw.

This is not a hypothetical. The American Association of Private Lenders has begun training its members specifically on AI-generated construction photo fraud, a category that barely existed 18 months ago.

Ten minutes and zero dollars

The mechanics of the scam are brutally simple. A borrower or contractor photographs a half-finished room. They open ChatGPT, Gemini, or any of a dozen free generative AI tools. They type something like "add hardwood floors, fresh white paint, and modern lighting to this room." The AI produces a convincing renovation in under two minutes.

Then comes the metadata. Free apps on any phone can spoof GPS coordinates, timestamps, and EXIF data, making it look like the photo was taken on site, at the right address, on the date claimed. Start to finish, the fabrication takes less than ten minutes and costs nothing.

Truepic, a company that authenticates visual evidence for lenders, demonstrated the problem by generating eight samples of AI-faked renovation and construction progress. They challenged viewers to distinguish the fakes from real photos. Most people couldn't.

The fakes aren't limited to still images. A single photo can now generate a convincing video walkthrough showing a finished room, complete with camera movement and consistent lighting. Submit that to a lender whose draw review process consists of one underwriter scrolling through emailed photos on a Tuesday afternoon, and you have a funded draw for work that never happened.

The draw process was built for paper fraud

A typical residential construction loan disburses funds in four to six draws, each tied to a completion milestone. Foundation, framing, mechanical rough-in, exterior, interior finishes, final. Each draw releases 15 to 25 percent of the total loan. On a $350,000 construction loan, that's $52,000 to $87,000 per disbursement.

Before each release, the lender sends an inspector, who photographs the site, compares progress to the approved plans, and submits a report. Turnaround runs 7 to 10 business days from request to funding. Some lenders, especially in the private and hard-money space, have moved to borrower-initiated inspections where the contractor or borrower submits their own photos. Faster, cheaper, more convenient. And wide open to fabrication.

Traditional defenses against draw fraud were designed for an era of paper: verifying that invoice addresses match the property, confirming lien waivers, checking that the budget line items add up. These controls catch clerical fraud, which used to be most of it. Discrepant paperwork, rushed closings, mismatched wire instructions.

What they do not catch is a pixel-perfect photo of a finished kitchen that does not exist.

Three minutes versus eight hours

In November 2025, Built Technologies launched Draw Agent, an autonomous AI that processes construction loan draws. The system reads the full context of each project: loan agreement, construction plans, budget, inspection photos, historical draw data, insurance certifications, lien waivers. It cross-references every data point against the lender's specific policies and flags inconsistencies.

Built's published numbers are striking. In production across thousands of construction loans, Draw Agent achieved 99-percent-plus accuracy, a 95 percent reduction in review time (from hours to as few as three minutes), and detected 400 percent more risks than human-led reviews. It cut draw turn time, the period from borrower request to funding, by 30 to 60 percent.

Those numbers come from Built, not a peer-reviewed study, so apply appropriate skepticism. But the directional claim is plausible: an AI that reads the full document stack for every draw and compares it against every prior draw on that project will catch patterns that a human reviewer, juggling 40 open loans and scanning photos between phone calls, will miss. A human reviewer gets tired at 2 p.m. An AI reads its 400th lien waiver with the same attention it gave the first.

Built offers three operational modes. Audit mode recommends actions for human approval. Assist mode handles routine steps while humans make final calls. Automate mode executes when policies are met and flags exceptions. Every decision gets a compliance trail. Each lender picks how much autonomy to grant, and the system documents every reasoning chain.

Catching fake photos at the shutter

Detection after the fact is one approach. Prevention at capture is another.

Truepic Vision requires inspection photos to be taken inside its app. No uploading from a camera roll. No pasting in a file from a laptop. Every photo must originate from a phone camera in real time. At the moment of capture, the system locks in cryptographic verification, GPS coordinates, and a timestamp, all tamper-proof. It runs automated checks for reverse image matches, duplicate submissions across projects, and signs of AI generation.

In June 2026, Truepic announced a strategic integration with Cotality (formerly CoreLogic's property intelligence arm), which operates Mercury Network, a platform that thousands of lenders use for appraisals and inspections. Lenders on Mercury Network can now trigger Truepic-authenticated virtual inspections directly within their existing workflow, covering home equity evaluations, 1004D final inspections, and construction draws.

Kiavi, a private lender that funds fix-and-flip projects, adopted Truepic Vision and reported faster draw turnaround without compromising integrity. "Our customers love having control over the speed of the inspection process," said Jacob Sherick, Kiavi's senior manager. Borrowers, it turned out, preferred authenticated self-service inspections over waiting a week for a physical inspector.

The fraud numbers are getting worse

This is not a niche concern. FundingShield reported that in Q3 2025, 46.6 percent of transactions in a $90-billion-plus portfolio spanning residential, commercial, and business-purpose loans were flagged for wire and title fraud risks. Each problematic transaction averaged 3.1 issues, a 35 percent increase over the prior quarter and a record high. A Celent study commissioned by Zest AI in May 2026 found that 93 percent of U.S. lenders said fraud now contributes to credit losses, and 82 percent reported those losses increased over the prior year.

Construction lending sits at the intersection of every vulnerability: high dollar amounts disbursed in stages, verification that relies on photos and documents rather than physical product delivery, multiple parties with access to submit evidence, and a review process optimized for speed. Add freely available generative AI capable of producing visually perfect fakes, and you have a system that was already strained now facing a threat it was never designed to resist.

What this means for you

If you are building a home with a construction loan, the fraud scenario doesn't require your contractor to be dishonest. It requires your lender to be unprotected. A fraudulent draw on your loan can trigger cascading problems. Funds depleted before the work is done. A lien filed by an unpaid subcontractor who assumed your project was further along. A budget shortfall that surfaces at the worst possible moment, when you're financing a project that suddenly costs more than anyone will lend against.

Ask your lender three questions before the first draw. How are inspection photos verified for authenticity? Can photos be submitted from a camera roll, or must they be captured in a controlled environment? What automated cross-referencing is run against prior draws, budget allocations, and policy requirements?

If the answer to all three is some version of "our team reviews them," your draw process is running on trust. Trust was sufficient when the worst a bad actor could do was photograph the same wall from three angles. It is not sufficient when the worst they can do is generate a finished kitchen from a bare concrete slab while sitting in a parking lot.
