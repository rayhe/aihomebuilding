# Research: AI Municipal Building Permit Review

## Topic
AI-powered building permit review deployed by municipal building departments. Multiple U.S. cities now using AI to pre-screen permit applications, cutting review times by 50–70%. The story: a handful of cities are dramatically faster. Most haven't started. The cost difference falls on homebuyers.

## Journalist
Catherine "Code" Chen — Policy & Regulation

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? YES. If you're in a city with AI-assisted permitting, your permit costs less and arrives faster. If you're not, you should be asking your building department why.

### 3. Honolulu DPP / Clariti CivCheck
- 20,000 building permit applications annually (Census Bureau)
- Only 1,400 new private housing units authorized per year
- CivCheck AI cut residential review times by 60-70%
- From 60-90 minutes per review to 15-20 minutes
- Piloted since 2022, expanding to commercial summer 2026
- Dawn Takeuchi Apuna, DPP Director: "It's kind of like TurboTax for permitting"
- Context: DPP underwent overhaul after federal bribery indictments in 2021
- Staff use it as training tool for new reviewers
- Also serves applicant-side: guides them through code requirements before submission
- Sources: StateScoop, GovTech, HousingWire, BusinessWire

## Original Contribution
**Per-permit delay cost calculation for Honolulu:**
- Hawaii auditor data: $202M in private-sector delay costs / 28,479 permits = $7,094 average delay cost per permit
- Average processing: 104 days → delay cost per day: $68.21
- With 70% reduction in review time: 73 days saved × $68.21 = $4,979 saved per permit
- Across ~14,240 annual permits: $70.9M/year in avoided delay costs
- Caveat: The 70% figure applies to review time specifically, not total calendar processing. Actual dollar savings depend on how much of total delay is attributable to review vs. other processing steps.

**National projection (conservative):**
- If 741 cities averaging 22.9 days adopted AI review cutting 50% of review time
- On 1.4M housing permits issued nationally
- At NAHB's $131,734 regulatory cost per home, even a 5% reduction = $6,587 per home
- Conservative estimate: AI-assisted review could save $2,000-$5,000 per residential permit in reduced carrying/holding costs

## Strongest Counterargument
AI pre-screening catches paperwork errors and dimensional compliance, but it cannot evaluate site-specific conditions: soil bearing capacity, existing drainage patterns, mature tree root zones, neighbor setback disputes. The most expensive permit delays come from site plan review and interdepartmental routing (environmental, fire, traffic), not the code-compliance check that AI handles well. A 70% reduction in code review time may translate to only a 10-20% reduction in total permit-to-start timeline. Cities that deploy AI without fixing staffing and interdepartmental routing may see limited real-world improvement.
