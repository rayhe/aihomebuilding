Japan Requires a Seismic Score for Every Building Sale. America Has 1.2 Million Vulnerable Homes and No Score for Any of Them.

By Catherine Chen July 22, 2026

Buy a house in Tokyo and your agent hands you five documents before you sign anything. A construction confirmation certificate tells you which seismic code governed the design. An inspection completion certificate proves the finished building matched the approved plans. A Housing Performance Evaluation Report assigns a Seismic Grade on a three-tier scale. For any building designed before Japan's 1981 code overhaul, a mandatory seismic diagnosis report tells you whether the structure has been assessed and retrofitted, what its performance index is, and whether it qualifies for insurance discounts that can cut your premium in half.

Buy a house in Menlo Park, or Pasadena, or Portland, and you get a seller's disclosure form that asks whether the owner is aware of any earthquake damage. No professional evaluation required. No score assigned. No indication of whether the house is bolted to its foundation, whether the cripple walls in the crawlspace are braced, or whether the structure meets any seismic standard written after the Truman administration. A seller checks a box, you move in, and fourteen miles east, the Hayward Fault waits for the stress to reach 31 bars.

Fifteen thousand out of 1.2 million

California Government Code Section 8897, enacted in 1993 after Loma Prieta damaged or destroyed over 23,000 homes, states the Legislature's finding plainly: approximately 1,200,000 homes in California may not be bolted or anchored to their foundations or do not have adequate cripple wall bracing, a vulnerability that exists because building codes did not require foundation bolting until approximately 1949 and some local jurisdictions did not enforce the requirement until nearly a decade later. Legislators declared a goal of ensuring every home be anchored, every cripple wall braced, every water heater strapped, and every deficiency disclosed to prospective buyers.

Thirty-three years later, almost none of that has happened.

California's Earthquake Brace + Bolt program, launched in 2013 to help homeowners offset the cost of a code-compliant seismic retrofit that typically runs $3,000 to $7,000 in total, offers grants of up to $3,000, and in twelve years of operation it has completed just over 15,000 retrofits, which works out to 1.25 percent of the 1.2 million vulnerable homes that legislators identified three decades ago. At roughly 1,250 retrofits per year, it would take 960 years to reach every eligible house in the state, a timeline that extends well past the next magnitude 7.0 on any fault in the system.

What Japan built after 6,434 people died

In 1995, the Great Hanshin earthquake killed 6,434 people and destroyed 104,906 buildings in the Kobe metropolitan area, and Japan's parliament responded the same year with the Act on Promotion of Seismic Retrofit of Buildings. Its original version required seismic evaluation for buildings larger than two stories and 1,000 square meters, but the 2013 revision expanded the mandate to all existing buildings, including single-family houses, while requiring local governments to periodically disclose seismic performance indices and retrofitting schedules for buildings judged vulnerable. Tokyo's metropolitan government now provides an "Earthquake Resistant Mark" free of charge to any building confirmed to meet current standards, posted at the entrance for tenants and buyers to see before they walk through the door.

Results followed rapidly. Japan's Ministry of Education reported that nearly all of the approximately 85,000 pre-1981 public school buildings still standing in 2004 have since been evaluated as seismically safe, meaning retrofitted, reconstructed, or confirmed adequate, which is what happens when a country makes evaluation mandatory and public instead of voluntary and invisible.

Insurance cemented the incentive. Under Japan's earthquake insurance system, premium discounts scale with seismic performance: 50 percent for seismically isolated buildings, 50 percent for Earthquake Resistance Class 3, 30 percent for Class 2, 10 percent for Class 1, and 10 percent for buildings that pass a seismic diagnosis confirming they meet the 1981 standard. When your insurance bill halves because you retrofitted, a $7,000 retrofit pays for itself in premium savings within a decade for most policyholders, which is the kind of math that actually changes behavior. California's CEA offers up to 25 percent off for EBB-completed retrofits, an incentive that 98.75 percent of eligible homeowners have not used, in part because nobody told them their house needs it, in part because no score exists to make the risk visible, and in part because the system was designed so that nobody ever has to look.

AI can score what inspectors cannot reach

Scoring 1.2 million homes with human inspectors is not feasible, and everyone involved knows it. California does not have enough licensed structural engineers or certified inspectors to evaluate every pre-1980 crawlspace in the state within any reasonable timeframe. Even Japan, despite maintaining 110,375 certified post-earthquake inspectors, has acknowledged that the number is insufficient to assess Tokyo's 1.9 million buildings after a major event. Physical access has always been the bottleneck.

Machine learning is dissolving it.

Italian researchers at the University of Bologna applied convolutional neural networks to satellite images and street-view photographs of residential buildings, automatically extracting the parameters that most influence seismic vulnerability: building height, construction material, roof type, structural regularity, and the geometric features that correlate with soft-story risk. Once trained, the system works at city scale for the cost of compute time alone and achieves meaningful parameter extraction without any inspector visiting any building, a capability that would have seemed speculative five years ago and now sits in a peer-reviewed journal as demonstrated methodology.

A 2025 Turkish study pushed further, applying XGBoost, Random Forest, and AdaBoost to predict seismic risk categories for 600 buildings based on physical parameters. XGBoost achieved superior accuracy compared to traditional Rapid Visual Screening methods of the kind FEMA prescribes in the United States. Feature importance analysis identified building height as the strongest predictor, with soft-story mechanisms and short columns close behind. A web-based tool automated the entire screening process and eliminated the manual calculation errors that plague paper-based RVS procedures, errors that accumulate precisely when you need accuracy most: in the chaotic aftermath of an earthquake or in the bureaucratic tedium of screening a million buildings one paper form at a time.

Most striking is a framework published in 2025 for predicting building damage from pre-event satellite imagery alone, validated against the 2023 magnitude 7.8 earthquake in southeastern Turkiye. Random Forest models predicted building cluster damage ratios with an R-squared of 0.862 at 500-meter spatial resolution, using building height and construction period as structural proxies combined with 26 indicators spanning ground motion parameters, topographic factors, and spectral-textural features extracted from satellite data. No blueprints. No inspection reports. No site visits. A satellite photograph, a construction date, and a model that explains 86 percent of the variance in how badly a neighborhood gets hit.

A score that could exist but doesn't

Consider what these pieces would look like assembled into a single product that a homebuyer could actually use. Satellite imagery and street-view data already cover every residential parcel in California through Google Earth, county GIS systems, and commercial providers. Construction dates sit in county assessor databases that are, for the most part, digitized and publicly accessible. Soil liquefaction maps, fault proximity data, and ground motion predictions are published by the California Geological Survey and USGS. Building footprints, heights, and typologies are extractable from LiDAR and aerial photography that most California counties already possess for tax assessment purposes.

An AI seismic vulnerability score for every residential building in California could be generated from existing public data without a single inspector visiting a single property. Not a precise structural engineering analysis, which still requires physical access to evaluate connection details, material degradation, and as-built conditions that no satellite can see through a roof. But a screening score, analogous to FEMA's P-154 Rapid Visual Screening but automated, scaled, and continuously updated: a number that tells a buyer whether this house likely sits in the bottom quintile for seismic performance in its zip code, or near the top, and what the estimated cost of bringing it to current standards would be.

Nobody has built it for consumers. Commercial real estate uses structural risk scoring from Verisk and CoreLogic, but those models target portfolio-level insurance pricing rather than individual homebuyer decisions. FEMA's P-154 requires a trained screener to physically walk a property. Japan's mandatory diagnosis requires a certified inspector. An AI alternative, derived from remote sensing and public records, exists in academic literature that no homebuyer would ever find while scrolling through Zillow listings wondering whether $1.2 million buys a house or an unanchored liability sitting on fill soil three miles from an active fault.

What buyers don't know and sellers aren't required to find out

California requires sellers to complete a Natural Hazard Disclosure statement indicating whether a property lies within a seismic hazard zone, a fault zone, or a flood zone, but this binary indicator captures geographic risk rather than structural vulnerability, which means that two houses on the same block, one bolted to its foundation with braced cripple walls and the other resting on unanchored mudsills that a moderate earthquake would shear apart, receive identical disclosures. Location, not condition.

Japan's system captures both, and the difference in buyer information is staggering. A purchaser in Osaka knows not only that a property sits in a seismic zone but whether that specific structure meets the 1981 standard, what its seismic performance index is, whether it has been retrofitted, and what insurance discount it qualifies for, information that transforms an opaque risk into a quantified decision with a dollar figure attached to each possible outcome.

UC Berkeley's PEER-CEA research project quantified what this information asymmetry costs. For an unretrofitted house near a fault, potential savings from a $3,000 to $7,000 retrofit range from $10,000 to over $200,000 after a major earthquake, representing 20 to 40 percent of the home's replacement value, depending on factors like the number of stories, exterior siding type, and proximity to the epicenter. Every buyer of a pre-1980 California house with a crawlspace is making a bet, and the probability of a magnitude 6.7 or greater earthquake somewhere in the state within 30 years exceeds 99 percent according to the USGS Working Group on California Earthquake Probabilities. But nobody at the closing table mentions the crawlspace, and nobody has to.

Six billion dollars against a hundred

Retrofitting all 1.2 million vulnerable California homes at an average cost of $5,000 would total $6 billion, which is 0.15 percent of the state's $4.1 trillion annual GDP. For context: Northridge in 1994 caused $20 billion in damage in 1994 dollars, roughly $44 billion adjusted, and USGS estimates project that the next comparable event on a populated fault, whether the Hayward, San Andreas, or Puente Hills, could cause $100 billion or more in losses. Six billion in prevention against a hundred billion in damage is not a question of whether the investment pencils out. It is a question of whether anyone will build the scoring tool that makes the risk visible to the people writing the checks and the incentive structure that makes a $5,000 retrofit feel like the obvious decision it already is on paper.

Japan built both after losing 6,434 people in one morning. California has built neither, thirty-seven years after Loma Prieta killed 63 and damaged 23,000 homes, because no code requires a score, no market demands one, and the machine learning models that could generate it for every home in the state from a satellite photograph remain in peer-reviewed journals that no homebuyer, no insurance agent, and no state legislator has read.

Limitations

Machine learning models cited in this article achieve strong predictive performance on aggregate building clusters and population-level screening rather than individual structural assessments: an R-squared of 0.862 at 500-meter resolution means the model explains 86 percent of variance in damage ratios across clusters, not that it can predict whether a specific house will collapse, because individual outcomes depend on as-built details, soil conditions, connection quality, and maintenance history that remote sensing cannot capture. A satellite-derived seismic score would function as a screening tool, not a substitute for a licensed structural engineer's evaluation, and any implementation would need to communicate that distinction clearly to avoid creating a false sense of security in homebuyers who see a favorable score and skip the crawlspace inspection.

California's 1.2 million figure from Section 8897 dates to 1993 and has not been formally updated. Some portion of those homes have been independently retrofitted outside EBB, demolished, or substantially remodeled in the three decades since, and the actual number of currently vulnerable homes is unknown, which is itself evidence of how little the current system tracks about its own problem.

Japan's mandatory evaluation program benefits from a cultural context, dense urban housing stock, and insurance market structure that differ substantially from California's, and direct policy transplantation would require adaptation to American federalism, property rights norms, and the fragmented landscape of 58 county building departments that do not share data systems, inspection standards, or political incentives. Building AI scoring into existing disclosure and insurance frameworks, however, faces fewer structural barriers than transplanting the regulatory architecture itself, because the data already exists and the models already work.
