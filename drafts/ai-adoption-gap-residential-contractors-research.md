# Research: AI Adoption Gap in Residential Construction

## Thesis
The residential construction industry is splitting into AI haves and have-nots. The data is now clear enough to quantify the gap — and it favors the smallest operators more than anyone realizes. The 75% who haven't adopted AI aren't losing because the technology is expensive. They're losing because nobody translated it into the language of a three-person crew running six jobs a week.

## Primary Sources

### 1. ServiceTitan 2026 Residential State of the Trades Report
- **Source:** ServiceTitan, April 2026, n=1,000+ residential contractors
- **URL:** servicetitan.com (referenced via HousingWire coverage)
- 25% of residential contractors are using AI in a "meaningful way"
- 74% say AI is important for efficiency
- Nearly half (≈49%) don't trust AI
- More than half don't know where to start
- Of the 25% who ARE using AI:
  - 48% report higher productivity
  - 45% report time savings
  - 32% report improved customer experience
- Top business risks for 2026: labor/overhead, skilled labor shortage, material prices
- 53% prioritizing existing customers vs 31% focused on new acquisition
- 73% of customers cite clear, upfront pricing as primary reason for choosing a contractor
- Quote: Angie Snow, Principal Industry Advisor at ServiceTitan: "They're experimenting, but they don't really fully have it embedded in their workflows."

### 2. NAHB/Wells Fargo Housing Market Index AI Survey (July 2025)
- **Source:** NAHB, nahb.org
- ~20% of single-family builders using AI for advertising/marketing materials
- 11% for market analysis and project planning
- <5% for any of 10 other business functions (design, automated equipment, etc.)
- Strong interest in future adoption — concentrated in marketing, not construction
- Majority of builders still on sidelines

### 3. RICS Report / Construction Dive (2025-2026)
- **Source:** Royal Institution of Chartered Surveyors, via constructiondive.com
- 45% of companies have "limited capability" — only exploring AI implementation
- 29% have NO capability or plans at all
- Significant gap between perceived importance and actual implementation
- Most respondents rated AI's significance as "moderate to high" for progress monitoring, safety, sustainability, risk management
- Contractor adoption fatigue: "There's so many point solutions, so many workflows, and you have to do so many different logins." — Tanja Kufner, Nemetschek Group

### 4. Bluebeam 2026 AEC Technology Outlook
- **Source:** Bluebeam/Nemetschek, surveyed 1,000 AEC professionals
- 27% currently use AI

- 65% of construction companies spend less than 10% of tech budget on training
- CEO quote: "It's not cost. It's complexity, culture, and connection." — Usman Shuja, CEO Bluebeam

### 5. Dodge Construction Network AI Survey (2025-2026)

## Novel Analysis

### The Productivity Dollar Gap
If 25% of contractors report 48% higher productivity, and the average small residential contractor generates ~$250K revenue:
- 48% productivity gain on labor-intensive work (≈60% of revenue = $150K in labor-driven costs)
- That's roughly $72,000/year in productivity value per contractor
- Cost of most AI tools: $50-500/month ($600-$6,000/year)
- ROI: 12x to 120x on tool cost alone
- The 75% who haven't adopted are leaving $72K/year on the table

### The Training Investment Mismatch
- 65% of companies spend < 10% of tech budget on training
- If tech budget is 2-3% of revenue ($5K-$7.5K for a $250K contractor):
  - Training budget: < $500-$750/year
  - That's less than one day of paid training per employee per year
- Compare: electricians spend 48+ hours/year on continuing education (NEC code cycle)
- The bottleneck isn't tool cost — it's the gap between buying the tool and knowing how to use it

### The Size Paradox
- Largest contractors (>$50M revenue): 86% see AI as competitive advantage
- Smallest contractors (<$250K): These are the ones who benefit MOST from AI
  - They wear the most hats (owner = estimator = scheduler = marketer = bookkeeper)
  - AI replaces the functions they can't afford to hire for
  - But they have the least capacity to evaluate, implement, and learn new tools
- This is the central paradox: the people who need AI most have the least time to adopt it

## Counterarguments (to engage seriously)
1. "Small contractors don't need AI — relationships drive their business." True, but 73% of customers now cite clear pricing as their primary selection criterion. The relationship advantage erodes when a competitor can produce a professional, AI-generated estimate in minutes vs. your napkin math.
2. "The tools aren't built for residential." Partially true — most construction AI is built for $50M+ commercial projects. But the adoption that IS happening (ServiceTitan, Jobber, Housecall Pro) is specifically in field service management, not BIM or project controls.
3. "Wait two years and the tools will be better." The Bluebeam data says otherwise: 94% of current adopters plan to increase usage. The compounding effect of early adoption means waiting costs more each year.
