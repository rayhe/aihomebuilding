You're Paying $60 a Month Per Worker to Watch Them Quit

By Marcus Washington • March 31, 2026

A framing crew foreman I know in Phoenix told me about the day he noticed the new hardhat cameras. Nobody on his crew had asked for them. Nobody explained what the footage was for. His site super mentioned "safety compliance" and moved on. Within two weeks, three of his best carpenters had taken jobs with a smaller GC across town. No cameras at that shop.

"They didn't file complaints," he told me. "They just left. And I couldn't blame them."

My father spent 28 years in a union shop. He told me the fastest way to lose a good worker was to make them feel like a suspect. Nobody had AI cameras on job sites in the 1990s. They didn't need them. Bad management found other ways to drive people out. Now it has a subscription model.

He isn't alone. Across residential construction, companies are deploying AI-powered cameras, wearable trackers, and GPS monitoring systems at a pace that would have seemed paranoid five years ago. Vendors like Viact, Buildots, and Smartvid.io sell these tools as safety infrastructure. It's a compelling pitch: AI watches the site so you don't have to. It catches PPE violations, flags fall hazards, tracks productivity. It pays for itself.

One problem with that pitch: the largest meta-analysis ever conducted on electronic performance monitoring says it doesn't work.

What the Research Actually Found

In 2023, Daniel Ravid and colleagues at George Mason University published a meta-analysis in Personnel Psychology synthesizing 94 independent studies covering 23,461 workers. They examined every measurable outcome of electronic performance monitoring: productivity, quality, accuracy, stress, satisfaction, turnover intent.

Unambiguous findings. Electronic monitoring showed no statistically significant relationship with improved worker performance. None. Across 94 studies. Null or slightly negative.

What monitoring did produce, consistently and across every study design, was increased stress and psychological strain. More transparent monitoring was somewhat less damaging than covert surveillance. But even the most carefully implemented systems produced measurable stress increases without corresponding performance gains.

A separate study from UC San Diego found that low-performing workers who were monitored without explanation saw a 17% productivity decrease. Not an improvement. A decrease. Surveillance without communication made the work worse.

Construction Can't Afford to Stress Its Workforce

In most industries, stressing out employees is bad management. In construction, it is an existential risk.

Construction quits hit a 1.8% monthly rate in December 2025, according to BLS JOLTS data. That is a 31% jump from 1.4% in December 2024. Workers are leaving because they can. Every residential GC in America is competing for the same shrinking pool of skilled labor.

Consider the broader picture. AGC's 2025 survey found 92% of hiring firms report difficulty finding qualified workers. Net new workers needed: 349,000 in 2026 and 456,000 in 2027, per Associated Builders and Contractors. Meanwhile, 41% of the current workforce is projected to retire by 2031. One in five construction workers is currently over 55.

This is the labor market into which construction companies are introducing AI surveillance. Terrible timing.

Running the Numbers Nobody Ran

AI monitoring systems for construction sites range from $40 to $100 per worker per month. Take the midpoint: $60. For a 20-person residential framing and finishing crew, that is $14,400 per year.

Now consider what the Ravid meta-analysis tells us about the return on that investment. Zero measurable performance improvement. That $14,400 buys cameras and dashboards and weekly reports. It does not buy faster framing, fewer punch-list items, or better quality control. Across 23,461 workers and 94 separate studies, the data is clear on this.

But the cost side gets worse. APA's 2023 workforce survey found that 56% of tech-monitored workers report feeling tense or stressed at work, compared to 40% of unmonitored workers. Stressed workers quit more. If monitoring pushes your crew's monthly quit rate from the national 1.8% to even 2.1% (a modest increase consistent with the stress literature), your 20-person crew loses roughly one additional worker per year that it wouldn't have lost otherwise.

So you spend $14,400 on a monitoring system that produces no measurable performance improvement, and it may cost you one additional worker per year. Replacing a skilled construction worker runs $10,000 to $20,000 in recruiting, onboarding, and lost productivity, per Construction Dive's 2023 analysis. Call it $15,000 at the midpoint. Net ROI: negative $29,400 before you account for the supervisor time spent reviewing dashboards nobody asked for.

Safety Monitoring and Productivity Tracking Are Not the Same Thing

Construction is the most dangerous major industry in the United States. OSHA recorded approximately 1,000 fatal work injuries in construction in 2023. AI cameras that detect missing harnesses at height, flag workers entering crane swing zones, or identify PPE violations in real time have a legitimate safety application. People die on job sites, and technology that prevents deaths is worth deploying.

But there is a meaningful distinction between a camera that alerts a safety manager when someone is 30 feet up without fall protection, and a camera that tracks how many minutes each worker spends on task versus standing idle. One is a safety tool. The second is a productivity surveillance system marketed as a safety tool.

Scope creep is the problem. Once the cameras are installed and the AI platform is running, the vendor's dashboard offers productivity analytics alongside safety alerts. Time-on-task tracking. Movement heatmaps. Idle time reports. Your GC who signed up for fall detection is now watching bathroom breaks.

Workers notice. They always notice.

What the Regulators Think

Federal labor regulators are paying attention. NLRB General Counsel Jennifer Abruzzo issued Memo GC 23-02 in October 2022 warning that electronic surveillance of workers presumptively violates employee rights under the National Labor Relations Act. DOL's AI principles, published in May 2024, state explicitly that "AI systems should not violate or undermine workers' right to organize."

For residential builders and GCs operating in states with strong labor protections (California, New York, Illinois, Washington), the legal exposure from AI monitoring is real and growing.

What a Builder Should Do Instead

If you run a residential construction company and you're considering AI monitoring, here is what the evidence supports.

Deploy camera-based safety systems narrowly. PPE detection, fall monitoring, proximity alerts near heavy equipment. Keep the scope to safety. Disable or refuse productivity tracking features. Tell your crew exactly what the cameras see and what they don't. Transparency reduces, though it does not eliminate, the stress effects the research documents.

Invest the $14,400 you would have spent on productivity monitoring into retention instead. That covers a meaningful raise for two workers, a tool allowance, or training hours. In a labor market where 92% of firms can't find workers, keeping the ones you have is the highest-ROI investment available to you. And talk to your crew before you install anything. UC San Diego's research shows that surveillance without communication makes performance worse regardless of whether the worker is high-performing or low-performing.

What This Analysis Does Not Prove

Ravid's meta-analysis covers all industries, not construction specifically. Construction work is physical, outdoor, and team-based. Those characteristics could amplify or dampen the stress effects. No study has isolated that question.

Turnover cost estimates ($10,000 to $20,000 per worker) use general industry benchmarks, not verified company-specific data. That 31% year-over-year increase in construction quits has many possible causes: wage competition, demographic shifts, pandemic-era career changes. Attributing any portion of it specifically to monitoring is an inference, not a measured effect.

And AI monitoring technology is evolving. Systems that give workers control over their own data or share benefits with the workforce could produce different outcomes. What the evidence does establish, across nearly a hundred studies, is that electronic performance monitoring does not improve the work. And what the labor data establishes is that construction cannot afford to lose workers for nothing.
