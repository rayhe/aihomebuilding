# Research: Data Center Labor Drain on Residential Construction

## Angle
The AI data center construction boom ($103.7B trailing twelve months through Jan 2026) is pulling electricians, HVAC techs, pipefitters, and laborers away from residential home building — widening an already critical labor shortage. This is a Marcus Washington workforce story about the workers caught between two booming sectors, with the people who need homes losing.

## Kill Test
✅ Helps someone building or buying a home — explains why their electrician is 3 months delayed and bidding 40% higher, why housing starts are sluggish, and what wage competition means for residential project costs.

## Wage Gap Analysis (Original Contribution)
Residential electricians earn ~$25/hr. Data center projects typically pay $35-50/hr with overtime premiums, per diem ($75-150/day for travel), and guaranteed hours. A residential electrician working 2,000 hrs/yr earns ~$50K. The same electrician on a data center project: ~$70-100K+ with OT and per diem. That's a 40-100% pay bump to switch sectors.

For a home builder, that means: either match data center wages (raising home costs $5-15K per house for electrical alone) or wait months longer. Both outcomes land on the homebuyer.

## Novel Calculation
At 19,000 homes lost/year (NAHB) and median new home price ~$430K (Census), that's $8.17 billion in housing supply NOT entering the market annually — while $103.7B/year flows into data center construction. For every $1 spent building data centers, roughly $0.08 in residential housing doesn't get built. The ratio only worsens as data center spending accelerates.
