Your Office Got 847 System Tests Before Anyone Moved In. Your New Home Got Zero.

By Frank "The Foreman" DeLuca

Before the last tenant moved into the new Class A office tower at 200 Park Avenue South in Manhattan, a team of commissioning agents had already tested 847 individual system functions, running each air handler, every VAV box, every chilled water valve, and every fire damper through multiple operating modes to verify performance against the original design documents. Its HVAC system alone required 112 functional performance tests across three configurations, a process that consumed fourteen weeks of labor and cost $1.8 million before anyone plugged in a laptop.

Six blocks north, a family closed on a $1.4 million brownstone renovation that included a two-zone forced-air system, a tankless water heater, and an energy recovery ventilator designed to meet the city's updated energy code. A city inspector spent forty minutes confirming code compliance, the builder walked through with a cosmetic punch list, and nobody tested whether the HVAC system actually delivered the airflow, temperature split, or efficiency it was designed to produce. The office got commissioning; the home got a handshake.

The Thirty Percent You're Already Paying

The National Institute of Standards and Technology quantified what that handshake costs in the first study to measure efficiency losses from common residential installation errors, a three-year research program led by NIST mechanical engineers Piotr Domanski and Vance Payne alongside Hugh Henderson of CDH Energy Corp. Their conclusion: improper installation increases household energy consumption for heating and cooling by roughly 30 percent over designed performance.

Those faults aren't exotic. Leaky ducts, wrong refrigerant charge, oversized equipment, restricted airflow. Field surveys reviewed by the NIST team found that the majority of residential air conditioning equipment performed below its rated efficiency due to one or more of these installation errors, each of which could have been detected with standard diagnostic procedures that take less than an hour and require equipment most HVAC contractors already own.

For a household spending $2,400 a year on heating and cooling (the national average for a single-family home, according to the Energy Information Administration), a 30 percent penalty means $720 per year in wasted energy, compounding silently for the life of the system, which adds up to $10,800 over a 15-year equipment lifespan from faults that could have been detected on the day the system was turned on.

One in Five Heat Pumps Doesn't Meet the Standard It Was Sold On

The problem extends well beyond older equipment and sloppy contractors. A 2025 study published in Nature Communications analyzed 1,023 heat pumps installed in residential buildings across Europe, many of them new installations completed in the last five years, and found a 2- to 3-fold difference in measured efficiency between the best-performing and worst-performing systems of the same type. Seventeen percent of air-source heat pumps failed to meet existing efficiency standards, and approximately 10 percent were oversized for the homes they served, which means the equipment was running in short cycles that prevent it from reaching its rated efficiency while simultaneously accelerating wear on the compressor.

Their conclusion was blunt: there is a "critical need for standardized post-installation performance evaluation procedures and the development of digital tools to deliver actionable feedback for users and installers." The equipment ships with a rating. Nobody verifies that the installed system delivers it. That gap between what you bought and what you got is invisible until you open the utility bill, and even then you won't know why it's high, because the bill doesn't tell you whether your system is running at 85 percent of its rated efficiency or 55 percent.

What Commissioning Actually Is

Commissioning is not an inspection. An inspection asks whether the installation meets code. Commissioning asks whether the installation performs as designed, which is a fundamentally different question because the code sets a minimum floor for safety while the design sets a target for performance, and the distance between those two benchmarks is where most of the wasted energy lives.

In commercial construction, the process is governed by ASHRAE Guideline 0 and Standard 202, which define a systematic protocol in which a commissioning agent, independent of the design and construction teams, develops a plan before construction begins, witnesses installation at critical milestones, runs functional performance tests on every major system, and verifies that the building operates at its intended capacity, efficiency, and control sequence before handing over a permanent record to the owner. Evan Mills, a staff scientist at Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory, conducted the most comprehensive meta-analysis of commissioning economics across 643 buildings and found a median cost of $1.16 per square foot for new construction, a median payback period of 4.2 years, and a lifetime return of $4.50 for every dollar spent, a figure published in the peer-reviewed journal Energy and Buildings.

For residential construction, no equivalent standard exists. No ASHRAE guideline, no certification requirement, no market expectation that the builder will demonstrate with data that the systems actually work. When the builder hands over the keys, you get a certificate of occupancy confirming that the building won't kill you, but you don't get any assurance that it works well.

Seven Percent

Lawrence Berkeley National Lab reviewed 469 academic papers on residential commissioning, a substantial body of work that you might expect to provide a comprehensive framework for evaluating whole-home performance. But of those 469 papers, only 33 examined a house as a system of interacting components: seven percent. Every other paper studied individual systems in isolation, analyzing ducts in one paper and insulation in another and refrigerant charge in a third, as though the performance of each could be evaluated without reference to the others.

That fragmentation matters because houses are systems in which every component affects every other component. A perfectly installed heat pump connected to leaky ductwork in an under-insulated attic doesn't deliver its rated performance, not because the equipment failed but because the system was never evaluated as a whole, and a window with an impressive U-factor installed in a wall with thermal bridging at every stud bay doesn't deliver its rated insulation value, because the lab-tested component and the field-assembled system are not the same thing.

Researchers at the University of Salford built an entire house inside a climate-controlled chamber to study exactly this problem, instrumenting it with nearly 300 sensors and subjecting it to controlled weather conditions that would be impossible to replicate in the field. When they measured the gap between designed and actual performance in new-build homes, they found that buildings could operate at less than half the efficiency they were designed to achieve, a deficit driven by material substitutions during construction, poorly fitted windows, and missing insulation sections, each one invisible at final inspection and each one bleeding energy for decades.

What $1,500 Would Buy You

A basic residential commissioning protocol is not science fiction, because the tests are individually routine and most of them are already performed on commercial buildings as a matter of course. Applied to a new 2,500-square-foot home, the cost breaks down like this: a blower door test to measure envelope air leakage runs $300 to $500 and is already required by some energy codes, though ignored by many builders when enforcement is lax; a duct leakage test costs $200 to $400 and measures how much conditioned air escapes into unconditioned spaces before reaching the rooms it was intended for; HVAC performance verification of refrigerant charge, airflow per ton, and supply-return temperature differential runs $300 to $500 and confirms whether the system delivers its rated capacity; a thermal imaging scan costs $200 to $400 and catches insulation gaps, thermal bridges, and moisture intrusion before drywall hides them; and a ventilation rate measurement at $150 to $300 verifies that mechanical ventilation actually exchanges air at the designed rate, which matters enormously for indoor air quality in the tight-envelope homes that current energy codes require.

Total: $1,150 to $2,100, or 0.2 to 0.4 percent of the purchase price on a $500,000 home. Against the NIST-documented 30 percent energy penalty, even the high end of that range pays for itself in under three years and saves $6,000 to $9,000 over a decade, a return ratio that closely mirrors the commercial commissioning ROI documented in the Mills study.

AI Could Make This Invisible

In commercial buildings, monitoring-based commissioning has already moved from pilot programs to standard practice for large portfolios, with companies like Verdigris, CIM, and Attune embedding sensor networks and AI analytics that continuously verify system performance against design intent, flagging a stuck chilled water valve or a drifting economizer setpoint before anyone files a comfort complaint.

A residential equivalent would be startlingly simple by comparison, because a new home has one or two HVAC zones instead of sixty, a handful of supply registers instead of hundreds, and a single electrical panel instead of a switchgear room. A sensor package required to continuously commission a house (a smart thermostat with airflow monitoring, a few duct-mounted temperature sensors, a current transformer on the compressor circuit) costs $200 to $500 in hardware, and the analytics to process the data are trivial for any machine learning model trained on the commercial side, where the same algorithms already monitor buildings with fifty times the complexity.

Nobody is building it. Commercial monitoring-based commissioning is is projected to reach $1.2 billion by 2028, while the residential performance monitoring market, stripped of its smart-thermostat-as-gadget category, is effectively zero.

Why Builders Won't Do It

I've been managing residential projects for two decades, long enough to know exactly why commissioning doesn't happen and long enough to understand that the reasons are rational even if the outcome is indefensible.

Liability comes first, because a commissioning report creates a documented record of system performance, and if that record says the duct system leaks 18 percent of its air (which is common in residential construction, where the ACCA Standard 5 threshold is 4 percent), the builder now has a documented deficiency that the homeowner can point to in a warranty claim, a legal exposure that simply doesn't exist when nobody measures anything.

Margin comes second, since the typical production builder operates on 18 to 22 percent gross margins, and every dollar spent on a process that doesn't add visible features or accelerate a sale is a dollar the sales team fights against, because commissioning is invisible quality and the market hasn't yet learned to reward it.

Speed comes third, given that the median time from permit to certificate of occupancy on a single-family home is 7.2 months according to the Census Bureau's Survey of Construction, and adding a commissioning step between mechanical rough-in and drywall installation (the only window when you can still fix what you find without tearing open walls) means absorbing one to three extra days in a schedule where every trade is stacked back-to-back and the carrying cost of the construction loan runs $150 to $250 per day.

The Strongest Argument Against This

A 2,500-square-foot home is not a 150,000-square-foot office tower. The systems are genuinely simpler, the interactions are fewer, the failure modes are more predictable. A reasonable person could argue that the city inspection combined with the builder's internal quality control process is sufficient for the complexity of a residential system, and that $1,500 per home multiplied across 1.4 million housing starts per year equals $2.1 billion in new costs for an industry already struggling with an affordability crisis.

That argument has a data problem. If builder quality control and code inspections were catching installation faults, the NIST numbers wouldn't show a 30 percent energy penalty across a majority of field-surveyed equipment. If residential HVAC systems were performing as rated, a study of 1,023 heat pumps wouldn't find that one in five falls below minimum efficiency standards. Simpler systems don't protect anyone; it just makes the failures harder to detect, because there's no monitoring infrastructure to catch them, no commissioning protocol to verify them, and no market incentive to measure them.

What This Means for You

If you're buying a new home, ask the builder for a blower door test result and a duct leakage test result, and don't accept a certificate of code compliance in place of an actual performance number. If they can produce one, compare the duct leakage to the ACCA Standard 5 threshold of 4 percent of total system airflow, keeping in mind that most homes leak two to five times that amount.

If you're building custom, write commissioning into the contract before ground breaks, specifying the tests, the performance standards, and the pass/fail criteria that will determine whether the final payment is released. A good HVAC contractor won't resist this because they know their work stands up to measurement; a contractor who tells you it's unnecessary is telling you something important about their confidence in their own installation quality.

If you're a builder looking to differentiate in a competitive market, commissioning is the cheapest quality signal you can offer: a $1,500 investment that produces a documented performance report no competitor in your market can match, in an industry where ServiceTitan's 2026 survey found that 74 percent of contractors believe AI-driven efficiency matters but only 25 percent are doing anything about it.

And if you're an AI company looking for a market that doesn't exist yet but should: residential performance monitoring. A sensor kit, a cloud dashboard, and a machine learning model that tells a homeowner within thirty days of moving in whether their home is performing as designed, backed by the same analytics that already monitor commercial buildings at fifty times the scale. Commercial buildings proved the economics. Homes are waiting.

Limitations

The NIST installation fault study was published in 2014, and no comparable large-scale measurement study has been conducted since, which means installation quality may have improved or degraded in the intervening decade without anyone quantifying the change. That heat pump study, published in Nature Communications, analyzed European installations where equipment standards, installation practices, and climate differ from the U.S. market in ways that may limit direct comparison. The Mills commissioning ROI analysis covers all commercial building types, and extracting a residential-specific return requires assumptions about the relative simplicity of residential systems that have not been independently validated. Residential commissioning cost estimates in this article are the author's calculations based on current market rates for individual tests, because no published study has costed a complete residential commissioning protocol, a gap that exists precisely because almost nobody performs one.
