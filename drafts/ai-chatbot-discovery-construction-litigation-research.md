# Research: AI Chatbot Discovery Risk in Construction Litigation

## Angle
AI chatbot search histories (ChatGPT, Claude, etc.) are emerging as discoverable evidence in civil litigation. For residential construction, this creates a specific and under-discussed risk: when architects, project managers, and contractors use consumer AI tools to research legal questions — "Am I liable for this code violation?" "What's our exposure on delay damages?" — those conversations are logged by the platform and may be obtainable by opposing counsel during construction defect discovery.

This is DISTINCT from our existing coverage of AI monitoring tools creating timestamped records (article #283: "Your Builder's AI Caught the Leak on March 5"). That article covered AI ON the job site. This article covers AI AROUND the job site — the chatbot conversations that construction professionals have when they suspect something has gone wrong.

## Key Sources

### Court Cases (all 2026)

1. **US v. Heppner**, No. 25-cr-00503-JSR (S.D.N.Y. Feb. 17, 2026)
   - 31 documents created by defendant using Claude chatbot ruled NOT protected by attorney-client privilege or work product doctrine
   - Court's three-part finding: (1) not communicated to an attorney, (2) not confidential because shared with third-party AI platform disclosable under user policies, (3) not for purpose of obtaining legal advice
   - CRITICAL for construction: this was NOT a pro se litigant. The reasoning applies directly to corporate employees.

2. **Warner v. Gilbarco**, No. 2:24-cv-12333 (E.D. Mich. Feb. 10, 2026)
   - Court DENIED motion to compel ChatGPT documents from pro se plaintiff
   - Reasoning: "ChatGPT and other generative AI programs are tools, not persons" — work-product waiver requires disclosure to adversary, not merely using AI as drafting aid
   - Favorable ruling, but specific to pro se context

3. **Morgan v. V2X, Inc.**, No. 25-cv-01991-SKC-MDB (D. Colo. Mar. 30, 2026)
   - Also rejected Heppner for pro se plaintiff
   - Holding: "AI systems like ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, and others… does not eliminate all expectations of privacy or automatically waive protections"
   - Again: pro se specific

4. **Alpha Tech Lending v. Recchio** (Nassau County Supreme Court NY, June 4, 2026)
   - Judge quashed ChatGPT subpoena for pro se defendant's AI chats
   - Cited Colorado ruling about "confidential, strategy-laden iterative work product"
   - Pro se again

5. **Fortis Advisors v. Krafton, Inc.**, No. 2025-0805-LWW (Del. Ch. March 2026)
   - THE DEVASTATING CASE: CEO's ChatGPT conversations used as evidence of bad faith in $250M earnout dispute
   - Court found CEO "contrived a corporate takeover strategy" using ChatGPT
   - AI chat logs provided "damning evidence" of pretextual intent
   - Court issued sweeping remedial order based partly on the AI logs
   - This is what happens to CORPORATE (non-pro-se) AI users

### The Pro Se vs. Corporate Distinction
- Every case where AI chats were PROTECTED: pro se litigant
- Cases where AI chats were discoverable/used as evidence: corporate contexts (Heppner, Fortis)
- Reuters analysis (June 8, 2026) explicitly warns: "the dangers of using publicly available AI tools have far greater impact on corporate litigants"
- Construction firms (GCs, architecture practices, engineering firms) are corporate entities. Their employees' AI use falls under the LESS protected category.

### AIA Trust Warning
- Source: "When AI Legal Searches Become Exhibit A: Discovery Risk for Architects and Engineers" by Jonathan C. Shoemaker, Lee/Shoemaker PLLC (April 2026)
- Published by The American Institute of Architects Trust
- Specific construction scenarios: PM asking about delay damages, firm owner asking about liability probability, junior staff searching for workarounds
- Key finding: "there is no established body of law creating a safe harbor for AI-based legal research conducted by non-lawyers"
- Recommendation: treat AI legal queries like internal emails about liability

### Reuters Practical Analysis (June 8, 2026)
- "When your AI tool becomes a witness: AI tools, privilege waiver, and the hidden risks of generative AI technology"
- DHS obtained first known federal search warrant for ChatGPT user data (2025)
- Florida AG sued OpenAI (June 1, 2026) — first state to do so
- AI transcription tools also create discoverable records in meetings
- Organizations should assume consumer AI tools offer NO privilege protection

### Construction Defect Litigation Context
- Hawaii UHERO study: 1 in 4 new homes built 2013-2023 involved in construction defect litigation
- Up from 1 in 6 (16%) in previous decade to 27% (including class actions)
- 15% of multifamily units in litigation, up from 4%
- Industry-wide trend of increasing construction defect claims = more discovery = more AI subpoenas

### AI Adoption Data
- NAHB/Wells Fargo HMI (July 2025): 49% of single-family homebuilders use AI
- Home Innovation Research Labs: AI in residential design/planning doubled from 9% (2024) to 17% (2025)
- ServiceTitan survey (2026): 25% of 1,000+ residential contractors actively deploy AI

## Original Contribution

### Calculation: The Discovery Exposure Window
- 49% of builders use AI × ~900,000 annual single-family starts = ~441,000 AI-touched homes/year
- If 1 in 4 homes faces litigation in high-litigation markets (Hawaii data), that's ~110,000 homes where AI discovery requests could arise annually
- Conservative: apply the 15% multifamily rate nationally = ~66,000 homes
- Each of these homes represents a potential subpoena to OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, or Microsoft for employee AI chat logs

### The Knowledge Trap (Novel Analysis)
AI searches create timestamped evidence of KNOWLEDGE. In construction defect cases, the critical question is often "when did you know?" AI logs answer that question with precision:
- "Your PM asked Claude about waterproofing code requirements on March 5. The inspection failed on June 12. What happened in between?"
- Under Heppner reasoning, those logs are discoverable for corporate employees
- This is worse than email because people ask AI more candid questions than they write in emails (per NBC analysis citing cybersecurity experts)

### Pro Se Shield Doesn't Apply to Construction Firms
All protective rulings (Warner, Morgan, Recchio) involved individuals representing themselves. Construction firms are corporate defendants represented by counsel. Under Heppner and Fortis, their employees' AI use is almost certainly discoverable.
