# Research: The Residential RFI Documentation Gap

## Core Thesis
In commercial construction, RFIs are a formal, tracked process. In residential custom home building, the same decisions happen over text messages, phone calls, and emails — undocumented, untracked, and indefensible when disputes arise. AI tools are racing to automate the formal RFI process, but the bigger opportunity is capturing the informal decision trail that residential builders never log at all.

## Key Data Points

### Commercial RFI Benchmarks
- **10-15 RFIs per $1M** in project value (Procore data, confirmed by InEight/Autodesk at ~9.9 RFIs/$1M)
- **Average cost per RFI: $1,080** (Procore study)
- **Average response time: 9.7 days** (Autodesk data)
- **25% of projects** receive no RFI response at all (Procore)
- **$5M commercial project = ~$100K in RFI-related costs** (Procore estimate)
- **One overdue RFI on a mid-market commercial project can cascade to $180K** in downstream costs when trade re-sequencing, procurement delays, and general conditions overrun compound (LinkedIn/Platforma analysis)
- RFIs can delay projects by up to 10% (Fieldwire)

### The Residential Gap
- Custom home projects ($500K-$2M) generate comparable decision density to commercial per-dollar, but almost none of it goes through a formal RFI process
- Most residential builder-architect communication happens via text, email, phone calls, and markup on printed plans
- No standard residential contract (AIA A105, ConsensusDocs 750) requires formal RFI tracking
- Residential projects rarely use project management software — only ~7% of builders under $10M revenue use tools like Procore (Procore's own investor presentations note residential as underpenetrated)
- ConstructionOnline and Buildern target smaller residential builders but adoption is low
- Buildxact targets residential specifically — new AI features for estimating, but RFI management is minimal

### What Gets Lost
- A $1M custom home likely generates 10-15 decision points per month during construction (~40-60 total over a 4-6 month build)
- Decisions about material substitutions, dimension clarifications, electrical/plumbing placement, change order approvals
- When these happen over text and nobody logs them, there's no:
  - Paper trail for warranty disputes
  - Evidence of what was agreed if the owner challenges scope
  - Documentation for the architect's professional liability insurer
  - Record of who authorized the change that later failed inspection

### AI Tools Entering This Space
- **Procore RFI Drafter Agent** (Beta, launched Sept 2025): AI drafts RFIs with subject, question, cost/schedule impact, related items. But Procore barely serves residential.
- **Buildxact** (residential-focused): AI features for takeoffs and estimating (Blu Takeoff Assistant, Blu Estimate Generator). RFI/communication tracking is basic.
- **ConstructionOnline** (UDA Technologies): Has RFI tracking module, targets custom builders, but it's form-based, not AI-powered
- **Buildern**: Digital RFI workflows with standardized templates and automated routing
- **Autodesk's "End of RFIs" thesis**: Argues that connected construction (BIM, shared models) should eliminate the NEED for RFIs by making all information available upfront. Contrarian position. Would reduce 50-75% of RFIs (Autodesk estimate).

### The Real AI Opportunity (the opinion)
- AI shouldn't just automate the form-filling part of RFIs (what Procore does)
- AI should capture informal communications (texts, emails, photos) and auto-generate decision records
- Think: WhatsApp thread between builder and architect → AI extracts the 3 decisions made, creates documented records with date, parties, scope impact
- This is closer to what AI daily log tools do (OpenSpace, Buildots) but for the decision layer
- Some early attempts: Newforma Konekt (construction admin automation), Part3 (reduce RFIs through better preconstruction)

### Harvard Design Magazine angle
- Article "Architects, Builders, and the Failed Promise of Deep Collaboration" documents how despite BIM/AI advances, the architect-builder communication gap persists
- The fundamental issue: construction documents from 1970s haven't evolved even as tools have
- RFIs are a SYMPTOM of disconnected design-build communication, not the disease
