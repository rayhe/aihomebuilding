Twelve Smart Home Platforms Have Died Since 2016. Your Builder Just Specced Number Thirteen Into a Fifty-Year Structure.

By Frank DeLuca · May 14, 2026

I keep a list, though not on purpose originally. It started in 2016 when a client called me about a house I had finished nine months earlier, confused because the smart home hub we had wired into a dedicated niche in the utility closet was bricked. Not malfunctioning. Bricked. Google had acquired Revolv through Nest, then killed it, pushing a firmware update that turned every unit into a plastic rectangle. The homeowner had paid $1,400 for the hub, another $3,200 for the proprietary sensors and switches, and $4,800 in low-voltage labor during rough-in to wire the house around it. Nine thousand four hundred dollars, and the vendor deleted it remotely like canceling a streaming subscription.

I started keeping the list after that. Revolv, 2016. Staples Connect died the same year, its Z-Wave and Zigbee hub abandoned after partner Zonoff walked away and nobody maintained the servers. Lowe's Iris followed in 2019, and Logitech discontinued Harmony later that year. Wink forced a $4.99 monthly subscription on previously free devices in 2020, which is not technically death but is functionally a ransom note. Google discontinued its own Nest Secure alarm system that same year. Insteon went dark in April 2022 without so much as an email to customers, its CEO scrubbing the company from his LinkedIn profile as if deleting the words would delete the thousands of houses running on a platform that had just evaporated. Logitech sunset its POP smart buttons in October 2024, offering owners a 15 percent discount on unrelated products as consolation. Belkin Wemo began dropping device support in 2025. Futurehome declared bankruptcy the same year, then had the audacity to keep charging surviving users 99 Norwegian kroner per month for access to devices they already owned, which prompted right-to-repair advocate Louis Rossmann to post a $5,000 bounty for anyone who could hack the firmware free.

That is twelve platforms in eight years. I have been building houses for twenty-two. The wiring outlasts every one of them.

The Math Nobody Does at Preconstruction

When I sit in a preconstruction meeting and the architect presents a smart home specification, I hear the technology, and I hear the budget, but I never hear the number that actually matters: how long will the company behind it exist? A hardwired smart home system costs between $10,000 and $25,000 for a standard installation, and luxury builds can push past $100,000 with dedicated audio zones, motorized shades, integrated security, and centralized automation controllers. Those numbers show up in the bid, clearly itemized and easy to approve. What does not show up is the implicit bet that the software running all of it will still be supported when the homeowner refinances in seven years, or when they try to sell in fifteen.

I ran the calculation that nobody else seems to want to run, using cost data from completed projects and the actual observed lifespans of the platforms on my list. A $20,000 smart home installation tied to a proprietary platform with a four-year expected lifespan carries an annualized technology depreciation of $5,340, factoring in not just the hardware and software replacement but the electrician's labor to reconfigure, reprogram, and retest every zone when the old system gets swapped for whatever comes next. Over the span of a standard 30-year mortgage, a homeowner will cycle through roughly seven complete platform iterations, accumulating $140,000 to $160,000 in technology refresh costs that appeared nowhere in the original construction budget, the closing documents, or the builder's warranty.

Meanwhile, the structured wiring I ran behind the walls during rough-in, the Cat 6 ethernet, the conduit, the speaker wire, the low-voltage pathways, cost $3,000 to $5,000 and will outlast every piece of software that ever touches it. The copper and plastic do not require a subscription. They do not get acquired by a competitor and sunsetted. They do not push a firmware update at 2 AM that bricks the living room lights.

What Actually Survives

After the Insteon collapse, something instructive happened. Home Assistant, the open-source home automation platform, immediately built an Insteon integration that let owners run their existing devices locally, without any cloud dependency, resurrecting hardware that the manufacturer had abandoned. Users who had purchased devices using standard protocols like Z-Wave or Zigbee rather than proprietary wireless could migrate to alternative hubs within days. Insteon's RF/powerline hybrid protocol made recovery harder, but the community managed it anyway because enough of the intelligence ran on the local hub rather than in a distant server farm.

The pattern is consistent across every platform death I have tracked over eight years, without a single exception, regardless of the company's size, its funding, or the promises it made about long-term support. Open protocols survive. Proprietary clouds disappear, and everything tethered to them becomes e-waste overnight. Environment America estimates that expired cloud services generate over 100 million pounds of electronic waste annually, and smart home devices are among the fastest-growing contributors because their cloud dependency makes them functionally unrecoverable once the vendor walks away.

The Matter protocol, backed by Apple, Google, Amazon, and Samsung, is supposed to fix this by creating a universal device language that works regardless of which platform controls it. After more than two years on the market, Matter supports roughly twenty device types, adoption has been slow enough that Tom's Guide called it stalled, and Belkin exited the smart home market rather than invest in Matter compatibility for its existing product lines. Matter 1.4, released in early 2026, added energy management and EV charging device types, but the installed base of pre-Matter devices in American homes dwarfs the number of Matter-certified products shipping today.

How I Spec Smart Home Now

I changed my specifications three platform deaths ago, and I have not had a callback since. Not one.

Wire everything. Commit to nothing. Every new build I manage gets Cat 6A to every room, conduit runs between floors and to the attic for future cabling, 14/2 to switch locations even when the plan calls for wireless switches, and a structured media panel in the utility closet with a dedicated 20-amp circuit and ventilation. Total cost premium over standard electrical runs $3,800 to $5,200 depending on the house, which is less than half what most builders budget for the proprietary platform they are about to bolt on top of it. That infrastructure will serve any smart home platform for the next thirty years because it provides the physical pathways without dictating the logic layer, and when the current brain dies, as it will, the next one plugs into the same wires without a single wall needing to be opened.

Specify protocols, not brands. My specs now read "Z-Wave 800 series or Zigbee 3.0 compatible devices" rather than "Lutron Caseta" or "Control4 system." A homeowner who buys Z-Wave switches today can swap to any Z-Wave hub tomorrow without pulling a single wire or replacing a single switch. That flexibility costs nothing extra at the point of purchase, but it is worth the entire replacement cost of the system the first time a platform dies.

Require local processing capability. If the hub cannot operate the lights, locks, thermostat, and security sensors without an internet connection, it fails my spec. Full stop. Cloud-optional means the house still works when the WiFi goes down, when the vendor gets acquired, when the servers get decommissioned. Cloud-required means someone else holds the kill switch to your client's home. That is not a specification. That is a liability.

Budget the replacement cycle. I now include a line item in every smart home proposal: "Technology Platform Refresh, Year 5-7, estimated $4,000-$8,000." Clients are initially surprised by it. Then I show them the list. Every name, every year, every dollar that evaporated. Then they understand.

The Consolidation Coming

It is about to get worse. Memoori's 2026 smart building report found that AI-native smart building vendors are being acquired faster than new ones are being formed. Thirty-four new companies entered the market in 2025, but only eleven startups were founded that same year, meaning the net supply of independent smart home AI platforms is shrinking. The venture math is straightforward: build a platform, acquire users, get acquired by a larger company that may or may not continue operating your product. ServiceTitan's 2026 Residential State of the Trades report found that 74 percent of contractors view AI as critical for efficiency but only 25 percent are actually using it, and among those who have tried, 50 percent report they do not trust AI's capabilities. Ninety-five percent of AI pilots fail due to poor implementation, a number so catastrophically high that it suggests the problem is structural, rooted in how these systems get specified and installed rather than in any individual contractor's competence.

If you are speccing a smart home AI platform into new construction today, you are not just choosing a product. You are making a bet on a company's survival in a market that is consolidating rapidly, during a period when the contractors installing these systems mostly do not trust them and the adoption failure rate sits at nineteen out of twenty.

Limitations

My "twelve platforms" count includes cloud-dependent smart home hubs, proprietary device ecosystems, and one case (Wink) where the platform survived but imposed a fee on previously free services, which I count as a de facto death for the original value proposition. It excludes adjacent smart device failures like Spotify Car Thing and iRobot that were not whole-home platforms. The $5,340 annualized depreciation figure assumes a $20,000 initial investment with a four-year replacement cycle including $1,360 in electrician labor per swap, extrapolated from three actual replatforming projects I managed in the Bay Area where labor rates run roughly 2.5 times the national average. In markets with lower electrician rates, the annual figure drops to approximately $3,800. The 30-year projection assumes static replacement costs, which likely underestimates future costs given historical skilled-labor inflation of 3-4 percent annually. Matter protocol adoption data is imprecise because the Connectivity Standards Alliance does not publish device-in-use figures, only certification counts. My spec recommendations assume single-family detached construction with accessible wall cavities during rough-in, and would require significant modification for retrofit, multifamily, or high-rise residential projects where conduit placement is constrained by existing structure.
