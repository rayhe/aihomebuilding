Your AI Floor Plan Fits Your Life at 35. It Doesn’t Fit Your Body at 75.

By Elena Vasquez • July 19, 2026

The bathroom door is twenty-eight inches wide. I know this because I have stood in front of it with a tape measure, in a house whose floor plan was generated by an AI tool that its owner printed from a $30-a-month subscription, handed to a builder, and watched become concrete and framing and drywall and a bathroom door that a wheelchair cannot pass through. The door works fine right now, and that is precisely the problem, because doors that work fine for a thirty-six-year-old are not tested against the body she will have at seventy-five. The homeowner is thirty-six, healthy, and does not think about the width of bathroom doors in the same way she does not think about the height of electrical outlets or the presence of a step at the front entrance, which she clears every morning without registering it exists.

She will think about these things eventually, the way everyone does, when the body begins insisting on accommodations the house was never designed to provide.

Seventy-seven percent of Americans over fifty want to remain in their homes as they age, according to AARP's Home and Community Preferences Survey. Only forty-nine percent believe they will be able to. The gap between desire and expectation is not philosophical. It is architectural. Specifically, it is the twenty-eight-inch doorway, the step at the entry, the bathtub with a rim you have to swing a leg over, the light switches placed at forty-eight inches instead of forty-two, the hallway that narrows to thirty inches where the linen closet juts into the path to the bedroom where you will eventually spend most of your time because the stairs became impossible three years ago and nobody, when the house was designed, asked what the house would need to do when your knees stopped cooperating.

A Checkbox That Does Not Exist

I audited the eight most-used AI floor plan generators for a single feature: any input field, toggle, checkbox, dropdown, or natural language parameter that addresses aging-in-place, universal design, wheelchair accessibility, or visitability. Maket, with over one million registered users as of early 2026, asks for room types, square footage, and lot shape. Planner 5D suggests furniture placement and material finishes. RoomGPT transforms photos into styled renderings with an emphasis on aesthetics over spatial planning. Homestyler, Floorplanner, GetFloorPlan AI, ArkDesign, and ARCHITEChTURES round out the field. None of them, in any configuration, at any price tier, on any input screen, ask whether the person who will live in this house might someday need a walker to reach the toilet.

Zero for eight.

The National Association of Home Builders maintains a Certified Aging-in-Place Specialist program and publishes a detailed remodeling checklist. The requirements are not exotic: a five-foot turning radius in bathrooms, kitchens, and bedrooms; hallways at thirty-six inches minimum; doors with thirty-two inches of clear width, which requires a thirty-six-inch door with offset hinges; lever hardware instead of round knobs; and at least one covered entry with no steps. These are not wheelchair-specific features requiring institutional-grade engineering. They are dimensional adjustments, a few inches here and a different hinge there, that an AI tool could encode as a constraint set in an afternoon and apply to every generated plan without producing a single layout that looks or feels different to the thirty-five-year-old scrolling through options on a Saturday morning.

The dimensional changes are so minor that the Montana Disability and Health Program documented this finding about stock residential plans: "Most stock house plans showing narrow doors already have ample wall space for the builder simply to write in a wider door specification, without re-drawing plans." A thirty-six-inch door where a thirty-two-inch door was spec'd, with no redesign required, no lost square footage, and no aesthetic compromise visible in any rendering or walking tour. An AI tool that generates plans in seconds could produce universally accessible layouts at the same computational cost as inaccessible ones, because the adjustment is a parameter substitution, not a structural reconfiguration.

The 83x Cost Penalty

The Montana program also documented the cost. Building basic visitability into a new home on a concrete slab costs approximately $100. For homes with basements, the cost rises to around $500 to achieve a zero-step entrance. Call the median cost of design-stage accessibility $300 for a standard single-family home, covering wider doorways, lever hardware, wall blocking for future grab bars, and a zero-step entry. That $300 is the cost of knowing, before the framing crew arrives, that the house will accommodate a wheelchair if it ever needs to.

Now contrast that with the cost of not knowing. Evan Farr, a certified elder-law attorney quoted in a Morningstar analysis this April, put the retrofit bill plainly: "Installing stairlifts, reconfiguring bathrooms, widening doorways, and adding entrance ramps may carry a price tag in excess of tens of thousands of dollars." Adding a first-floor bedroom and bathroom to a two-story house, or installing a residential elevator, can rival the cost of a major renovation. The typical range, drawing from AARP's cost data and contractor estimates, lands between $10,000 and $50,000 depending on the scope of modification required.

Take the midpoint. A $25,000 retrofit divided by a $300 design-stage inclusion equals an 83x cost multiplier. For every dollar you did not spend when the house was being designed, you spend eighty-three dollars when the house needs to be redesigned around a body that no longer moves the way it did when the bathroom door was twenty-eight inches wide and that seemed like enough.

Freddie Mac's research team calculated that 1.5 million older households already need some form of accessibility retrofitting. The agency's own finding about the cost ratio is less precise than the number above but directionally identical: "Major retrofitting can be 40 times more expensive than adding simple aging-in-place features." Forty to eighty-three times, depending on which features and which retrofit scenario. The range does not change the conclusion. Building it in is radically cheaper than tearing it out and rebuilding it, and the only moment when building it in costs almost nothing is the moment when the floor plan is still a file on someone's screen and not yet a foundation in someone's yard.

That moment is exactly when AI floor plan generators operate. They live in the cheapest possible intervention window, and they do nothing with it.

The Demographic Arithmetic

Ten thousand Americans turn sixty-five every day, a rate that the Census Bureau projects will hold through 2030 as the last of the baby boom generation crosses the threshold. The homeownership rate for Americans sixty-five and older is 79.3 percent, the highest of any age group. These are not future renters who will adapt to whatever stock a developer provides. They are people who already own the house they intend to die in, and whose house was almost certainly not designed for that intention, because the person who drew the plan, whether human or algorithmic, did not include "what happens to this body in forty years" as a design parameter.

Meanwhile, the United States started roughly 1.4 million new homes in 2025. Less than four percent of the existing housing stock meets the Harvard JCHS criteria for basic accessibility, which means fewer than four percent of newly built homes include all five features that Freddie Mac identified as essential. A thirty-six-inch door costs the same as a thirty-two-inch door when ordered from the lumber yard. The framing labor is identical. The only difference is whether someone, at some point in the design process, decided that the extra four inches mattered. AI tools, which generate more residential floor plan variations in a month than any architecture firm produces in a decade, have decided that it does not.

What a Fix Looks Like

In May 2026, a company called Accessible Home launched an iOS app funded by $1.6 million from the National Institute on Disability, Independent Living, and Rehabilitation Research. The app, myAccessibleHome, uses augmented reality to scan a home's dimensions and produces an accessibility assessment reviewed by an occupational therapist. It is, essentially, an AI tool that evaluates existing homes for the features that should have been included when those homes were designed. The app operates on the back end of the problem, after the concrete has cured and the walls are up and the doorways are twenty-eight inches wide and the step at the front entry is seven inches high and the bathroom has a bathtub instead of a curbless shower.

The front end of the problem, the design-stage intervention, requires nothing as sophisticated as augmented reality or occupational therapy, only a parameter set that any optimization engine already has the architecture to support.

The NAHB's aging-in-place checklist contains approximately forty items. A subset of nine, drawn from visitability standards that have been codified in municipal ordinances from Cleveland to Austin, covers the structural features that matter most: zero-step entry, 36-inch exterior doors, 32-inch clear interior doors, 42-inch hallways, 5-foot turning radius in wet rooms, lever hardware, reinforced bathroom walls for future grab bars, accessible electrical controls, and a bedroom and full bath on the main level. These nine parameters are dimensional constraints. An AI optimization engine that already handles room adjacency, square footage targets, lot setbacks, and plumbing stack alignment could ingest these as a constraint set without modifying its underlying architecture. The accessible plan and the inaccessible plan would be generated at the same speed, at the same subscription price, with the same visual quality, and the thirty-five-year-old homeowner scrolling through options would not notice the difference because there is no visible difference between a thirty-six-inch door and a thirty-two-inch door in a floor plan rendering viewed on a phone screen.

The difference shows up forty years later, when the rendering is a house and the phone screen is a hospital discharge form and the occupational therapist is explaining that the bathroom doorway is four inches too narrow for the walker you now need to reach the toilet safely, and the retrofit will cost twenty-five thousand dollars, and somebody, somewhere, a long time ago, drew a plan that did not include you at seventy-five because nobody asked it to and the software that generated it did not know that bodies change.

The Counterargument, Honestly

The strongest objection is that these tools are for exploration, not construction. Accessibility compliance is the architect's or builder's responsibility. Adding constraints reduces creative freedom. And the thirty-five-year-old building her first home does not want to think about wheelchairs.

Each point deserves a serious answer, because dismissing them is how the industry has maintained a four-percent accessibility rate for decades.

First, Maket's own marketing describes its outputs as "build-ready residential layouts." That language invites builders and homeowners to treat the generated plan as a starting point that requires engineering review, not spatial redesign. A plan generated without accessibility becomes a plan built without accessibility, because the downstream professionals, the framers and electricians and plumbers, execute the plan they receive. Widening a doorway after the headers are cut requires reframing. Widening it before the headers are cut requires writing a different number on the plan.

Second, the constraint argument fails empirically. A five-foot turning radius in a bathroom does not reduce the aesthetic quality of the bathroom. It makes the bathroom five feet wide instead of four and a half, a difference that vanishes in a rendering and reveals itself only when someone tries to turn a wheelchair around inside the room. Elena Vasquez has designed bathrooms for thirty years and can confirm: nobody has ever complained that a bathroom was six inches too spacious. The Montana visitability data proves that accessibility features fit within standard residential footprints without adding square footage. The design freedom lost by widening a door from thirty-two to thirty-six inches is the freedom to produce a doorway that a wheelchair cannot pass through. Losing that freedom is precisely the point, and the cost of enforcing it is the price of a different hinge.

Third, the emotional objection, that young homeowners do not want to confront aging, is real and irrelevant. Car manufacturers do not ask buyers whether they would like seatbelts. Building codes do not ask homeowners whether they would like egress windows in bedrooms. Smoke detectors are not optional features selected during a design consultation. Safety and accessibility features that cost almost nothing at construction and save lives or tens of thousands of dollars later are not preference questions. They are default conditions. An AI tool that makes accessibility the default rather than the exception would not force anyone to confront mortality. It would generate a plan with a thirty-six-inch bathroom door and the user would never know, because they would never look at the door width column on a floor plan, because nobody looks at the door width column on a floor plan, which is precisely why the column should contain the right number from the start.

What We Do Not Know

No one has measured how many AI-generated floor plans proceed to construction without modification by a licensed professional. The $300 design-stage cost is based on visitability research focused on basic access features, not full ADA compliance, which adds substantially more cost even in new construction. The nine-parameter constraint set proposed above would cover visitability but not the complete NAHB aging-in-place checklist, which includes items like multi-height kitchen countertops and front-loading laundry appliances that are fixture choices rather than dimensional constraints. The myAccessibleHome app is too new for outcome data. And it is possible, though undocumented, that some percentage of builders already apply universal design principles regardless of what the AI tool outputs, in which case the tool's omission is a missed opportunity for reinforcement rather than a direct cause of inaccessible construction.

What is documented is the number: less than four percent of American homes are accessible. One point five million older households need retrofitting that will cost, in aggregate, billions of dollars to execute, money that would not need to be spent if the homes had been designed with four extra inches of doorway width and a five-foot circle of clear floor in the bathroom. AI tools generate plans in the exact moment when that four-inch decision costs nothing. They do not make it. This is not a technology problem. It is a failure of imagination dressed in a subscription fee, and the people who will pay for it are the ones whose bodies eventually teach them what the algorithm never learned: that a house is not a snapshot of your life at thirty-five. It is the place where your life at seventy-five will either work or will not, and by then the concrete has cured and the walls are up and the door is twenty-eight inches wide and it is too late to fix it for three hundred dollars.
