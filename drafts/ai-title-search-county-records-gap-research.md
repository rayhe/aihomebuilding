# Research: AI Title Search and the County Records Gap

## Angle
AI is being sold as the solution to faster, cheaper title searches. But the county records AI reads from are fragmented, inconsistent, and sometimes literally stored in dusty courthouse boxes. The technology can process 400 pages in minutes — but it can't fix the 3,600 county recording systems that feed it incomplete data.

**Journalist:** Catherine "Code" Chen — policy, regulation, legal framework
**Kill test:** Does this help someone buying or building a home? YES — title issues are the #1 cause of closing delays, and homebuyers need to know what AI title search can and can't catch.

## Key Data Points

### Industry Scale
- Title insurance premiums: $18.5B in 2025 (+13.8% YoY) — ALTA
- Claims paid: $667 million in 2025 (down from $676M in 2024) — ALTA
- Top 5 firms handle ~95% of workload (First American, Fidelity, Old Republic, Chicago Title, Stewart Title)
- First American Financial: $7.5B total revenue in 2025

### Workforce Crisis
- 96,000 title insurers/abstractors employed in 2025 — BLS
- Down 11% from 107,000 in 2021
- Aging workforce, same pattern as appraisers (median age 62 in appraisal industry)
- "An obscure little industry that doesn't attract a lot of people" — Pat Stone, founder, Williston Financial Group

### AI Adoption
- First American's AgentNet Assist: Title Intelligence — AI-powered document analysis
  - Reduces processing time by up to 30 min per file
  - Analyzes title search packages, extracts key info, flags breaks in chain of title
  - Rolling out to internal teams first; not yet broadly available to agents
  - "Fueled by First American Title's underwriting excellence, proprietary data assets developed over decades" — Steve Vincini, president Agency Division
- DataTrace white paper: "Title Search Automation: Reality, Risk, and Responsibility of AI" (April 2026)
  - Largest provider of property/ownership data
  - 8.5 billion recorded document images
  - Covers 1,850+ U.S. jurisdictions
  - Key finding: "There is no mechanism for AI alone to deliver complete, accurate and insurable title from public records, because the record itself is not complete or verified" — Annette Cotton, CDO

### The Data Problem
- 3,000+ county offices maintain land records across the US (Balcony co-CEO Gregg Lester)
- Public records function as "system of notice" — they don't validate accuracy, completeness, or legal validity
- Records are often "nothing more than paperwork stored in dusty corners of a county courthouse" — Bisnow
- In rare cases, agents need to go back decades to decipher faded records with only names and plot numbers
- 1% variance in data accuracy × 5M annual home sales = up to 50,000 inaccurate titles — DataTrace
- These issues don't emerge immediately — surface over 5-10 years during refinance, sale, or litigation

### Fraud Escalation
- Real estate fraud losses: $275.1M in 2025 (up from $173M in 2024 — 59% YoY increase) — FBI IC3
- AI is being used by criminals to amplify fraud attacks
- Deed fraud rising for residential owners
- Suffolk County (NY) 2022 ransomware attack shut down clerk's office for weeks, closing off transactions

### Regulatory Landscape
- CFPB + FTC scrutinizing AI chatbots for consumer harm
- ALTA CEO Chris Morton: "As fraud schemes become more sophisticated and transactions grow more complex, the expertise of title and settlement professionals has never been more important"
- State-by-state regulatory frameworks create compliance requirements beyond AI's reach
- "Title insurance exists because of fragmented, inconsistent historical records and the need to transfer legal risk. That doesn't disappear with better technology." — Ashkán Zandieh, CRETI

### The Homeowner Angle
- Average title insurance cost: ~$1,000-$3,000 per transaction depending on state/value
- Title search is typically $200-$400 (separate from insurance premium)
- Closing delays from title issues add weeks and can kill deals
- Homebuyer has almost zero visibility into what the title search actually checked

## Original Contribution
Calculate: If First American saves 30 min per file with AI, and the average title examiner handles X files per day, what's the productivity gain vs. the 11% workforce decline? Does AI actually close the gap, or just slow the bleed?

Also: Cross-reference the 50,000 potential inaccurate titles (DataTrace's 1% figure) against the $667M in actual claims. What's the implied per-claim cost, and how many of those 50,000 ever get caught?

## Strongest Counterargument
Title plants (normalized, validated datasets maintained by the big firms) ARE significantly more reliable than raw county records. Companies like DataTrace have spent decades building these. AI applied to title plant data — not raw public records — is genuinely useful and probably more consistent than a tired human examiner at 4 PM on a Friday. The risk isn't AI on good data; it's startups applying AI to garbage county records and calling it "automated title."

## Limitations
- No independent audit of First American's "30 min savings" claim
- DataTrace's 1% variance figure is hypothetical/illustrative
- No comprehensive survey of county digitization rates
- Loss ratio in title is notoriously opaque; claim payments don't capture unreported issues
