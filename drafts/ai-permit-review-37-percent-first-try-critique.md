### 6. 🔬 Research Rigor — 9.5/10 ✅

**Original contribution:** The article's central analytical move — cross-referencing Denver's $4.6M CivCheck deployment with the simultaneous 59-position staff reduction in the same budget cycle and asking who captures the efficiency gain — is genuine original journalism. No press release, vendor pitch, or industry coverage frames the question this way. The article combines three independent data points (AI contract, staff cuts, existing shot clock/refund mechanisms) into a novel analysis that meets the guide's standard: "a dataset combination nobody made."

**Limitations:** Dedicated "What We Don't Know" section with four specific, honest blind spots:
1. Post-deployment data unavailable — CivCheck's 80% target unverifiable yet
2. NAHB $131,734 has wide confidence interval; regional variation immense (SF >$200K, TX maybe half)
3. No census of AI permitting deployment exists — "a few dozen" is Beyer's characterization, not a count
4. None of these tools address discretionary review delays, which dominate in restrictive jurisdictions

This is exemplary — specific, honest, and properly scoped. Not inline hedging but a dedicated structural accounting of what the analysis cannot claim.

**Strongest counterargument:** Stated at full strength in "Why Only a Few Dozen Cities." The political-barriers argument (delay serves certain constituencies; NIMBYism as procedural obstruction; reduced staffing/budgets against officials' interests) is given its own section, attributed to a named analyst (Beyer) at a named institution (Independent Institute), and then extended by the article itself: "removing one source of friction just shifts the chokepoint downstream to discretionary reviews, design review boards, neighborhood hearings, and environmental impact proceedings." Not strawmanned, not immediately dismissed.

**Verifiability:** All major claims traceable. Primary sources hyperlinked where possible. Others attributed with enough specificity for independent verification.

**Methodology transparency:** The article relies on published data rather than original calculations, so methodology transparency applies to source attribution rather than math. Where numbers are uncertain (NAHB's "wide" CI, "roughly 40 percent"), the article says so explicitly.
