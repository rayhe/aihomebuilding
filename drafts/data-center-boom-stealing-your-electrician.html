Your Panel Upgrade Was $3,500 Two Years Ago. Data Centers Made It $4,200.

AI data centers absorbed an estimated 296,700 construction workers in 2026, most of them electricians and HVAC techs. Licensed trade labor costs in data-center corridors have climbed 12 to 18 percent in two years. If you are scheduling a residential project with electrical scope, your timeline just got longer and your budget just got thinner.

Frank DeLuca
June 24, 2026

Last month I sat in a scheduling meeting for a 14-unit infill project in north Phoenix and watched my electrical subcontractor tell me, without any particular drama, that his lead journeyman and two apprentices had already taken positions at a data center campus east of town, not next month, not pending, gone, and that he could still staff my rough-in but not on the week I needed it and probably not the week after that either.

I have been managing residential construction for twenty-two years, and subs have always juggled priorities, ghosted phone calls, and stretched timelines when work got heavy, but what is happening right now is not a cycle or a seasonal squeeze or any of the familiar patterns that eventually correct themselves. It is a structural reallocation of the skilled trades workforce from an industry that pays them adequately to an industry that pays them lavishly, and the residential side of the ledger has no competitive response.

Where the Workers Went

In 2026, U.S. data center construction spending is projected to hit $86 billion, according to analysis from the Associated Builders and Contractors. ABC's own workforce model estimates that every billion dollars in construction spending requires approximately 3,450 workers. That puts data center labor demand at roughly 296,700 positions this year alone, accounting for 85 percent of the total construction workforce need ABC projected for 2026.

Most of those positions require electricians, and the economics of why are straightforward: according to the International Brotherhood of Electrical Workers, between 45 and 70 percent of a data center's entire construction budget goes to electrical subcontractors. Conduit, high-voltage distribution, backup generator wiring, UPS systems, rack-level power. A single hyperscale facility can employ hundreds of electricians for 18 to 30 months straight, and the facilities are not coming one at a time. Over 400 data centers are under active development across the country, according to ITIF research citing the Wall Street Journal.

Twenty thousand electricians leave the profession through retirement every year. One in three is between ages 50 and 70. And the pipeline to replace them takes years, not months. Becoming a licensed journeyman electrician requires a four-to-five-year apprenticeship. "You can't just snap your finger and say, in six months, we're going to have all these other electricians," Scott Norman of the Texas Association of Builders told Governing.

So when a data center campus in Abilene offers double the prevailing wage, the electricians go, because of course they do, because a journeyman with a family and a mortgage will take a 94 percent raise every single time you offer it, and no amount of loyalty to a residential GC changes the arithmetic.

What It Costs You

Danny Niemela, vice president and CFO at ArDan Construction in Arizona, has been tracking this for two years. In the Phoenix metropolitan area, he told Realtor.com, licensed trade labor costs have risen 12 to 18 percent since 2024. A 200-amp panel upgrade that cost $3,500 two years ago now runs $4,200. Material costs have risen too, but the labor premium is the larger driver.

Beyond price, there is schedule. AC repair that used to mean a two-day wait for a qualified technician in Phoenix now stretches to five or six days, sometimes longer during peak summer months when every residential unit with a compressor issue competes for the same shrinking pool of HVAC techs. On a remodel or new-build timeline, that kind of slippage cascades. Electrical rough-in delays push drywall. Drywall delays push trim. Trim delays push the cabinet installer who booked you for a specific week and has three other jobs behind yours.

I run schedules with float built into every critical path, and electrical rough-in has always been one of the gates I watch. What has changed is the magnitude of the uncertainty. Two years ago, I could budget a one-week buffer on a four-week electrical scope and feel confident, but today, in a data-center corridor market like Phoenix or Northern Virginia or central Texas, I am building in two to three weeks of contingency on the same scope, which is not a buffer so much as an admission that I cannot reliably predict when my electrical sub will have bodies available.

Calculating the Residential Damage

Here is a rough calculation nobody seems to have run yet, using publicly available inputs from iRecruit, BLS, and ABC. According to iRecruit's 2026 labor report, data center projects in Northern Virginia are paying electricians upward of $120,000 per year, while BLS data puts the national median for electricians at $61,790, a 94 percent premium that explains every scheduling gap I have encountered this year.

Now consider the residential side, where a typical single-family home requires roughly two electricians for three weeks of combined rough-in and finish work. A modest estimate of 50,000 electricians net-absorbed by data center projects (accounting for the portion of the 296,700 total workforce demand filled by electrical trades, minus new entrants and retirees who would have left anyway) translates to approximately 433,000 residential project-weeks of electrical capacity removed from the market per year.

NAHB reported roughly 1.36 million single-family housing starts in the most recent annualized data. If each start requires about six electrician-weeks of labor across all phases, total residential electrical demand runs around 8.2 million electrician-weeks annually. Losing 433,000 of those weeks represents a 5.3 percent capacity reduction nationwide, concentrated in the corridors where data centers are actually being built. In those markets, the real figure is likely two to three times higher.

In practice, five percent sounds manageable only if you have never tried to schedule a residential remodel in a market with zero labor slack, and the construction industry entered 2026 already short by 439,000 workers according to ITIF, which means adding a 5 percent drain to a market operating beyond capacity does not produce a proportional delay. It produces bidding wars, broken commitments, and schedule uncertainty that makes fixed-price residential contracts dangerous for anyone writing them.

The Case That This Is Temporary

It deserves to be heard at full strength. Data center construction is, by definition, a build-out, because you do not construct the same facility twice, and once the current wave of hyperscale campuses reaches operational status, the crush of demand for construction electricians should ease substantially, transitioning to a smaller operational workforce of technicians and maintenance engineers, and what remains after the build-out is a labor market that has absorbed the shock and moved on.

More importantly, data center wages are pulling new entrants into the trades at a pace not seen in decades. IBEW Local 26, serving the Washington, D.C., Maryland, and Virginia corridor, has doubled its membership since 2018 to 14,700 members, driven almost entirely by data center demand. Meta alone has committed $115 million to data center workforce training, focused specifically on electrical trades and commissioning skills. In five years, the argument goes, the trades pipeline will be larger than it was before the boom started, and residential construction will benefit from a bigger overall labor pool.

This argument has real merit, and it also does not help you if your kitchen remodel is scheduled for this September.

What You Can Actually Do

If you are a homeowner planning a project with significant electrical scope in a data-center corridor market, here is what twenty-two years of scheduling tells me.

Lock your electrical sub early, before you finalize design, and not just "get a quote" early but actually get on their calendar with a deposit, because in tight labor markets calendar position is the only currency that matters and the sub who gave you a number in April may not have the same crew available in August.

Budget 15 to 20 percent above 2024 electrical line items, not because materials changed that much but because the labor rate your sub charged two years ago no longer reflects what they need to pay a journeyman who has alternatives paying nearly double. If your bid comes in lower than that buffer, good, you saved money. If it comes in at $4,200 where you expected $3,500, you are prepared.

Ask your general contractor specifically how they are handling electrical sub availability risk. If the answer is vague reassurance, press harder, because a GC who is not actively managing this constraint in Phoenix, Austin, or the Virginia data center corridor is not paying attention to the single largest labor market disruption in residential construction since the post-2008 exodus.

For builders and GCs running multiple projects: AI scheduling tools like Buildots and ALICE Technologies exist specifically to optimize trade sequencing across constrained labor pools. They will not conjure electricians who do not exist, but they can minimize idle days between trades on a single project and identify sequencing alternatives that reduce your dependency on a specific sub showing up on a specific date. Whether the subscription cost justifies itself depends on your volume, but the problem these tools address is exactly the one this labor market is creating.

Limitations of This Analysis

Regional variation dominates this story in ways that national statistics obscure. A builder in rural Wisconsin is not competing with data center wages. A builder in Loudoun County, Virginia, is living inside the problem. The 5.3 percent national capacity estimate masks enormous local disparities, and the practical impact in any given market depends on proximity to active data center construction.

Isolating the data center effect from concurrent labor pressures is genuinely difficult because immigration enforcement has reduced the foreign-born construction workforce that ABC estimates at roughly one-third of the industry, retirements are accelerating independently of data center demand, material cost inflation has its own trajectory, and the 12 to 18 percent labor cost increase reported in Arizona reflects all of these forces acting simultaneously rather than data centers alone.

Wage data for data center electrical work is largely anecdotal, drawn from contractor interviews and recruiting platforms rather than systematic BLS surveys that separate data center construction from other nonresidential categories. The $120,000 figure in Northern Virginia likely represents a ceiling, not a median, across all data center electrical positions.
