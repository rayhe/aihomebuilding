# Research: AI Law Firm Licensed to Practice Law, Targeting Construction Contracts

## Topic
Superlegal has launched the first AI law firm in the U.S. authorized to practice law, targeting construction companies with contract review at $117/contract vs. traditional $500/hr lawyers. Authorized under Utah Supreme Court's Legal Services Innovation Sandbox.

## Journalist
Catherine "Code" Chen — policy, legal, regulation. This is a legal services innovation story with direct residential construction implications.

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? **YES.** Small contractors and GCs managing residential projects sign dozens of subcontractor and supplier agreements per project. Most can't afford $500/hr legal review on every contract. Missed clauses lead to disputes, change orders, and cost overruns. $117/contract review is potentially transformative for small/mid-size residential builders. The story also raises important questions about whether AI legal review is adequate for construction-specific liability.

## Original Analysis (Novel Contribution)
**Per-project legal cost calculation for residential construction:**
- Typical $500K-$2M custom residential project involves 15-30 subcontractor/supplier agreements
- Traditional review: ~2-3 hours/contract × $450-500/hr = $900-$1,500/contract
- 20 contracts × $1,000 avg = $20,000 in legal review per project
- Superlegal pricing: 20 contracts × $117 = $2,340
- Savings: ~$17,660 per project (88% reduction)
- That's 1-3.5% of total project cost — roughly equivalent to one avoided change order

**BUT:** This analysis assumes Superlegal's AI + attorney sign-off catches the same issues as a 3-hour deep-dive review. No independent verification of error rates exists. The "licensed attorney signing off" could be cursory at $117/contract.

## Strongest Counterargument
1. **Jurisdictional limitation:** Superlegal is authorized only in Utah. Construction contracts are governed by state law. A contractor in California or Texas can't use a Utah-authorized law firm for California-governed contracts without separate authorization. The Utah sandbox itself is shrinking and expires August 2027.

2. **Quality ceiling:** Construction contracts involve state-specific lien law, prevailing wage requirements, bonding provisions, and mechanic's lien waivers that vary dramatically by jurisdiction. An AI trained on general commercial contracts may miss the residential-specific poison pills (waiver of consequential damages, indemnification caps, pay-if-paid vs pay-when-paid clauses).

3. **Liability gap:** If the AI misses a clause that leads to a $200K dispute, who's liable? The $117/contract fee won't support meaningful malpractice coverage. Traditional attorneys carry E&O insurance sized to their billings.

## Limitations
- All cost savings figures come from Superlegal or its customers; no independent audit
- The "attorney signs off" process is not described in detail — unclear how much attorney time is involved
- No data on error rates or missed-clause frequency
- Utah sandbox data from Stanford is as of April 2025; current entity count may differ
- No residential-specific customers named (Western Partitions appears commercial)
- Long-term viability uncertain given sandbox contraction
