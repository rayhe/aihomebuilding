# Research: AI Flood Risk Prediction vs. FEMA Maps — The Gap That Could Cost You $100,000

## Journalist: Priya Greenwood (Sustainability & Green Building)

## Angle
FEMA flood maps are backward-looking and don't model climate change. AI-powered flood risk models (First Street Foundation, ClimateCheck) show millions of properties face unpriced flood risk. For someone building a new home, the lot they choose based on current FEMA maps may be in a flood zone by 2040. The 30-year cost of this blind spot: potentially six figures in uninsured losses and rising premiums.

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? **YES** — directly actionable. Before choosing a lot or signing a contract, check First Street's Flood Factor (free) and ClimateCheck. A $0 lookup could save $100K+ over a mortgage lifetime.

## Primary Sources (6)

### 1. First Street Foundation Flood Model
- AI flood model considers rainfall flooding AND climate change projections
- Found **6 million properties** face substantial flood risk **not accounted for by FEMA**
- Covers all 142 million properties in the US
- Property-level risk scores (Flood Factor 1-10)
- Models future conditions out to 2050
- Source: https://help.firststreet.org/hc/en-us/articles/1500000359741-Flood-Model-Methodology-Calculating-property-level-risk

### 3. FEMA Risk Rating 2.0
- New actuarial pricing methodology for NFIP flood insurance
- 37% of single-family NFIP policies cost $0-1,000/year
- 32% cost $1,000-2,000/year
- Some policyholders on "glide path" toward full risk-based costs (premiums rising annually)
- Risk Rating 2.0 uses replacement cost, flood type, distance to water, building characteristics
- Still doesn't fully model future climate scenarios
- Source: https://www.fema.gov/flood-insurance/work-with-nfip/risk-rating/single-family-home

### 4. ClimateCheck + Zelman Associates Analysis
- **63% of single-family building permits** now issued in regions with extreme heat risk (up from 40% in 1990)
- New construction at **lower overall flood risk** than existing housing stock (newer codes, better siting)
- BUT new builds in AZ, CA, TX at significantly higher drought risk
- Insurance premiums spiking in FL, TX, CA — some insurers pulling out entirely
- Source: https://climatecheck.com/blog/zelman-and-associates-builder-insights-new-homes-face-extreme-heat-risk-climatecheck-data

### 5. Insurify Analysis — NFIP Subsidy Impact

- Source: https://insurify.com/homeowners-insurance/insights/flood-insurance-cost-without-nfip/

## Novel Contribution
**A 30-year mortgage cost comparison:** What does it cost to build on a lot that FEMA says is safe but First Street rates as high-risk?

Calculation framework:
- NFIP flood insurance: $900-1,500/year currently in "low risk" zones
- If risk re-rated to match First Street assessment: $2,500-5,000/year
- Premium delta over 30 years: $48,000-$105,000
- Plus: 1% annual flood probability × average flood damage ($50,000) = $500/year expected loss = $15,000 over 30 years
- Total unpriced risk exposure: $63,000-$120,000 over mortgage life

A $0 lookup on floodfactor.com before buying the lot.

## Strongest Counterargument
First Street's models, while more forward-looking than FEMA's, rely on climate projections that carry significant uncertainty. SSP scenarios (shared socioeconomic pathways) span a wide range. The model has not been validated against 30 years of actual flood events because it's too new. FEMA's maps, while backward-looking, are based on decades of actual observed flood data. Over-predicting risk could make housing MORE expensive by pricing people out of areas that may never flood.

## Limitations
- First Street's methodology is proprietary — independent peer review is limited
- The 30-year cost calculation uses current premium structures that will change
- FEMA is actively updating maps and Risk Rating 2.0 is closing some of the gap
- Climate models disagree on regional precipitation changes
- No published study directly compares First Street predictions to actual flood events over a meaningful time period
- New construction already benefits from higher building codes (elevated foundations, flood vents) that mitigate damage

## What You Can Do (Actionable)
1. Before buying a lot: check floodfactor.com (free) for property-level flood risk
2. Check ClimateCheck.com for comprehensive climate risk (flood, heat, fire, drought, storm)
3. Compare to FEMA's flood map: msc.fema.gov — if they disagree, ask why
4. If building in ANY flood risk area: consider elevated foundation ($15,000-30,000 premium) vs. slab-on-grade
5. Get a flood insurance quote BEFORE closing on land — some zones now cost $5,000+/year
6. Ask your builder about flood-resistant materials (sealed concrete, elevated mechanicals)
7. Check if your city/county has adopted higher freeboard requirements (1-3 feet above base flood elevation)
