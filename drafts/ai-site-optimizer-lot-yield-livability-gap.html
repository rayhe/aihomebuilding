The Software That Plans 650 Neighborhoods a Week Has Never Walked Through One

By Elena Vasquez · July 18, 2026

Stand at the edge of a new subdivision in any fast-growing Sun Belt market and you will notice something missing before you can name it. Every lot is roughly the same width, every house sits the same distance from the curb, and the roads follow clean geometric arcs that minimize pavement cost per linear foot. And there is no shade anywhere, because every tree that once stood on the parcel was removed to clear the site for grading, and the saplings staked along the sidewalks will not cast meaningful shade for another twenty years.

Now open TestFit, the AI site planning platform that developers use to configure subdivision layouts in minutes instead of weeks. Plug in your parcel boundary, your zoning setbacks, your target lot count, your road width requirements, and your minimum lot dimensions. Press solve. Watch the algorithm iterate through hundreds of possible configurations and present you with the one that maximizes dwelling units per acre within your constraints, complete with road footage calculations, lot area statistics, and a preliminary cost estimate. The whole exercise takes less than two hours, down from two weeks.

Notice what the software asked you to input: lot count, setbacks, road widths, parking ratios, FAR targets, yield on cost.

Now notice what it did not ask. Where are the mature trees, and which lots face south? Where does water collect after a storm, and what will the wind feel like between these buildings in January? Is there a place where a child could ride a bicycle without crossing a collector road?

What the Optimizer Sees

Two platforms dominate AI-assisted site planning for residential development. TestFit, founded in Dallas in 2016, uses a proprietary parametric algorithm to generate buildable layouts in real time. Its subdivision tool divides a raw parcel into individual lots for single-family homes, townhomes, ADUs, and mixed-density configurations, automatically calculating road networks, utility corridors, and lot dimensions against user-defined constraints. Developers report saving $4,000 or more per feasibility study and planning sites five times faster than manual methods. Autodesk Forma, the successor to Spacemaker (acquired for $240 million in 2020), adds environmental analysis layers including daylight simulation, wind comfort modeling, and noise mapping to a generative design engine that produces and evaluates hundreds of massing options against zoning envelopes.

Both tools are genuine engineering achievements that accelerate a process previously manual, slow, and expensive, which meant that developers frequently committed to site plans without exploring alternatives that might have been superior. Both make it practical to test ideas that nobody would have spent the time to draw by hand, and that is a real and measurable improvement over the status quo.

But an optimizer is only as good as its objective function. And the objective function, in every case I have examined, is developer return: dwelling units per acre, yield on cost, building efficiency percentage, floor area ratio.

These are legitimate metrics, but they are also profoundly incomplete.

What Gets Optimized Out

Consider a 40-acre parcel in the exurban ring of any growing metropolitan area. It has rolling topography, a drainage swale along the western edge, and a stand of mature oaks clustered in the northeast quadrant where the land rises slightly and the soil drains well. Some of those oaks are 60 years old. Their canopies spread forty feet across and they shade nearly an acre of ground in aggregate.

Feed this parcel into a site optimizer and set a target of 95 single-family lots on minimum 6,000-square-foot parcels with 20-foot front setbacks and 5-foot side yards. Watch the algorithm work.

It will route the collector road to minimize total pavement. It will subdivide the buildable area into lots that meet the minimum dimensional requirements with as little wasted space as possible. It will cluster the lots tightly enough to hit the dwelling-units-per-acre target and spread them broadly enough to satisfy the setback constraints. It will present you with a clean, efficient, buildable plan that satisfies every parameter you gave it.

It will route the road directly through the oak stand, because the oak stand occupies buildable land and the algorithm has no input field for the biological infrastructure that took six decades to grow. It has no way to know that those oaks are worth anything. Not because the data does not exist, but because nobody told the optimizer to ask.

What the Research Says Those Trees Are Worth

Multiple nationwide surveys conducted by the University of Washington and the National Gardening Association found that mature trees in a well-landscaped yard increase a home's value by 7 to 19 percent. According to the National Arbor Day Foundation, a single mature tree can account for $10,000 or more of a home's resale value and typically represents 15 percent of the property's total worth. A Des Moines study analyzing 24,203 single-family residential sales from 2007 to 2015 confirmed that tree cover within 100 meters of a property significantly predicted higher sale prices, though the effect varies enormously by city and climate: a Virginia Tech/U.S. Forest Service study across nine metropolitan areas found that the value of existing tree cover ranges from negligible in hot, arid markets like Fresno to over $3,500 per home in cities like Milwaukee, where canopy confers cooling, curb appeal, and stormwater benefits simultaneously.

But here is the number that should stop every developer and every site planner cold: it takes 40 years or more for a newly planted tree to reach mature size. A thesis from the University of Texas at Arlington studying the Montgomery Farm conservation development in North Texas put it bluntly: "It would take half a lifetime for homeowners to realize the economic value of plantings in new neighborhoods established with young trees." The research directly challenged the common developer assumption that clearing a site and replanting is equivalent to preserving what was already there, but it is not equivalent at all. A 60-year-old oak and a six-foot nursery stock sapling are not substitutes for each other any more than a finished house and a pile of lumber are substitutes: one is a structure, and the other is raw material.

And yet, as that same thesis documented, "many developers frequently remove all vegetation from a site before developing the land." They do it because accommodating existing trees into a site plan costs more in design time, grading complexity, and root protection measures than clearing the site and starting with bare dirt. A site optimizer accelerates this impulse by producing its cleanest, most efficient layouts on blank ground, because constraints make the math harder and existing trees are constraints.

Beyond Trees: What Else the Algorithm Cannot Measure

A walkability study in Tucson examining four neighborhood design types found that traditional neighborhoods scored highest for walkability and physical activity, with vegetation and trees identified as critical to the "experience" dimension of walking. Cluster housing scored highest for social interactions with neighbors and perceived safety from crime. Enclosed communities, the gated subdivisions that site optimizers love because their road networks are self-contained and efficient, showed no standout benefit on any measured wellbeing dimension.

Vegetation was strongly and inversely associated with perceived crime. Not surveillance cameras, not cul-de-sac road patterns, not gated entries, but trees.

None of this is exotic knowledge, and urban planners have understood for decades that the qualities driving neighborhood satisfaction cannot be reduced to lot dimensions and parking ratios. Jan Gehl's Life Between Buildings in 1971 documented how edges, shade, and human-scale detail determine whether people linger or flee. Christopher Alexander's A Pattern Language in 1977 codified "tree places" and "accessible green" as measurable conditions for functional communities. Kevin Lynch's The Image of the City in 1960 mapped how residents navigate by landmarks and vegetated corridors, not by lot lines. We have known, for longer than these software companies have existed, that the qualities that make a place feel like somewhere worth living cannot be reduced to lot dimensions and parking ratios.

An AI site optimizer is not doing anything wrong. It is doing exactly what it was designed to do. It maximizes the variables its creators chose to maximize, and those variables reflect who is paying for the software: developers pay for TestFit, and developers want yield. Homebuyers want shade, privacy, and streets their children can cross safely, but homebuyers do not configure the site plan.

The Developer Math, Laid Bare

Here is the calculation that drives the bulldozer.

FactorValue
Mature oak tree, property value premium~$10,000 (Arbor Day Foundation)
Cost to remove that oak$500–$2,000
Ground area freed by removal (canopy footprint)~400 sq ft
Value of freed area at median finished lot value$40,000–$80,000 depending on market ($100–$200/sq ft)
Developer's short-term calculusSpend $1,000 to unlock $40K–$80K in additional lot area
Homebuyer's long-term loss$10,000 in property value + 40 years to regrow

From the developer's perspective, the math is difficult to argue with even in lower-value exurban markets. Removing the tree costs $1,000 and frees $40,000 or more in lot value. Preserving it sacrifices buildable area, complicates grading, requires root-protection fencing during construction, and constrains where the house pad can sit. Every dollar of complexity in design and construction comes directly out of the developer's margin.

From the homebuyer's perspective, the math runs in reverse. A lot with a mature tree is worth 7 to 19 percent more at resale. A lot without one will not achieve parity for four decades, if ever, because the replanting rate in new subdivisions is low, the survival rate of newly planted trees in construction-disturbed soil is lower, and most homeowners who buy in treeless subdivisions never invest the $2,000 to $5,000 it costs to plant and establish a shade tree properly.

TestFit's configurators evaluate more than 650 sites per week for developers, not all of them greenfield parcels with trees to lose, but a substantial fraction in the Sun Belt and exurban growth corridors where they are deployed most heavily. Across a year, that is roughly 33,800 site plans, each one optimized to maximize yield, each one representing a neighborhood where people will raise children and grow old. None of those plans include a field for "the sound of wind in a canopy" or "the feeling of walking home on a street where the light filters through leaves."

The Counterargument That Matters Most

Every lot sacrificed to tree preservation is a home not built. The National Association of Home Builders estimates the United States is short 1.5 million housing units, and the industry faces a chronic labor shortage of roughly 250,000 workers per month. In this context, an article arguing for 8 to 12 percent density reductions is not arguing for shade. It is arguing for fewer homes, higher prices, and longer commutes for the families priced out of the denser version of the same subdivision.

This is the strongest case for the optimizer as it currently works. When housing is scarce and families are driving farther from job centers to find anything they can afford, maximizing lot yield is not greed. It is a legitimate response to a supply crisis. A developer who preserves an oak grove and prices the remaining lots 15 percent higher has not solved a housing problem; that developer has built a premium enclave while the families who needed the cheaper lots go elsewhere.

I take this objection seriously because it is correct on its own terms. Density matters for affordability, and affordability matters more than shade for families who cannot afford the house at all. But the 8 to 12 percent density reduction in conservation-design layouts typically represents four to ten lots on a 95-lot subdivision, and the per-lot premium from mature landscaping often absorbs the lost revenue. Montgomery Farm in Allen, Texas, one of the few well-documented conservation subdivisions in the state, preserved 60 percent of its existing tree canopy, achieved lot premiums that offset the reduced count, and sold out faster than adjacent conventional developments. The choice between density and livability is real, but it is not as binary as the optimizer's default output implies.

Forma Sees More. Does It Use It?

Autodesk Forma, to its credit, includes environmental analysis tools that TestFit does not. Daylight simulation shows how sunlight reaches building facades and outdoor spaces. Wind comfort modeling predicts pedestrian-level wind conditions between buildings. Noise mapping estimates acoustic exposure from nearby roads and infrastructure. These are meaningful capabilities, and they distinguish Forma from tools that optimize purely on geometry and cost.

But a 2026 study published in MDPI Urban Science tested generative design plugins embedded in Forma's platform against the provisions of a Polish local spatial development plan and found significant compliance gaps. In one case, a building generated with specified dimensions of 12 by 15 meters "occupies the entire width of the lot," exceeding the intended footprint. In another, "objects were placed too densely, which made it impossible to meet the requirement specified in the LSDP regarding the minimum plot area." Critically, the tool could not incorporate biologically active area requirements, a regulatory constraint mandating that a certain percentage of each lot remain unpaved and vegetated, because no input parameter for that constraint exists in the generative design interface.

Forma's environmental analyses function as informational overlays rather than design constraints. They tell you what the daylight conditions will be after the optimizer places the buildings. They do not constrain the optimizer to place the buildings in ways that prioritize daylight, wind comfort, or green space. Optimization and analysis are separate workflows, and when they conflict, the optimization wins, because that is what the user pressed the button to do.

What Could Be Different

Nothing prevents a site optimizer from requiring tree canopy coverage as an input constraint alongside setbacks and lot dimensions, ingesting LiDAR-derived tree location data and treating every specimen above a certain caliper as a fixed obstacle around which roads and lot lines must route. Nothing about this is technically impossible, since LiDAR datasets that map individual tree locations and canopy dimensions are commercially available for most US markets. Walkability scoring algorithms already exist, and privacy distance calculations are straightforward geometry. Adding these as optimization constraints would increase computational cost and reduce maximum achievable density, and that reduction in density is exactly why it has not been done.

A few municipalities have started requiring tree preservation as a condition of subdivision approval, with King County, Washington, publishing a model tree regulation framework that mandates minimum canopy coverage percentages on developed lots. Under these regulations, a site optimizer that ignores trees would produce non-compliant plans that fail permit review. Regulatory pressure could force the optimization function to evolve, because the market alone will not.

For the Builder, Architect, or Buyer

If you are a developer using AI site planning tools, understand what your optimizer is leaving out. Run the yield-maximized layout, then run a second scenario with 15 percent of the site area reserved for preserved trees and green corridors, and compare the lot yields. If the density reduction is 8 to 12 percent, as it typically is in conservation-design subdivisions, you have a decision that the software cannot make for you: whether the per-lot premium from mature landscaping offsets the lost lot count.

If you are an architect working with a developer who uses these tools, advocate for running the environmental analysis before the generative layout, not after. Place the trees, the drainage corridors, and the view-sheds first, and let the optimizer fill in the lots around them. You will get fewer lots, but you will get better ones.

If you are a homebuyer walking a model home in a new subdivision, look up. If there is no canopy, ask what was there before the grading contractor arrived. Ask how many trees the developer preserved and how many it planted. Ask for the species and caliper of the replacements. A six-foot October Glory red maple in a tree tube is not a replacement for the 60-year-old white oak that used to stand where your driveway now is. It is a promise that your grandchildren might have shade.

What This Analysis Does Not Prove

Property value studies linking tree canopy to sale prices rely on hedonic pricing models that measure correlation, not causation. It is possible that neighborhoods with mature trees also tend to be older, more established, and located in desirable areas for reasons unrelated to the trees themselves. A Virginia Tech/USFS study found that the property value effect of tree cover varies enormously by city and is sometimes negligible in hot, arid markets, which suggests that tree value is heavily mediated by local market conditions, climate, and buyer preferences rather than a universal constant.

I have not used TestFit or Forma to design a subdivision myself. My assessment of their optimization functions is based on their published input parameters, manufacturer documentation, and independent reviews, not direct testing. It is possible that sophisticated users are already incorporating tree preservation and livability metrics into their workflows through manual constraints and post-processing, and the published descriptions do not capture this practice because it is not a product feature.

Conservation-design subdivisions do exist, and some of them achieve comparable or superior returns despite lower lot counts. But they remain a small fraction of new residential development, and their financial success often depends on market positioning, premium pricing, and buyers who specifically seek out tree-preserved communities, conditions that do not hold in every market.

AI site optimization tools are not the cause of barren, treeless subdivisions. Developers were clear-cutting sites long before TestFit existed. But these tools accelerate and systematize the yield-maximization impulse by making it effortless to produce the densest possible plan and expensive, in time and complexity, to produce anything else. When the default is maximum density, and every alternative requires manual intervention, the default wins, and that is how defaults work.

Six hundred fifty sites evaluated a week, each one optimized, each one missing something the algorithm was never asked to find.
