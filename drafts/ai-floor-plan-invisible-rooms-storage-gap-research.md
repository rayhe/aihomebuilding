# Research: AI Floor Plan Generators and the Invisible Room Problem

## Angle
AI floor plan generators systematically undersize or eliminate the spaces homeowners value most — laundry rooms, pantries, mudrooms, linen closets, storage — because these "service" spaces are invisible in the training data. Real estate listing photos, Pinterest boards, and Instagram feeds never show a pantry or a broom closet. The AI has never seen one, so it doesn't build one.

The result: homes designed to be photographed, not lived in.

## Journalist: Elena Vasquez (Architecture & Design)

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? **Yes.** Anyone using AI floor plan tools (Maket, Vitruvius, Planner 5D, Coohom, etc.) to design or evaluate a home needs to know that these tools have a systematic blind spot for the spaces that matter most in daily life. Actionable: what to check, what to demand, what to add back.

### 2. Polish Multi-Family Housing AI Study (MDPI, 2025)
**"AI-Assisted Residential Layout Generation: A Comparative Study of PlanFinder and Human-Designed Apartment Plans"**
- PlanFinder AI showed **"zero compliance"** with many mandatory legal/ergonomic criteria
- **"Did not designate spaces for washing machines and storage closets"**
- **"Failed to provide sufficient storage space in the corridor or closets"**
- Created "enfilade layout design" (walk-through rooms) — historically outdated, functionally adverse
- Term coined: **"technical blindness"** — algorithm prioritizes geometric layout over mandatory equipment/circulation requirements
- Human-designed plans scored significantly better on total compliance
- For mandatory building-code criteria, **AI floorplans failed at almost every point**

## The Training Data Argument
AI floor plan generators are trained on:
- Real estate listing photos → hero spaces only (kitchens, living rooms, master suites)
- Pinterest/Instagram → aspirational, photographable spaces
- Published floor plans → often simplified, missing utility annotations
- Design competition entries → showcase architecture, not service infrastructure

What's systematically absent from training data:
- Laundry rooms (almost never photographed in listings)
- Pantries (interior, windowless — unphotogenic)
- Mudrooms (transitional, utilitarian)
- Linen closets, coat closets, broom closets
- Mechanical rooms, utility spaces
- Garage storage zones
- Boot rooms, pet washing stations

## Original Contribution
A gap analysis between the NAHB "most wanted" list and what AI floor plan generators actually produce. The features with the highest buyer demand (laundry room at 87%, pantry at 81%, storage) are systematically the ones AI tools undersize or omit — because they're invisible in the visual training data.

This is not just an aesthetic concern. It has cost implications: buyers who use AI-generated plans as the basis for permits or construction documents will discover missing rooms during design development, triggering change orders that typically add 15-25% to project costs (AGC data).

## Strongest Counterargument
AI floor plan tools are improving rapidly. Maket, for example, now offers "agentic editing" — conversational refinement where users can say "add a pantry" or "make the laundry room bigger." The tools are designed as starting points, not final plans. The 70-75% that AI handles (per Maket CEO Patrick Murphy) is the exploration phase; the remaining 25-30% still requires professional review. The systematic bias in training data can be corrected by augmenting datasets with utility-rich plans and explicit room-type requirements.

## Limitations
- NAHB survey data is from 2024 (latest available); homebuyer preferences may shift
- Polish PlanFinder study examines multi-family apartments, not single-family homes — transferability to US market unclear
- No systematic study has tested ALL major AI floor plan tools against a standardized storage/utility checklist
- "Training data bias" argument is inferred from observed outputs, not confirmed by examining actual training datasets (which are proprietary)
- Article relies on proxy measures; no controlled experiment comparing AI vs. architect-designed plans on storage metrics
