# Research: Kitchen Cabinet Tariff — The Pricing Fog

## Journalist: Frank "The Foreman" DeLuca (Project Management & Operations)

## Angle
It's July 2026 — seven months into the 25% Section 232 tariff on kitchen cabinets. The 50% increase was supposed to hit January 1, 2026, but was delayed to January 1, 2027 via a Dec 31 proclamation. Builders pricing kitchens right now face a moving target: will the 50% rate kick in, or will trade deals exempt certain countries? Nobody knows. The result is a pricing fog that makes fixed-price contracts for homes and renovations nearly impossible to hold.

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? YES — directly actionable for anyone pricing a kitchen in the next 12 months. Tells them what the tariff actually affects, which cabinet tiers are hit hardest, and how to structure contracts to manage the uncertainty.

## Primary Sources

### 1. Tariff Structure (Section 232, Trade Expansion Act of 1962)
- Sept 29, 2025 proclamation: 25% on cabinets/vanities, 10% on timber/lumber (effective Oct 14, 2025)
- Dec 31, 2025 proclamation: DELAYED the 50% cabinet increase to Jan 1, 2027 (citing "productive negotiations with trade partners")
- The Jan 2027 increase won't apply to imports from countries reaching an agreement with the US
- Source: Sandler, Travis & Rosenberg, P.A. (strtrade.com)

### 2. US Kitchen Furniture Market Data (IndexBox, 2024 data)
- US consumption: 401M units, $39.1B
- Domestic production: 312M units ($36.5B) — a supply gap of 89M units filled by imports
- Imports: 89M units ($2.7B), 22% of consumption by volume
- Top import suppliers by value: Vietnam ($1B, 39% share), Canada, Malaysia
- Average import price collapsed from $195/unit (2013) to $30/unit (2024)
- Average export price: $117/unit — 4x the import price
- Indonesia cheapest at $14/unit

### 3. CAP/Brookings Analysis
- Total tariff impact: ~$30B added to annual residential construction investment
- 90% ($27B) falls on new home construction
- Per-home cost increase: $17,500 (at current building rates), rising to $18,500 if starts decline as predicted
- 450,000 fewer homes built through 2030 (100,000/year from 2030+)
- Equivalent to eliminating 6% of all homes built 2020-2024
- Source: Center for American Progress, Urban-Brookings Tax Policy Center

### 4. Builder/Industry Reaction
- PulteGroup CFO: tariffs could increase build costs by ~$1,500/home starting in 2026
- KB Home CEO: "some pressure on material costs from lumber"
- Lennar CEO: "cost structure in the industry is pushing higher and is difficult to manage"
- Allison Harlow (Curio Design Studio): "The volatility around pricing is damaging to the remodeling industry"
- Source: Reuters, AP, HousingWire

### 5. "Buy American" Not a Shield
- Houzz economist Marine Sargsyan: "Domestic manufacturers often rely on imported components such as hardware, slides, and finishes"
- Even prices on US-made cabinets affected by tariffs
- Stock cabinets ($3,000) could jump to $4,500 at 50% tariff
- Semi-custom ($10,000) could reach $15,000
- 85% of kitchen projects include cabinet upgrades (2025 Houzz Kitchen Trends Study)
- 91% of homeowners say they'll proceed with 2026 projects despite tariffs
- Source: The Kitchn, Houzz

### 6. Canadian Lumber Duties (compounding)
- Existing anti-dumping + countervailing duties on Canadian softwood: ~35%
- Section 232 adds 10% more = total ~45% on Canadian lumber
- Canada supplies 85% of all US lumber imports
- The US doesn't produce enough softwood lumber to meet domestic demand
- Source: NAHB, ProBuilder, Brookings

### 7. IEEPA Additional Layer
- Broad IEEPA tariffs on imports from Mexico, Canada, China add ANOTHER surcharge
- Gypsum (drywall): >50% of US imports from Canada/Mexico
- Doors, windows, frames: significant share from Canada and China
- IEEPA constitutionality pending before Supreme Court
- Source: Brookings, Tax Policy Center

## Original Contribution
Per-kitchen pricing fog calculation across cabinet tiers, factoring in the delay:

**Stock RTA cabinets (heavily imported, avg ~$3,000):**
- At 25% tariff (now): ~$3,750 (+$750)
- At 50% tariff (Jan 2027?): ~$4,500 (+$1,500)
- Builder's uncertainty range: $750/kitchen

**Semi-custom (mixed import/domestic, avg ~$10,000):**
- Import component ~40% (hardware, panels, finishes)
- At 25%: ~$11,000 (+$1,000)
- At 50%: ~$12,000 (+$2,000)
- Builder's uncertainty range: $1,000/kitchen

**Full custom domestic (avg ~$20,000):**
- Import component ~15% (hardware, hinges, slides, specialty finishes)
- At 25%: ~$20,750 (+$750)
- At 50%: ~$21,500 (+$1,500)
- Builder's uncertainty range: $750/kitchen

**For a builder pricing a 20-home development with semi-custom kitchens:**
- Current tariff cost: ~$20,000 total
- If 50% kicks in during build: ~$40,000 total
- Swing: $20,000 across the project — that's a margin-killer for production builders operating on 8-12% margins

## Strongest Counterargument
Tariff uncertainty is overstated. The Dec 31 delay suggests the administration is responsive to industry pressure, and the conditional carve-out for countries reaching trade agreements means the 50% rate may never apply to key suppliers. Vietnam, the #1 source, has incentives to negotiate. Domestic production (312M units) already supplies 78% of consumption — the import gap is real but not catastrophic. And 91% of homeowners are proceeding anyway.

## Limitations
- The per-kitchen cost calculations assume tariff passthrough rates of roughly 60% (consumer absorbs majority, per Houzz economist), but actual passthrough varies by market power and competitive dynamics
- The IndexBox market data is 2024; post-tariff 2025-2026 import volumes likely shifted and we don't have that data yet
- We cannot verify whether individual builders are actually re-pricing mid-contract or absorbing costs
- The $30B aggregate figure from CAP/Brookings includes the full tariff regime (50% assumption); at the current 25%, the actual impact is lower
