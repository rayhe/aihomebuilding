# Research: HUD $10M Robotics Bet vs Factory-Built Housing Graveyard

## Core Thesis
HUD just offered up to $10M per award for companies to demonstrate robotics and AI in factory-built housing. It's the federal government's first serious bet on the idea. But the private sector already burned through $3B+ trying the exact same thing — Katerra ($2B raised, bankrupt 2021), Veev ($647M raised, shuttered 2024, 170 units in 15 years). Meanwhile, factory-built housing's actual US market share is going *backwards*: 3% in 2024, down from 7% in 1998. The survivors aren't trying to build whole homes anymore. They're doing components, panels, micro-factories. Is HUD learning from the wreckage, or repeating it?

## Primary Sources

### HUD Program Details
- Formally titled: "Mass Market Solutions for Leveraging Robotics and AI Technologies for Home Construction Demonstration"
- Awards up to $10M, minimum $3M per award
- Expects to identify successful applicants by September 2026
- Seeks projects that "move beyond a single pilot and be scaled for wider use"
- Assessed whether robotics/AI manufacturing can reduce build times and costs vs conventional construction
- Covers panelized construction systems to fully volumetric modular housing
- Source: HUD online post, reported by Homes.com (Jun 9, 2026) and HousingWire (Jun 8, 2026)

### The Graveyard
- **Katerra**: Raised $2B+ (SoftBank, Foxconn). Valued at $3B. Bankrupt June 2021. Had 14 distribution centers planned, opened 2. Laid off 7,500 employees. Factory in Tracy, CA sold to VBC for $21.25M. Core problem: couldn't convince developers/contractors to abandon existing subcontractors. Executives lacked construction experience. "Every day is a fire drill." Components left factory with errors and were returned unused.
- **Veev**: Bay Area unicorn. $647M raised. Shuttered US operations Nov 2024 after constructing only 170 units in 15 years. KB Home SVP: "They viewed themselves as a supplier to the industry instead of a competitor" — fatal strategic flaw. Should have bought land and developed communities.
- Combined: $3B+ in private capital burned across two companies alone

### Market Share Data (NAHB/Census Bureau)
- 2024: 28,000 single-family homes built using modular/panelized out of 1,019,000 total = **3% market share**
- 2023: Also 3%
- 1998: **7%** — market share has been *declining* for 25 years
- Only 1% of single-family builders use AI to operate equipment (NAHB 2025 survey)
- Multifamily offsite: dropped from 7% (2023) to 3% (2024)
- Regional: Midwest 7%, Northeast 5%, South has largest absolute volume (13,000 homes)

### Market Size
- Global SF modular/prefab market: $52.24B (2025) → projected $65.92B by 2030 (4.8% CAGR)
- North America: $28.6B (2025)
- Transportation/assembly = 15-20% of project costs (major adoption barrier)
- 30% faster construction, 20% lower costs vs traditional (industry claim)

### The Survivors — What They Do Differently
- **Reframe Systems**: Raised $20M. Operates "microfactories" — small, local facilities vs mega-factories. First completed all-electric ADU in Massachusetts for low-income family. Claims 35% cost reduction, 50% build time reduction. Now in SoCal doing fire-resilient rebuilds. 20-year goal: 1M housing units. Key difference: localized, not centralized.
- **VBC (Volumetric Building Companies)**: Bought Katerra's Tracy, CA factory for $21.25M. Uses it for components (windows, cabinetry, countertops, trusses, panels) — NOT whole homes. Existing nationwide design/installation.
- **Connect Housing Blocks**: Robotics-fabricated stackable modular units in Ohio. Reducing 2-year timelines to 10 months for multifamily.
- **ABB Robotics + Cosmic Buildings**: Mobile robotic microfactories in LA. Millimeter-level precision. Claims 70% time reduction, 30% cost reduction.

### Why It Keeps Failing (Analysis)
- Developers/contractors have "very sticky relationships with their own engineers, suppliers, construction relationships" — Peter Lewis, Wharton Equity Partners
- "Industrial construction is very unlike a car factory because each project is different" — standardization demands don't match market reality
- Social stigma against prefab homes persists
- State/federal manufacturing regulatory barriers
- Local building codes vary wildly
- Inflexibility of prefab for single-family designs
- Transportation logistics for volumetric modules

### The HUD Bet's Unique Position
- Federal validation could de-risk private investment
- HousingWire: "federal validation could give private-sector innovators more runway and better capital investment terms"
- Separate HUD initiative: Advancing Building Technology Grant Program also supports AI tools for streamlining permitting
- Context: 80% of affordable housing projects delayed, resized, or canceled in past 6 months due to cost pressures (TD Bank survey)

## Article Angle
Frank DeLuca voice — twenty years of watching construction technology promises. He's seen Katerra's pitch deck. He watched Veev's Super Bowl ad. Now HUD is writing checks. The data says factory-built housing is losing ground, not gaining it. The survivors learned something the dead companies didn't: you don't replace the job site, you feed it better components. The $10M question isn't whether robots can build houses. It's whether anyone will buy them.
