The Person Who Graded Your Lumber Gets It Right Less Often Than a Coin Flip. The Machine That Replaces Him Runs at 92 Percent.

By Jake Kowalski • July 21, 2026

A board moves down the line at a hardwood mill, and a grader glances at it for two seconds, maybe three, scanning for knots and splits and wane and discoloration before marking it FAS, or No. 1 Common, or dropping it a grade. That mark sets the price. Between FAS and No. 1 Common red oak, the gap is roughly a third of the board's value, and between No. 1 Common and No. 2, another 30 percent can evaporate. He makes this call hundreds of times an hour, every hour, all day long, board after board after board, and on a board-by-board basis he gets the grade right 48 percent of the time.

Worse than a coin flip.

48%
Board-by-board grading accuracy of human line graders, per USDA Forest Service and Virginia Tech evaluation of 89 red oak boards against NHLA-certified inspector baseline

That number comes from a study conducted by the USDA Forest Service and Virginia Tech's Thomas M. Brooks Forest Products Center, in which researchers scanned 89 red oak boards with an automated multi-sensor system, had a production line grader grade the same boards, and then had a certified National Hardwood Lumber Association inspector establish the true grade. On a board-by-board basis, the line grader matched the certified grade 48 percent of the time, while the automated scanner matched 63 percent, a 31 percent improvement that still sounds modest until you look at the value estimate: the scanner pegged total lumber value to within 6 percent of the NHLA-certified figure, whereas the human overestimated by a full fifth.

That study used early-generation scanning technology. Current state of the art is Microtec's Goldeneye 300, a multi-sensor quality scanner manufactured in Italy and evaluated at Purdue University, which fires six different sensor types at each board: color cameras, black-and-white cameras, profile cameras, line lasers, dot lasers, and an X-ray source that reads internal structure invisible to any human eye. Board-by-board accuracy: 92.22 percent. Value accuracy per NHLA sales code: 99.50 percent. Not an incremental improvement over the 48 percent baseline, but a fundamentally different category of performance that separates guessing from knowing.

129 Years of Squinting at Wood

NHLA published its first hardwood grading rules in 1897, the same year William McKinley took office and J.J. Thomson discovered the electron, and the core system has not changed in any structurally meaningful way since then. A human examiner evaluates each board's "clear face cuttings," which are the rectangular defect-free areas that can be extracted from the board's surface, and the number, size, and arrangement of those cuttings determine what grade the board receives. FAS requires 83.3 percent clear yield from minimum cuttings of 3 inches by 7 feet or 4 inches by 5 feet. No. 1 Common requires 66.7 percent from smaller minimums. NHLA now has more than 650 members worldwide, and these specifications remain the foundation of the entire American hardwood trade.

Rado Gazo, a professor in Purdue's Department of Forestry and Natural Resources who led the Goldeneye evaluation, described the human grading experience in four words: "It's like driving in a blizzard." He is not exaggerating. A line grader at a grade mill evaluates boards moving at full production speed, which in a modern operation means hundreds of boards per hour, each one requiring a spatial judgment about defect location, size, and yield potential that involves mental arithmetic, pattern recognition, and the kind of sustained focus that degrades after the first few hours of a shift and never fully recovers by the next morning.

What Misgrading Actually Costs

Lumber accounts for roughly one-third of all materials in a new home, according to NAHB data. On a house with $150,000 in materials, approximately $50,000 goes to lumber, and while framing is mostly softwood graded by a different set of rules, finish lumber for cabinets, trim, flooring, and millwork is hardwood graded under NHLA rules, where grade accuracy directly sets the price.

When sawmill line graders overvalue hardwood by 20 percent and that markup flows through the supply chain to the builder, a custom home with $15,000 in hardwood finish materials carries roughly $3,000 in overpayment baked into the grade stamps. A production builder running 50 homes a year absorbs an aggregate cost reaching six figures annually, and nobody tracks it because nobody at the job site has any mechanism to verify whether the grade on each board actually matches the wood in their hands. Grade stamp says FAS, so FAS it is, and the builder trusts a mark made by someone who gets it wrong more than half the time.

Reverse the problem and the sawmill loses too, because boards that deserve a higher grade get stamped lower and sell for less than their true value, which is revenue the mill earned on the stump and the kiln but forfeited at the grading station. Gene Wengert, the late wood scientist universally known as "The Wood Doctor," spent decades documenting how combining grades strategically could reduce a furniture shop's lumber cost by 15 to 25 percent while maintaining yield, but his optimizations depended on accurate grading as a baseline assumption, and when the grades themselves are wrong half the time, every optimization built on top of them inherits that error and amplifies it.

A Scanner That Sees Through Wood, a Supply Chain That Does Not Care

Microtec's Goldeneye 300 is commercially available and deployed in sawmills across Europe and in some North American operations. Purdue demonstrated it grading kiln-dried rough hardwood at production speed using NHLA's own GradeView algorithm, and it achieved Japanese Agricultural Standard certification in 2023, meaning Japan trusts this machine to grade structural timber for one of the most quality-demanding construction markets on earth.

Researchers have pushed further still. A bilateral defect cutting system published in 2025 used a YOLO-based deep learning model to detect knots, cracks, and pith defects on both faces of a board simultaneously, achieving 0.94 mean average precision and producing a 12.3 percent increase in volume yield compared to current production cutting strategies, which means more usable wood from each log, graded more accurately and cut more efficiently, without a human squinting at boards for eight straight hours.

But residential builders do not buy from sawmills. A supply chain stretches from mill to wholesaler to lumberyard to contractor, and at each step the grade stamp travels with the board unchanged, no questions asked, no verification performed. A builder in Raleigh or Phoenix or Portland buys hardwood from a distributor who bought from a regional wholesaler who bought from a mill that may or may not have invested in scanner technology, and no price signal rewards the mill for making that investment because no one downstream can tell whether any given board was graded by a person or a machine.

Framing lumber has a partial answer to this problem through machine stress-rated grading, which runs each board through rollers that measure deflection under a known load and assigns a grade based on measured mechanical properties rather than visual characteristics alone, carrying a distinct stamp designation and commanding a modest premium. But MSR applies to structural softwood only, and the hardwood that goes into your cabinets, trim, stair treads, and flooring is still graded by a person working at production speed, getting it wrong more often than right, at a moment when framing lumber just spiked 5.11 percent in Q2 2026 to $916.62 per thousand board feet according to Gordian's RSMeans data.

What a Builder Can Do

Ask your hardwood supplier whether the mill uses automated grading equipment. Most suppliers will not know, and the question itself will be unusual enough to register. For significant volumes of finish-grade hardwood, request the mill source and inquire directly, because mills that have invested in scanner technology are generally proud of it and will say so.

For softwood framing, specify MSR-graded lumber when structural performance matters, particularly for engineered applications like long-span headers and floor joists where actual stiffness rather than visual grade determines performance. Cost premium runs 5 to 10 percent over visually graded lumber, and in exchange you get a grade that was measured instead of guessed.

If you are a homeowner buying hardwood for a renovation project, bring a moisture meter and a grading reference card to the lumberyard. NHLA sells its rulebook for under $100, and while you will not become a certified inspector overnight, you will know enough to recognize when a board stamped FAS has a knot the size of your thumb sitting squarely in the middle of it, and that recognition alone puts you ahead of the person who graded it in the first place.

What This Analysis Cannot Prove

That 48 percent accuracy figure comes from a single study of 89 red oak boards at one production facility. Sample size is small, species is specific, and accuracy rates at other mills, with other species, under different production conditions could be higher or lower. No large-scale multi-mill study of line grading accuracy has been published, and it is possible that the proportion of domestically graded hardwood passing through automated systems is already higher than the literature suggests, in which case builders are receiving scanner-graded lumber without knowing it. Equally possible that they are not.

Overvaluation of 20 percent applies to the sample as a whole, not to each individual board. Some boards were overgraded and others undergraded, and the aggregate overvaluation reflects the net effect after errors partially cancel. Cost impact on a residential project depends entirely on the mix of species, grades, and volumes purchased, and on how much of that overvaluation survives the supply chain markups between mill and job site.

Why a Sawmill Operator Would Disagree

A mill operator would point out, correctly, that NHLA's grading system is designed for aggregate accuracy, not board-by-board precision, and the rules explicitly allow a "money value allowance" of 4 percent, meaning a graded package can deviate from true value by up to that amount and still be within specification. Under this standard, 48 percent board-by-board accuracy is beside the point, because nobody buys one board at wholesale. Packs are what trade, and overgraded boards and undergraded boards cancel each other out close enough to the true value.

Merit to that argument, certainly. But "close enough in aggregate" is a different standard from "accurate for each board you install in someone's house," and a builder who buys a pack of No. 1 Common and finds two boards that should have been 2A Common is not made whole by three boards in the same pack that should have been FAS. He has two substandard boards in his cabinets. Neither the accountant's reconciliation nor NHLA's 4 percent allowance changes what went into the wall.

NHLA's system has served the American hardwood industry for 129 years, and the scanners that might improve on it have been commercially available at current performance levels for less than a decade. Fair to ask whether the technology is mature enough for the industry to depend on, and whether disrupting a functional if imperfect system is worth the cost. But 48 percent is not imperfect. It is a coin flip with a 20 percent surcharge, and the boards that carry it are going into someone's house.
