# Research Notes: Robotic Framing in Pacific Palisades Fire Rebuild

**Journalist:** Jake Kowalski
**Beat:** Construction Technology, Tools, Robotics
**Date:** May 12, 2026

## Core Story

Cosmic Buildings has deployed a mobile robotic factory — a robotic arm under a tent — directly to job sites in Pacific Palisades, where it cuts, drills, nails, and assembles wood framing panels. The company claims it can frame a 2,000 sq ft home in 10 days, deliver a finished home in 4-6 months, and do it 30% cheaper than conventional construction.

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? **YES.** If you lost your home in the Palisades fire, you're looking at 2,100+ permitted rebuilds competing for the same labor pool. A framing robot that actually works could save you months and tens of thousands. But the claims need scrutiny.

## Original Contribution: The Framing Math

**The 30% claim needs unpacking.**

Framing typically represents 10-15% of total residential build cost. If Cosmic reduces framing labor by 60%, that's a 6-9% reduction in total labor costs. Even adding material efficiency gains (less waste), getting to 30% total savings requires significant reductions in other phases too.

Calculation:
- Conventional 2,000 sq ft at $475/sq ft (midpoint) = $950,000
- Framing: ~12% of total = $114,000
- 60% labor reduction on framing = ~$68,400 saved on framing labor
- That's ~7.2% of total build cost from framing alone
- To hit 30% ($285,000 savings), they need to save another $216,600 on non-framing work
- Speed savings are real: shorter construction = less general conditions cost (superintendent, crane rental, porta-potties, insurance carry)
- General conditions typically 10-15% of total = $95K-$142K. If timeline shrinks from 18 months to 6 months, that's 67% less = $63K-$95K savings
- Combined: $68K framing + $80K general conditions ≈ $148K = ~15.5% savings
- The remaining 14.5% gap to reach 30% is unexplained by published data

**Robot throughput bottleneck:**
- 1 robot, 10 days per home, 40 clients
- If the robot runs continuously: 40 homes × 10 days = 400 working days = ~18 months just for framing
- Cosmic likely plans to deploy multiple robots, but current funding ($8.8M) limits scale
- Each mobile factory setup costs (estimate) $200K-$500K for industrial robotic arm + tooling

## Strongest Counterargument

The construction tech graveyard is deep. Katerra ($2B) and Veev ($647M) both promised to revolutionize residential construction and both went bankrupt. Both had more funding than Cosmic's $8.8M. The fundamental challenge isn't the robot — it's everything else: permitting, inspections, subcontractor coordination, client changes, weather, material supply chains. Framing is roughly 10 days of a 6-month build. The robot optimizes one sprint in a marathon.

## Limitations

- All cost and timeline claims come from Cosmic directly; no independent verification or completed-home data available
- 40 clients are "in various stages of design or permitting" — unclear how many homes have been completed
- The $400-550/sq ft Altadena benchmark may not perfectly apply to Palisades (higher land value, potentially different building requirements)
- Mobile factory model is untested at scale — one robot on one lot at a time
- No data on robot maintenance, downtime, or failure rates

## Angle

Jake Kowalski voice: excited about the hardware, skeptical about the economics, respects the craft. The robot is genuinely cool — a Kuka arm (or similar) under a tent, humming away while it nails framing panels. But cool hardware doesn't save a company. The question is whether a $8.8M startup with one robot can actually dent the 3,800-home queue in LA's most expensive rebuild since the 1994 earthquake.
