# Research: Data Center Construction Boom Is Draining Residential Electricians

## Slug: data-center-labor-drain-residential
## Journalist: Marcus "Steel" Washington (Workforce & Labor)
## Date: June 9, 2026

## Angle
Big Tech's AI infrastructure buildout is pulling skilled trades workers—especially electricians—away from residential construction. Data center construction just hit $50.7B annual rate (surpassing public transportation spending), and companies like Meta are investing $115M in free training programs with guaranteed jobs. Meanwhile, small homebuilders can't compete on wages and are watching their electricians walk out the door. The AI revolution is making your house harder to build.

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? YES — if you're building or renovating, you need to understand why your electrician might not show up, why electrical work is taking longer, and what the wage gap means for your project timeline and cost.

## Primary Sources

### 1. Insurance Journal / Texas Tribune (April 29, 2026)
- Gene Lantrip, Abilene TX builder: "It's taken us two months longer to build the houses than what it did before the data centers were coming in."
- Scotty Wristen, owner WE Electric (Abilene): lost 5 workers to data centers. Pays $20/hr vs $35/hr at data centers.
- "Some of them were guys that I've had for eight years, five years. They came, they got trained, and they left to go out there."
- Texas: 71,000 electricians employed, 300+ data centers operating, ~100 more planned
- 45-70% of data center construction budget goes to electrical subcontractors (IBEW)
- Stargate (OpenAI/Crusoe/Oracle): 4 million sq ft in Abilene area
- Texas loosening licensing: reciprocity with Iowa, Alabama, Arkansas
- Source: https://www.insurancejournal.com/news/southcentral/2026/04/29/867616.htm

### 2. Meta America's Workforce Academy (June 8, 2026)
- $115M investment, free 5-week training, guaranteed jobs at Meta contractor data center sites
- Partnership with CBRE and Associated Builders and Contractors
- Pilot: Louisiana, Ohio, Indiana, Texas
- NCCER credential + America's Workforce Certificate
- Dina Powell McCormick: "Now a new generation will pour the foundations and lay the fiber that secures American strength in this new age."
- Previous program LevelUp: 35,000 applications in 7 days for 1,000 slots
- Meta already spent $600B pledged for US infrastructure over 3 years
- 8,000 white-collar layoffs → $115M blue-collar training investment (irony)
- Sources: WSJ, Reuters, Fox Business, GlobeNewsWire

### 3. HBI/NAHB Fall 2025 Construction Labor Market Report (Oct 2025)
- Skilled labor shortage costs $10.8 billion/year total
- $2.663B in higher carrying costs + $8.143B in lost home production
- 19,000 fewer single-family homes built per year due to shortage
- Average construction time increase: 1.98 months (smaller builders: 2.36 months)
- 3.3 million payroll residential construction workers
- Immigrant workers: 25.5% of construction workforce (historic high); 1 in 3 craftsmen
- Gen Z in construction doubled from 6.4% (2019) to 14.1% (2023)
- Women in construction hit 20-year high of 11.2% (2024)
- Source: NAHB.org

### 4. Associated Builders and Contractors (ABC) Report (Jan 2026)
- Construction industry needs 349,000 net new workers in 2026, 456,000 in 2027
- Employment grew by only 14,000 in 2025
- 13% of ABC members under contract for data center projects
- Nearly 1 in 5 electricians older than 55
- 20,000 electricians leaving workforce each year; 1 in 3 aged 50-70
- Source: Bisnow

### 5. Census Bureau / MarketWatch (June 2, 2026)
- Data center construction spending: $50.7B annual rate in April 2026
- Up 28% year-over-year
- Just surpassed public transportation construction spending ($49.3B)
- Data center share of nonresidential market: from 2% to over 6%
- Source: MarketWatch

### 6. Industrial Info Resources (May 2026)
- $44.7B invested in data centers Q1 2026 (28% YoY gain)
- 2,913 data centers planned, valued at ~$2.4 TRILLION over 2026-2030
- Previous 2023-2025 period: only 313 data centers completed ($142.2B)
- Source: industrialinfo.com

### 7. JLL 2026 Market Outlook
- Data center construction costs increasing at 7% CAGR (2020-2025)
- Average global cost: $7.7M/MW (2020) → $10.7M/MW (2025) → $11.3M/MW forecast 2026
- "Limited availability of skilled trades" explicitly called out as constraint
- Source: jll.com

## Original Contribution
Calculate the wage premium data centers pay over residential electricians and what that means for a typical home build cost. $35/hr vs $20/hr = 75% wage premium. If residential builders match even half that premium to retain workers, the electrical portion of a typical home build ($15K-$25K) could increase 25-40%, adding $4K-$10K to the cost of a new home. Nobody has done this math publicly.

## Strongest Counterargument
The data center boom could actually be good for residential construction long-term: it's drawing more people INTO the trades (Gen Z doubling, 35,000 applicants for Meta's program), creating a larger trained workforce that eventually cycles back to residential work when data center construction peaks. Also, data center construction is project-based — those workers will need new projects eventually.

## Limitations
- Wage data is anecdotal (two Abilene sources), not a systematic national survey
- The 2-month delay is one builder's experience in one Texas market, not nationally representative
- Can't separate data center impact from other factors causing construction delays (materials, permitting, interest rates)
- No data on how many residential electricians specifically have moved to data center work nationwide
