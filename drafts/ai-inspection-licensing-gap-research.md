# Research: AI Home Inspection Software — Three Tools Ship, Zero Liability Frameworks Follow

## Journalist: Catherine "Code" Chen (Policy & Regulation)

## Thesis
Three AI inspection tools shipped in 2026. No state licensing framework addresses them. The E&O insurance question is unresolved. Colorado's AI Act (effective June 30, 2026) creates the first potential regulatory collision.

## Primary Sources

### 1. Spectora AI Report Assist (June 2026)
- Source: BusinessWire press release, June 2026
- Denver-based, 10,000+ inspectors use Spectora platform
- AI Report Assist: voice + photo → template matching. Inspector speaks observations, AI matches to pre-approved comments
- Early access: ~25% time savings per inspection
- AI Scheduling Agent also launched
- Quote (Efra Rivera, NxtMove Inspections): "Instead of stopping to search for comments, I can queue up multiple defects using audio, and the AI matches them to the right narratives."
- Key: AI drafts report language based on inspector's voice input. Who is the "author" of the report?

### 2. Palmtech 11 AI Image Defect Detector (Feb 2026)
- Source: EINPresswire, Feb 17, 2026; Capterra listings
- Porch Group (NASDAQ: PRCH) product
- AI Image Defect Detector: scans inspection photos for cracks, moisture, visible defects
- Generates detailed report comments from photo analysis
- $50/user/month
- Quote (Adam Long, GM of Inspection): "Inspectors told us they want smarter assistance, fewer repetitive steps, and more control over how their reports look and read."
- Key: AI identifies defects the inspector may have missed — creates duty-of-care question

### 3. Alpine Intelligence (March 2026)
- Source: 5280 Magazine (Denver), Alpine press release via PRLog
- Alpine Building Performance, Denver, founded 2016
- Free ChatGPT-powered predictive forecaster
- Input: MLS property sheet → Output: era-based risk analysis, system-by-system flagged risks
- Founded by Andrew Sams, developed with Mason Minor
- Origin: pandemic housing boom, agents buying sight-unseen needed pre-showing risk intel
- Disclaimer: "predictive tool intended solely for general informational use and does not replace a professional property inspection"
- Key: What happens when AI predicts a defect, buyer skips inspection, defect materializes?

### 4. Licensing Landscape
- 38 states + DC license home inspectors (InterNACHI data)
- ZERO state licensing frameworks mention AI tools, AI-assisted reporting, or automated defect detection
- NHIE (National Home Inspection Examination) has no AI-related content
- ASHI Standards of Practice (SoP) and InterNACHI SoP: both define inspection as "non-invasive, visual examination" — AI image analysis arguably extends beyond "visual examination by the inspector"
- No state has issued guidance on whether AI-generated report language constitutes the inspector's professional opinion

### 5. E&O Insurance Gap
- Most home inspector E&O policies cover "negligent acts, errors, or omissions in the performance of professional services"
- Unresolved: If AI flags a defect the inspector overrides, and the defect causes damage → was the inspector negligent for ignoring AI?
- Unresolved: If AI misses a defect the inspector would have caught without AI reliance → does the AI vendor share liability?
- Unresolved: If AI drafts report language that mischaracterizes severity → who authored the mischaracterization?
- CFPB has addressed AVMs (Automated Valuation Models) under Dodd-Frank 1125, but no equivalent for inspection AI
- Insurance carriers have begun adding AI-specific exclusions to some professional liability policies (Wiley Law, 2026)

### 6. Colorado AI Act (SB21-169, effective June 30, 2026)
- Source: Kiteworks compliance guide, Lexology 2026 outlook
- Requires documented risk assessments for "high-risk AI systems"
- Defines high-risk: AI that makes or is a "substantial factor" in consequential decisions
- Home inspection AI that flags/misses defects affecting purchase decisions → plausibly high-risk
- Algorithmic discrimination safeguards required
- First state law that could theoretically apply to inspection AI tools

### 7. Market Data
- $4.94B global home inspection market (2025)
- $333 median US home inspection cost
- 18% of inspections flag safety issues
- 18% of home inspectors report being sued or threatened with lawsuits
- ~77,000 licensed home inspectors in the US

## Original Contribution
**Economic pressure calculation:** If AI tools save 25% of inspection time (Spectora claim), and the average inspector does 4 inspections/week at $333 each, that's ~$17,300/year in additional revenue capacity (52 additional inspections × $333). The economic incentive to adopt is strong. But the liability framework is zero.

**Three unresolved liability scenarios:**
1. AI flags → inspector overrides → defect materializes → negligence claim
2. AI misses → inspector relied on AI → missed defect → malpractice
3. AI drafts language → language mischaracterizes severity → buyer relies on report → loss

**Colorado collision:** The Colorado AI Act took effect June 30, 2026. All three tools are being used by Colorado inspectors. None appear to have published risk assessments. This is the first live regulatory exposure for inspection AI.

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? **YES.** If you're a homebuyer, you need to know whether your inspector's report was partly written by AI and what that means for your legal protections. If you're a home inspector, you need to know the liability exposure you're taking on.
