# Research: BlackRock's $100M Skilled Trades Bet and the Residential Squeeze

## Angle
The world's largest asset manager is spending $100 million to train electricians, plumbers, and HVAC techs. The press releases frame it as philanthropy. The financial logic tells a different story: BlackRock backs multi-billion-dollar data center projects that can't get built without bodies. The same workers who wire your kitchen are being recruited, trained, and funneled into infrastructure that has nothing to do with housing. Marcus Washington — investigative, human-centered, follows the money.

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? **YES.** If you're building or remodeling, the electricians, plumbers, and HVAC techs you need are being systematically recruited away from residential work. Understanding the capital flows behind the shortage helps you plan timelines and budgets. If you're a tradesperson, the training landscape is shifting in ways that create real career decisions.

## Novel Contribution
Calculate the **cost per worker** of BlackRock's program ($100M ÷ 50,000 workers = $2,000/worker) and compare it to the actual cost of training an electrician through a union apprenticeship program (~$40,000-$80,000 over 4-5 years). $2,000/worker is supplemental funding for existing programs, not standalone training. Then cross-reference BlackRock's infrastructure fund returns (~8-12% on $40B+ AUM) against the training spend: $100M to protect multi-billion-dollar investments is a rounding error. This isn't philanthropy, it's portfolio insurance.

## Strongest Counterargument
More trained workers benefits everyone, including residential. BlackRock's $100M doesn't specify that trainees must work on data centers. A plumber trained through Future Builders can wire your kitchen just as easily as a data center. The argument that this hurts residential is a zero-sum fallacy — the alternative is that these workers never get trained at all. The residential shortage predates the data center boom. BlackRock is adding supply to a market that desperately needs it, and the residential sector will benefit from the overflow.

## Limitations
- We don't have data on where Future Builders graduates actually work after training — the residential vs. infrastructure split is inferred, not measured
- The $2,000/worker calculation is rough — some of the $100M goes to JFF administration, program design, not direct training costs
- Union apprenticeship completion rates (which vary by trade and region) would contextualize the 50,000 target, but we don't have Future Builders-specific projections
- BlackRock's infrastructure AUM figure varies by reporting period; the $40B+ figure is approximate based on public filings

## Methodology Note
The "portfolio insurance" framing compares BlackRock's $100M training spend against its infrastructure fund AUM and projected returns. If BlackRock manages $40B in infrastructure assets earning 10% annually ($4B/year in returns), the $100M over 5 years ($20M/year) represents 0.5% of annual returns — a modest insurance premium against workforce-driven project delays that could erode returns by multiples of that amount.
