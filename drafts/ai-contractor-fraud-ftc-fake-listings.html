One Company Created Thousands of Fake Contractor Listings. The Calls Went to the Philippines. The FTC Took Eight Years to Catch Them.

By Marcus Washington · July 20, 2026 · 9 min read

Tens of thousands of homeowners searched Google for a local plumber, an electrician, a garage door repair company. They found a listing with a local address, a local phone number, and a spread of five-star reviews praising fast service and fair prices, so they called. A voice on the other end said a technician could be there Tuesday.

Every address was fabricated, the calls routed to a center in the Philippines, and the reviews were ghost-written by employees and their relatives. In many cases, nobody showed up at all.

On May 11, 2026, the Department of Justice, on behalf of the Federal Trade Commission, along with the state of Illinois, sued B.E.S.T. GDR LLC, a Chicago company doing business as Premium Home Service, and its owner Yosef Bernath. According to the complaint, PHS had been doing this since at least 2018: creating thousands of fake online business profiles for home repair companies that did not exist, in cities where PHS had no office, no technicians, and no intention of doing the work itself. When PHS did arrange for someone to show up, the complaint says, the technicians were “not licensed or qualified to perform the job, resulting in subpar—and at times dangerous—work.”

Eight years. That is how long Premium Home Service operated before a federal agency filed suit.

The Playbook Was Simple

PHS targeted the search terms real people type when something breaks at 2 AM on a Saturday and they need someone who can be there by Monday: “electrical services,” “plumbing,” “heating and cooling,” “garage door repair.” For each term, in each city, PHS created a business listing with a name that sounded local, an address that belonged to an unrelated third party or was entirely made up, and a phone number with the area code of wherever the listing claimed to be, and then it posted fabricated five-star reviews written by employees and their relatives to boost these phantom businesses in search results and dilute any legitimate one-star reviews from consumers who had already been burned.

When a homeowner called, representatives pretended to be local and promised a technician within a specific window, but what happened next, according to the complaint, was consistently worse than doing nothing at all: either no one showed up, or an unqualified person showed up and performed work that the FTC characterizes as substandard and, in some cases, outright dangerous to the occupants of the home. PHS also allegedly violated the Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act by collecting consumers’ financial information through fraudulent pretenses.

Commissioners voted 2-0 to refer the case to the Department of Justice.

Why Eight Years Matters

PHS started operating in 2018, a federal rule on fake reviews was finalized in 2024, and the suit landed in 2026. That timeline is the gap contractors and homeowners are living inside, and it is about to get wider.

By today’s standards, PHS was labor-intensive: creating thousands of business listings one at a time, writing reviews by hand, managing a call center in the Philippines with enough staff to handle incoming service calls from cities where nobody from PHS had ever set foot, dispatching whoever they could find as “technicians.” It required coordination, payroll, infrastructure. According to the National Insurance Crime Bureau, contractor fraud cases have increased 38 percent over the last three years, a trend that predates AI tools and reflects an enforcement system that moves slower than the fraud it chases. By the time a federal agency builds a case, files it, and litigates it, the scam has been running for most of a decade.

What has changed since 2024 is the cost of running this same playbook.

Commercially available AI website builders can produce a full contractor website (branding, portfolio, contact form) in under five minutes. A single prompt to a large language model generates 50 distinct five-star reviews in two minutes, each with different vocabulary, different reviewer names, different neighborhood details. Feed the model 15 actual reviews from a legitimate competitor for tone matching, and the output is indistinguishable from organic feedback without statistical analysis.

Lead aggregation platforms sell the same lead to four to six contractors simultaneously, creating a marketplace where legitimate bids compete against fraudulent ones and neither the platform nor the homeowner can tell the difference. A fraudulent operation buys leads alongside the real ones. Four of the five bids a homeowner receives could be fake, and the infrastructure required to place them is a laptop, a credit card, and an afternoon.

Maybe less.

What This Does Not Prove

Nothing in the FTC’s complaint against Premium Home Service mentions artificial intelligence. PHS appears to have operated with human labor: employees and relatives writing reviews, call center staff in the Philippines handling calls. AI acceleration described in this article draws on the documented capabilities of commercially available tools and the mechanics described by industry analysts, not on the specific PHS case. PHS proves the playbook works at scale with human effort, and AI reduces the cost of replicating it to almost nothing.

NICB’s 38 percent increase in contractor fraud includes all types, not exclusively the fake-listing variant. Insurance claim inflation by real contractors accounts for the largest share, not fabricated businesses. Total consumer fraud losses reported to the FTC for 2025 reached $15.9 billion across all categories; home improvement is a subset that the FTC does not break out as a standalone line item in the Sentinel Data Book summary.

No vetting protocol is a guarantee. A sufficiently sophisticated scam operator with real local knowledge could pass all four tests. What these questions catch are the fast, cheap operations that make up most of the problem. And it is worth noting that platforms are not standing still: Google says its machine-learning systems flagged and removed more than 170 million policy-violating reviews in 2023 alone, a number that has grown every year. Whether detection is improving fast enough to match AI-powered generation is an open question this article cannot answer.

The Legitimate Contractors Pay Twice

Natalia Piper has been running a general contracting business long enough to build a legitimate five-star reputation, but she got hit from the other direction. In mid-2025, scammers posted fake negative reviews on her Google Maps listing unless she paid. Her rating dropped from 5.0 stars to 3.6. Overnight.

“It took me eight years to get my reputation in the market, and one guy can damage it in one day.”

What Piper described is the cost every licensed, insured contractor absorbs when unverified operators flood the market: a race to the bottom that punishes the people who invested in apprenticeships, bonding, and code compliance.

Google and Amazon have reported removing hundreds of millions of fake reviews each year. Fake review supply grows faster than the removal rate. Piper’s eight years of earned reputation and PHS’s eight years of undetected fraud share the same structural cause: no platform, no regulator, and no licensing board can verify at the speed the fraud operates.

Boston’s police department issued a community alert in March 2026 warning residents about traveling contractors with “recently created websites with glowing reviews” and “generic business names” like A1 Paving and City & Town Paving. It advised homeowners to verify Home Improvement Contractor registration through the state, which is useful if you know how to check, know which state agency handles it, and know that the polished website with 47 five-star reviews might be six weeks old.

The Four Questions a Fake Contractor Cannot Answer

AI-generated shell businesses fail in a specific and predictable way. They cannot produce local knowledge that was never in the training data and is not indexable by a search engine. Fraud breaks when you ask questions whose answers live in the kind of tacit local knowledge that no website builder, no review generator, and no large language model can fabricate from a prompt, because the information was never digitized in the first place.

Ask these before you sign anything:

1. Where do you dump construction debris in this county? Every jurisdiction has specific transfer stations, permitted dump sites, and restricted-material rules. A real contractor who has worked in your area for more than six months knows the name and location of the facility they use. A fake one will give a generic answer or redirect the question.

2. Which permit office covers this zip code? In unincorporated areas the county handles permits, in incorporated cities the city does, and in some metro areas the jurisdictions overlap in ways that confuse even experienced builders. A contractor who has pulled permits locally will name the specific office, the typical wait time, and probably complain about the parking.

3. Can you name two subcontractors you have used on jobs within 20 miles of here? AI-generated identities fail cross-referencing, so ask for names, then call those subcontractors and ask if they have worked with this general contractor. Real relationships are verifiable in a single phone call; fabricated ones collapse the moment someone picks up.

4. What is your contractor’s license number, and which state board issued it? Then verify it yourself on the state licensing board’s website before you continue the conversation, a step that takes under three minutes and eliminates more fraud than every other filter combined. PHS, according to the FTC, used company names “typically not registered with the state.”

The Enforcement Gap Nobody Is Closing

One verification step requires no conversation at all: run a WHOIS lookup on the contractor’s domain before you even call. If the domain was registered less than six months ago but the website says “Serving the Greater Phoenix Area Since 2014,” stop. A scam operation built last month will have a website and maybe a Google listing, but it will not have a LinkedIn company page with employees, an Instagram with job-site photos spanning years, or a BBB profile that matches the claimed founding date.

In 2024, the FTC finalized a new enforcement tool: the Reviews and Testimonials Rule. It targets businesses that buy fake reviews for themselves. It does not impose verification requirements on the platforms that host the reviews. Google allows businesses to report fakes, but individual business owners cannot reach a human at Google to escalate removal. What the rule gives the FTC is a cleaner enforcement path against operators like PHS. It does nothing to prevent the next PHS from launching tomorrow, because the barrier to creating a convincing fake contractor presence dropped from months of coordinated human labor to hours of prompting an AI, and the detection and enforcement cycle still runs in years.

In 2025, the FTC received 3 million fraud reports and brought suit against PHS. One company, after nearly a decade of operation, out of what the Commission itself acknowledges is an undercount of actual fraud. State contractor licensing boards verify that a real person holds a real license. They do not monitor whether the 400 Google Business listings claiming that license number are legitimate or whether 399 of them were generated last Tuesday.

If you are hiring a contractor for any project over $1,000: check the license number on your state’s licensing board website, run WHOIS on their domain, ask them where they dump debris, and call a subcontractor they name. Those four steps take less than 15 minutes combined. The FTC took eight years.
