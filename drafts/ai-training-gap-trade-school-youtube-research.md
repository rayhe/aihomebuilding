# Research: AI Training Gap in Construction Trades

## Core Thesis
87% of construction tradespeople say AI training belongs in trade schools. None of them learned it there. The construction industry's AI training infrastructure is YouTube videos and corporate PR announcements.

## Journalist
Marcus "Steel" Washington — workforce & labor beat

## Primary Sources

### DEWALT / Stanley Black & Decker Study (April 23, 2026)
- Source: dewalt.mediaroom.com, The Manufacturer (themanufacturer.com)
- 90% of construction professionals believe AI will be indispensable within 5 years
- **Only 8% are currently using AI in day-to-day work**
- 88% expect AI use to increase within next year
- 83% believe it will become standard within 3 years
- **86% feel "somewhat or very prepared" to work with AI**
- **But**: YouTube (40%), Coursera (39%), video tutorials (42%) are actual training sources
- **87% argue AI education should be embedded in trade schools and technical programs**
- 59% highlight need for hands-on, task-based training
- Early adopters: 46% site ops/monitoring, 46% planning/design, 41% estimation/procurement
- Top benefits: productivity (35%), cost savings (34%), QC (35%)
- DEWALT response: $75,000 to ABC's Trimmer Construction Education Fund + pilot with ABC Central Florida

### Microsoft / NABTU Partnership (April 21, 2026)
- Source: news.microsoft.com, constructiondive.com, nabtu.org

- Launching free AI literacy courses and industry-recognized credentials
- Extending to TradesFutures nonprofit in 34 states
- Delivery via LinkedIn Learning platform
- Aligned to Microsoft's "Community-First AI Infrastructure" commitments

### OpenAI / NABTU Partnership (March 11, 2026)

- Focus: AI infrastructure construction → union careers

### Workforce Shortage Data
- **649,300** avg annual openings in construction (BLS, 2024-2034)
- **349,000** additional workers needed in 2026, **456,000** in 2027 (ABC)
- **439,000** additional workers needed in 2025 (ABC estimate)
- **41%** of construction workforce nearing retirement by 2031 (NCCER)
- Construction trades share: **71%** (2005) → **59%** (2024) — NAHB/ACS
- Total construction labor force: 12.1 million, of which 7.1 million in trades
- At peak (2006): 8.5 million trades workers
- Labor shortage costs: **$10.8 billion/year** (HBI Fall 2025 report)
  - $2.663B in higher carrying costs
  - $8.143B in lost single-family production (19,000 homes)
- 1 in 5 electricians over 55
- 1 in 3 construction tradesmen are immigrants (26.3% overall, higher in trades)
- Data centers siphoning electricians with per diem incentives even for local workers

## Original Analysis

### The Per-Worker Math
DEWALT committed $75,000 to ABC's Trimmer Construction Education Fund for AI training grants.
- ABC has ~23,000 member companies
- ABC's own workforce model says 349,000 additional workers needed in 2026
- $75,000 ÷ 349,000 workers needed = **$0.21 per worker**
- Even against just the pilot program participants, this is a marketing expenditure, not a training budget.

### The "Prepared" Paradox
- 86% feel prepared → 8% actually use AI day-to-day
- Gap: 78 percentage points between confidence and practice
- The 86% learned from YouTube and Coursera — platforms with zero jobsite context, zero safety certification, zero tool-specific training
- Compare to actual apprenticeship structure: OSHA 30, NCCER certifications, 4-5 year programs with 8,000+ hours
- AI training: 10-minute YouTube video watched between tasks

### The Microsoft/LinkedIn Learning Funnel
- Microsoft/NABTU "no-cost AI literacy" delivered via LinkedIn Learning
- LinkedIn Learning is a subscription product ($29.99/mo)
- "No-cost" through partnership → funnel to LinkedIn ecosystem
- Teaching construction workers to use Copilot isn't teaching them to fly drones or interpret thermal imaging
- Actual AI skills for construction: BIM model interpretation, computer vision QC, drone photogrammetry, laser scanning point clouds, AI-assisted scheduling — none are on LinkedIn Learning's construction track

### What Trade Schools Would Actually Need to Teach
1. Drone operation + photogrammetry interpretation (Part 107)
2. AI-assisted takeoff software (Togal.AI, STACK, Buildxact)
3. Computer vision QC systems (Buildots, Doxel)
4. BIM model navigation and clash detection
5. AI scheduling tools (ALICE Technologies)
6. Laser scanner / point cloud interpretation
7. AI safety compliance systems (PPE detection, proximity alerts)
8. None of these are in any NCCER or ABC curriculum as of July 2026.
