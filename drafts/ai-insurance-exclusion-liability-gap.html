Your Architect Used AI to Draft the Specs. The Insurer Just Excluded AI From the Policy. Nobody Told You.

By Catherine Chen

On January 1, 2026, Verisk Analytics published two standardized endorsement forms, CG 40 47 and CG 40 48, that give insurance carriers ready-made language to exclude losses arising from the use of generative artificial intelligence. Those forms name specific tools, including ChatGPT, Midjourney, and DALL-E, covering outputs that are "created, generated, or produced" by AI systems, and they landed on desks at Berkley, AIG, and Great American, three of the largest writers of professional liability insurance for architects and engineers in the United States.

Those carriers are now filing for regulatory approval to attach the exclusions to Errors & Omissions policies, the very policies that protect homeowners when design professionals make mistakes that show up in concrete, steel, and the structural calculations that keep a roof from collapsing under its own snow load.

Nobody is required to tell you this is happening.

What the Exclusion Actually Says

Professional liability insurance for architects and engineers operates on a claims-made basis, meaning the policy that matters is the one in force when the claim is filed, not the one in force when the error occurred. A structural specification generated by AI in March of 2026, relied upon by the contractor in June, and discovered to be wrong when the foundation cracks in November, would be adjudicated against whatever policy the architect carries at the time of the claim. If that policy now includes Verisk's CG 40 47 endorsement, the insurer's position is straightforward: the loss arose from AI-generated output, and AI-generated output is excluded.

"AI creates liability exposures that traditional policies weren't designed to address," said Travis Landers, President and Founder of Risk Specialty Group, in January. "Firms renewing policies this quarter need to review the new language carefully." He was talking to the architects. Not to you.

Critically, the endorsement does not distinguish between an architect who asks ChatGPT to draft a complete structural specification and one who uses a purpose-built AI tool to check a hand-calculated beam schedule against the International Residential Code. Both involve AI-generated output, and both are potentially excluded under the forms as written, despite the fact that the distinction matters enormously in practice and not at all in the policy language.

Why Insurers Are Running

Stanford Law School researchers measured hallucination rates across general-purpose AI tools and found error rates between 58 and 88 percent for factual claims. Specialized tools performed better but still showed error rates of 20 to 33 percent, numbers that would be career-ending for a human professional and that are apparently considered acceptable for a tool that costs $20 a month and never sleeps.

No published benchmark exists for construction-specific AI accuracy on residential specifications, not from NIST, not from ASTM, and not from any of the AI construction startups currently raising money on the promise that their tools will revolutionize the design process. An industry being asked to price a risk for which no actuarial data exists, and its answer, rationally enough, is to decline the invitation entirely until someone produces the data.

A June 2026 paper in the journal Buildings became the first academic work to map generative AI's liability chain across the architecture, engineering, and construction lifecycle. Its authors identified what they called "autonomous hallucination entering safety-relevant design information" as a primary pathway of harm, a phrase that sounds clinical until you realize it describes the scenario where an AI tool produces a specification for your home's structural framing that looks correct, passes a cursory review, gets stamped by a licensed professional, and is wrong in a way that nobody discovers until something moves that shouldn't.

A Contract Gap Nobody Has Closed

None of the three major standard-form construction contract families in the United States, not the AIA (American Institute of Architects), not the EJCDC (Engineers Joint Contract Documents Committee), not ConsensusDocs, contain a single clause addressing AI use, AI disclosure, or AI liability allocation. These documents govern hundreds of billions of dollars in annual construction spending and they were last substantially updated before generative AI entered the professional workflow.

This means your architect is not contractually required to tell you whether AI touched your plans. Your engineer is not contractually required to disclose whether the HVAC load calculation was run through an AI model or a hand calculation. Your contractor is not contractually required to verify which parts of the specification set were AI-generated before building from them. This absence is not an oversight in the sense that someone forgot to include AI language. It is an oversight in the sense that nobody in the standard-form drafting committees has yet determined how to allocate the risk, and the committees are not known for speed.

Common law fills the vacuum. Researchers in the Suffolk University Journal of High Technology Law analyzed existing case law and concluded that US courts hold the professional who used the AI tool to the same standard of care as if they had done the work by hand. In Copper Mountain v. Industrial Systems, the court enforced a contractor's obligation to remedy damages even when the owner had mandated the use of specific methods. Applied to construction: using AI does not lower the standard of care, and choosing to rely on AI output does not shift liability to the AI provider.

That sounds like adequate protection until you combine it with the insurance exclusion. The architect is liable, but the architect's insurance no longer covers the thing the architect is liable for, which means the homeowner's recourse is a judgment against the architect personally, useful only if the architect has assets sufficient to cover the cost of tearing out and replacing the work that the AI got wrong.

Calculating Your Uninsured Exposure

Consider a custom home with $500,000 in construction costs and $60,000 in design fees, numbers that are median for a custom build in the coastal markets where AI adoption among design professionals is highest. A standard E&O policy carries $2 million in aggregate coverage, shared across all active projects. Depending on the firm size, that aggregate might cover eight to fifteen concurrent projects.

If the architect used an AI tool to generate or assist with MEP specifications, structural calculations, or code compliance checks, and an error in those AI-assisted outputs leads to a defect, the claim hits the E&O policy. With the new Verisk endorsement attached, the carrier denies the claim. A homeowner can still sue the architect directly, but the insurance backstop that was supposed to make the architect's promise meaningful is gone.

Repairing a structural defect in residential construction costs $15,000 to $85,000 depending on severity and location, per NAHB warranty claims data, and foundation failures run significantly higher, because an incorrectly specified structural member that requires partial demolition and reconstruction of framing above it can exceed $120,000 in a two-story home with finished interiors. These are the numbers the E&O policy was designed to cover, and these are the numbers that now fall into the gap.

Why the Exclusions Might Be Right

The insurance industry's position is defensible, and it deserves to be stated without qualification: insurers cannot price risk without data, and generative AI in professional design workflows has no actuarial history, no claims database, no loss development triangles, and no published reliability benchmarks against which to measure expected losses. Underwriting a risk you cannot model is not insurance. It is speculation, and speculation is not what the premiums collected from every other policyholder in the risk pool are for.

And the exclusions may serve a productive function. Cyber insurance followed an identical trajectory: blanket exclusions in the early 2010s gave way to risk-based underwriting once the industry developed minimum security requirements, third-party auditing frameworks, and actuarial models built on a decade of claims data. Pressure from the exclusions could force the construction industry to develop validation protocols, documentation standards, and third-party verification processes that would make AI-assisted design genuinely safer, not just faster. Coverage would return once insurers had something to underwrite against. In this reading, the gap is temporary and corrective.

Whether that correction arrives before the first wave of AI-related construction defect claims is the open question the insurance industry has not answered and the homeowners caught in the gap have not been given the information to ask.

What This Means for You

If you are building a custom home or hiring an architect for a major renovation in 2026, three questions now belong in your initial conversation with the design team, and you should ask them in writing.

First: does your firm use generative AI tools in any part of the design, specification, or code-compliance process? It is not an adversarial question, and it mirrors the same inquiry you would ask about subcontractors, material suppliers, or any other party whose work product becomes part of your home's permanent record.

Second: does your current professional liability policy contain an AI exclusion endorsement? Ask for a certificate of insurance and read the endorsement schedule. If you see Verisk CG 40 47 or CG 40 48, or carrier-specific language referencing "artificial intelligence," "machine learning," or "generative" output, the policy may not cover AI-related errors, and you need to understand what is and isn't protected before construction begins.

Third: will you add a contract clause specifying which design elements were AI-assisted and which were produced through traditional methods? No standard form requires this, but a custom clause can be added to any AIA or ConsensusDocs agreement as a supplementary condition. It costs nothing to add and it creates a record that could matter enormously if something goes wrong.

For builders and general contractors: the Verisk exclusion does not apply to your commercial general liability policy, but it does apply to the design professional you are relying on for specifications. If the architect's E&O no longer covers AI-generated errors, and you build from those specifications without independent verification, the liability chain shortens. You become the deepest pocket in the room.

Limitations

I could not independently verify which state insurance departments have approved the Verisk CG 40 47 and CG 40 48 forms, and approval status varies by jurisdiction. No public data exists on what percentage of architecture and engineering firms have already received AI exclusion endorsements on policy renewals as of mid-2026. Residential-specific AI adoption rates are estimates extrapolated from surveys that primarily cover commercial construction, and the actual penetration of AI tools in residential design workflows may be lower than industry-wide figures suggest. Verisk's forms are standardized templates, and individual carrier implementations may include narrower or broader exclusionary language than the base forms.
