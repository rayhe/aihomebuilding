# Research: Big Tech Construction Training vs. Housing Gap
## Journalist: Marcus "Steel" Washington (Workforce & Labor)

### Angle
Meta and Google have pledged $365M+ to train construction workers — but for data centers, not homes. While Big Tech solves its own labor shortage, the 1.5M housing deficit grows. The workers trained with these dollars will build server racks, not bedrooms.

### Kill Test
**Does this help someone building or buying a home?** YES — if you're waiting for a home to be built, these programs are actively pulling your electrician toward a data center job that pays more and lasts longer. This article explains why your builder's timeline just got worse.

### Primary Sources

**1. Meta — America's Workforce Academy (AWA)**
- $115M investment in first year (Reuters, WSJ, June 2026)
- Free 4-5 week training program
- Guaranteed jobs for graduates — at Meta data center construction sites
- Pilot: Louisiana, Ohio, Texas, Indiana
- Partners: ABC, National Urban League, CBRE, US Hispanic Chamber of Commerce
- Separate $250M program for data center construction training (Outlook Business)
- Meta has pledged $600B total for US infrastructure over 3 years
- "America needs hundreds of thousands of skilled tradespeople" — Rachel Peterson, Meta VP Data Centers
- Meta Texas data center: 1,800 workers at peak construction → ~100 permanent jobs
- Meta Oklahoma: 1,000 peak construction → ~100 operational
- Context: Meta laid off 8,000 white-collar workers in 2026 restructuring

**2. Google Training Investment**
- $50M for skilled trades training (announced June 12, 2026)
- Target: 300,000 workers across 20 states
- Partners: 14 labor unions, 4 trade associations
- Focus: HVAC, electricians, construction workers for AI infrastructure
- Source: Business Insider, Construction Dive, LinkedIn

**3. ABC — Construction Labor Shortage Data**
- 349,000 net new workers needed in 2026 (ABC proprietary model, Jan 2026)
- 456,000 needed in 2027 when spending growth resumes
- Every $1B in construction spending ≈ 3,450 jobs
- $86B estimated data center spend in 2026 → 296,700 jobs = 85% of total need
- 82% of firms report difficulty filling hourly craft positions (AGC survey)
- 13% of ABC members under contract for data center projects
- 1 in 5 electricians older than 55
- Source: abc.org, Bisnow, Construction Dive

**4. Home Builder Institute (HBI) — Housing Impact Data**
- Skilled labor shortage costs homebuilders $10.8B/year
- Prevents construction of ~19,000 homes annually
- Residential construction workers: ~$39.40/hr
- Broader construction average: ~$39.70/hr
- Workers shift from residential to commercial when housing slows — data center jobs are longer, more stable, better paying
- "If they don't have the work, they're going to leave the residential industry" — Edward Brady, HBI CEO
- Source: Homes.com, HBI Fall 2025 Construction Labor Market Report

**5. NAHB — Housing Deficit**
- US housing deficit: ~1.5 million homes (builder estimates)
- Industry needs 723,000 workers/year to replace departures + meet demand
- 64% of single-family builders report lot shortage
- Residential construction lost 26,100 jobs over last 12 months
- Women at 20-year high of 11.2% of construction workforce
- Immigrants: 25.5% of construction workforce (historic high), 1 in 3 skilled trades
- Source: NAHB, HBI Fall 2025 Report

**6. Immigration Enforcement Impact**
- 33% of firms affected by immigration enforcement in past 6 months (AGC survey)
- 6% report ICE officers at jobsite or office
- Fear keeping documented AND undocumented workers off job sites
- "Whether you're undocumented or documented, people are afraid" — Edward Brady, HBI
- Source: Bisnow, AGC, Homes.com

**7. Wage Data — Data Center vs. Residential**
- Data center electricians: $71K-$121K total pay ($92K median) — Glassdoor
- Rosendin (data center electrical contractor): $34-$49/hr total pay
- Construction electricians: $60,802 avg annual ($29.23/hr) — ZipRecruiter
- Residential electricians with certification: $20.82/hr avg — PayScale
- BLS national median: $62,350 for all electricians; government sector: $77,080

- Source: BLS, Glassdoor, PayScale, ZipRecruiter

**8. Data Center Job Lifecycle**
- Construction jobs outnumber permanent positions 10-to-1 or worse
- Typical: 50 full-time workers once operational (Wisconsin Watch)
- Meta Texas: 1,800 construction → 100 operational
- Meta Oklahoma: 1,000 construction → 100 operational

- Foxconn had promised 13,000 jobs on same site where Microsoft now promises 500
- Tax breaks amount to $2M+ per permanent job (Business Insider analysis)
- Source: Reuters, Wisconsin Watch/Daily Reporter, Brookings, Latitude Media

### Novel Contribution
Calculate the "labor extraction cost" — for every data center electrician Big Tech trains, how many residential projects stall? 

Key math:
- $86B data center spend → 296,700 jobs → 85% of total 349,000-worker need
- HBI: labor shortage prevents 19,000 homes/year → $10.8B lost production
- That's ~$568K in lost housing value per missing worker
- Meta's $115M trains "thousands" (assume 5,000) → $23K/worker trained
- But each worker pulled from residential means ~$568K in housing not built
- The subsidy per trained worker is 4% of the economic damage caused by their absence from homebuilding

### Strongest Counterargument
These programs are NET positive for the construction workforce — they're training people who aren't currently in construction at all (new entrants, career changers). The total pool gets bigger, which eventually helps everyone including homebuilders. Mike Rowe's argument: "It all comes down to workforce." More trained workers = more workers for everyone.

Counter to the counterargument: The jobs GUARANTEE placement at data center sites. Even if trainees weren't in residential construction before, they won't be afterward either. The funnel's exit empties into data centers, not subdivisions. The net effect on residential construction labor supply is zero or negative (wage pressure upward).

### Limitations
- Exact number of AWA graduates not disclosed; "thousands" is Meta's only figure
- Residential vs. data center wage gap varies enormously by region
- Some trained workers may eventually transition to residential — no data on mobility
- The 19,000 lost homes figure is an estimate based on delay costs, not a direct count
