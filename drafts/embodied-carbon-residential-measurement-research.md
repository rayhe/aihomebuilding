# Research: Embodied Carbon in Residential Construction Is Finally Measurable

## Journalist: Priya Greenwood (Sustainability & Green Building)
## Article #362

## Angle
Your builder counts operational carbon (heating, cooling, appliances). Nobody counts the carbon baked into the materials before you move in. A 100-home Massachusetts study just proved that embodied carbon accounts for up to 32% of a home's lifetime emissions — and the "greenest" homes aren't any better at reducing it. New tools and standards are about to change that.

## Key Sources

### 1. Massachusetts 100-Home Embodied Carbon Study (Dec 2025)
- Source: NEHERS Alliance, Stephens & Company, Ekotrope, Builders for Climate Action, NMR Group
- Funded by: MassCEC, National Grid, Eversource
- First large-scale study measuring BOTH operational and embodied carbon in newly constructed homes
- 100 newly constructed single-family homes across Massachusetts
- **Key findings:**
  - Average: 55.5 tons CO₂e per home in embodied carbon (cradle-to-gate, stages A1-A3)
  - Total for 100 homes: 5,555 net tons CO₂e (equivalent to driving 14 million miles)
  - Embodied carbon = up to 32% of a home's overall 25-year carbon emissions
  - Operational emissions: average 5.5 tons CO₂e per year per home
  - Three categories = 68% of all embodied carbon: concrete (39%), MEP systems (18%), insulation (11%)
  - XPS foam: ~9,948 kg CO₂e per 1,000 sqft; closed-cell spray foam: ~4,306 kg CO₂e per 1,000 sqft
  - Cellulose and wood fiber board insulation are net-NEGATIVE (store carbon)
  - Highest-ECE home: 144.3 tons CO₂e (8,400 sqft, walkout basement, spray foam, dual-fuel)
  - Lowest-ECE home: 18.5 tons CO₂e (under 700 sqft, minimal concrete, single heat pump)
  - All-electric homes: 60% lower total 25-year carbon vs fossil-fuel homes, 52% lower than mixed-fuel
  - **BOMBSHELL:** Embodied carbon intensity DID NOT VARY by HERS Index score or Carbon Index. Above-code homes are NO BETTER at limiting embodied carbon than conventional homes.
  - The most common concrete mix was 46% higher GWP than the lowest available alternative
  - HERS raters: 60-70% of data already in their files; after 5-10 projects, assessment time dropped from 3.5 to 2.7 hours
  - Massachusetts: 88% of new homes receive HERS rating (20% higher than any other state)
  - Extrapolation: 11,390 new homes in MA in 2024 × 55.5 tons = 632,145 tons CO₂e in embodied carbon

### 2. RESNET/ICC Standard 1550 — Embodied Carbon
- First consensus standard for measuring/reporting embodied carbon in residential
- Committee chaired by Chris Magwood (Rocky Mountain Institute)
- Vice-chair: Brian Shanks (Beazer Homes)
- Members include NAHB, ICC, California Energy Commission, Taylor Morrison
- PDS-02 draft completed, public comment closed Oct 2025
- Uses existing HERS rater workflow — no additional site visits required
- Standardized reporting template

### 3. BEAM Estimator (Builders for Climate Action)
- Turn-key tool for modeling/comparing materials by embodied carbon
- Interface between Environmental Product Declarations (EPDs) and practitioners
- Integrates with Ekotrope energy modeling software
- Shows: baseline home vs net-zero with same products vs net-zero with optimized products
- Developed by Jacob Deva Racusin (lead researcher, Builders for Climate Action)

### 4. University of Bath AI Text-to-Carbon Tool (2026)
- Published: Architectural Engineering and Design Management journal, March 2026
- Authors: Daniel Favour O. Oshidero et al., Prof. David Coley
- DOI: 10.1080/17452007.2026.2613773
- Predicts embodied carbon from natural language text descriptions of buildings
- NLP + ML, trained on 150,000 synthetic buildings
- Tested by 43 building professionals
- 80% accuracy identifying key materials from text descriptions
- Provides instant feedback at earliest design stage, where carbon saving potential is greatest

### 5. Regulatory Landscape
- California AB 2446: addressing embodied carbon of building materials
- Colorado SB23-016: embodied carbon building code
- Growing trend toward whole-life carbon policies

## Original Contribution
Cross-reference: the Massachusetts study shows above-code homes have the SAME embodied carbon as conventional homes. The insulation data reveals why: the materials used to achieve high HERS scores (XPS foam, spray foam) have among the highest embodied carbon of any building material. Cellulose insulation — which achieves similar R-values — is net-negative. Building "green" with foam insulation can INCREASE total carbon.

Calculation: A 2,400 sqft home insulated with XPS (walls + foundation) uses approximately 4,800 sqft of XPS = ~47,750 kg CO₂e just from insulation. The same home insulated with dense-pack cellulose: net-negative contribution. The delta: roughly 50 tons of CO₂e — nearly the entire average embodied carbon of a home.

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? YES.
- If you're building: switch from XPS/spray foam to cellulose insulation. Use lower-GWP concrete mixes. These are cost-neutral or cost-saving choices that cut embodied carbon by 30-50%.
- If you're buying: ask your builder for an embodied carbon assessment. RESNET 1550 + BEAM make this possible for the first time.
- For all: an all-electric, right-sized home with cellulose insulation and low-carbon concrete has dramatically lower lifetime emissions than a "green" home built with high-carbon materials.

## Limitations
- Massachusetts study: 100 homes, not randomly selected, may not represent national diversity
- Cradle-to-gate only (A1-A3) — doesn't include construction, maintenance, end-of-life
- Biogenic carbon (carbon stored in wood products) excluded due to methodological uncertainty
- MEP embodied carbon data is still limited
- University of Bath tool trained on synthetic data, not real-world projects yet
- RESNET 1550 not yet finalized/adopted

## Strongest Counterargument
Operational carbon still dominates over a home's lifetime. A home with high-carbon insulation but superior R-values will use less energy for decades. The grid is decarbonizing, making operational carbon decline over time while embodied carbon is a fixed upfront cost. Some argue that optimizing for embodied carbon at the expense of operational performance would be counterproductive — the Massachusetts study itself shows all-electric homes produce 60% lower total emissions regardless of embodied carbon.
