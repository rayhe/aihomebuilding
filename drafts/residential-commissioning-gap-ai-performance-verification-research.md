# Research: The Residential Commissioning Gap — AI Performance Verification

## Topic
The massive gap between commercial building commissioning (systematic performance verification of all systems) and residential construction (nothing). Commercial buildings get hundreds of tests before occupancy. New homes get a city code inspection and a cosmetic walkthrough. AI monitoring-based commissioning tools exist for commercial buildings but are completely absent from residential construction.

## Journalist
Frank "The Foreman" DeLuca — Project Management & Operations

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? YES — directly. A homebuyer paying $500K for a new home has zero assurance that the HVAC, plumbing, electrical, and envelope actually perform as designed. This article tells them what to demand and what it would cost.

## Key Data (3+ Primary Sources)

### Source 1: NIST Research Report (Domanski, Henderson, Payne)
- "Improper installation could increase household energy use for space heating and cooling on the order of 30 percent over what it should be"
- First study to quantify efficiency losses from common installation errors
- Common faults: leaky ducts, improper refrigerant charge, system oversizing, restricted airflow
- "The majority of air-conditioning equipment evaluated in the field performed below rated energy-efficiency levels due to one or more installation faults"
- HVAC accounts for 30% of residential electrical energy use (cooling + heating)

### Source 2: Nature Communications (2025) — 1,023 Heat Pumps Study
- Studied 1,023 heat pumps installed in residential buildings
- 17% of air-source heat pumps fall short of European efficiency standards
- ~10% of systems are oversized; ~1% undersized
- 2-3 fold difference between lowest and highest efficiency systems
- "Significant variability in performance among individual HPs"
- Highlights "critical need for standardized post-installation performance evaluation procedures"

### Source 3: LBNL (Lawrence Berkeley National Lab) — LBNL-44535
- Reviewed 469 documents on residential commissioning
- Only 33 of 469 papers (7%) addressed a house as a system of interacting components
- Most treat HVAC, envelope, and other systems in isolation
- Identified gaps: diagnostics for steady-state capacity, refrigerant charge, ventilation effectiveness
- Areas covered: Building Envelope, Cooling Equipment, Air Distribution, IAQ, Combustion Appliances, Controls

### Source 4: University of Salford Energy House
- Full-scale house built inside a climate-controlled chamber for testing
- "Research on new build homes has found this 'performance gap' can make these buildings less than half as efficient as they were supposed to be"
- What's designed ≠ what's built: "Wall insulation could be missing, windows poorly fitted, actual materials different from design"

### Source 5: SES Consulting (2026 presentation)
- "Performance commissioning is often the missing step in both new construction and low-carbon retrofit projects"
- "A leading reason buildings fail to meet energy targets, systems do not function as designed, and low-carbon technologies experience premature failure"

### Source 6: ServiceTitan 2026 Reports
- 38% of commercial contractors reporting measurable AI impact (up from 17% a year earlier)
- 74% of residential contractors see AI as key to efficiency
- Only 25% of residential contractors actually using AI

### Source 7: Constructor.net.au
- "There's no required post-build testing to prove your home performs as rated"
- Tests like blower door testing and thermal imaging are available but not standard
- Commissioning: "If skipped or done poorly, even top-quality systems won't work efficiently"

## Commercial vs. Residential Commissioning Comparison

### Commercial (standard practice):
- ASHRAE Guideline 0 (The Commissioning Process)
- ASHRAE Standard 202 (Commissioning Process for Buildings and Systems)
- Typical commissioning: $1-3 per sq ft ($150K-$450K for 150K sq ft office)
- ROI: $4.50 saved for every $1 spent (Mills 2011/2018 meta-analysis)
- Average deficiencies found: 28-50 per project (PECI/Oregon study)
- Required by LEED, many building codes for buildings >50,000 sq ft
- AI monitoring-based commissioning (MBCx) becoming standard

### Residential (current practice):
- No ASHRAE standard equivalent
- No required performance testing
- City inspection: code compliance only (safety, not performance)
- Builder walkthrough: cosmetic punch list only
- Blower door test: only required in some jurisdictions under IECC 2012+
- Duct leakage test: only required in some energy codes
- No system performance verification at all
- Zero post-occupancy monitoring

## Novel Contribution
Calculate what residential commissioning would cost and save:

A basic residential commissioning protocol for a new 2,500 sq ft home:
- Blower door test: $300-500
- Duct leakage test: $200-400
- HVAC performance verification (refrigerant charge, airflow, temp split): $300-500
- Thermal imaging scan: $200-400
- Ventilation rate verification: $150-300
- Total: $1,150-$2,100

vs. the cost of NOT commissioning:
- 30% HVAC energy penalty (NIST): $600-900/year on a $2,000-3,000 annual HVAC bill
- Over 10 years: $6,000-$9,000 wasted
- ROI of commissioning: $1,500 spent saves $6,000-9,000 = 4-6x return
- This mirrors the commercial commissioning ROI of $4.50 per $1 (Mills)

## Strongest Counterargument
Cost and complexity. A 2,500 sq ft home isn't a 150,000 sq ft office tower. The systems are simpler. The commissioning agent costs $1,500+ and the home only costs $400K-600K — the fee seems disproportionate. And builders would argue that their own QC process and the city inspection are sufficient.

Response: The NIST data says otherwise. A 30% energy penalty isn't a rounding error. And the 17% heat pump failure rate from the Nature Communications study isn't a fringe case — that's nearly 1 in 5 systems installed wrong. Simpler systems don't mean simpler errors; they just mean fewer chances to catch them.

## Limitations
- NIST data is from 2014 — installation quality may have improved (or worsened) since
- Nature Communications study is European (may not directly apply to US market)
- Commercial commissioning ROI data (Mills) covers all building types, not residential specifically
- No randomized controlled trial of residential commissioning exists
- Cost estimates for residential commissioning are author-calculated, not from a published study
