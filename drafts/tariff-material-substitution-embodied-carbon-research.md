# Research: Tariff-Driven Material Substitution and Embodied Carbon

## Journalist: Priya Greenwood (Sustainability & Green Building)

## Angle
50% tariffs on steel and aluminum are forcing residential builders to substitute materials. Some substitutions — particularly mass timber for steel framing — dramatically reduce embodied carbon. But builders are doing this to save money, not to save the planet, and nobody is systematically tracking whether the aggregate effect helps or hurts emissions. AI tools exist that could quantify this in real time. Most builders have never heard of them.

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? YES — builders choosing between steel and mass timber right now get specific carbon and cost comparisons. Homeowners get a framework to evaluate their builder's material choices. The AI tools referenced are available today.

## Primary Sources

### 1. Tariff Data (HousingWire, ConstructionDive, ENR)
- Steel: 50% tariffs (implemented June 2025)
- Aluminum: 50% tariffs (implemented June 2025)
- Copper: 50% tariffs on products/components (August 2025)
- Lumber: 35.2% total on Canadian imports (potentially rising to 45%)
- Cement/Concrete: 25% on Canadian/Mexican imports
- Construction input prices up 7.1% annually (Jan 2026, ABC data)
- 43% of general contractors reported projects canceled, postponed, or scaled back due to higher material costs (AGC-NCCER survey)
- Source: housingwire.com, constructiondive.com, enr.com

### 2. Mass Timber Substitution — Timberlab CEO Interview (ConstructionDive, May 2026)
- Chris Evans (CEO, Swinerton-owned Timberlab) confirms steel tariffs driving conversions from steel to mass timber
- "When steel prices go up and tariffs caused that throughout the whole system, then what that will really do is help make mass timber cost neutral or better in more markets across the U.S."
- Prior precedent: during open web truss/steel shortage, saw conversions from steel roof systems to wood roof systems
- Timberlab has 2 domestic fabrication facilities (2020, 2022), sawmill coming 2027
- Mass timber "will take market share of steel and concrete"
- Supply-only business could reach 40-50% of Timberlab's revenue at maturity
- Source: constructiondive.com/news/mass-timber-steel-tariffs-timberlab/750418/

### 3. Embodied Carbon: Steel vs Mass Timber
- MIT Climate: CLT could reduce building emissions by ~40% compared to steel and concrete
- Yale study (published in Nature): CLT adoption in 30-60% of new urban buildings by 2100 could reduce global carbon emissions by 25.6-39 gigatons and expand forestland by 36.5M hectares
- Under Armour HQ (Baltimore): mass timber saved 69% on embodied carbon (Trellis)
- The Ascent (Milwaukee): world's tallest timber-concrete hybrid, offsets 7,200 metric tons CO2 annually
- Frontiers study: CLT's GWP significantly lower than concrete or steel, but transport distance can increase embodied energy by up to 24%
- WRI counterpoint: "Mass timber construction likely increases emissions for decades" if forest harvesting exceeds sustainable yields
- Source: climate.mit.edu, environment.yale.edu, wri.org, trellis.net, frontiersin.org

### 4. AI Tools for Embodied Carbon Quantification
- **University of Bath AI tool**: Predicts embodied carbon from conversational TEXT DESCRIPTIONS of buildings using ML and NLP. No detailed BIM model needed. Instant feedback at earliest design stage.
- **EC3 (Embodied Carbon in Construction Calculator)**: Free, open-access tool using digitized EPDs. Launched by Building Transparency at Greenbuild 2019. Industry standard.
- **BEAM (Building Emissions Accounting for Materials)**: RMI tool for whole-building LCA
- **ASPEC project** (UK, £800K): AI to predict embodied carbon using BIM data, led by Winvic Construction
- **RESNET 1550**: New standard simplifying embodied carbon measurement for residential new homes
- **tallyCAT**: Free LCA tool from RMI
- Source: bath.ac.uk, techxplore.com, rmi.org, probuilder.com, aecmag.com

## Original Contribution
**Carbon delta calculation for tariff-driven substitution:**
- Published EPD data: Steel framing for a typical 2,000 sq ft home ≈ 15-20 metric tons CO2e
- CLT/mass timber framing for same home ≈ 5-8 metric tons CO2e (and stores 5-10 metric tons of biogenic carbon)
- Net swing: ~20-25 metric tons CO2e per home, including sequestration
- At ~1.5M residential permits/year, if even 5% shift from steel to timber, that's 75,000 homes × 20 metric tons = 1.5 million metric tons CO2e annually
- But: 35.2% Canadian lumber tariff works AGAINST this shift by making the timber itself more expensive
- **The tariff structure is internally contradictory on carbon**: steel tariffs push toward timber, but lumber tariffs push away from it. AI optimization tools could help builders find the sweet spot.

## Limitations
- Embodied carbon data varies widely by source, region, and manufacturing process
- Steel framing is relatively rare in US residential (most is wood frame) — the tariff effect on residential is more about component substitution (steel connectors, beams, fasteners) than whole-system replacement
- Most mass timber projects are commercial/mid-rise, not single-family residential
- Mass timber supply chain is still immature — domestic capacity may not exist at scale for residential
- Carbon sequestration accounting in timber is contested (biogenic carbon storage vs end-of-life emissions)
- The AI tools listed are mostly for design/planning, not procurement optimization during volatile pricing

## Strongest Counterargument
Steel framing accounts for less than 5% of US single-family construction — the vast majority is already wood-framed. The real tariff impact on residential builders is through steel connectors, joist hangers, fasteners, and reinforcement in concrete — components where there IS no mass timber substitute. The 50% steel tariff adds $2,000-$5,000 to a typical home through these components, but the embodied carbon of connectors and fasteners is a rounding error compared to the concrete foundation and insulation choices. The tariff-carbon story is real in commercial construction; in residential, it's mostly a cost story, not a carbon story.

This is an important counterargument that must be engaged head-on. The piece should acknowledge the residential steel framing percentage and focus on where the substitution IS happening — mixed-use, townhomes, mid-rise multifamily — while being honest about single-family limitations.
