Your Builder Dropped His Price $24,000 to Close the Deal. Tariffs Added $36,000 to His Materials. The Software That Could Split the Difference Hasn’t Been Built.

By Frank “The Foreman” DeLuca · July 19, 2026

Tim Zimmerman runs Mitchell Metal Products in northern Wisconsin. Last fall, he ordered a $1.2 million expansion to house a new line of robotic welders. An identical addition cost $727,000 in 2018, and the lowest bid this time came in at $2.1 million, so he abandoned the project entirely. “The realities of contemporary pricing sobered us up very quickly,” Zimmerman told the Wall Street Journal.

Zimmerman is not a homebuilder, but his story contains the arithmetic that is quietly dismantling residential construction margins across the country. The same tariff-inflated materials and labor costs that killed his expansion are landing on every framing package, every steel connector, every sheet of aluminum flashing that goes into a single-family home. The difference is that Zimmerman could walk away. A production builder sitting on 200 permitted lots cannot.

The Squeeze Has Two Jaws

In July, NAHB reported that its Housing Market Index dropped to 34, the fifteenth consecutive month below 40 and the longest sustained period of builder pessimism since 2012. Thirty-seven percent of builders cut prices in July, up from 35% in June, offering average discounts of 6%. Sixty-three percent are running sales incentives, a figure that has been above 60% for sixteen straight months, the kind of streak that stops being a promotion and starts being a structural concession.

On a $400,000 home, a 6% price cut is $24,000 in revenue the builder no longer collects.

Now look at the other side. NAHB estimates that tariff-driven lumber price increases alone have added roughly $36,000 to the cost of building a single-family home. The Trump administration doubled steel and aluminum tariffs from 25% to 50% in June, and kitchen cabinets and vanities, products that account for a measurable share of a home’s interior finish cost, are tariffed at 25% under a national security provision and scheduled to rise to 50% in January. AGC’s most recent data shows steel prices up 67%, brass and copper up 49%, aluminum up 20.5%, and plastic construction materials up more than 12%.

Revenue down $24,000 per home from price cuts. Costs up $36,000 per home from tariffs. Total margin compression on a house that was already tight: $60,000 per unit.

A builder running 50 homes a year just absorbed $3 million in margin compression, which is not a rounding error but the difference between a profitable operation and one that is burning through its line of credit while it waits for Congress to pass something useful.

The Bipartisan Bill That Won’t Help Yet

Congress did pass something, a bipartisan housing affordability bill that became law in mid-July, waiving or accelerating environmental reviews for construction projects and including provisions to discourage institutional investors from buying single-family homes. NAHB called it “a positive step,” cautiously.

But Samuel Tombs, chief economist at Pantheon Macroeconomics, put the timeline in perspective: “The potential uplift to housing starts from streamlining environmental reviews, easing rules on manufactured housing and encouraging zoning reform will take time to filter through. Real residential investment will drag modestly on GDP growth over the coming quarters.”

“Over the coming quarters” is a polite way of saying not this year, and probably not next year either, because a builder whose framing package just got $36,000 more expensive cannot wait for zoning reform to filter through anything. Margin erosion is happening on the current draw schedule, not the next legislative session.

Where the Money Actually Goes

When a builder orders framing lumber for a production home, the process looks roughly the same as it did in 1997. A purchasing manager calls two or three distributors, compares quotes that arrived by email or fax, picks the cheapest one that can deliver on time, and enters the purchase order into a spreadsheet, or maybe an ERP system if the builder is sophisticated, or maybe a legal pad if not.

Those quotes reflect whatever the distributor is paying that week for Canadian softwood, which now carries a 10% tariff under the national security track plus additional countervailing duties that have been in place since the first Trump administration. They reflect whatever the mill charged for southern yellow pine, which is not tariffed but whose price rises in lockstep because domestic producers raise their prices when import competition gets more expensive, a dynamic that is not a conspiracy but simply pricing power. What the quotes do not reflect is what the same framing package would cost if the builder specified engineered lumber, or if the order were timed to hit the distributor’s inventory cycle differently, or if a substitute species from a non-tariffed country could meet the grade stamp requirements for the structural application.

Nobody is optimizing any of this, and the builder takes the quote, adds it to the budget, and if the budget is now $36,000 over where it was last year, the builder either raises the price (losing buyers in a market where pending sales just fell 5.4%), cuts the price (losing margin), or value-engineers the home down to a spec that the buyer did not choose and will not thank anyone for.

Commercial Construction Has the Tool. Residential Doesn’t.

Skanska USA, the Swedish-based developer and builder, hosted a webinar in June 2026 about tariff impacts on commercial construction. In a case study the company shared, the new steel and aluminum tariffs could add approximately $22 million to a $375 million healthcare development. That is a 5.9% cost increase, and nearly half of it came not from raw steel but from derivative components, products that embed steel or aluminum in their assemblies in ways that are not immediately obvious from a bill of materials.

Skanska’s national director of strategic supply chain, Rob Cantando, described the company’s process for assessing these impacts: systematic material-by-material analysis, supply chain modeling, identification of substitution opportunities, and real-time coordination with suppliers. The word “AI” was not used in the webinar. But the analytical infrastructure that makes this process possible at Skanska’s scale—the procurement databases, the material performance models, the supply chain visibility tools—is effectively an intelligence platform that a team of procurement professionals operates continuously across a portfolio of billion-dollar projects.

A residential builder doing 50 homes a year has no version of this at all, not a smaller version, not a simpler version, just nothing.

Autodesk Research published a prototype AI-assisted material selection workflow in 2025, but it exists as an academic paper and a graph-based interface for wall assembly design and has never been commercialized.

Academic literature is full of AI material optimization tools: BIM-integrated life cycle sustainability assessment tools that compare steel, concrete, and wood structural systems; neural network models that predict compressive strength of alternative concrete mixes; decision support systems that rank insulation materials by cost, thermal performance, and embodied carbon simultaneously. Every one of them lives in a journal article, a conference proceeding, or a university lab, and none are commercially available to a builder who needs to know, this morning, whether switching from imported Canadian SPF to domestic SYP on a 47-unit subdivision saves enough to offset the cabinet tariff increase that just hit.

The Math the Industry Is Not Doing

Consider a builder running 50 single-family homes per year at an average sale price of $400,000. In the current environment, that builder faces two forces that accountants can measure precisely and that nobody is measuring together.

On the revenue side, at 6% price cuts across 37% of sales, the weighted average revenue loss is approximately 2.2% across the full book, or $440,000 per year, and if the builder is in a market where 100% of sales require concessions, the loss climbs to $1.2 million.

On the cost side, at $36,000 per home in lumber tariff costs alone, plus material increases across steel, aluminum, copper, and plastics, a conservative estimate of total tariff-driven cost increase is $40,000 to $50,000 per home, which across 50 homes adds $2 million to $2.5 million in additional cost that did not exist 18 months ago.

Total margin compression: $2.4 million to $3.7 million per year on a 50-home operation. For context, the typical production builder operates on gross margins of 18% to 22%, which on $20 million in revenue means $3.6 million to $4.4 million in gross profit, and the tariff-and-price-cut squeeze is consuming between 55% and 100% of it.

Now consider what an AI procurement optimization tool could do if one existed: material substitution analysis across tariff categories, timing optimization to exploit distributor inventory cycles, alternate sourcing from non-tariffed origins where grade stamp equivalency allows it, dynamic specification adjustment within code-compliant bounds such as switching from dimensional lumber to engineered wood products whose manufacturing inputs carry different tariff exposure, and real-time tracking of tariff schedule changes and their downstream impact on component pricing through the derivative supply chain that Skanska’s team traces manually on $375 million projects.

A conservative estimate of achievable savings from systematic procurement optimization is 8% to 15% of material costs, which on a 50-home operation spending $10 million on materials means $800,000 to $1.5 million, not enough to close the entire gap but enough to determine whether the builder survives the downturn or joins the 1,200 homebuilding firms that closed in the last cycle.

Why Nobody Built It

Construction technology venture capital has a clear investment thesis, and procurement optimization is not in it.

Higharc’s $95 million Series C, announced in late June, funds AI for the design-to-construction lifecycle: floor plans, 3D spatial data, estimating, sales configuration, and construction documentation. Navigate.AI is building an AI copilot for job site supervision. Spacial automates permitting. Buildots and Doxel use computer vision for progress tracking, and Procore, the industry’s dominant project management platform, has integrated AI features for document search and risk flagging.

Every one of these tools addresses the design phase, the construction phase, or the compliance phase, and not one addresses the procurement phase, which is the phase where actual money leaves the builder’s account. Venture capital’s thesis in construction AI is that design and supervision are where the technology risk is lowest and the user experience payoff is highest, because a beautiful AI-generated floor plan demonstrates immediately while a procurement optimization that saves $16,000 on a framing package requires the builder to trust a recommendation about material substitution that contradicts twenty years of ordering habits, and the savings show up not as a visible feature but as a slightly smaller draw request that the builder’s accountant notices three months later.

A deeper structural reason lurks beneath the venture preference. Procurement optimization requires real-time pricing data from distributors, which distributors do not share because their margins depend on information asymmetry; it requires tariff schedule data mapped to HTS codes at the component level, a classification exercise that customs brokers charge for by the hour; and it requires material performance equivalency data tied to building code acceptance criteria, which varies by jurisdiction and is not available in any single database. Building an AI procurement tool for residential construction means assembling a data layer that does not currently exist, from sources that have no incentive to create it, for a customer base that has never paid for procurement intelligence and does not know to ask for it.

That is a harder business than generating floor plans from text prompts. But the floor plan tool saves the builder time, while the procurement tool saves the builder money. In a market where margins are collapsing, the distinction matters in ways it did not two years ago.

What the Downturn Actually Demands

Single-family housing starts fell for the third consecutive month in June, slipping 0.2% to a seasonally adjusted annual rate of 895,000 units, which represents a 3.2% year-over-year decline. Permits for future single-family construction fell 2.4% to 871,000, the lowest level since August 2025, and pending home sales tumbled 5.4% in June, five times worse than the 0.5% decline economists expected. NAR’s chief economist, Lawrence Yun, described the market in terms that leave no ambiguity: “The highest mortgage rates in nearly a year and the record-high national median home price together are contributing to a tepid housing market that is especially difficult for first-time homebuyers.”

Fannie Mae cut its 2026 home sales forecast to 4.76 million units, down from its June projection of 4.81 million, and expects mortgage rates to average 6.3% through 2027. In Southwest Florida, single-family building permits are down 21.4% through May compared to the same period last year, according to Florida Gulf Coast University’s Regional Economic Indicators report.

Every downturn creates survivors and casualties. Survivors are builders who found margin where others gave it away. In the 2008 cycle, the survivors were the ones who owned their lots outright and could wait. In the 2018–2019 slowdown, the survivors were the ones who had diversified into build-to-rent. In this cycle, the survivors will be the ones who can build the same house for less—not a cheaper house, but the same house at lower cost. That is a procurement problem, and right now, not a single AI company in the construction technology ecosystem is working on it for the segment of the market that needs it most.

The Counterargument

There is a reasonable case that production builders already optimize procurement through scale. A builder doing 500 homes a year negotiates volume pricing directly with mills and manufacturers, bypasses distributors on high-volume SKUs, and employs a purchasing department whose full-time job is cost reduction. For those builders, the marginal value of an AI procurement tool may be small relative to its implementation cost, because the human procurement team already captures most of the available savings through relationships and volume leverage that an algorithm cannot replicate.

That argument has force for the top 20 builders, who collectively produce roughly 40% of new homes. It has no force for the approximately 44,000 smaller builders who produce the other 60%, many of whom are running fewer than 25 homes a year with no dedicated purchasing staff, no direct mill relationships, and no systematic process for evaluating material substitutions when tariff costs change mid-project. These are the builders whose margins are most exposed to the current squeeze, and they are the ones for whom an AI procurement tool would be most transformative. They are also the ones least likely to be served by construction technology companies that design their products for enterprise customers.

What It Would Take

Tariff data exists, since the U.S. International Trade Commission publishes the Harmonized Tariff Schedule with updates after every presidential proclamation. Material equivalency data exists in fragments: the American Wood Council publishes span tables by species and grade, ICC-ES reports evaluate engineered products against code acceptance criteria, and ASTM standards define material performance thresholds. Distributor pricing is the hardest piece, but a procurement platform that aggregated anonymized pricing data from participating builders, the way Procore aggregates project management data, could build a pricing index over time.

A straightforward business model suggests itself: a subscription platform that ingests a builder’s bill of materials, maps each line item to its tariff exposure, identifies substitution opportunities that maintain code compliance, and tracks pricing across distributors to recommend optimal ordering timing and sourcing, priced at $500 to $1,000 per month. At 8% material savings on a 50-home operation, the tool pays for itself inside the first project.

Nobody is building it because nobody has built the underlying data layer, and nobody will build the data layer until somebody demonstrates the ROI, which cannot be demonstrated until the tool exists. It is a chicken-and-egg problem that venture capital typically solves by funding the first mover. But venture capital in construction technology is currently infatuated with design tools and AI copilots, products that demo beautifully and address a pain point that architects and superintendents already articulate. Procurement optimization is invisible, unglamorous, and requires data partnerships with distributors who would rather not share pricing information with a platform that exists to make their customers smarter buyers.

That is exactly why it matters. Visible tools help builders design better homes. The invisible one helps builders keep building homes at all.

Limitations

NAHB’s $36,000-per-home lumber cost figure is an aggregate estimate and will vary by region, home size, and lumber species mix. Average price cuts of 6% reflect national NAHB survey data; individual markets range from zero concessions to double digits. Skanska’s $22 million tariff impact on its healthcare project is a case study from one company’s internal analysis and may not generalize to all commercial construction. Estimated savings of 8% to 15% from AI procurement optimization are extrapolated from commercial procurement efficiency literature and have not been validated in a residential context, because the tool does not exist to validate against. Distributor pricing data is proprietary; the assertion that building a pricing index is possible is based on analogies to other industries where similar platforms exist, not on demonstrated feasibility in construction materials distribution.
