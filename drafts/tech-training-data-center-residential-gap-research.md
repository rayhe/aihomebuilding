# Research: Tech Companies Spent $300M Training Construction Workers — For Data Centers, Not Houses

## Angle
Meta, Google, and Anthropic have collectively committed $315M+ in a single week (June 2026) to train construction workers. Every dollar is earmarked for data center builds. Meanwhile, the residential construction sector is hemorrhaging 41,000 jobs, bleeding $10.8 billion/year from labor shortages, and failing to build 19,000 homes annually. The programs actively siphon workers from homebuilding by offering free training, airfare, lodging, and guaranteed employment at wages residential builders can't match.

## Journalist: Marcus "Steel" Washington (Workforce & Labor beat)
Connects to his earlier article #347 about the electrician who left for data center work. This is the institutional sequel — poaching is now official corporate strategy with nine-figure budgets.

## Primary Sources

### Meta "America's Workforce Academy" (announced June 8, 2026)
- $115M invested in first year (separate from $250M broader program announced earlier)
- 5-week free training program: tuition, airfare, lodging, daily stipend included
- Guaranteed data center construction job upon completion
- Pilot locations: Indiana, Louisiana, Ohio, Texas
- No prior experience required — open to veterans, recent grads, career changers
- Partners: National Urban League, Associated Builders & Contractors (ABC), CBRE, U.S. Hispanic Chamber of Commerce, STRIVE
- Meta Louisiana "Hyperion" data center: "so large it would cover a significant part of Manhattan"
- Meta Texas data center: 1,800 workers at peak construction → ~100 permanent operational jobs
- Context: Meta already laid off 8,000 white-collar employees partly to fund infrastructure buildout; pledged $600B US infrastructure spending over next 3 years
- Source: Reuters (June 9, 2026), WSJ, Daily Caller, People

### Google Skilled Trades Training (announced June 11, 2026)
- $50M via Google.org "AI Opportunity Fund"
- Goal: train 300,000+ workers across 20+ states
- Targets: electricians, welders, pipefitters, HVAC, fiber technicians, manufacturing
- Working with 14 labor unions and 4 trade/contractor associations
- Includes electrical training ALLIANCE (etA) — joint program with NECA and IBEW
- CEO Sundar Pichai quote: "America's digital economy relies on our physical infrastructure and...who build and maintain it."
- Source: Google Blog (June 11, 2026), Construction Dive, LinkedIn

### Anthropic Fellowship (same week)
- $150M national fellowship program
- Places 1,000 skilled tradespeople in nonprofits
- Source: Plant Engineering (June 2026)

### Combined: $315M+ in one week, all for data center/AI infrastructure

## Labor Shortage Data

### Residential Construction Impact (HBI/NAHB Fall 2025 Report)
- $10.8 billion/year aggregate economic impact of skilled labor shortage
- $2.663B in higher carrying costs
- $8.143B in lost home production (19,000 homes not built)
- 3.3 million payroll residential construction workers currently
- Home building lost 41,000+ jobs in 2025
- Home building non-supervisory wages rising 9.2% — still not enough
- Source: NAHB (Oct 10, 2025), University of Denver study commissioned by HBI

### National Workforce Gap
- ABC: 349,000 new workers needed in construction this year alone
- JLL Research: 2.1 million skilled trades jobs unfilled by 2030
- NAHB 2026 Legislative Conference: need 2.2M additional workers over next 3 years
- 25.5% of construction workforce is immigrant (historic high)
- NAHB: 1.2 million housing unit deficit nationwide
- 300,000 open construction jobs currently
- Source: NAHB, ABC, JLL Research, Contractor Magazine

### Demographic Shifts
- Gen Z in construction: doubled from 6.4% (2019) to 14.1% (2023)
- Women in construction: 11.2% (20-year high, up from 9.1% in 2017)
- Average construction worker age: 42.1 (CPWR 2023)

## Wage Gap Data

### Residential
- Average residential construction worker: $19.35/hr (PayScale 2026)
- Total pay: $31K-$59K range
- Early career (1-4 years): $16.28/hr average
- BLS May 2025: all construction/extraction average $65,360/yr ($31.42/hr)

### Data Center
- Reports of six-figure salaries and "man camps" with five-star cuisine and game rooms (Bloomberg/Payscale)
- Texas "man camps" built specifically to attract construction workers to data center sites
- Construction Planner/Scheduler: $86,500 national median, 17.6% wage growth
- Cost Estimating Manager: $135,000 national, $153,000 in Dallas
- HVAC Mechanic II: $69,000
- Plumber II: $76,600
- Even trades roles pay significantly more than residential average
- Source: PayScale (April 2026)

### The Pull Factor
- Meta offers: free training + guaranteed job + airfare + lodging + stipend
- Residential builders offer: $19.35/hr, show up Monday, bring your own tools

## The Structural Problem
- Data center construction creates huge temporary workforce demand (1,800 workers per site) but minimal permanent jobs (~100 per site)
- When the data center buildout ends (or slows), where do these workers go? Back to residential? Or out of construction entirely?
- Programs train for "generalist" roles but the skills pipeline is one-way — nobody runs a "come build houses" academy
- ABC called it "the AI revolution bringing change but also historic opportunities" — but the opportunities aren't for homebuilders

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? YES.
- If you're a GC, this explains why your sub quotes are 15-20% higher and your timelines are stretching
- If you're a homebuyer, this is why your new home costs more and takes longer
- If you're a young person choosing a career path, this shows where the money is (and isn't) in construction
- If you're a policy maker, this quantifies the residential construction subsidy flowing to tech companies

## Counterargument (at full strength)
The tech companies argue they're growing the total pie — training people who WEREN'T in construction at all. Meta's Academy targets "new entrants to the trades" with no prior experience. If these workers weren't going to frame houses anyway, maybe this isn't zero-sum. ABC data shows Gen Z construction participation doubled since 2019. And higher data center wages create a rising tide: residential non-supervisory wages rose 9.2%. The tech training programs may normalize blue-collar work for a generation raised to think college was the only path.

## Limitations
- No direct survey data asking workers "would you have done residential if data center wasn't an option?"
- Meta and Google programs are new (June 2026) — no completion or retention data yet
- Bloomberg "man camp" reporting is about specific Texas sites, not industry-wide
- Residential wage data is broad average; specific trade wages (electrician, plumber) can be much higher
- UK apprenticeship dropout data (47%) used as proxy — US data harder to find
