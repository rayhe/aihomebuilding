# Research: The 21st Century ROAD to Housing Act — Implementation Gap

## Article Angle
Congress passed 47 housing provisions in the biggest housing bill in a generation. But most of them are optional — incentive-based grants, voluntary zoning guidelines, and study mandates that require cities to volunteer. The one hard federal mandate (350+ home investor ban) is narrow. The rest depends on HUD implementing 35 programs with a gutted staff, and on municipalities choosing to adopt reforms that historically face fierce local opposition.

**Differentiation from chassis article (Jul 10):** The chassis article focused narrowly on one provision (Title 3 chassis rule removal). This article examines the FULL law's implementation math — what's mandatory vs. optional, what requires local adoption, and why the Goldman Sachs 2.5M unit estimate is probably optimistic.

## Key Facts

### The Law
- **Full name:** 21st Century ROAD to Housing Act (S. 1398)
- **Became law:** July 11, 2026 (Trump refused to sign, didn't veto — became law automatically under Article I, Section 7 of the Constitution after 10 days)
- **Vote margins:** House 358-32, Senate 85-5 (massive bipartisan)
- **Sponsors:** Senators Tim Scott (R-SC), Elizabeth Warren (D-MA), Reps. French Hill (R-AR), Maxine Waters (D-CA)
- **47 provisions** across multiple titles

### Provision Categories (Original Analysis)
**Hard Federal Mandates (happen regardless of local action):**
- Title 10: Ban on investors owning 350+ single-family homes (with 7-year disposal requirement for BTR operators)
- Title 3: Chassis rule removal for manufactured housing
- Title 2: Streamlined NEPA reviews for housing projects
- Title 9: Community bank regulatory caps

**Incentive/Grant-Based (require state/local opt-in):**
- Pre-approved plan grants (pattern books for residential designs)
- CDBG incentive funding for zoning reform
- Zoning framework guidelines (parking minimums, FAR, ADUs, by-right development, transit-oriented development) — but these are GUIDELINES, not mandates
- Grant funding for local code reform

**Study/Report Mandates (just research, no construction):**
- Multiple HUD-directed studies on barriers to manufactured housing
- Federal assessments of modular production scaling
- Various reporting requirements

### Goldman Sachs Estimate
- Relaxed land-use regulations could add **2.5 million housing units** over next decade
- But this assumes widespread local adoption of zoning reforms
- Historical precedent: zoning reform is among the hardest local policy changes to achieve (NIMBY opposition, property value concerns, local politics)

### Urban Institute Assessment
- HUD must implement **35 different programs/regulations** from the ROAD Act
- HUD is currently short-staffed (DOGE cuts, hiring freezes)
- Implementation capacity is a serious constraint

### NAHB Stat (already published on site)
- $131,734 in regulatory costs per new home
- ROAD Act's zoning provisions could theoretically reduce this, but only where adopted

### HousingWire Fault Lines
- Three fault lines: (1) BTR 7-year disposal requirement controversy, (2) manufactured housing practical impact, (3) zoning reform voluntary nature
- BTR operators argue 7-year forced disposal destabilizes rental communities
- Manufactured housing gains are real but market share is small (~9% of new housing)

### CNN Analysis
- 47 provisions address supply constraints
- Investor limits: narrow (only hits firms with 350+ homes — most institutional investors own fewer)
- Lock-in effect (current homeowners stuck with low-rate mortgages) NOT addressed
- Demand-side factors (interest rates, lock-in) untouched

### Congress.gov Bill Text — Zoning Provisions
- Zoning framework guidelines include: parking minimums, FAR, ADUs, by-right uses, transit-oriented development
- These are GUIDELINES published by HUD, not mandates imposed on localities
- Local jurisdictions can ignore them entirely
- CDBG incentive: jurisdictions that adopt reforms get priority for CDBG funding — but this is a carrot, not a stick

## Original Contribution
**Provision classification exercise:** Of the 47 provisions, how many are:
1. Self-executing federal mandates: ~5-7 (investor ban, chassis rule, NEPA streamlining, community bank caps, a few others)
2. Incentive-dependent (require local opt-in): ~15-20 (zoning guidelines, pre-approved plans, CDBG incentives)
3. Study/report requirements: ~10-15 (various HUD studies, barrier assessments)
4. Administrative/technical: ~5-10 (definitions, effective dates, conforming amendments)

This means roughly 60-70% of the provisions produce NO new housing unless a city council somewhere votes to act — and most city councils, historically, do not voluntarily upzone their neighborhoods.

## Strongest Counterargument
The CDBG incentive is genuinely powerful — cities that want federal community development money will have to adopt reforms. This is how the federal government has historically driven local policy change (see: drinking age, highway funding). If HUD ties enough money to zoning reform, adoption could be higher than historical precedent suggests.

## Limitations
- We cannot independently verify the "47 provisions" count without reading every section of the enrolled bill
- Goldman Sachs' 2.5M unit estimate methodology is not publicly available in detail
- Urban Institute's "35 programs" count may differ from other analyses
- Whether the investor ban actually affects enough firms to move the market is debatable — most institutional SFR operators own fewer than 350 homes per entity (subsidiary structuring may provide workarounds)
