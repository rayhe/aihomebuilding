Your Soil Was Already Mapped. Your Builder Probably Didn’t Check.

By Jake Kowalski • April 1, 2026

A crew in North Texas pours a slab-on-grade on a Tuesday morning. Six months later, diagonal cracks run from the window corners. A year after that, the kitchen floor slopes enough to roll a marble from fridge to pantry. Foundation repair quote: $38,000. Root cause: montmorillonite clay, the kind that swells to fifteen times its dry volume when wet and generates pressures exceeding 30,000 pounds per square foot. Nobody tested the soil before the pour.

According to the USDA and ASCE, half of all American homes sit on expansive soils. A quarter have already suffered damage. Annual losses exceed $7 billion, making problem soils a bigger cumulative destroyer of property than earthquakes, tornadoes, or hurricanes. Standard homeowner’s insurance doesn’t cover it.

Meanwhile, the federal government already classified the soil under virtually every buildable parcel in the country. For free. And machine learning can predict bearing capacity from limited soil data with better than 92% accuracy. So why are we still building blind?

Free Data, Sitting Unused

The USDA’s Natural Resources Conservation Service maintains SSURGO, a spatial database covering texture, drainage, organic matter, erosion risk, and shrink-swell potential for every surveyed tract in America. Type an address into the Web Soil Survey, get a map of what’s under your lot. It includes a shrink-swell rating for each soil unit. High shrink-swell means montmorillonite clay. It means your slab will move.

In Texas, Colorado, Virginia, and the Carolinas, you can drive through entire subdivisions built on high-shrink soil where nobody upgraded the foundation design. All of it was right there, on a government website, before the first permit application.

IBC Says Test. Most Builders Don’t.

IBC Section 1803 requires a geotechnical investigation for every project. But there’s a critical exception: the building official can waive it if soil data from an adjacent project exists. In practice, that exception swallows the rule. As one forensic engineering firm documented: “Many new construction projects are designed and built without a subsurface investigation and geotechnical report ever having been done.”

A standard residential geotechnical report costs $2,000 to $3,500. Two to three borings, lab tests for moisture and shear strength, foundation type recommendation. On a $400,000 house, that’s less than 1% of construction cost. Builders skip it anyway because the code lets them and the schedule demands it.

ML Models That Actually Work

A 2025 study in MDPI Geosciences tested ensemble machine learning models against 472 soil samples for predicting shallow foundation bearing capacity. Gradient Boosting Regression Trees hit an R² of 0.928, explaining 93% of variance using SPT blow count, footing width, embedment ratio, and applied pressure. A separate study using Rough Set Theory for liquefaction prediction achieved 86.2% accuracy, outperforming standard-of-practice methods (70–85%). Another model predicted clay activity from just the liquid limit at R² of 0.94.

None of these require a full geotechnical investigation. They work with limited inputs, exactly the kind of data you can extract from SSURGO classifications.

Running the Numbers Nobody Ran

The U.S. Census Bureau reports roughly 1.4 million housing starts per year. If 50% land on expansive soils, that’s 700,000 new homes per year on ground with meaningful shrink-swell risk. In jurisdictions following the IBC waiver exception, a conservative 30% skip geotechnical investigation entirely. That is 210,000 homes per year on risky soil with no professional assessment.

Apply the ASCE’s 25% lifetime damage rate: about 52,500 of those untested homes will develop foundation problems. Foundation repair runs $10,000 to $50,000+, per Foundation Systems Engineering. At the low end, that is $525 million in preventable damage per annual cohort of new homes.

Screening all 1.4 million lots against SSURGO shrink-swell data costs functionally nothing. It is a database query. An ML model could flag the 700,000 high-risk lots and recommend full geotech reports for the worst subset. Even ordering reports for only those 210,000 flagged lots, at $2,500 each, runs $525 million. Spend $525 million to prevent $525 million to $2.6 billion in damage. And that damage won’t be insured.

Why Experienced Builders Might Shrug

Any builder with twenty years in the DFW Metroplex already knows the soil is garbage. Same for Front Range Colorado and the Virginia Piedmont. Local knowledge is real, and a veteran GC doesn’t need machine learning to tell him to specify post-tension in Arlington, Texas.

But local knowledge doesn’t scale. It doesn’t transfer when a Phoenix builder takes a project in Charlotte. It doesn’t help a first-time buyer in Raleigh who doesn’t know what “shrink-swell” means and whose builder chose the cheapest foundation design that would pass inspection.

SSURGO resolution is also a legitimate concern. Soil conditions can change dramatically within a hundred feet, and the survey maps sometimes blur those transitions. A triage tool is not a replacement for boots-on-ground investigation. It is a trigger for one.

Nobody Built This Tool Yet

All the ingredients exist. SSURGO data is free and API-accessible. Bearing capacity ML models are published with R² above 0.9. Foundation repair claims data exists within insurance companies. Parcel-level permit data is public in most counties. Combining them into a per-lot risk score would flag green (standard foundation, no report needed), yellow (recommend investigation), or red (mandatory geotech, specific tests recommended based on predicted clay mineralogy).

A building department could integrate this into permit workflows. A title company could include it in due diligence. A mortgage lender could require investigation for red-flagged parcels, like they require flood insurance in FEMA zones. As of April 2026, nobody has built it.

What I Didn’t Prove

The $7 billion annual damage figure traces back to 1970s-era estimates, inflation-adjusted. No modern study has revisited it. It could be higher given Sun Belt expansion into clay-rich areas. It could be lower if foundation engineering has improved enough. Nobody has funded the study.

My 30% estimate for the geotech skip rate is exactly that: an estimate. No national survey quantifies it. Could be 20%. Could be 50%. Nobody tracked it, which is itself part of the problem.

And the ML models (GBRT at R² 0.928, liquefaction at 86.2%) have only been validated academically. A model trained on 472 samples needs validation against tens of thousands of parcels before anyone should bet a foundation on it.
