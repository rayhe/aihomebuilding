# Research: AI Listing Photos, "Housefishing," and the Trust Gap in Home Sales

## Topic
AI-generated and AI-altered real estate listing images — now called "housefishing" — are creating a measurable trust gap between what buyers see online and what they find when they walk through the door. California legislated disclosure requirements effective January 2026. New York issued a formal state warning. Production homebuilders using AI renderings to presell homes face the same gap from the other direction: what gets rendered vs. what gets built.

## Journalist
Elena Vasquez — Architecture & Design. She sees buildings as art first, is skeptical of tools that flatten design into optimization, and would have sharp opinions about how AI replaces honest architectural representation with algorithmic idealization.

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? **YES.** If you're buying new construction sold from renderings, or shopping resale listings online, you need to know what's real and what's AI. And if you're a builder or agent using AI images, you need to know the legal exposure.

## Primary Sources

### 1. California Law (effective January 2026)
- Requires licensed real estate brokers and salespersons to disclose when listing images have been digitally altered
- Must provide access to original, unaltered images via link, URL, or QR code
- Disclosure triggers: adding/removing furniture, fixtures, appliances, flooring; changing paint colors, landscaping, exteriors; altering views, neighboring structures, street features; virtual staging or AI tools creating representations that don't exist
- Permitted without disclosure: lighting corrections, color/white-balance, sharpening, cropping, resizing
- Source: Lewis Brisbois Bisgaard & Smith LLP analysis (Mondaq, Dec 19, 2025); California DRE Licensee Advisory
- Consumer Reports also supporting AB 2025 to extend to rental listings (April 2026)

### 2. New York Department of State Official Warning
- Formal "Trend Alert" to homebuyers about "significant rise in artificially generated pictures on real estate listings"
- Secretary of State Walter T. Mosley: "these automated technology tools may produce misleading or exaggerated representation of properties"
- Real Property Law §441-c prohibits dishonest/misleading advertisements; 19 NYCRR §175.25(c)(9) requires "honest and accurate depiction"
- General Business Law §§349, 350, 350-a: deceptive acts and false advertising with "substantial monetary penalties"
- Cited International Journal of Information Management study: consumers distrust AI-generated images, perceive them as "less authentic, less professional and misleading"
- Source: dos.ny.gov official press release

### 3. Realtor.com / Mansion Global Investigation
- "Housefishing" coined (play on "catfishing") — using egregiously AI-doctored listing images
- Parisa Afkhami (Coldwell Banker Warburg, NYC): client from Dubai walked out within minutes after listing showed "beautiful streamlined modern upholstery and spectacular Central Park views" but reality was "lower level with a view of a wall"
- Greg Field (HomeSmart, AZ): "Once a buyer feels misled, they often spend the entire showing looking for other hidden defects rather than focusing on the property's potential"
- Heather Amalaha (Showhomes Austin): reviewed listing where AI changed architectural features and fixtures, spent considerable time trying to distinguish real from AI. Home unsold with 12.5% ($200K) price reduction. Traditional staging would have cost <1% of list price.
- Michelle Rhyne (Premier Sotheby's): "I've pulled up to homes before where the online photos showed lush green grass and beautiful trees, only to find the yard looked nothing like the photos in person. As in mud and no trees."
- Source: realtor.com/news/trends (March 2026)

### 4. Market Data
- Virtual staging market: $1.22 billion (2025) → $1.33B (2026) → $2.96B by 2032, 13.51% CAGR (360iResearch)
- Cost comparison: $2.67/image virtual vs. $2,500-$5,000 for physical staging of a 3-bedroom (Collov AI 2026 cost analysis)
- 82% of real estate agents now use AI in some capacity (RPR 2026 survey / HousingWire)
- 81% of buyers say staging helps them visualize living in a home (NAR)
- Virtually staged properties sell 73% faster on average (V7 2026)
- Offer prices 1-5% higher than non-staged comparables
- Major franchises (RE/MAX, Keller Williams, Coldwell Banker, Century 21) incorporating AI staging into standardized listing toolkits
- Real estate agents = 44.7% of AI staging platform revenue (DataIntelo 2025)

### 5. Detroit Case Study
- Dexter-Linwood bungalow with AI-enhanced listing photos went viral on social media (millions of views)
- Renter compared AI image vs. actual appearance
- Jasmine Jackson (resident): "It's beautiful, but it's AI. It looks fake. It looks like a painting."
- Sami Abdallah (RE/MAX City Centre): brokerage created AI usage guidelines — AI can "enhance rooms and colors and furniture" but shouldn't "enhance the structure and adding things and removing things that aren't there"
- Source: WDIV Detroit (March 2026)

### 6. The Register UK Investigation
- Estate agent listing where AI edited out an entire adjacent commercial property (hair and beauty salon whose roof literally touches the bay windows)
- AI-generated bathroom moved toilet between walls, added floor-length curtain cutting through waste pipe
- Adrian Tagg (MRICS, University of Reading): "altered images are a property misdescription or misrepresentation"
- Source: The Register (Aug 2025)

### 7. FOX 13 News Test
- Used Google's Veo 3.1 to take a real home photo and generate a "more modern, high-end look"
- Result: "a noticeably different property"
- Agent Katy McBrayer-Lynch (Premier Sotheby's): "When I did that, it changed the structure of the home. It didn't just put furniture in, it moved a wall, it changed the window."
- Source: FOX 13 (May 2026)

## Novel Contribution
Nobody has calculated the cost of the trust gap for sellers who over-AI their listings. Amalaha's case study provides a data point: 12.5% price reduction ($200K loss) on a home that could have been traditionally staged for <1% of list price. Compare that to virtual staging ROI claims (73% faster sales, 1-5% price lift). The question: at what point does AI manipulation destroy more value through trust erosion than it creates through visual enhancement?

## Strongest Counterargument
AI staging democratizes presentation. A first-time seller who can't afford $5,000 in physical staging can now present a competitive listing for $200. The tool isn't the problem — agents who alter structural features are. California's law draws the right line: stage furniture, yes; move walls, no.

## Limitations
- No systematic data on buyer walkaway rates at AI-staged vs. traditionally staged open houses
- California law is 6 months old — enforcement data doesn't exist yet
- The line between "acceptable enhancement" and "deceptive alteration" is subjective (California tried to define it, but edge cases remain)
- Most data on virtual staging benefits comes from virtual staging companies (conflict of interest)
