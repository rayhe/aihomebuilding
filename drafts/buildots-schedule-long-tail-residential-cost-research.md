# Research Notes: The Long Tail of Residential Construction Schedules

## Topic
Buildots Intelligence Lab (launched June 25, 2026) published the construction industry's first AI-powered benchmarks from aggregated global project data. Their finding that the final 20% of any activity takes 27% of its duration isn't a planning failure — it's a structural pattern. Applied to residential, where NAHB pegs average build time at 6.3 months and construction loans run ~7.5%, the long-tail effect adds ~$1,066 in pure carrying cost per home. Across 935K annual single-family starts, that's roughly $1 billion/year the industry absorbs without ever naming it.

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? **YES.** Homeowners can use this data to: (1) negotiate more realistic timelines instead of accepting "6 months" that structurally becomes 6.7, (2) budget an extra 2 weeks of carrying cost, (3) understand why "almost done" always takes longer than expected, and (4) evaluate their GC's track record against industry benchmarks that now exist.

## Original Contribution

**The Long-Tail Carrying Cost Calculation:**
1. NAHB 2026: Average single-family construction = 6.3 months
2. Buildots: Final 20% of an activity takes 27% of its duration → 7 excess percentage points
3. 7% of 6.3 months ≈ 0.44 months ≈ 13 extra days of structural schedule drag
4. Typical construction draw: $400,000 at 7.5% APR = $82.19/day in interest
5. 13 days × $82.19 = ~$1,069 per home in pure carrying cost from the long tail
6. Census Jan 2026 SAAR: 935,000 single-family starts
7. 935,000 × $1,069 = ~$999 million ≈ $1 billion/year industry-wide

**The Residential Schedule Adherence Gap:**
- Buildots data covers healthcare (65%), data centers (57%), commercial (40s%), education (<39%)
- Residential is not measured. No builder publishes schedule adherence rates.
- If residential falls in the commercial range (40-50%), your 6.3-month build has a coin-flip chance of hitting schedule.
- No homeowner is told this before signing.

## Strongest Counterargument
Buildots data comes from commercial projects — data centers, hospitals, offices. Residential has shorter timelines, smaller crews, fewer MEP interdependencies. A 3-person plumbing crew doing residential rough-in doesn't face the same coordination overhead as a 50-person MEP team on a hospital wing. The 20-50% MEP productivity gap may overstate residential patterns. However, the long-tail effect is likely structural across all project types — it's about task completion psychology and coordination handoffs, not scale.

## Limitations
- Buildots publishes no residential-specific data — we're extrapolating from commercial
- The $1B industry-wide estimate assumes every home experiences the average long tail
- Schedule adherence rates for residential construction are genuinely unknown
- No independent verification of Buildots' aggregated data methodology
- The 3x productivity gap between top and average MEP teams may reflect project complexity differences
