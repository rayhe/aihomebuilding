Structure is logical and builds pressure: crisis → law → arithmetic → counter → gap → limitations. "Nobody coordinated the calendar" is a standout one-liner. Closing three sentences are strong.

**Issues:**

   **Fix:** Add 2-3 sentences before the Limitations section or as part of "A Mandate Without a Grid Clause" that directly address the builder: *If you're filing a residential building permit in New York right now, budget $15,000-$25,000 for the all-electric premium (panel upgrade + heat pump + induction), expect 200-amp service as a baseline, and verify with your utility whether the local transformer can handle the additional load before you break ground — because the grid clause the mandate doesn't include is now your problem to solve.*

2. **Visual monotony.** Many paragraphs are 1-2 sentences of very long construction. The article would benefit from one more short-paragraph punch in the middle sections to break the density.

3. **"Last week" in the closing** — the article is dated July 8, and the ruling was ~July 7. "Last week" is ambiguous since July 7 was yesterday or the day before. Consider "On Monday" or "this week" for precision.

## 6. 🔬 Research Rigor — Score: 8.0

**Original Contribution:** ✅ Strong. The 15 kW delta × 40,000 permits = 600 MW annual load / 3,000 MW in 5 years calculation is genuinely original back-of-envelope analysis. The comparison to PJM's 2,900 MW headroom is striking and novel. This is exactly the "dataset combination nobody made" the STORY_GUIDE demands.

**Limitations:** ✅ Explicit, dedicated section. Acknowledges climate zone variability, coincidence factor overstatement, permit volume fluctuation, and missing demand-response/storage offsets. Meets the standard.

**Counterargument:** ✅ Given its own section with full weight. Efficiency, insulation, lock-in costs, climate math all stated without strawmanning.

**Issues:**

1. **Two key arithmetic inputs are unsourced.** The entire original contribution rests on the 15 kW and 30 kW peak demand figures and the 40,000 permits/year estimate. Neither is cited:
   - Where does "roughly 15 kilowatts" / "roughly 30 kilowatts" come from? The research notes say these are averages but don't cite a source. Is this from NYSERDA data? DOE? An industry report?
   - The 40,000 permits figure — the research notes say "~30,000-50,000/year (need to verify)." Was this verified? The article says "approximately 40,000" which is the midpoint of an unverified range.
   
   The STORY_GUIDE requires methodology transparency: "show the math with inputs, assumptions, and calculation." The math is shown but the inputs aren't sourced. This undermines the credibility of the article's central claim.

   **Fix:** Cite the source for peak kW figures (even if it's "based on NYSERDA's all-electric home readiness guidelines" or "per DOE residential energy consumption survey data") and cite a Census Bureau or NY Building Congress source for the permit figure.

2. **PJM geographic scope conflation.** PJM serves 13 states across the Mid-Atlantic and Midwest. New York's new all-electric homes add load to the NY grid, which overlaps with but is not identical to PJM's territory. Specifically, NYISO (New York Independent System Operator) manages the NY grid, not PJM. PJM's peak demand figure is used as a reference point for "grid nearly broke" but the 3,000 MW calculation is about NY-specific permits. The comparison is slightly misleading — Con Edison is in the NYISO, not PJM. The article uses PJM for the drama of the near-record and Con Edison for the outages without clarifying they're different grid operators.

   **Fix:** Either note that the PJM near-record affected the broader regional grid while NY's own operator (NYISO) faced similar strain, or reframe the comparison to use NYISO capacity data instead.

3. **"Compounding to 3,000 megawatts over five years" — this assumes the 40,000/year permit rate remains constant AND all permitted homes are built AND all are in the AEBA-covered category.** The limitations section mentions permit volume variability but doesn't flag that not all residential permits are for new construction (renovations, additions) and not all new construction falls under the ≤7-story threshold. The arithmetic likely overstates.
