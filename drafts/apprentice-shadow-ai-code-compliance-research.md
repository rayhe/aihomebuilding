# Research: Apprentice Shadow AI and Code Compliance Risk

## Angle
Your electrician's apprentice is already using ChatGPT to interpret the National Electrical Code on your job. NABTU focus groups confirmed it. General-purpose AI gets NEC questions wrong 60%+ of the time. Three separate federal and industry programs are now racing to formalize what workers are already doing in the shadows — but nobody's asking who's liable when an AI-interpreted wire gauge fails inspection, or worse, starts a fire.

## Journalist
Marcus "Steel" Washington — Workforce & Labor

## Kill Test
YES — directly affects anyone building or renovating a home. If your electrician's apprentice is using ChatGPT to interpret code and it hallucinates, you could have code-violating wiring behind your walls.

## Primary Sources

### 1. NABTU / Microsoft Partnership (April 21, 2026)
- 1,500 instructors trained in AI literacy at hands-on training centers nationwide
- Now launching free AI literacy courses on LinkedIn Learning for apprentices and journey-level workers
- Industry-recognized AI literacy credential on completion
- Extended to TradesFutures (nonprofit) across 34 states
- **Key finding:** Focus groups with apprentices revealed many are ALREADY using AI tools informally — interpreting electrical codes, staying current with changing regulations
- Source: news.microsoft.com, blogs.microsoft.com, constructiondive.com
- Tom Kriger (NABTU Director of Research and Education): "AI can't turn wrenches and it's not going to lay bricks. AI is more likely to support decision-making and knowledge access than replace craft labor."

### 2. Dakota Prep NEC AI Accuracy Test
- Tested ChatGPT's newest model on 100 NEC electrical code questions
- Result: **60%+ were wrong**
- "Prepping with it is a fast track to failing your exam"
- Built purpose-built AI Tutor specifically because general-purpose AI fails on code questions
- Source: dakotaprep.com

### 3. New York Electrical Inspection Agency (NYEIA)
- Inspector testimony: "I've personally reviewed countless AI responses to code questions, and more often than not, they are incorrect or dangerously incomplete"
- "These answers may appear legitimate and are often written in a confident tone, but they lack the nuance, article references, and real-world application knowledge"
- Real-world consequences: job sites where installations had to be partially or fully redone due to incorrect AI-sourced code interpretations
- Contractors pushing back during inspections, later admitting their understanding came from AI chatbot
- Source: nyeia.com

### 4. SMACNA — "Bring Your Own AI" Dilemma
- Shadow AI phenomenon: workers using personal/unapproved AI applications for work tasks
- Risks: data leakage, inconsistent outputs, potential IP violations, liability exposure
- Example: foreman uses free AI app for materials list, unknowingly uploading proprietary assembly details
- Example: estimator uses AI chatbot for code interpretations not verified for local jurisdictions
- Source: smacna.org

### 5. DeWalt / Business Journal Daily Survey
- Only 9% said AI is part of current day-to-day work
- 37% are piloting and researching AI
- 86% feel somewhat or very prepared to work with AI — BUT learning is fragmented
- Self-directed learning: YouTube (40%), Coursera (39%), video tutorials (42%)
- Top benefits cited by early adopters: increased productivity (35%), cost savings (34%), improved quality control (35%)
- DeWalt launching pilot program with ABC Central Florida's Innovation and Technology Center
- DeWalt committed $75,000 to ABC's Trimmer Construction Education Fund for AI education
- Source: businessjournaldaily.com

### 6. DOL Registered Apprenticeship AI Skills Initiative
- Federal initiative to integrate AI skills into Registered Apprenticeships nationwide
- Website launched with resources in three key areas: AI literacy, industry-specific AI skill building, three integration options
- Contract: single award, 1-year base + 4 option years
- Assistant Secretary Henry Mack: "building the workforce of the future, today"
- Source: dol.gov

### 7. ICC AI Navigator
- International Code Council's purpose-built AI for building codes
- Answered 140,000+ code questions since 2023 launch
- Trained on amended and specialized variants of International Codes
- Intentionally rejects "chatty" character of general-purpose chatbots
- Focuses on linked, high-quality sources — does not speculate or guess
- Source: mid-east.info (Ed Cilurso, ICC Senior Product Manager)

### 8. ASCE Paper — Electrical Construction AI Chatbot
- Analyzed 542 YouTube videos (1,388 transcript pages) + pilot interviews with service electricians
- Key challenges: quality control, safety, code reference, communication
- Prototype chatbot showed improved jurisdiction-specific accuracy vs general-purpose AI tools
- Source: ascelibrary.org (Computing in Civil Engineering 2025)

### 9. Construction Safety Context
- 1,000+ construction workers die on the job each year in the US (MIT Tech Review)
- Construction is the most dangerous industry for fatal slips, trips, and falls
- DroneDeploy Safety AI: deployed on hundreds of sites, claims 95% OSHA violation detection accuracy
- Source: technologyreview.com

### 10. Labor Market Context
- 456,000 construction workers needed by 2027 (ABC/Fortune, Feb 2026)
- 2% observed AI usage in construction (Anthropic Labor Market Report, March 2026)
- Construction job openings increasing (BLS JOLTS, May 2026)

## Novel Contribution
Cross-referencing three independent data points that nobody has combined:
1. NABTU focus groups confirm informal AI adoption among apprentices for code interpretation
2. Dakota Prep testing shows 60%+ error rate on NEC questions from general-purpose AI
3. Only 9% formal day-to-day AI usage (DeWalt) vs. widespread informal shadow use

The risk calculation: If even 20% of the ~370,000 active construction apprentices (BLS) are using ChatGPT for code lookups and 60% of answers are wrong, that's potentially tens of thousands of code interpretations per year being made based on incorrect AI output — with no audit trail, no verification protocol, and no clear liability framework.

## Strongest Counterargument
Workers have always used unreliable sources — asking a buddy, misreading the codebook, working from outdated editions. AI isn't introducing a new category of error; it's just a new medium for an old problem. The difference: AI answers sound authoritative and come without the humility of a peer who says "I think" before sharing. And unlike a dog-eared NEC copy with highlighted annotations, ChatGPT leaves no trail of which interpretation was used and when.

## Limitations
- NABTU focus group data is qualitative (no published sample size or methodology)
- Dakota Prep's 60% error rate is from their own testing (no independent replication published)
- No published data on how many apprentices are using AI for code interpretation specifically
- Shadow AI usage by definition is untracked — all estimates are extrapolations
- The ASCE prototype chatbot study is a proof-of-concept, not a deployed solution
