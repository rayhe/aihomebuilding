## 6. 🔬 Research Rigor — Score: 8.5

**Original Contribution:** ✅ Strong. The 15 kW × 40,000 = 600 MW / 3,000 MW over five years calculation remains genuinely original. The NYISO/PJM transparency actually strengthens the contribution: the article now presents a more defensible version of the same arithmetic by framing it as a regional pattern rather than a direct grid-to-grid comparison.

**Limitations:** ✅ Dedicated section. Now includes: climate zone variability, coincidence factor overstatement, AEBA coverage caveat (not all 40K permits are covered), permit volume fluctuation, and missing demand-response/storage/solar offsets. This is a strong limitations section that meets the scholarly rigor standard.

**Counterargument:** ✅ Full-strength. Efficiency, insulation, lock-in, and climate math all presented without strawmanning. "Half-right" framing is honest.

**Methodology Transparency:** ✅ Improved. Inputs cited (DOE RECS, Census Bureau), calculation shown (15 kW × 40,000 = 600 MW), assumptions stated ("roughly," "approximately"), hedges explicit.

**Issues:**

1. **The EIA RECS link doesn't directly support the "15 kilowatts at peak electrical demand" claim.** The RECS measures annual electricity consumption in kWh, not instantaneous peak demand in kW. Peak demand of 15 kW for a gas-heated home is a standard industry load-calculation figure (derived from NEC Article 220 or Manual J), not something you'd pull from the RECS. Saying "according to DOE residential energy survey data" is a misattribution — it lends the authority of a specific dataset to a figure that actually comes from electrical load calculation practice. The "roughly" hedge mitigates this, and the figures themselves are defensible, but the citation is weak. A more honest attribution would be "per standard residential load calculations" or "based on NEC Article 220 service load estimates."

2. **The 40,000 permits citation (FRED series NYBPPRIVSA) is for all structure types in New York State, seasonally adjusted.** The AEBA applies to new construction ≤7 stories. Not all 40K permits are new construction (some are renovations/additions), and not all are ≤7 stories. The Limitations section now flags that "not all of the 40,000 annual permits represent new construction covered by the AEBA's seven-story threshold" — this is the right fix. The methodology is now transparent about this assumption.

3. **"Compounding to 3,000 megawatts over five years" still assumes additive annual stacking.** This is reasonable for a back-of-envelope but could be noted: new homes add load permanently, so Year 5's peak demand includes all five years of additions. The word "compounding" slightly implies exponential growth when the math is linear (600 MW × 5 = 3,000 MW). Minor precision issue.

## Remaining Notes (non-blocking)

These are quality observations for future articles, not revision requirements:

1. **The EIA RECS citation for peak kW is a misattribution** — the RECS reports annual consumption, not peak demand. Future articles should cite NEC Article 220 load calculations or NREL electrification studies for peak demand figures.

2. **72.7% long sentences is an extreme skew** — the rhythm gate passes on variance, but the distribution doesn't match the STORY_GUIDE's vision of alternating short and long. Future Chen articles should target 50–60% long with more short punches in the middle sections.

3. **One pro-electrification voice quote** would strengthen source balance. The Earthjustice press release (already linked) has quotes from NY-GEO, PUSH Buffalo, and Earthjustice attorneys that could serve this purpose in future coverage.

4. **"Compounding" implies exponential; the math is linear.** Consider "accumulating" in future pieces.
