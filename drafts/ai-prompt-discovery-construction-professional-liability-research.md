# Research: AI Prompt Discovery & Construction Professional Liability

## Core Angle
A federal judge just ruled that AI prompts used by an expert witness are discoverable as methodology under Rule 26. Meanwhile, 27-75% of AEC professionals are using AI for design and planning work — almost none documenting their prompts. When a construction defect case goes to litigation and the architect or engineer used AI, opposing counsel can now demand the AI interaction logs. The industry is wildly unprepared.

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? YES — if your architect or structural engineer uses AI to design your home and something goes wrong, the legal pathway to accountability just changed. You can now demand to see their AI prompts. Conversely, if you're a builder using AI and your project fails, you need to know your prompts are now discoverable.

## Primary Sources

### Source 1: Conservation Law Foundation v. Shell Oil Co. (D. Conn., May 18, 2026)
- Magistrate Judge Thomas O. Farrish, U.S. District Court, District of Connecticut
- Case No. 3:21-cv-00933, ECF No. 970
- Expert (Dr. Naomi Oreskes) used generative AI to filter/identify documents from defendant productions
- Court ordered production of AI prompts as discoverable methodology
- Treated prompts like "formulas, code, or other methodological steps" routinely disclosed
- Rejected argument that prompts were protected "notes, drafts, or communications"
- Currently stayed pending district court resolution of objection
- Key quote: "An expert witness's methodology is fair ground for discovery"
- Source: Reuters (Jul 17, 2026), Mayer Brown analysis (Jun 2026), Exterro analysis (Jul 2026), Mondaq/Baker Botts (Jun 8, 2026)

### Source 2: AEC AI Adoption Data
- Bluebeam survey (1,000 AEC pros): Only 27% use AI in operations, but 94% of users plan to increase
- 52% still use paper during design phase, 49% during planning
- Arup global survey (10 countries): 36% of engineers/architects use AI daily for design
- 80%+ use AI at least weekly
- 91% believe ethical guidelines for AI in built environment are important
- APM/Censuswide 2025: 75% of construction orgs now use AI in projects (up from 15% in 2023)
- Builder Online: 94% of designers and 92% of contractors using AI have NO formal training
- 42% of all pros use AI for planning and design
- AGC annual survey: 61% of firms now use or plan to increase AI investments (up from 44% in 2024)
- RICS 2025 (2,200+ pros): 45% no AI, 34% early pilot, <12% regular use, <1% org-wide

### Source 3: Liability Framework
- MDPI Buildings 2026: "Governing Data Integrity Risks in Digital Construction" — qualitative proportional-liability framework for GenAI across AEC lifecycle
- Identifies two harm pathways: autonomous hallucination entering safety-relevant design info, and adversarial/erroneous data in digital twin feedback loops
- No existing AIA or ASCE standard of care guidance specifically addressing AI prompt documentation
- Construction Week Online: "Legal experts are arguing that contractors who fail to adopt available predictive tools could face higher liability exposure after jobsite incidents"

### Source 4: Multi Housing News (Jul 17, 2026)
- "AI in Construction: Builder Beware"
- "When regulatory reviews emerge, or in scenarios of litigation or arbitration, team leaders will be required to demonstrate how they ensured compliance, fairness and proper oversight measures when using AI"
- "In the event of litigation, companies will be required as part of the discovery process to show the AI inputs and responses, including what documents or data the AI tool was trained on or used as inputs"

## Original Contribution
Cross-referencing the prompt disclosure ruling with AEC adoption data:
- If 42% of pros use AI for planning/design and 94% have no formal training → vast majority creating undocumented AI workflows
- The ruling treats prompts as methodology = same category as "dataset selection criteria or statistical model parameters" (Mayer Brown)
- But AI sessions are typically ephemeral — no one preserves chat logs
- This creates a new professional liability: the ABSENCE of documentation is itself damaging (spoliation argument)
- Specific to residential: custom home architects and small engineering firms are MOST vulnerable — large firms have compliance teams, small firms don't
- Cost to preserve prompts: ~$0. Cost of not preserving: potentially case-destroying

## Strongest Counterargument
The ruling is a single magistrate-judge order, currently stayed, from a narrow factual context (document culling, not design work). Construction design AI use is categorically different from document filtering. A structural engineer using AI to verify a load calculation is forming professional judgment — the AI is a tool like a calculator, and we don't require disclosure of every keystroke on a calculator. The precedent may not extend to design-phase AI use.

## Limitations
- The ruling is stayed and may be reversed
- No construction-specific AI prompt discovery case exists yet — this is extrapolation from a climate litigation case
- AI adoption surveys vary widely (27% to 75% depending on methodology and definition of "AI")
- No data on how many residential-focused architects/engineers specifically use AI for design work (surveys mostly cover commercial)
- The article assumes the ruling's logic will be applied to construction professionals, which is reasonable but not guaranteed
