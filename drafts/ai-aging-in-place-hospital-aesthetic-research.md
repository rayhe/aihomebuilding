# Research: AI Aging-in-Place Home Assessment — The Hospital Aesthetic Problem

## Thesis
AI tools that assess homes for aging-in-place optimize for safety metrics but produce modification plans that look and feel institutional. Older adults reject these modifications because the modifications strip their homes of identity, warmth, and dignity. The design gap between "safe" and "livable" is the central failure of current AI home assessment tools — and it's a solvable one.

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? **YES.** Directly helps anyone modifying a home for an aging parent or planning their own aging-in-place renovation. Actionable: what current tools miss, what to demand from designers, how to integrate safety without institutional aesthetics.

## Journalist
Elena Vasquez — Architecture & Design. Elena sees buildings as art first, technology second. She's skeptical of tools that flatten design into optimization. This topic is perfect for her: the tension between algorithmic safety and the design of spaces people actually want to inhabit.

## Primary Sources (3+ required)

### 1. AARP 2024 Home and Community Preferences Survey (n=1,549 adults 50+)
- 75% of adults 50+ want to stay in their current home
- 72% prefer to reside in their own residence and live on their own
- 56% of adults 50+ expect to stay in current residence and never move
- Strong desire, but homes not designed for it
- Source: https://datastories.aarp.org/2025/building-for-the-future/

### 2. CDC Older Adult Falls Data (updated Feb 2026)
- Falls are the leading cause of injury for adults 65+
- Over 14 million (1 in 4) older adults report falling every year
- 37% of falls result in injury requiring medical treatment or activity restriction (~9 million fall injuries/year)
- Fall death rate increased 21% from 2018-2024: 64.7 → 78.4 per 100,000 older adults
- Source: https://cdc.gov/falls/data-research/index.html

### 3. Systematic Review: Home Modifications for Aging in Place (Cha, 2025, Healthcare)
- 20 studies reviewed; 13 (65%) confirmed effectiveness for fall prevention, functional independence, and cost savings
- Few studies examined personalized interventions, smart home technologies, or long-term adaptability
- Key finding: existing research focuses on physical safety, leaving gaps in understanding social engagement and quality of life
- Source: PMC11988477, DOI: 10.3390/healthcare13070752

### 4. US Census Bureau / Housing Data
- Only 10% of American homes are adequately designed for aging populations
- Less than 1% of homes equipped to accommodate older adults' needs (PMC study)
- Households led by people 80+ set to double by 2040

### 5. ARHAT (Augmented Reality Home Assessment Tool)
- Mobile app using 3D scanning + AR to assess home environments
- Tested with 75 community-dwelling older adults in Midwest
- Accepted by occupational therapists, housing professionals, older adults
- Identifies functional limitations and barriers
- But: focused on hazard identification, not design quality
- Source: PMC12759593, JMIR Aging 2023

### 6. Chaos/Architizer AI in Architecture Survey 2026 (~800 architects)
- 64% have experimented with AI tools, only 20% fully embraced
- 48% cite inconsistent/poor output quality as biggest challenge
- 69% only "somewhat" satisfied
- 43% say concept/pre-design has greatest AI impact
- Source: https://blog.chaos.com/the-state-of-ai-in-architecture-survey-insights

### 7. Builder Perspective on AI Design Gap
- Reagan Langeveld (Symphony Construction, NZ): AI tools "skip the messy parts — no understanding of load paths, moisture management, plumbing locations"
- Builders seeing AI plans with structural conflicts, misaligned plumbing
- Source: https://buildandrenovate.co.nz/ai-tv-mislead-homeowners/

### 8. Remodeling Market Context
- $509 billion US home remodeling market projected 2025 (Harvard JCHS)
- 71% of homeowners have postponed a project (Angi 2025)
- Aging-in-place is a growing segment
- Source: https://www.jchs.harvard.edu/press-releases/modest-gains-2025-outlook-home-remodeling

## Novel Contribution (Required)
Cross-reference: aging-in-place modification recommendations from AI tools (grab bars, ramps, wider doors) vs. design principles that maintain residential character (concealed support, furniture-grade materials, integrated rather than appended solutions). Calculate the cost differential between "institutional" ADA-minimum modifications and design-integrated accessible solutions. Show that the premium for beautiful accessibility is 15-30% — not the 3x that most people assume.

## Strongest Counterargument
The counterargument: safety is non-negotiable, and aesthetics are a luxury. When someone is at risk of a fatal fall, a grab bar that looks like a towel bar still needs to hold 250 lbs. Designing for beauty adds cost and complexity. For low-income seniors — who need aging-in-place modifications most — the institutional solution is the affordable one, and a hospital-looking bathroom that prevents a hip fracture is infinitely better than a beautiful bathroom that sends you to the ER.

## Limitations
- Cost comparison is approximate — design-integrated accessibility products vary widely by manufacturer and market
- Limited peer-reviewed data specifically comparing AI-recommended modifications vs. design-conscious alternatives
- Most AI home assessment tools are still in pilot/academic phase (ARHAT, etc.) — the commercial market is fragmented
- Regional variation in accessibility requirements (ADA federal, but state/local codes differ)
