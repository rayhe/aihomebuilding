# Research: AI Design Liability Insurance Gap

## Angle
When an AI tool generates a flawed structural design or picks a non-code-compliant material and a homeowner gets hurt or suffers costly defects, who pays? As of January 1, 2026, the answer increasingly is: nobody's insurer. Verisk's new standardized AI exclusion forms (CG 40 47, CG 40 48) give carriers ready-made language to strip AI-related liability from architect/engineer E&O policies. Major carriers — Berkley, AIG, Great American, Philadelphia Insurance, Hamilton Select — are already using them or filing for approval. Meanwhile, 1 in 3 architecture firms now uses AI tools daily (AIA March 2025 survey), and the gap between usage and coverage is widening fast.

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? YES — if your architect used AI to design your home and something goes wrong, you need to know whether their insurance will cover it. Right now, it might not.

## Primary Sources

### 1. Verisk AI Exclusion Forms (CG 40 47, CG 40 48)
- Effective January 1, 2026
- Standardized language excluding losses arising from generative AI use
- Specifically names tools like ChatGPT, Midjourney, DALL-E
- Source: Risk Specialty Group / Financial Content reporting, Jan 16, 2026

### 2. Insurance Carrier Actions
- Berkley, AIG, Great American: filing for regulatory approval to exclude AI liabilities from E&O
- Philadelphia Insurance, Hamilton Select: already excluding AI-related claims from E&O coverage
- Source: DailyMoss, AreaDevelopment reporting

### 3. AIA AI Adoption Survey (March 2025)
- One-third of architecture firms incorporate AI tools in daily operations
- Large firms (50+ employees): 61% adoption
- Small firms: 27% adoption
- Generational divide: architects under 35 are 66% likely to use AI image generators vs 41% of those over 50
- Only 8% of firms have formally implemented AI solutions; 20% working on implementation
- Source: AIA (aia.org) research published March 11, 2025

### 4. Stanford Law School AI Hallucination Research
- General-purpose AI tools produce inaccurate outputs at rates between 58% and 88%
- Even "legal-grade" AI tools: 20-33% error rates
- Source: Stanford Law School, cited by Risk Specialty Group

### 5. Bilzin Sumberg Legal Analysis (August 2025)
- "The Legal Risks of AI in the Homebuilding Industry" by Philip R. Stein
- Identifies: contractual liability, IP concerns, data privacy, labor law, regulatory compliance, vendor agreements, consumer protection
- Key finding: contracts rarely allocate AI liability explicitly
- AI tool suggesting non-code-compliant materials → costly remediation or liability
- Source: bilzin.com/insights/publications/2025/08

### 6. Michelman & Robinson Analysis (Q4 2025)
- Jane Kutepova analysis in Area Development
- Standard AIA and ConsensusDocs contracts were NOT drafted with AI in mind
- Don't address: who selects/configures AI, who owns AI-generated data, who's liable for autonomous system decisions, whether AI malfunctions = force majeure
- $48B projected global market value of AI in construction by 2030
- Source: areadevelopment.com/construction-project-planning/q4-2025

### 7. AI Hallucination in Construction Context (Pelles.AI)
- Hallucinated spec references, nonexistent requirements, wrong calculations
- "AI doesn't 'know' things — it generates text... can't distinguish between information extracted from your document vs. training data vs. generated to fill gaps"
- Construction-specific examples: wrong CFM calculations, nonexistent sections referenced, phantom manufacturers listed

## Novel Contribution (original analysis)
Cross-reference AIA adoption rates with Verisk exclusion timeline to calculate the coverage gap:
- 33% of firms using AI daily × unknown % disclosing to insurers = massive uninsured exposure
- Calculate hypothetical: avg residential project $400K, structural defect remediation 15-30% of project cost = $60K-$120K per claim. If E&O excludes AI and AI contributed to the error, architect is personally exposed.
- The Mata v. Avianca precedent (lawyer sanctioned for AI-fabricated citations) translates directly: an architect citing AI-generated code compliance that turns out wrong faces similar professional sanctions PLUS uninsured liability.

## Strongest Counterargument
Architects already bear professional liability for everything they stamp — AI is just another tool, like a calculator or CAD software. The standard of care doesn't change just because the tool changed. Carriers might be overreacting to theoretical risk, and market competition will force some insurers to cover AI use (creating a competitive advantage). The gap may self-correct.

## Limitations
- No public data on how many E&O claims have actually cited AI involvement yet — this is a coverage gap in theory that hasn't been widely tested in court
- Verisk forms are new (Jan 1 2026), so actual denial-of-coverage disputes haven't been litigated
- AIA survey is from March 2025 — adoption rates have likely increased
- We don't know what percentage of AI-using firms have disclosed AI use to their carriers
