### 3. Permit Validity Periods (6–12 months)

**Verdict: ACCURATE**

The article states: "Most jurisdictions set validity at six to twelve months, with extensions that aren't guaranteed and cost additional fees."

This is well-supported:
- **IBC/IRC Section 105.5** (the model code adopted by most states): Permits become invalid if work is not commenced within **180 days (6 months)** or if work is suspended for 180 days. Extensions up to 180 days each may be granted.
- **Multiple municipal codes** confirm 6–12 month validity (some as short as 6 months, others 12 months for residential).
- **Chicago** allows up to 18 months before expiration, slightly beyond the article's range, but the article says "most jurisdictions" which is appropriately hedged.
- **Angi itself** states: "Building permits last for six months, on average."
- The claim that extensions "aren't guaranteed and cost additional fees" is confirmed by multiple municipal codes (25% of original fee in some jurisdictions, $100 flat fee in others, double the fee in Beverly Shores, IN).

### 4. Mandatory vs. Discretionary Spending Framing

**Verdict: FAIR, with one editorial note**

The closing paragraph states: "Most of that is mandatory: permit fees, impact fees, code compliance, studies that jurisdictions require before you can touch a shovel. Nobody chose that spending."

Reviewing the NAHB breakdown, the costs are indeed overwhelmingly mandatory:
- Building code changes: $40,288 (code mandates)
- Permit/inspection/hook-up fees: $20,154 (mandatory)
- Land dedicated to government: $13,593 (required by subdivision regulations)
- Compliance fees and studies: $10,755 (mandatory)
- Zoning approval: $7,007 (mandatory)
- OSHA/labor compliance: $9,125 combined (mandatory)

The characterization "most of that is mandatory" is factually accurate.

**Editorial note:** "Nobody chose that spending" is an editorial judgment, not a factual claim. It frames regulation as an external imposition rather than a policy choice society made (arguably, society "chose" these regulations through democratic processes for safety, environmental, and community purposes). The NAHB study author explicitly noted it "did not delve into the causes of the increases or offer an opinion on which costs may be excessive." The article's framing is acceptable for a trade publication with an editorial voice, but it's worth noting this is opinion, not fact. The article immediately pivots to contrast this mandatory spending with the *absence* of discretionary spending on decision-support tooling, which is a fair editorial argument.

## Remaining Issues

1. **$93,871 vs. $93,870**: The stat-callout box cites $93,871 as the 2021 baseline; NAHB's own text says $93,870. Trivial but technically imprecise. (Severity: minimal)
2. **Angi source alignment**: The article says "$1,500 to $3,000" for new construction permits and links to Angi, but Angi's general permit range starts at $525 with an average of $1,688. The article appears to have narrowed the range to new construction specifically, but the linked source doesn't isolate new construction permits at that range. Other sources (JDJ Consulting: $1,000–$3,000) support the claim. (Severity: low)
3. **Plan review percentage unsourced inline**: The "65 to 75 percent" plan review claim is accurate per multiple municipal fee schedules but has no inline citation. Adding a representative municipal fee schedule link would strengthen it. (Severity: low)

## Suggestions

1. Fix the $93,871 → $93,870 in the stat-callout to match the NAHB primary source exactly, or cite the secondary source that rounds to $93,871.
2. Consider adding "according to industry fee schedules" or a representative municipal link for the 65–75% plan review claim.
3. No changes needed to the mandatory/discretionary framing — it's fair editorial opinion in a trade publication context.
