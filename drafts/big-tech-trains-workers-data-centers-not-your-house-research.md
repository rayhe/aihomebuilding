# Research: Big Tech Is Training Construction Workers — For Data Centers, Not Your House

## Journalist: Marcus "Steel" Washington
## Date: June 14, 2026

## Thesis
Big Tech companies are investing hundreds of millions in training construction workers, but every dollar is aimed at data center construction, not residential. This is actively pulling workers out of an already-depleted homebuilding workforce. The people who could be framing your house are being retrained to pour foundations for server farms.

## Primary Sources

### 1. Meta America's Workforce Academy (June 2026)
- $115M invested in first year
- Free 4-5 week bootcamp, guaranteed job upon completion
- Training in: fiber-optic installation, electrical, welding, plumbing
- Pilot locations: Louisiana, Ohio, Indiana, Texas
- Partners: National Urban League, ABC, CBRE, US Hispanic Chamber of Commerce
- Covers tuition, airfare, lodging, daily stipend
- No prior experience required
- Source: Daily Caller, Black Enterprise, People, Reuters/WSJ

### 2. Meta broader workforce investment
- Separate $250M program for data center construction training (Reuters)
- Source: Traders Union, Outlook Business

### 3. Google $50M Skilled Trades Training (June 2026)
- $50M in US upskilling programs
- Targeting: construction workers, electricians, plumbers, pipe fitters, welders
- Partners: TradesFutures (NABTU), IBEW/NECA (etA), UA's International Training Fund
- Some partnerships already underway
- Source: Google Blog, Business Insider, Outlook Business

### 4. HBI Construction Labor Market Report (Fall 2025)
- Skilled labor shortage costs homebuilders $10.8 billion/year
- Prevents construction of ~19,000 homes annually
- $2.663B in higher carrying costs + $8.143B in lost single-family home building
- 3.3 million payroll residential construction workers
- Residential workers: $39.40/hr vs broader construction avg $39.70/hr
- Immigrants: 25.5% of construction workforce (historic high), 1 in 3 skilled tradespeople
- Women: 11.2% of construction employment (20-year high)
- Gen Z participation doubled from 6.4% (2019) to 14.1% (2023)
- Source: NAHB/HBI Report (Oct 2025)

### 5. Edward Brady / HBI CEO Interview (Homes.com, March 2026)
- "Those commercial jobs are more stable and, in many cases, better paying"
- Workers shift to commercial/data center when housing slows
- Construction industry shrank by 11,000 jobs in February (BLS)
- Residential construction will struggle to ramp back up when demand returns
- Need 723,000 workers/year to keep up with demand + replace departures
- Immigration enforcement fear keeping workers off job sites
- Source: Homes.com

### 6. NAHB 2026 Legislative Conference (June 2026)
- 200,000+ construction worker shortage
- Need 2.2 million additional workers over next 3 years
- 25%+ of workforce is foreign-born
- 1,100+ builders on Capitol Hill calling for workforce legislation
- Source: tEDmag

### 7. NAHB/Wells Fargo Housing Market Index
- 61% of builders reported serious problems with cost/availability of labor in 2025
- 300,000 open construction jobs
- Need 740,000 workers annually
- Source: NAHB, Contractor Magazine

### 8. NAHB Regulatory Cost Study (June 2026)
- $131,734 in regulatory costs per average new home
- 26.4% of $499,500 average sale price
- Up 40% from $93,870 in 2021
- Source: NAHB/NY Post

### 9. Housing Deficit
- 1.5 million homes (HBI/builders estimate)
- 4.03 million homes (Realtor.com)
- Source: Multiple

### 10. Data Center Scale
- 4,149 active + 2,788 planned data centers in US
- Expected to create 5.4 million temporary and permanent jobs, $27B in tax revenues over next decade
- 176 new data center permits across 34 states for 2025
- 70% of Americans oppose data centers near them (Gallup, May 2026)
- Wyoming "man camp" — 5,600 workers for data center construction (WSJ/NY Post)
- Monterey Park, CA first city to permanently ban data centers (June 2026)
- Source: American Edge Project (Dec 2025), Gallup, NY Post, WebProNews

### 11. ABC (Associated Builders and Contractors)
- Construction industry needs 349,000 new workers this year
- Source: Traders Union / ABC

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home?
YES — If you're a homebuilder, this explains why your sub-contractor bids are coming in higher and slower. If you're a buyer, this explains one reason your house costs more and takes longer. If you're a construction worker, this tells you where the money is going.
