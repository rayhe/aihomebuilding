# Research: Insurance Companies as De Facto Building Code — Smart Water Shutoff Mandates

## Angle
Insurance companies are quietly mandating smart water shutoff valves through premium discounts and policy requirements — becoming the building code authority that local governments couldn't be. No jurisdiction requires them in residential new construction. But your insurer might not renew your policy without one.

## Journalist
Catherine "Code" Chen — policy, regulation, legal. Perfect fit.

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? YES. Directly affects insurance costs, which affect buying decisions and mortgage affordability. Actionable: should you install one? What does it cost? What's the ROI?

## Novel Contribution
Cost-benefit calculation: $500 device + $60/yr subscription vs. $13,954 average claim + premium increases. Break-even analysis for homeowner. Insurance discount ROI.

## Strongest Counterargument
No building code requires these devices. No independent certification standard exists. The insurance industry is creating a de facto mandate without the public review process that building codes require. Homeowners in high-cost markets are being squeezed from both sides — rising premiums AND requirements to buy proprietary devices.

## Limitations
- Moen's "96% reduction" claim is from their own data with an unnamed insurance partner — not independently verified
- Installation costs vary wildly ($200 for Moen Flo to $5,000 for Phyn Plus)
- No standardized testing protocol — Utah State study is one of few
- Insurance discount amounts are state-specific and poorly documented
- Long-term reliability data is limited (devices are 5-7 years old)
