# Research: Energy Credit Cliff — Green Home Economics After the OBBB

## Topic
The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (signed July 4, 2025) terminated or accelerated expiration of every major residential energy tax credit. The 45L New Energy Efficient Home Credit ($5,000/home) expired June 30, 2026 — 11 days ago. This is the most significant policy shift in green homebuilding incentives in a decade, and its effects are just starting to hit builders who planned their pipelines around credits that were supposed to last until 2032.

## Key Data Points

### Credits Terminated by OBBB
- **45L (New Energy Efficient Home Credit):** Up to $5,000/home for builders. Expired June 30, 2026. IRA had extended through 2032 — OBBB cut 8 years short. (Source: IRS.gov, NAHB)
- **25C (Energy Efficient Home Improvement Credit):** Up to $3,200/year for homeowners (windows, insulation, HVAC). Expired Dec 31, 2025. (Source: IRS.gov)
- **25D (Residential Clean Energy Credit):** 30% credit for solar, geothermal, battery storage. Expired Dec 31, 2025. (Source: NAHB)
- **30C (EV Charging Credit):** Expired June 30, 2026. (Source: Miller Canfield)
- **179D (Commercial Buildings Deduction):** Expired June 30, 2026. (Source: NAHB)
- **Solar ITC (48E):** Must begin construction by July 4, 2026, placed in service by Dec 31, 2027. (Source: NAHB)

### 45L Usage & Impact (ACEEE Analysis)
- 350,000 homes used 45L credit in 2024 — 22% of all new homes built
- Average credit: $2,700/home
- Total cost to Treasury: $1.7B from 2025-2032 (projected under IRA)
- Lifetime energy savings: $17,500/home (Energy Star) to $50,000/home (ZERH)
- Average annual savings: $400 (Energy Star) to $1,000+ (ZERH)
- Discounted energy savings benefit is 4× the cost of the credit (ACEEE)

### Construction Cost Context
- NAHB 2026 study: Regulations add $131,734 to average new home (26.4% of $499,500 avg price)
- Construction-phase regulation cost: $84,939 (up from 13.3% to 17.0% of price)
- Regulatory costs increased 40% in 5 years; disposable income rose only 18.3%
- Energy input costs to construction: +62.8% YoY (May 2026, BLS PPI via NAHB)
- Diesel: +105.9% YoY
- Metal trim: +42.9% YoY
- Softwood lumber: +5.6% YoY

### Energy Star Program Details
- Energy Star: 10% savings over code-built home
- ZERH (DOE Zero Energy Ready Home / DOE Efficient New Homes): "so energy efficient that a renewable energy system could offset most or all the home's annual energy use"
- Energy Star incremental construction cost: ~$2,000-$4,000 over code minimum (varies by climate zone)
- ZERH incremental cost: ~$5,000-$15,000 over code minimum

### Policy Context
- Executive Order July 7, 2025: directs Treasury to "strictly enforce" credit terminations
- IRS expected to interpret phase-outs strictly
- Leased residential solar remains eligible under 48E (barely survived OBBB)
- No replacement program announced

## Original Contribution
Calculate the break-even economics for a builder deciding whether to continue building to Energy Star after 45L expiration:
- With 45L: $2,500-$5,000 credit offset ~$2,000-$4,000 incremental cost → net positive for builder
- Without 45L: $2,000-$4,000 incremental cost absorbed by builder or passed to buyer → builder incentive eliminated
- On a $499,500 home with $131,734 in regulatory costs, adding $2,000-$4,000 for voluntary green features without a credit is a hard sell
- For 350,000 homes/year at $2,700 avg credit = ~$945M/year in builder incentives disappeared overnight

## Strongest Counterargument
- State-level programs may fill the gap (SRP in Arizona still offers per-kWh rebates)
- Code minimums keep ratcheting up (2024 IECC is more efficient than 2021)
- Market demand for efficient homes may sustain green building without credits
- Green premiums at resale could justify the incremental cost

## Limitations
- ACEEE data is from 2024; 2025-2026 45L uptake may differ
- Incremental cost ranges vary widely by climate zone and builder
- State-level replacement programs are in flux
- Some builders may have already priced in credit expiration
