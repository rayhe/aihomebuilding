# Research: AI Robot Building Adobe Homes — Code Gap

## Angle
A startup's AI robot built a home out of dirt in Texas. The building code for it exists (IRC Appendix U, approved 2021). But most jurisdictions haven't adopted it. And the first home isn't even fully adobe — two walls are wood-framed. The robot can't do corners yet.

This is a story about the gap between what's technically possible and what's legally buildable. And about whether a robot can make architecture — not just walls.

## Kill Test
YES. If you're a builder or homeowner considering alternative construction, you need to know: (1) the code exists but your city probably hasn't adopted it, (2) energy code compliance is a blocker even in warm climates, (3) seismic restrictions limit this to low-risk zones, (4) the first house is a hybrid prototype.

## Primary Sources

### Source 1: Terran Robotics — First-Party Data
- Company: Terran Robotics (HQ: Bloomington, IN; Build site: Luling/Lockhart, TX)
- Robot: "Terry" — AI-powered cable-driven pick-and-place system
- Suspended from 4-tower gantry, anchored by earth screws
- Two-person setup in one day, truck + trailer
- Uses real-time depth cameras + AI spatial awareness + reinforcement learning
- Picks up ~10lb adobe chunks, places them, then hammers/sculpts smooth
- Runs 24/7 autonomously
- Material: ~20% clay, soil, water, straw — sourced on-site ("zero-mile")
- First home completed April 27, 2026 at Proto-Town near Lockhart, TX
- Plans: 20+ units next year
- **CRITICAL DETAIL (ZME Science):** First home has TWO adobe walls and TWO wood-framed sides. Not fully adobe. Robot can't do clean corners yet.
- Claims: 80% less embodied carbon, fireproof, mold-free, soundproof, zero-VOC
- Code: IRC Appendix U — Cob Construction (Monolithic Adobe)
- Partners: NSF grants, Autodesk, ARUP engineering
- Valuation: $5M pre-seed (from Eric Jorgenson deal memo)
- Team includes Anthony Dente, P.E. — literally co-wrote the structural provisions of Appendix U
- Co-founders: Zach Dwiel (CEO), Daniel Weddle
- **IMPORTANT:** Company originally used drone swarms (per investor deal memo), pivoted to cable-driven system. The drone approach didn't work.

### Source 2: ICC Building Safety Journal — Code Authority
- IRC Appendix U: Cob Construction (Monolithic Adobe) — approved October 2019, published in 2021 IRC
- Developed by Cob Research Institute (non-profit, founded 2008)
- Approved 93-6 by Code Council voting members
- **KEY LIMITATION:** Appendices must be specifically adopted by each jurisdiction. Not automatic.
- Strawbale appendix (2015 IRC) adopted by only ~6 states and 9 local jurisdictions in 6 years
- California adopted it as Appendix AU in 2022 California Residential Code
- **SEISMIC:** Buildings in SDC D or E require engineered structural design (licensed civil engineer). Covers California, PNW, much of urban West.
- **ENERGY CODE:** "Even in warm climates, it is difficult for cob walls to comply without additional insulation." Cob treated as mass wall (like concrete block) but still can't meet energy code without external insulation. Most rigid insulation isn't vapor-permeable enough for cob code requirements.
- **FIRE:** Cob is so fireproof it's used for fireplaces and wood-fired ovens. But no certified lab fire-resistance rating yet — can't be used close to property lines or as common walls between units.
- University testing: Santa Clara U + Cal Poly — full-scale seismic testing of reinforced cob panels

### Source 3: ZME Science — Independent Reporting
- First home at Proto-Town has 2 adobe walls + 2 wood-framed walls
- "It's promising, but it's not a finished product just yet"
- Next version aims to be entirely from earth
- Robot still needs to learn clean corners
- Workers still prepare soil mixture manually — robot doesn't self-source
- Conventional trades (electrical, plumbing, HVAC) still handle interiors
- Earthen exterior needs protective coating for weather
- CEO Zach Dwiel, co-founder Daniel Weddle, software engineer Anastasia Nikoulina

### Source 4: MDPI Thermal Study
- Structural cob thermal conductivity: 0.4 W/(m·K)
- That translates to roughly R-2.5 per foot of wall thickness
- A typical 24-inch cob wall ≈ R-5, vs energy code requirement of R-20+ for walls
- Non-uniform fiber distribution increases heat loss by up to 50%
- Dual-walling approach (structural + insulation cob) being researched but not production-ready

### Source 5: Construction Tech Graveyard
- Katerra: $2B+ raised, collapsed (vertical integration failure)
- Veev: $647M raised, collapsed (scaling failure)
- 3D-printed housing project in Cairo, IL: $590K deposit abandoned, never built
- Pattern: demo → press → scaling failure → collapse

## Original Contribution
1. Cross-reference Appendix U's SDC restrictions with Terran's target markets: TX is mostly SDC B (fine), but Indiana includes SDC C-D zones requiring engineering
2. Energy code math: 24" cob wall at R-5 vs IRC R-20 requirement. That's a 4x gap.
3. The "two walls" detail is buried in one article — most coverage treats this as a fully adobe house. It isn't.
4. The 80% carbon reduction claim has no third-party verification
5. Adoption gap: If strawbale (2015) only reached 6 states in 6 years, cob (2021) is likely at fewer

## Strongest Counterargument
Adobe has lasted centuries where it was built — the American Southwest, British Devon, North Africa. The fact that the first prototype is a hybrid doesn't mean the technology won't mature. IRC Appendix U is real code backed by real structural testing. And Terran's team includes the engineer who helped write the code. The graveyard argument (Katerra, Veev) is about vertical integration at scale, not about the fundamental material.
