# Research: Copper Hit a Record $6.65 a Pound. Your New Home Needs 439 Pounds of It.

## Journalist: Jake "Jackhammer" Kowalski

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? YES — directly impacts construction budgets, gives specific cost numbers, identifies material substitution options with tradeoffs.

## Core Thesis
COMEX copper hit an all-time record of $6.6495/lb on June 2, 2026 — up 50% from its 52-week low of $4.33 and up ~16% YTD. The average US single-family home contains 439 pounds of copper (CDA data). With Section 232 tariffs layering 25-50% duties on imported copper products, builders are facing a perfect storm: record commodity prices + tariff premiums. The math adds $2,000-$5,000 per home in copper costs alone. Builders are scrambling to substitutes, but alternatives like PEX plumbing and aluminum wiring carry their own risks and code restrictions.

- "Further Adjusting the Tariff Regimes for Imports of Aluminum, Steel, and Copper"
- Section 232 tariffs on copper (Proclamation 10962, July 30, 2025)
- Current rates (effective April 6, 2026):
  - **50%** on semi-finished copper products (raw materials, coils, sheets)
  - **25%** on copper-intensive derivative products (wire, pipe, appliances)
  - **15%** on HVAC equipment, farm/construction equipment (reduced from 25% on June 2)
  - **10%** if 95%+ copper content is US-sourced
  - **Exempt** if metal content is ≤15% by weight
- Changes effective June 8, 2026, temporary through Dec 31, 2027

## Original Analysis: The Per-Home Copper Cost Math

### Raw commodity cost:
- 2024 baseline (~$4.35/lb): 439 lbs × $4.35 = **$1,910**
- June 2026 (~$6.51/lb): 439 lbs × $6.51 = **$2,858**
- Increase: **$948 per home** in raw copper alone (commodity price, before fabrication)

### Finished product cost (fabrication markup 2-4x raw):
- Estimated copper product cost per home (wire, pipe, fittings, fixtures): **$8,000-$12,000** at 2024 prices
- With 15-40% price increases already passed through: **$10,000-$15,000** in mid-2026
- Net increase per home: **$2,000-$5,000** vs pre-tariff baseline

### Electrified home premium:
- Traditional gas home: 439 lbs baseline
- Heat pump replaces furnace + AC: net similar copper (48 lbs HP vs ~52 lbs AC, but HP needs additional wiring for backup heat)
- Add EV charger: 50+ ft of 6 AWG wire, ~15 lbs additional copper
- 200A panel upgrade (from 100A): additional copper for larger service entrance, feeder
- Solar system: additional wiring from panels to inverter to panel, ~10-20 lbs
- Estimated electrified home total: **490-520 lbs copper** (vs 439 baseline)
- Electrification premium at $6.51/lb raw: ~$330-$530 more in raw copper

## Substitution Options & Tradeoffs

### PEX Plumbing (replaces 151 lbs copper plumbing)
- Cost savings: PEX is roughly 60-70% cheaper per linear foot
- Savings potential: $1,500-$3,000 per home
- Tradeoffs: shorter lifespan (25-50 yr vs 70+ yr copper), can't be used outside or for hot recirculation in some codes, rodent susceptibility, potential UV degradation
- Code status: Widely accepted in IRC and UPC jurisdictions

### Aluminum Wiring (replaces 195 lbs copper building wire)
- 1970s aluminum wiring debacle: caused house fires due to oxidation at connections, thermal expansion mismatch with copper-rated devices
- Modern aluminum: AA-8000 series alloy (since 2005 NEC), significantly improved — used widely in feeders and service entrance
- BUT: 85%+ of electrical contractors still refuse aluminum for branch circuits in their own homes (CDA survey)
- Code status: NEC allows aluminum for certain circuits with proper connectors and terminations
- Practical reality: most residential electricians won't install it for branch circuits

### Design changes:
- Reduce number of circuits by planning layout more efficiently
- Use structured wiring (centralized distribution) to reduce total wire runs
- Specify PEX-A (highest quality) if substituting plumbing

## Strongest Counterargument
84% of intermediate copper products are already domestically sourced. The direct tariff hit only applies to 16% of imported products. But this misses the point: domestic producers raise prices to match the tariff-protected price, because why wouldn't they? The tariff sets a price floor. Even Skanska's data shows 14-40% price increases on domestically-sourced copper products. The tariff doesn't just tax imports — it gives domestic producers pricing power they didn't have before.

## Limitations
- CDA's 439 lbs figure is industry data, not independently verified. The breakdowns haven't been publicly updated since ~2020
- Actual copper costs per home vary enormously by region, design, and local codes
- The electrification copper premium estimate is my calculation, not an industry-sourced figure
- Tariff classification of specific products (50% vs 25% rate) is complex and depends on HTS codes — the effective rate for a given builder's copper purchases varies
- Domestic price transmission isn't 1:1 with tariff rates — competition, inventory, and supply elasticity all factor in
