# Research: AI Aerial Insurance Roof Assessment

## Angle
Insurance companies are using AI-powered satellite and drone imagery to assess homeowners' roof conditions — and dropping coverage based on algorithmic assessments homeowners never knew were happening. The regulatory response is a patchwork: a few states have pushed back, most haven't acted, and the NAIC is still catching up. Meanwhile, the false positive rate for these aerial assessments has never been independently audited.

## Journalist
Catherine "Code" Chen — Policy & Regulation

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? YES. If you own a home with insurance, your insurer may be assessing your roof from above right now. This article tells you what to do about it.

## Primary Sources

### 1. KUT/NPR Texas Investigation (May 14, 2025)
- Travelers Insurance threatened non-renewal of Austin homeowner Tracy Gartenmann's decade-long policy
- Used Nearmap aerial imagery showing trees "too close to roof"
- Gartenmann: "I thought it was a scam... It felt like an infringement on my rights"
- KUT reviewed hundreds of pages of complaints and state filings
- At least one company uses computer models to flag moldy roofs, askew tree branches, missing shingles
- Source: https://www.kwbu.org/news-from-across-texas/2025-05-14/in-texas-insurers-are-watching-your-home-from-above-it-could-cost-you-coverage

### 2. Pennsylvania Insurance Department Bulletin
- PID received multiple complaints: insurers citing drone footage to cancel/non-renew
- Found images "barely identify the structure of the home, much less the detailed condition of the roof"
- Some images showed only discoloration, streaking, or cosmetic issues
- Bulletin: aerial images alone aren't sufficient for adverse action
- Recommends physical inspection for validation
- Commissioner Humphreys: "Innovation needs to be reasonable"
- PA is 8th state to adopt NAIC AI bulletin
- Source: https://insurify.com/homeowners-insurance/news/pa-tells-insurers-drone-footage-not-enough-to-drop-home-policies/

### 3. California Assembly Bill 75 (March 2026)
- Assemblymember Lisa Calderon
- Requirements: notify homeowners of aerial photos, supply photos on request, limit use in policy decisions
- Images older than 45 days can't be used unless problem is persistent AND verified by in-person inspection
- If canceling based on aerial photos: must send images + explanation to homeowner
- Homeowner gets time to remedy the problem
- Insurance Commissioner Ricardo Lara supports it
- "Investigated numerous complaints where flawed aerial imagery led to wrongful cancellations"
- Currently in Assembly Committee on Privacy and Consumer Protection
- Source: https://insurify.com/homeowners-insurance/news/california-roof-image-regulation/

### 4. Zurich North America + Nearmap Partnership (2025)
- AI-enhanced aerial imagery and roof scoring integrated into underwriting systems
- "Faster risk assessments and tailored property insurance coverage"
- Reduces manual processes
- Source: https://www.zurichna.com/media/news-releases/2025/nearmap-and-zurich-advance-property-underwriting-with-ai-integration

### 5. Verisk Risk Analytics (Roof Report)
- "Limited roof visibility from the ground level can cause inspectors to miss signs of damage"
- Justification for aerial approach from insurer perspective
- Source: Referenced in Insurify/Zebra coverage

### 6. NAIC AI Bulletin (December 2024/2025)
- National guidance for insurers on AI use
- PA, MA, CT among states adopting it
- Reminds insurers that AI decisions must comply with state regulations
- Notes AI presents "unique risks to consumers, like inaccuracy and lack of transparency"

### 7. Other State Actions
- Massachusetts: Detailed guidance with consumer rights list (March 2025)
- Connecticut: Warned insurers can't rely on cosmetic roof conditions (streaking, discoloration) from aerial images
- Most states: No specific guidance or regulation

### 8. Key Companies
- **Nearmap** — aerial imagery provider, partnered with Zurich, used by Travelers
- **EagleView** — virtual inspection tool, 3D sketches, historical imagery
- **Cotality** (formerly CoreLogic) — AI-powered "Age of Roof" estimates from aerial imagery
- **Verisk** — risk analytics, roof reports

## Original Contribution
Cross-referencing state regulatory responses: As of May 2026, only ~5 states (PA, CA, MA, CT, and partially TX through complaint investigations) have addressed AI aerial imagery in insurance. The remaining ~45 states have no specific guidance. 30+ states allow insurers to use aerial assessments without any notification requirement, right of homeowner review, or validation mandate.

No independent third-party accuracy audit of aerial roof assessment AI has been published. Insurer claims of "improved accuracy" are self-reported. The false positive rate — homes wrongly flagged for damage that doesn't exist or is cosmetic — is unknown publicly.

## Strongest Counterargument
Aerial assessment IS more consistent than human inspections, covers more properties at lower cost, and can catch legitimate safety issues (branch proximity, missing shingles) that protect both insurer and homeowner. Human inspectors are also inconsistent — the same roof might pass one inspection and fail another. At scale, AI reduces the arbitrariness of human judgment.

## Limitations
- No access to proprietary accuracy data from Nearmap, EagleView, or Cotality
- Texas complaints data from KUT investigation may not be representative nationally
- California AB 75 not yet passed — may be amended or fail
- Insurer non-renewal rates specifically attributable to aerial imagery unknown (no regulatory body tracks this)
- Can't distinguish whether aerial assessments catch more legitimate damage vs create more false positives
