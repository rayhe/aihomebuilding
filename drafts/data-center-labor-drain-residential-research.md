# Research: Data Center Construction Boom Draining Residential Workforce

## Angle
Big Tech companies (Meta, Google, Oracle, Microsoft) are spending $300M+ to train construction workers — but exclusively for data center construction, not homes. Meanwhile, residential builders are losing electricians, plumbers, and welders to data center jobs that pay 75% more. The same companies whose AI products promise to transform homebuilding are actively making it harder to build homes by siphoning the workforce.

## Journalist
Marcus "Steel" Washington — Workforce & Labor

## Key Data Points

### The Drain (Primary Sources)
- **Abilene, TX case study (Texas Tribune, April 2026):** Builder Gene Lantrip reports homes taking 2 months longer to build since Stargate AI campus (OpenAI/Crusoe/Oracle, 4M sqft) construction began nearby
- **Wage gap:** Residential electrician in Abilene: ~$20/hr. Data center electrician: $35/hr + overtime + per diem. That's a 75% premium.
- **IBEW stat:** Electrical work = 45-70% of entire data center construction budget
- **Texas context:** 300+ data centers operating, ~100 more planned. State added 2.6M residents since 2020 needing housing.
- **HBI/Edward Brady quote (Homes.com, March 2026):** "The housing market is sluggish right now. If they don't have the work, they're going to leave the residential industry and go to the commercial."

### The Training Programs (All for data centers, not homes)
- **Meta:** $250M program for data center construction training + $115M "America's Workforce Academy" (launched June 8, 2026). Free 5-week bootcamp, guaranteed data center construction job. Pilots in Louisiana, Ohio, Indiana, Texas. Trades: fiber-optic, electrical, welding, plumbing.
- **Google:** $50M in skilled-trades training (June 2026). Partners: IBEW/NECA, TradesFutures/NABTU, United Association ITF.
- **Oracle and Microsoft:** Also outlined workforce pipeline programs.

### The Housing Cost (HBI/NAHB/University of Denver Study)
- **$10.806 billion/year** aggregate economic impact of skilled labor shortage
- **19,000 homes not built in 2024** due to workforce shortage
- **$2.663 billion** in higher carrying costs from extended build times
- **$8.143 billion** in lost home production value
- **1.98 months** average construction delay
- **Industry needs 349,000 new workers in 2026** (ABC estimate), 456,000 in 2027
- **723,000 additional workers per year** needed to keep pace (HBI)
- **3.3 million** payroll residential construction workers currently
- **1 in 3** construction craftsmen are immigrants (historic high of 25.5%)

### Broader Labor Market
- Construction trades workers declined from 71% of workforce (2005) to <59% (2024) — NAHB
- 2.17 million adjusted net hires needed 2024-2026 — NAHB/HBI
- Data center construction: $86B predicted spend in 2026, accounting for 296,700 jobs (85% of total need) — ABC/Census/BLS
- ~3,000 new US data centers could generate 4.7 million temporary construction jobs
- PayScale 2026: Average residential electrician hourly pay $25.13, range $16.91-$39.07
- BLS FRED data: Full-time male electricians peaked at 857,000 (2024), dropped to 830,000 (2025)
- Immigration enforcement (ICE, TPS rescission) further shrinking foreign-born construction workforce (~2.8M workers)

### The Irony / Counterargument
- Tech companies whose AI products promise to "transform" and "automate" construction are the same ones physically draining the workforce that builds homes today
- Meta's Dina Powell McCormick: "skilled workers electrified rural America... now a new generation will pour the foundations and lay the fiber that secures American strength"
- The training creates real skills and good jobs — but channels them exclusively into infrastructure humans don't live in
- Hill County, TX approved one-year moratorium on new data center construction (May 2026) — first in Texas
- IBEW DC/MD/VA membership doubled from ~7,350 to 14,700 since 2018 — data centers ARE growing the trade, just not for housing

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? YES — if you're a homeowner or GC in a data center corridor (Texas, Ohio, Indiana, Virginia), your electrician may be about to leave. Understanding the wage dynamics helps you plan timelines and budgets. The article quantifies the cost of delay ($10.8B/yr, 1.98 months average) and identifies which regions are most affected.
