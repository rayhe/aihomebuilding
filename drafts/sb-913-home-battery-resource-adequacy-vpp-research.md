# Research: California SB 913 — Your Home Battery as Resource Adequacy

## Slug: sb-913-home-battery-resource-adequacy-vpp

## Journalist: Priya Greenwood (Sustainability & Green Building)

## Thesis
California SB 913 would let home batteries and EVs qualify as Resource Adequacy capacity — the same grid reliability credit that gas peaker plants sell to utilities. This isn't "sell excess solar back to the grid" net metering. It's your battery competing directly with a 50 MW gas turbine for reliability payments. The math is better than most homeowners think, but the catch is who actually controls dispatch.

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? **YES.** If you're installing a battery with a new home or retrofit, this law changes the ROI calculation. Understanding RA payments vs. TOU arbitrage vs. backup-only helps homeowners make a $15-25K decision.

## Primary Sources

### 1. California SB 913 (Sen. Josh Becker, D)
- Classifies customer-owned clean energy resources (home batteries, EVs, smart thermostats) as resource adequacy capacity
- Allows energy aggregators to bundle residential batteries into VPPs and bid that capacity into California's RA and utility markets
- 8,000 new home batteries added to California grid per month (~100 MW new storage capacity/month per CPUC data)
- Source: Electrek (Apr 25, 2026), pv-magazine-usa (Mar 26, 2026), Canary Media

### 2. Ava Community Energy SmartHome Battery Program (Apr 9, 2026)
- $11+ million program in Northern California
- Rebates: $500/kWh for income-qualified customers, $90/kWh for market-rate
- $3/month/kWh participation payment for shared capacity
- Tesla Powerwall customers must share 80% of stored energy with Ava's VPP
- NEM 2 customers with <4 kWh enrolled: daily battery dispatch schedule (2 hrs/weekday, all year)
- Source: Ava Community Energy, CalCCA, Morningstar

### 3. Lunar Energy $232M Raise (Feb 4, 2026)
- Series C ($130M, Activate Capital) + Series D ($102M, B Capital + Prelude Ventures)
- Total funding: $532M+
- CEO: Kunal Girotra (former head of Tesla Energy)
- Currently: ~2,000 homes installed, 10,000 systems/yr capacity
- Target: 20,000/yr by end of 2026, 100,000/yr by 2028
- Gridshare software manages 130,000 Sunrun VPP batteries (non-Lunar brands too)
- "Android-like strategy" — interoperable, not walled garden (contrast with Tesla/Enphase)
- 10% price premium over Tesla Powerwall
- Source: Canary Media, Heatmap, TechCrunch, GlobeNewsWire

### 4. Tesla VPP Earnings Data
- $2/kWh during grid emergency dispatch events
- Homeowners reporting $400-$750 per payout event
- One homeowner earned $600 for grid support during blackouts
- Source: The Cooldown, EVShift

### 5. Delta Green (Europe) — Negative Price Arbitrage
- German households earning €30-40/day during negative price spikes (May 1, 2026 example)
- ~€40/month in flexibility value
- Czech company aggregating distributed assets into VPP across European markets
- Source: Prometheus Institute, pv magazine Deutschland (May 5, 2026)

### 6. RMI (Rocky Mountain Institute)
- VPPs can meet 20% of US peak demand by 2030
- Ontario, Canada: 90 MW residential VPP already operational
- Arizona: 40 MW annual VPP additions
- Source: rmi.org

### 7. FERC Order 2222 (Sept 2020)
- Federal order allowing distributed energy resources (DERs) to participate in wholesale power markets
- Removes barriers for DER aggregators
- Implementation still uneven across RTOs/ISOs
- Source: Sidley Austin, FERC

## Novel Contribution — The Math Nobody's Done
Calculate the actual homeowner revenue difference between three scenarios for a typical California home with a 13.5 kWh battery (Tesla Powerwall 3):

**Scenario A: Backup-only** — Battery sits idle except during outages. Revenue: $0/year (insurance value only).

**Scenario B: TOU arbitrage only** — Charge during off-peak ($0.25-0.30/kWh), discharge during peak ($0.45-0.55/kWh). Revenue: ~$200-400/year in bill savings.

**Scenario C: RA + VPP (post-SB 913)** — Battery enrolled in VPP, qualified for RA payments.
- Ava program: $3/mo/kWh × 13.5 kWh = $40.50/month = $486/year in participation payments
- PLUS TOU arbitrage on non-dispatched days
- PLUS emergency dispatch payments ($2/kWh × 10-15 events/year × ~10 kWh dispatched = $200-300/year)
- Total: $686-786/year

**Battery cost: ~$12,000-16,000 installed (after 30% ITC: $8,400-11,200)**
- Backup-only payback: Never (pure insurance)
- TOU-only payback: 21-56 years
- RA + VPP payback: 10.7-16.3 years
- With battery degradation factored: RA+VPP gets battery to ~60% payback over 10 years vs ~20% for TOU-only

## Strongest Counterargument
You're giving up dispatch control. When your battery is enrolled in a VPP with RA obligations, the aggregator (not you) decides when to discharge. If a grid emergency hits while you're also in a local blackout, your battery might dispatch to the grid rather than power your house. Ava's program requires 80% shared capacity from Powerwall owners. You installed backup for peace of mind — and now someone else controls 80% of it.

## Limitations
- RA payment rates are set by CPUC and fluctuate; the $3/kWh/month Ava figure is one program, not a market rate
- SB 913 hasn't passed yet — still in committee
- Tesla VPP payout data is self-reported by homeowners on Reddit/forums; no independent audit
- Delta Green data is European, different grid economics
- Battery cycle count from VPP dispatch accelerates degradation; warranty implications unclear
- Calculation assumes California NEM 2.0 rates, not NEM 3.0 (which drastically reduced export value)
