90% of Construction Workers Say AI Is Coming. The Industry Has Trained 1,500 of Them.

By Marcus Washington · May 3, 2026

A journeyman electrician in Orlando sat through a case study session at ABC Central Florida's Innovation and Technology Center last week. DEWALT had organized the session during National Apprenticeship Week, pairing apprentices with a senior virtual design and construction manager from a national firm who walked them through actual jobsite AI applications. Scheduling optimization, material takeoff automation, defect detection from site photos, all of it practical and hands-on and directly applicable to projects these workers would touch within months.

It was the first formal AI training any of them had received. In 2026.

Not because they were uninterested, and not because their employers considered it a waste of money, but because no structured program existed in their region until DEWALT built one as a pilot. And DEWALT's national survey of construction professionals, released April 23, explains why the pilot was necessary: only 8 percent of U.S. construction professionals currently use AI on the job, not "don't like it" or "haven't heard of it" but genuinely incorporated it into their daily work in any capacity. Everyone else is watching from the sideline, aware that something fundamental is shifting beneath the industry, unable to find a ladder into it.

8%
Share of U.S. construction professionals who currently use AI on the job, per the 2026 DEWALT AI in the Trades survey. 90% believe it will be indispensable within five years.

That 90 percent number deserves scrutiny. Ninety percent of an industry saying a technology will be indispensable within five years is not a forecast. It is a collective admission that the workforce knows it is unprepared and suspects the consequences will be severe. Eighty-eight percent expect adoption to increase over the next year alone. Eighty-three percent say AI will be standard within three years. And 87 percent want AI education embedded in trade schools and technical programs, because the current training infrastructure is a joke.

Where are workers actually learning? YouTube, cited by 40 percent of respondents as their primary AI education resource, followed by Coursera at 39 percent and video tutorials of any kind at 42 percent. Not employer-sponsored training, not union apprenticeship modules, not trade school curricula, but self-directed videos on personal time, squeezed between the commute home and dinner, teaching yourself a technology that 90 percent of your peers say will reshape your livelihood.

Arithmetic Nobody Wants to Do

BLS counts roughly 8.3 million construction workers in the United States. If 90 percent will need AI competency within five years, that is 7.47 million workers, and subtracting the 8 percent already using it leaves a gap of 6.8 million workers who need to go from zero to functional within 60 months, which works out to 1.36 million per year.

Now look at what exists to train them.

NABTU and Microsoft announced their expanded partnership on April 21, which has so far trained 1,500 instructors through hands-on training centers nationwide. Assume each instructor can train 20 to 30 workers per year, a generous estimate given that instruction is not their primary role. That yields 30,000 to 45,000 workers annually through what is currently the largest formal AI training program in the construction industry.

DEWALT launched its ABC Central Florida pilot and committed $75,000 to the Trimmer Construction Education Fund for AI-related grants that ABC chapters can apply for nationally. At $500 to $1,000 per worker in training costs, that covers 75 to 150 people.

DOL launched its AI Apprenticeship Portal on April 29, targeting 1 million total registered apprentices across all industries with $85 million in funding. Construction is one of many sectors. Even if construction captures 20 percent of the apprenticeship slots and half receive AI-specific training, that is 100,000 over the program's lifetime.

Add it all up optimistically, include every announced partnership, every pilot program, every federal initiative, and assume full enrollment, full capacity, no overlap between programs, and no attrition. Maybe 50,000 to 100,000 workers per year through every formal program combined.

~5%
Estimated share of annual AI training need covered by all current formal programs. Annual need: 1.36 million workers. Formal capacity: 50,000 to 100,000.

Formal programs cover roughly 4 to 7 percent of the annual need. Everyone else is on YouTube, building a skill set from algorithmic recommendations and 12-minute tutorials that may or may not match what their employer needs next quarter.

Everyone Agrees on Everything Except Who Pays

What makes this moment unusual is the unanimity of the diagnosis combined with the fragmentation of the response. Everyone agrees construction workers need AI training. Nobody agrees on who pays for it, who delivers it, or who issues the credential that proves a carpenter can use a scheduling optimizer.

NABTU and OpenAI announced their collaboration on March 11. Sam Altman himself showed up. "Ensuring the benefits of AI reach everyone starts with the workers who will build the infrastructure that powers it," he said. NABTU president Sean McGarvey pointed to the building trades' $2.5 billion annual training investment and 270 apprenticeship readiness programs across 34 states. Six weeks later, NABTU expanded a separate partnership with Microsoft to deliver free AI literacy courses with industry-recognized credentials through LinkedIn Learning at zero cost to workers, with Brad Smith calling for equipping "electricians, ironworkers, and pipefitters" with AI skills.

Three partnerships announced in seven weeks, involving two Fortune 50 tech companies, one major union federation, and one federal agency, with a combined training output that amounts to a rounding error against the need.

Where AI Actually Lives on the Jobsite

Among the 8 percent already using AI, applications skew heavily toward office work: 46 percent report exploring AI in site operations and monitoring, the same percentage for planning and design, and 41 percent apply it to estimation, procurement, and supply chain.

These are project managers, estimators, and VDC coordinators, workers who spend their days in front of screens rather than behind nail guns. Early adopters report real benefits: 35 percent cite productivity gains, 34 percent cost savings, 35 percent improved quality control. But these results are self-reported by a self-selected group that chose to explore AI independently, which makes them about as representative of the broader workforce as a Tesla owner is of the average American commuter.

Randstad's April analysis captures the demand-side picture with uncomfortable precision. Since late 2022, demand for robotics technicians has spiked 107 percent, HVAC engineer demand has risen 67 percent, and general construction roles are up 30 percent. AI is not replacing construction workers. It is creating ferocious demand for workers who can build the data centers, energy plants, and transmission lines that AI infrastructure requires. Randstad reports a "labor flip" in which skilled trades now take 56 days to fill versus 54 for knowledge workers. For the first time in decades, the person swinging a hammer is harder to find than the person writing the memo about the hammer.

Resume Now's 2026 rankings, built on Lightcast Workforce Risk Outlook data, place construction fifth among the least-prepared industries for AI workforce disruption. Behind hospitality, healthcare, financial services, and logistics. Not because construction workers are less capable, but because the industry has historically underinvested in training infrastructure and now faces the dual burden of a chronic labor shortage that devours every available dollar and an AI transition that demands investment in the workers it cannot find.

Why This Might Not Matter as Fast as Surveys Suggest

Maybe 90 percent is aspirational, not predictive.

Survey respondents who call AI "indispensable within five years" are expressing a belief shaped by headlines, not a forecast built from project data. "Indispensable" is a loaded word in a survey designed by a tool company that sells to construction professionals, and when 90 percent of an industry says a technology is imminent while 8 percent have adopted it, one plausible reading is that adoption will surge. Another equally plausible reading is that people are reliably terrible at predicting their own technology futures and that construction's 8 percent rate represents not a lagging indicator but a reasonable equilibrium for an industry where the core work remains stubbornly, irreducibly physical.

A framer does not need AI to frame a wall. A roofer does not need AI to nail shingles. Residential construction's AI-relevant tasks concentrate in estimation, scheduling, quality verification, and design, which together account for a fraction of total labor hours on a typical home build. If "indispensable" means every worker on site needs AI literacy, the data does not support that timeline. If it means every company needs at least one AI-capable person, 664,000 workers starts to look less alarming.

Nobody knows which interpretation is correct. And the training programs being designed right now do not know either, because they are being built without a clear definition of what competency they are training toward. An electrician who can prompt ChatGPT for a load calculation lives in a different universe from a VDC coordinator who deploys computer vision for progress tracking, and both are "using AI on the job."

What This Means for Your Build

If you are hiring a builder: ask whether their team has formal AI training, or whether their superintendent uses AI tools for scheduling and quality control. Not because AI guarantees a better outcome, but because builders who invest in training tend to retain their crews, and crew stability matters more to your project quality than any individual technology.

If you run a crew: free Microsoft/NABTU courses on LinkedIn Learning are the lowest-friction starting point available right now. No cost, industry-recognized credentials, designed for tradespeople rather than software engineers. DOL's Apprenticeship Innovation Portal at apprenticeship.gov maintains a directory of programs integrating AI skills. DEWALT's ABC partnership is accepting applications for Trimmer grants that fund AI education at the chapter level.

If you run a trade school: 87 percent of your future graduates are telling you they want AI in the curriculum, their employers agree, federal funding exists, and private-sector partnerships are available. Schools that move first will recruit stronger students, place more graduates, and produce the workers that 90 percent of the industry says it will need within the decade. Schools that wait will discover that YouTube has eaten their value proposition.

What This Analysis Did Not Measure

DEWALT's survey is funded by Stanley Black & Decker, a tool company with financial interest in technology adoption narratives. Survey methodology, sample size, and selection criteria have not been publicly released. "Currently use AI" is not defined in the published results, and the threshold for what counts could range from running ChatGPT for email drafts to deploying computer vision for defect detection. BLS workforce figures include roles from laborers to project executives, and AI relevance varies enormously across them. Training capacity estimates in this analysis are rough, based on published program descriptions rather than enrollment data, because none of these programs publish enrollment data. Our 4-to-7 percent coverage figure assumes all announced programs are fully operational and at capacity, which they are not, meaning actual coverage is likely lower. Randstad's demand data covers all construction, not residential specifically, and data center construction demand dynamics differ substantially from single-family homebuilding.
