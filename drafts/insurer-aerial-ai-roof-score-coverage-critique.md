## 1. GENERAL CRITIC (Editorial Quality)
**Score: 9.0/10**

Strong opening with the Linda Bennett anecdote — specific, named, sourced (ABC7), with concrete dollar figures ($28K). Logical flow from anecdote → industry context → how it works → what fails → regulatory response → builder implications → actionable advice → counterargument. Headline is punchy and passes the curiosity test. Article passes the kill test: directly helps someone building (what to design for) or buying (how to challenge aerial AI decisions) a home.

Minor weaknesses: Could use one more concrete builder case study rather than hypothetical design implications. The word "insurer" appears frequently — slight repetition.

**Verdict: PASS**

## 2. VOICE CRITIC (Catherine Chen Consistency)
**Score: 8.8/10**

Catherine's voice is "sharp, analytical legal/policy journalism" that "translates building codes, zoning laws, and regulatory frameworks into readable prose." This article nails that: it walks through AB 75's specific provisions (30-day notification, 45-day image freshness), names the legislative author (Calderon), the Commissioner (Lara), and cites specific regulatory actions from PA, CT, MA, and NAIC. ## 3. ETHICS CRITIC
**Score: 9.0/10**

Article presents the technology's legitimate actuarial value (2.5x claims frequency for poor roofs) alongside its failures (misidentified shadows, outdated images). Commissioner Lara's complaint about "erroneous data" is balanced against CAPE's real loss-data findings. No demonization of insurers — frames the problem as a transparency and accountability gap, not a conspiracy. Named sources on both sides. Strongest counterargument addressed directly in final section.

**Verdict: PASS**

## 6. RIGOR CRITIC (Scholarly Standards)
**Score: 8.6/10**

**Original contribution:** Connecting aerial AI insurance scoring to residential design decisions — an angle no existing publication has covered. Novel and defensible.

**Methodology transparency:** Data sources clearly attributed (CAPE press releases, Eagleview/CompassData benchmark, ABC7 reporting, state regulatory documents). No unattributed statistics.

**Limitations acknowledged:** Final section explicitly states no published study quantifies false-positive rates nationally, no independent peer-reviewed validation of condition assessment accuracy (vs. measurement accuracy), and that the uncertainty falls on homeowners. This is honest.

**Strongest counterargument:** Addressed directly — the 2.5x actuarial differential is real, aerial inspection has legitimate value, and the problem is transparency not the technology itself.

**Verifiability:** CAPE Analytics data (Moody's company), Eagleview's CompassData benchmark (June 2025), CA Commissioner press release (March 28, 2025), PA bulletin (2026), all verifiable via named sources.

Minor gap: The "15 states" pricing approval claim could use a direct hyperlink to CAPE's filing documentation.

**Verdict: PASS**

## 7. DATA PRESENTATION CRITIC
**Score: 8.7/10**

Two stat callouts:
1. "2.5×" claims frequency differential — clean, well-labeled, sourced to carrier loss-year data
2. "300+" approved filings — clean, well-labeled, contextualizes the scale of adoption
