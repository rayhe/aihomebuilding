# Research: AI Payroll Tools and Wage Theft in Residential Construction

## Thesis
AI-powered payroll and scheduling tools promise to modernize construction payment. Instead, many are making wage theft harder to detect — splitting workers across shell entities, algorithmically avoiding overtime triggers, and automating misclassification. The same AI that could catch $15B+ in annual wage theft is being used by dishonest contractors to scale it.

## Kill Test
✅ Helps homeowners: They're often liable when their contractor steals wages (joint employer doctrine, California's AB 1701). Underpaid workers do worse quality work. Stolen wages = hidden subsidy in your bid price.

## Novel Angle
Most coverage frames AI payroll tools as efficiency gains. Nobody is writing about how these same tools — entity splitting, algorithmic scheduling, automated 1099 classification — are being weaponized to scale wage theft in residential construction. The California $2.3M case (Oct 2025) shows the pattern: multiple pay stubs from different entities, same supervisor. That's not manual fraud anymore — it's software-assisted.

## Actionable Takeaways
- Homeowners: Ask your GC for payroll records, not just lien waivers. Joint employer liability is real.
- Builders: AI compliance tools exist (certified payroll, prevailing wage monitoring) — use them proactively
- Workers: Document everything. Take photos of job site, screenshot scheduling apps, keep own hour records.
- States: California AB 1701 model — hold project owners liable for subcontractor wage theft
