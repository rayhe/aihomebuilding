# Research: Construction's Treadmill Effect — Your Builder's Crew Won't Be the Same Crew That Finishes Your House

## Journalist: Marcus "Steel" Washington — Workforce & Labor

## Angle
The construction industry's labor shortage isn't about not having enough workers. It's about not keeping them. New Bridgit data from 114,000+ workers shows a 20.7% annual attrition rate that erases nearly all hiring gains — what they call the "treadmill effect." For homeowners, this means the crew that breaks ground on your project may not be the same crew that finishes it. And the replacements? Over a third of them have been on the job less than a year.

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? YES. This tells homeowners:
- Ask your GC about crew stability and turnover
- Understand why your project timeline keeps slipping
- Why the quality of work varies between framing and finishing
- What "labor shortage" actually means for YOUR specific build

## Primary Sources

### 1. Bridgit 2026 Construction Workforce Benchmark Report
- Source: Construction Dive / gobridgit.com (March 31, 2026)
- Data: 233 contractors, 114,000+ professionals, ~40% of ENR 400
- Key findings:
  - 46% of contractors achieved ZERO net headcount growth in 2025
  - Industry median attrition rate: 18.7%
  - Average attrition rate: 20.7%
  - A contractor targeting 100 net new hires with 20% attrition must hire 125; at 35% attrition, 154
  - Senior Project Managers: 3.6% attrition, 5.6-year median tenure
  - Junior PMs/Superintendents: attrition >2x senior, median tenure 3.7-3.8 years
  - "Rookie ratio" (team members with <1 year tenure): average 36.4%
  - Large teams (51+): rookie ratio ~56%
  - Small teams (3-5): ~25% rookie ratio
  - ENR Top 50 plan 6.8 years ahead vs 4.7 for others
  - Industrial/Manufacturing starts surged 68.1% YoY
  - Solar starts FELL 44.4%

### 2. CURT / Clark Lowe analysis — "Why Your Best Workers Keep Quitting"
- Source: Construction Users Roundtable (CURT), August 2025
- Key insight: Workers don't quit for pay — they quit for conditions
  - Disorganized projects, overwhelming overtime, no career investment
  - "Workers don't want to be hired onto a sinking ship"
  - Newly hired workers quit as fast as predecessors — "never-ending cycle"
  - 454,000+ positions vacant in 2025
  - Industry must find "middle ground" on healthcare, predictable schedules, family benefits
  - Younger workers want careers aligned with values + work-life balance

### 3. Randstad USA Skilled Trades Report (March 2026)
- Source: ConstructConnect / Construction Dive
- 150 million+ U.S. job postings analyzed (2022-2026)
- Time-to-hire for skilled trades: 56 days (vs 54 for desk-based professionals) — "labor flip"
- Robotics technicians: vacancies up 113%
- HVAC engineers: demand up 78%
- Electricians/welders/construction: up 30%
- For every 100 young workers entering trades, 102 are exiting (net -1.72%/year)
- Manufacturing losing workers faster than replacing

### 4. Housing market context
- Single-family housing starts April 2026: 930,000 SAAR, down 9% from March, down 2.4% YoY (Census Bureau)
- Housing deficit: ~1.5 million homes (industry estimate)
- Skilled labor shortage costs homebuilders $10.8B/year, prevents 19,000 homes/year (HBI)
- 723,000 workers needed annually to replace retirements + meet demand (NAHB/BLS)
- Residential worker average hourly wage: $39.40 vs broader construction: $39.70 (HBI)
- Construction added only 15,000 jobs in all of 2025 — worst since 2011 (ENR)

### 5. ABC Construction Employment Data
- Construction employment fell 11,000 in December 2025
- 349,000 net new workers projected needed for 2026 (ABC)
- Private construction spending down 0.6% MoM in January 2026
- Only data centers growing (+31.3% YoY)

## Original Contribution
Calculate the expected crew turnover on a typical residential project. If a homeowner's $400K addition uses a 10-person crew over 6 months, and the GC's attrition rate is the industry median 18.7%, how many of those 10 workers will still be on the job at closeout? What does that crew instability mean for defects, rework, and punch lists?

Math: 18.7% annual attrition = ~9.7% over 6 months (prorated). On a 10-person crew, expect 1 departure. At the higher-end 35% attrition? 18.5% over 6 months = nearly 2 workers gone. On a 20-person crew for a larger project, 2-4 workers gone mid-build. And replacements carry rookie ratio — 36% have <1 year on the job.

But residential attrition is likely HIGHER than industry median (shorter projects, less stable employment, lower wages). If residential runs 25-30%, you're looking at 13-16% over 6 months on a 10-person crew.

## Skepticism
- Bridgit's data skews toward larger ENR contractors, not the small 5-person residential GCs
- Residential attrition rates not specifically broken out in the Bridgit report
- Some churn is healthy — removing poor fits
- Not all projects use the same crew start-to-finish (subs rotate by trade anyway)

## Strongest Counterargument
Residential construction has ALWAYS run on subcontractor rotation — framers hand off to electricians hand off to plumbers. "Crew turnover" is partly just how the industry works. The real question isn't whether faces change, but whether *within each trade* the same skilled workers show up consistently. The Bridgit data measures contractor-level attrition, not trade-crew stability on individual projects. A homeowner's experience depends more on their GC's relationship with reliable subs than on industry-wide attrition stats.
