## Overall Assessment

The revised article is substantially improved. ## Evaluation by Criterion

### 1. TECHNICAL ACCURACY (8.5/10)

**What's right:**
- The carrying cost calculation is clean and verifiable. $75K lot × 60% LTV × 9% ÷ 12 = $337.50/month interest, which the article rounds to "roughly $340." That checks out.
- The $600–$1,000/month total carrying cost range is reasonable when you add property taxes ($100–300/month), vacant land insurance, and opportunity cost to the base interest figure.
- Permit fee ranges ($1,500–$3,000 nationally, $4,000+ in LA, $5,370 in Oregon) are in the right ballpark. Permit costs vary enormously by jurisdiction and home value, but these figures are defensible.
- The 6% average price cut on a $499,500 home = $29,970 is straightforward arithmetic, correctly calculated.

**Minor concerns:**
- The "9% acquisition and development rate" is described as "close to where AD&C loans sit right now." NAHB's own Q1 2026 AD&C survey shows *contract* rates at 7.2–7.5%, while *effective* rates (including points) run 9.8–11.0%. The article's 9% is actually between the two — slightly high for a contract rate, slightly low for an effective rate. The carrying cost estimate is therefore conservative rather than aggressive, which is actually the more responsible editorial choice. Not a factual error, but a more precise builder would specify contract vs. effective rate.
- Permit expiration "six to twelve months" is correct as a general range, though many jurisdictions now default to 12 months with one 12-month extension. The statement is accurate as written.

### 2. INDUSTRY REALISM (8.5/10)

**What's right:**
- The opening scene is the most realistic part of the article. The cold coffee, the rate-qualifying mental math, the drive to the next site where you're sure — that's Tuesday for a lot of builders.
- The Berner quote about treating permits as option contracts is an excellent framing. Production builders do exactly this, especially in uncertain rate environments.
- The crew scheduling mention in the counterargument is well-integrated and accurate: "losing your framer to another builder's pipeline is a six-month setback no spreadsheet captures." In my experience, it can be even longer — good framers in hot markets have 4–6 month backlogs. The point stands.
- The distinction between the commercial AI stack ($50M projects, six-person dev teams) and the residential reality ($500K spec home, one builder, binary decision) is genuinely insightful.

**What could be stronger:**
- The article still primarily describes the small-to-mid custom/spec builder (8–15 homes/year). But a significant portion of those 144,000 idle permits belong to production builders (DR Horton, Lennar, Meritage, NVR) doing 50–500+ homes per division. Their go/no-go process is fundamentally different: dedicated land acquisition departments, rolling option contracts with scheduled takedowns, and quarterly start pacing models driven by corporate finance teams — not a guy on a lot with cold coffee. The article doesn't need to become a treatise on production builder operations, but one sentence acknowledging that the largest builders have more sophisticated (if still imperfect) internal processes would sharpen the thesis. Right now the article implies the *entire* 144,000-permit gap is driven by data-poor decision-making, when a meaningful chunk is actually strategic pipeline management by large public builders.
- **Lot option contracts vs. owned lots** still not distinguished. For production builders, the carrying cost on optioned lots isn't $600–$1,000/month — it's the option deposit (5–15% of lot price) at risk of forfeiture if the option expires without a takedown. This is a categorically different financial exposure than the owned-lot scenario the article models. For the custom builder audience this article primarily addresses, owned lots are more common, so the carrying cost model is valid for its target reader. But the omission means the $1.3B aggregate figure is likely overstated, since some of those 144,000 permits sit on optioned land with different economics.

### 3. MARKET KNOWLEDGE (8.5/10)

**What's right:**
- HMI at 34, fifteenth month below 40, longest streak since 2012 — all correct per the latest NAHB/Reuters data.
- 37% cutting prices, 63% offering incentives — matches July HMI data.
- Single-family starts at 895,000 SAAR, permits at 871,000 — correct per recent Census data.
- The "build-to-order" strategic pivot is a real and significant trend that most trade publications haven't covered well. Good catch.

**What could be sharper:**
- The $75,000–$91,000 lot value range is sourced from NAHB's national construction cost survey, which shows a national average finished lot cost of ~$91K. That's accurate as a national figure. However, the markets where most spec activity and permit stalling occurs — DFW, Phoenix, Raleigh, Tampa, Nashville, Boise — typically have finished lot values of $100K–$175K+ in active subdivisions. Using the national average understates the carrying cost problem in exactly the markets where it matters most. The article's limitations section partially covers this ("actual carrying costs vary enormously by market"), which is responsible. A parenthetical noting that Sun Belt growth market lots often run significantly higher would strengthen the argument without undermining the methodology.
- The $499,500 average price is cited as "the Census Bureau's January 2026 average." This is the national average sale price for new homes. Median would arguably be more representative (~$418K per recent Census data), since average is skewed by luxury builds. Minor point.

### 4. PRACTICAL VALUE (9/10)

This is where the article genuinely shines. The "What This Means If You're Standing on the Lot" section provides concrete, actionable guidance:
- Track carrying cost per lot per month (most builders genuinely don't)
- Pull weekly permit activity from county open data portals
- Cross-reference with MLS days-on-market for new construction
- Check FRED mortgage rate data against qualification thresholds

This is real advice a builder could act on Monday morning. The FRED link is provided. The county data portal suggestion is accurate — most jurisdictions do publish this, though the data quality and update frequency varies enormously. The article correctly frames this as "a crude model" that would "beat the current process," which is honest and realistic rather than overselling.

### 5. MISSING PERSPECTIVES (7.5/10)

**Addressed since first review:**
- ✅ Trade scheduling/crew availability as go/no-go factor — well-integrated
- ✅ Permit expiration as forcing function — properly placed

**Still absent but less critical:**
- Land banking strategies by larger builders (strategic holds that aren't "indecision")
- The role of municipal utility connection timelines (can force starts or delays independent of market conditions)
- Bond and subdivision improvement obligations that sometimes trigger starts regardless of demand
- Seasonal construction windows in northern markets (you start when the ground thaws, not when the spreadsheet says so)
- The surety bonding perspective — builders with active performance bonds on infrastructure may need to start lots to meet completion obligations

These are "would make it better" items, not "must fix" items. The article is already 1,700+ words and covering the ground it chose to cover well. Not every missing perspective is an omission; some are editorial scope decisions.

## Remaining Issues

1. **Production builder gap.** The article's thesis ("the go/no-go decision has no data behind it") is true for small/mid custom builders but less true for large production builders who have internal analytics teams and pacing models. One sentence acknowledging this would prevent the thesis from being dismissable by the audience segment that builds the most homes.
2. **Lot option economics omitted.** The carrying cost model assumes owned lots. Option-controlled lots have different economics (deposit at risk vs. monthly bleed). This affects the aggregate $1.3B estimate.
3. **AD&C rate precision.** "9% acquisition and development rate" is in the right neighborhood but sits between contract rates (~7.3%) and effective rates (~10%). Specifying which would be more precise.

---

## Suggestions

1. Add one sentence in the carrying cost section noting that lot values run higher in the Sun Belt growth markets where most spec construction occurs ($100K–$175K vs. the $75K–$91K national average), which would increase the per-lot carrying cost proportionally.
2. In the limitations paragraph, add a clause noting that the carrying cost model assumes builder-owned lots and doesn't account for lot option contract structures common among production builders.
3. Consider changing "a 9% acquisition and development rate" to "an effective acquisition and development rate around 9–10%" to be more precise about what the number represents.
