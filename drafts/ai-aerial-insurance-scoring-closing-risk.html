An Insurer Dropped Your Buyer Using a Satellite Photo. California Had to Pass a Law About It.

By Catherine Chen · May 17, 2026

A builder in suburban Dallas finished a $480,000 home last September. Slab-on-grade, composition roof, three-car garage, everything to code, everything inspected. Three days before closing, the buyer's insurance carrier issued a non-renewal notice on the binder. Not a rate increase, but a flat refusal to insure.

Reason cited: "roof condition." That roof was four months old.

What happened, according to the buyer's agent who later pieced together the timeline, was that CAPE Analytics had generated a Roof Condition Rating from aerial imagery captured before construction finished. The algorithm scored the property based on an image showing exposed sheathing and partial shingle coverage, which is what a roof looks like mid-installation, and the underwriting system treated that score as current. Nobody at the carrier flagged the image date, and nobody called the builder. The closing delayed 22 days while the buyer scrambled for replacement coverage.

Twenty-two days on a $480,000 home. The builder's carrying costs alone ran north of $2,800.

How Your Roof Gets Graded Without a Ladder

Three companies dominate the AI aerial scoring market for residential insurance. CAPE Analytics processes geospatial imagery to generate property-level risk attributes, including its Roof Condition Rating, which grades roofs on a scale that underwriters can plug directly into pricing and renewal decisions. Nearmap captures high-resolution aerial imagery and layers AI analysis on top, scoring everything from roof material and age to vegetation encroachment and lot-level hazards. In early 2026, Zurich North America integrated Nearmap's AI-enhanced imagery and roof scoring directly into its underwriting workflow through the Betterview platform, making satellite-derived risk scores a standard input for middle-market property decisions. EagleView, the oldest player, launched its Horizon AI engine built on more than 25 years of verified property data, and its drone-based Assess tool has replaced physical roof inspections for carriers like Hastings Mutual.

Nobody at these companies sells directly to builders or homeowners. They sell to carriers. The property owner finds out about the score when something goes wrong.

In aggregate, this logic is sound. A carrier that can score every roof in its portfolio from imagery rather than sending adjusters to 50,000 homes saves millions in inspection costs while catching deterioration patterns that field visits miss because adjusters see one roof once every three to five years while the satellite sees every roof multiple times per year. CAPE claims its Roof Condition Rating reduces claims frequency for properties with favorable scores. Nearmap's integration with Zurich showed measurable improvement in risk classification accuracy during their pilot. The economics are obvious and the adoption curve is steep.

The problem is what happens at the edges, which is where individual homeowners and their builders live.

A Dozen Complaints in Texas Alone

Since 2023, at least a dozen Texas homeowners have filed complaints after their coverage was non-renewed or their premiums spiked based on aerial imagery assessments, according to reporting by NPR affiliate KWBU and subsequent investigations by public radio stations across the state. The pattern is consistent: the homeowner receives a non-renewal notice, requests an explanation, and is shown an aerial image they have never seen before that purportedly documents a condition on their property. In several cases, homeowners disputed the accuracy of the images, arguing that the photographs showed conditions on neighboring properties, debris that had since been cleared, or construction activity that was misinterpreted as damage.

At its core, this is a transparency problem: the homeowner has no input into the scoring process, no advance notice that their property is being evaluated, and in most states no right to see the specific image that produced the adverse decision. The carrier's underwriting system ingests the score as a data point alongside a hundred others, and when the composite risk profile crosses a threshold, the system generates a non-renewal. A human may review the decision, or may not.

Florida's non-renewal rates have climbed to 279% above baseline in the most climate-vulnerable ZIP codes, according to Insurify. Not all of that increase is driven by AI scoring. Much of it is driven by reinsurance repricing, catastrophe modeling, and carriers simply exiting markets where they cannot write profitable business. But the aerial imagery layer adds a new mechanism: the ability to non-renew a specific property based on a condition the homeowner may not know about, may not agree with, and may not have any opportunity to contest before the decision is final.

Two Bills, One State, and the Transparency Problem

California now has two bills attempting to regulate how insurers use aerial imagery. Assembly Bill 75, which passed the State Senate in July 2025, requires carriers to provide at least 30 days' advance notice before conducting aerial imaging inspections of a policyholder's property and mandates that homeowners can request copies of the images used. California Insurance Commissioner Ricardo Lara endorsed the bill, and the California Association of Realtors supported it. A second bill, AB 1559, goes further by requiring insurers to disclose the specific aerial images used in any underwriting decision, giving homeowners the ability to challenge assessments based on outdated or inaccurate imagery.

Pennsylvania requires 60 days' notice, but most other states require nothing at all.

A Troutman Pepper Locke analysis of the emerging regulatory landscape identified three recurring themes: regulators want insurers to validate image quality before acting on AI-derived scores, to distinguish material risks from cosmetic issues, and to provide consumer dispute mechanisms when adverse actions are taken based on imagery. But the analysis also noted that most state insurance departments have issued guidance rather than binding rules, leaving compliance effectively voluntary.

What This Actually Costs a Builder

Mortgage lenders require proof of homeowner's insurance before funding. No insurance, no closing. If a buyer's coverage gets pulled or denied based on an aerial score, the closing timeline stretches until the buyer finds an alternative carrier, which in states like Florida and California can take weeks. In some wildfire-zone and hurricane-zone markets, replacement coverage may only be available through a state-backed plan of last resort at substantially higher premiums, which can push the buyer's monthly payment above their debt-to-income ratio and kill the deal entirely.

For a builder carrying a $650,000 finished home, each month of delayed closing costs roughly $2,700 to $4,200 in financing, property tax, insurance, and maintenance. That number assumes a construction loan at current rates in the 8.5% range, annual property tax at 1.1% of assessed value, and $200 per month in utility and maintenance costs to keep a vacant house sale-ready. Over a 30-day delay, those costs eat directly into margin. Over 60 days, they can reduce net profit on a typical spec home by 15 to 25 percent.

I have not found a published study quantifying how many residential closings have been delayed or killed specifically because of AI-derived aerial scoring, and the carriers and technology providers do not publish false positive rates for their roof condition ratings. This is a data gap that matters, because the anecdotal evidence from Texas is enough to establish that the problem exists while telling us almost nothing about its prevalence. It could affect one in a thousand closings. It could affect one in fifty. Nobody outside the carriers knows, and the carriers have no incentive to find out.

New Construction Should Win This Game

In theory, new homes are the easiest properties for aerial AI to score favorably. Brand-new roof, clean lot, modern materials, no deferred maintenance. A builder delivering a completed home in 2026 should sail through any algorithmic assessment.

In practice, three things go wrong. First, image timing: the aerial databases are updated on varying schedules, and a property captured during construction will show exposed sheathing, staging materials, or graded-but-unfinished lots that an algorithm trained on completed structures may misclassify. That Dallas case is not an outlier. Every home under construction looks damaged from above. Second, context scoring: newer systems like Nearmap's don't just score the roof. They score vegetation proximity, neighboring property conditions, and lot-level hazards like slope and drainage patterns. A new home built adjacent to a mature oak with overhanging branches may receive a lower composite score regardless of its own condition, and the builder has no mechanism to contest that score before it reaches an underwriter. Third, material classification errors: metal roofs, which are increasingly popular in fire-prone and hail-prone markets precisely because they reduce risk, can be misclassified by algorithms trained predominantly on asphalt shingle imagery, producing a score that penalizes the exact upgrade a safety-conscious builder installed.

Some production builders with the scale to negotiate directly with carriers have begun pre-coordinating insurance during the planning phase rather than leaving it to the buyer at closing. D.R. Horton and Lennar, the two largest U.S. homebuilders, both offer embedded insurance products through their mortgage affiliates, which sidesteps the aerial scoring problem by placing the policy before the algorithm has a chance to intervene. Custom builders and small-volume spec builders do not have this option.

What a Builder Can Do Right Now

Document your roof from above before the buyer applies for insurance. A timestamped drone photo showing the completed roof in good condition, taken after final inspection and before closing, gives the buyer's agent ammunition to dispute an adverse aerial score. Cost: a 20-minute flight with a DJI Mini 4 Pro, which most builders or their surveyors already own, and a dated file uploaded to the transaction folder.

Coordinate with your buyer's insurance agent early. The standard practice of leaving insurance to the last two weeks before closing leaves no buffer if the AI flags something. Moving the insurance application to 45 or 60 days before close gives time to identify and contest any aerial-derived issue before it becomes a closing emergency.

Check whether your state has adopted disclosure requirements. California's AB 75 and AB 1559, if fully enacted and enforced, will at least ensure homeowners can see the images being used against their properties. Pennsylvania's 60-day notice requirement provides a longer contestation window. In states without these protections, the buyer's first indication that their property was scored from space may be the non-renewal letter in the mailbox, and by that point the closing is already in jeopardy.

What This Article Doesn't Know

I don't have false positive rates for any of the three major aerial scoring providers, because none publish them. I don't have data on how many new-construction closings have been affected, because nobody tracks that metric. The Dallas anecdote came through an agent, not a court filing, and I cannot independently verify the timeline. CAPE Analytics, Nearmap, and EagleView did not respond to inquiries for this article. AB 75 passed the California Senate in July 2025, but its implementation timeline and enforcement mechanisms are not yet finalized. The U.S. Treasury report on homeowner insurance identified ZIP code-level trends in non-renewals but did not isolate the contribution of aerial imagery scoring to those trends.

What I can say with confidence is that the technology exists, the carriers are adopting it at scale, the regulatory framework is not keeping pace, and the individual builder or buyer who gets caught in a false positive has almost no recourse that doesn't involve weeks of delay and thousands of dollars in carrying costs.

An algorithm that scores a million roofs correctly and flags ten thousand incorrectly has a 99% accuracy rate. That is an excellent number if you are a carrier managing portfolio risk. It is a catastrophic number if you are one of the ten thousand, your buyer just lost coverage, and the closing is in three days.
