Your Sewer Lateral Costs $7,000 to Replace. An Algorithm Knows When It Will Fail. Nobody Sells It to Homeowners.

July 23, 2026

The plumber pulled the camera out of the cleanout and tilted the screen toward Diane Reyes. She'd bought the three-bedroom ranch in Arvada, Colorado, fourteen months earlier. The sewer scope hadn't been part of the deal. Now the screen showed Orangeburg pipe, bituminized fiber installed sometime in the 1960s, crushed flat in two spots, roots poking through a third. Replacement quote: $11,400 from house to main, including the section under the public sidewalk that Denver's rules say is her problem.

"I would have asked for a credit," she told me. "Or walked."

She didn't know. Her agent didn't know. The home inspector noted that the toilets flushed and moved on.

Here is what a machine learning model published three months before Diane's purchase could have told her, using only the pipe's age, material, and GPS coordinates: the probability of a structural defect in that segment was north of 0.75.

The Pipe Nobody Talks About

Every house connected to a municipal sewer system has a lateral, the private pipe running from the building's plumbing to the public main in the street. In most American cities, the homeowner owns and maintains the entire lateral, including the portion buried under the city's sidewalk and right-of-way. Denver spells it out plainly: your responsibility extends "from the home's plumbing all the way to the connection at the city main."

When that pipe fails, the homeowner pays. National averages land between $1,388 and $5,323 for a straightforward replacement, according to HomeAdvisor. In Denver, the going rate is $150 to $300 per linear foot. In New York City, labor alone runs $150 to $500 an hour. Add under-slab trenching at $150 to $200 per foot and you're looking at a project that routinely crosses $10,000 for a 50-foot lateral in a city with a deep frost line.

And the lateral is the pipe most likely to fail. It's smaller-diameter, typically four to six inches, which the research consistently flags as a risk factor. It's shallow enough for tree roots to find. It was installed by the home's original builder, not the city's engineering department, with whatever materials were cheap at the time: cast iron, vitrified clay, Orangeburg, early PVC with solvent-welded joints that weaken over decades.

The U.S. has approximately 2.1 million kilometers of sewer infrastructure. The EPA logs roughly 75,000 sanitary sewer overflows per year. Those numbers describe the municipal side. Nobody aggregates the private lateral failures because nobody tracks them.

The Algorithm That Already Exists

In February 2026, a team led by Seng and colleagues published a study in Water Environment Research that trained four decision-tree-based ML models on CCTV inspection data from two municipal utilities. The best performer, a LightGBM model with cost-sensitive learning, achieved an AUC-ROC of 0.79 and an AUC-PR of 0.62 for predicting both infiltration and structural defects at the individual pipe level.

That's not a coin flip. An AUC-ROC of 0.79 means the model correctly ranks a defective pipe above a non-defective one 79% of the time, using nothing but the pipe's attributes.

The SHAP analysis (a technique for explaining which features drive the model's decisions) identified two dominant predictors: pipe location and pipe age. Not exotic sensor data. Not real-time flow monitoring. Location and age. Both of which exist in every city's GIS database.

A 2022 study in Ålesund, Norway, went further. Extra Trees Regression achieved R² = 0.90 using twenty input factors: ten physical (age, diameter, depth, slope, length, material, pipe type, form, network type, connection type) and ten environmental (rainfall, geology, landslide area, population density, land use, building area, groundwater depth, traffic volume, distance to road, soil type). Sewer material and age, again, dominated the results.

A Wisconsin study from early 2025 trained artificial neural networks on reinforced concrete pipe data and hit R² = 0.9066, outperforming multiple linear regression's 0.8474. The key predictors: pipe length, age, and diameter. Depth, soil type, and segment showed minimal influence in that specific dataset, though the authors noted those factors matter more in other regions.

A Spanish study applied logistic regression with a genetic algorithm and found that annually replacing just 4% of pipe segments, specifically those whose estimated failure probability exceeded 0.75, prevented roughly 30% of unexpected failures. Vitrified clay pipes and smaller diameters were the most failure-prone. Sound familiar? That describes millions of residential laterals.

Every Model Was Built for the City

Not one of these models was built for a homeowner.

Every training dataset came from municipal CCTV inspection programs. Every deployment target was a public works department deciding which mainlines to rehabilitate first. The output is a prioritized capital improvement plan for a utility with 10,000 pipe segments and a $40 million annual budget.

The homeowner, who owns the lateral that's statistically more likely to fail than the main it connects to, gets nothing.

What they get instead is a $200 to $500 CCTV scope, a camera crawled through the pipe by a NASSCO-certified technician who codes every crack, offset joint, and root intrusion against a standardized defect grading system (LACP for laterals, BSDI for building sewers and drains). That scope tells you what the pipe looks like today. A LightGBM model trained on the same kind of data could tell you what it's likely to look like in five years. The scope is reactive. The model is predictive. The difference is the difference between an X-ray and a prognosis.

The data to feed a residential prediction model already exists. City and county records contain pipe installation dates. GIS databases map pipe materials and routing. The USDA's SSURGO dataset provides soil type at any GPS coordinate in the country. Aerial imagery reveals tree canopy proximity. Rainfall and groundwater data are public. Most of the twenty features from the Ålesund study are sitting in public databases right now.

The Point-of-Sale Moment Nobody Uses

California is accelerating the opportunity. A growing number of cities and counties now require a NASSCO LACP-certified sewer lateral inspection before a property can change hands. The inspection itself is standardized. The reporting format is standardized. The defect codes are standardized. What isn't standardized, what doesn't exist, is a predictive layer that takes those inspection results and the pipe's attributes and outputs a failure probability over the next five, ten, or twenty years.

Think about what a buyer could do with that number. Not a pass/fail from a scope. A probability curve. This lateral is 67 years old, vitrified clay, in expansive soil, with moderate root intrusion at two joints. Based on 48,000 similar laterals in the training set, the five-year failure probability is 0.41. Ten-year: 0.73. Budget $8,000 to $12,000 within the decade, or negotiate the credit now.

That tool could exist today. The math is published. The data is available. The market is 130 million single-family homes.

Why It Doesn't Exist Yet

Three barriers, none of them technical.

First, the training data problem. Municipal models train on CCTV inspections of public mains. Private lateral inspections are sporadic, inconsistent, and rarely aggregated. The California point-of-sale programs are generating lateral inspection data at scale for the first time, but that data sits in local agency databases, not in a centralized repository an ML researcher can access. NASSCO's certification programs (BSDI for building sewers, LACP for laterals) provide the coding standards but not the dataset.

Second, the business model gap. The customer for a sewer prediction model is either a homebuyer (one-time transaction) or a homeowner (infrequent, reactive). Compare that to a municipal utility that inspects thousands of pipes per year and has a capital budget that depends on prioritization. The utility is a recurring customer with a procurement department. The homeowner is a person who doesn't think about their sewer lateral until sewage backs up into their basement.

Third, liability. If a model predicts low failure probability and the pipe collapses six months later, who's responsible? The researchers behind the published models are careful to frame their results as decision-support tools, not warranties. A consumer-facing product would need to navigate that exposure, probably through the same kind of disclaimer language that home inspection reports already use: "This assessment is based on observed conditions at the time of inspection and does not guarantee future performance."

What Would Actually Work

The fastest path is a point-of-sale integration. A company with access to California's growing lateral inspection database trains a model on LACP-coded results, pipe attributes from county GIS, and SSURGO soil data. The product isn't a standalone app. It's an addendum to the lateral inspection report that every buyer is already paying for: a predictive score, a failure probability curve, and a cost estimate for proactive replacement. The plumber running the camera gets an upsell. The buyer gets a prognosis instead of a snapshot. The real estate transaction, already the moment when everyone is paying attention to the sewer, gets a better number.

Cost to build: modest. A LightGBM model with cost-sensitive learning and SHAP explainability trains in hours on consumer hardware. The data aggregation from LACP inspections is the hard part, and California's mandates are solving that problem from the regulatory side.

The Spanish study showed the math is clean: flag the 4% most at-risk pipes and you prevent 30% of failures. Applied to residential laterals at scale, that's a tool that pays for itself with a single avoided emergency dig.

What Homeowners Should Do Now

Until the prediction tool exists, the sewer scope remains the best available diagnostic. If you're buying a home built before 1980, pay the $200 to $500 for a CCTV lateral inspection. Ask for NASSCO LACP or BSDI coding. It means the technician is using standardized defect descriptions, not "pipe looks kinda rough." In California, check whether your jurisdiction requires the inspection at point of sale. In Colorado, Denver's SUDP (Sewer Use and Drainage Permit) process means any work on the lateral requires city permitting, which means records exist.

If you're a plumber or inspection company running lateral scopes, you're sitting on the training data for the product nobody has built yet. The NASSCO BSDI certification costs $225 and is self-paced. Every coded inspection you perform and retain is a row in a future dataset. The company that aggregates 50,000 coded lateral inspections and pairs them with outcome data will own the category.

An R² of 0.90 is not a research curiosity. It's a product waiting for someone to ship it to the 130 million households that don't know their pipe material, don't know their pipe age, and won't think about either until the basement floods. The algorithm already knows. Somebody should tell the homeowner.
