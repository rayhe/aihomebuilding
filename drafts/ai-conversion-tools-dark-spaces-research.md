# Research Notes: AI Conversion Tools vs. the Dark Space Problem

## Angle
AI feasibility tools like Gensler's Conversions+ can evaluate 150+ building factors in hours and tell you whether an office building CAN be converted to residential. But they can't solve the fundamental architectural problem: deep floor plates create "dark spaces" — 12-19% of usable area that gets no natural light. The algorithm says "convert." The architecture says "into what?"

## Journalist: Elena Vasquez (Architecture & Design)
- Skeptical of tools that flatten design into optimization
- Sees buildings as art first, technology second
- Elegant, precise, essayist's flow

## Primary Sources

### 1. Gensler Conversions+ Tool
- Assessed 1,300+ buildings across multiple cities
- Found 25% suitable for conversion
- Evaluates 150+ building factors including floorplate depth, window access, HVAC, elevator counts
- Claims 30% lower cost vs new construction
- Has produced 5,500+ housing unit conversions
- Fast Company: algorithm runs in hours, not weeks
- Source: gensler.com, ULI Urban Land, Fast Company

### 2. RentCafe Pipeline Data (2026)
- 90,300 office-to-apartment units in the 2026 pipeline (up 28% YoY)
- NYC leads with 16,358 units
- Denver and Philadelphia joining top 10
- Driven by post-pandemic office overcapacity
- Source: RentCafe/Yardi

### 3. Brookings/HR&A/Gensler Study (6 case studies)
- Examined 18 buildings across 6 U.S. markets
- In 5 out of 6 markets, NO conversions were economically viable without public policy intervention
- Even buildings that pass architectural feasibility fail financial tests
- Used Railway Exchange Building (St. Louis) as detailed example
- Source: Brookings Institution

### 4. Charles Bloszies / Dark Space Research (SF)
- SF-based architect studying dark spaces in office conversions
- Dark spaces range from 12-19% of floor area in SF office buildings
- Dark = no natural light or ventilation; residual space after placing residential units along windows
- Possible uses: storage, gyms, retail, AV garages, shared amenities
- Older pre-1950 buildings (narrow floorplates, light wells, U-shapes) more suitable
- Modern post-1950 buildings with deep floorplates are the problem
- "The economic goal is finding uses for dark spaces that have value"
- Source: Multi-Housing News interview

### 5. CommercialEdge Conversion Feasibility Index
- 228 million sq ft of Tier I office space with strong conversion potential
- 2.7% of U.S. office space in Tier I (best candidates)
- 12.1% in Tier 2 (moderate potential)
- Manhattan leads Tier I at 16.8%
- Suburban offices: only 2.6% Tier I
- Source: CommercialEdge/Yardi

### 6. Conversion Costs
- $500-600/sq ft (CommercialEdge)
- 55 Broad St, NYC: $220M for 571 units = ~$385K/unit
- LEED-certified, rooftop pool, self-contained HVAC
- Source: JLL, Multi-Housing News

### 7. Trust Gap in AI CRE Tools
- 66% of CRE professionals use AI weekly or daily
- Only 5% trust it for real deal decisions
- 53% use AI for support only
- 17% require heavy verification
- Source: First American Data & Analytics / DealGround study

### 8. National Office Vacancy
- 18.2% national vacancy rate
- 91.1 million sq ft removed from U.S. office supply since 2016
- Source: CommercialEdge

### 9. NYC Comptroller Analysis
- 467-m tax exemption program
- Could absorb 15.2 million sq ft of office space
- Producing 17,400 apartments
- BUT: reduce property tax revenues by $5.1 billion over lifespan
- Source: NYC Comptroller's Office

## Original Contribution
Cross-reference Gensler's "25% suitable" finding with Brookings' "5/6 markets not viable without subsidies." Of the 1,300+ buildings Gensler assessed, ~325 passed architectural feasibility. But the Brookings financial model shows most of those STILL don't pencil. Then factor dark space waste: if 12-19% of floor area is unlightable dead space, the effective cost per livable square foot jumps 14-24% above headline conversion costs. On a $500-600/sq ft base, that's $570-744/sq ft of actual livable space.

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? YES — if you're a developer evaluating a conversion project, or a city planner writing incentive programs, or a buyer considering a converted unit, you need to understand the gap between what the algorithm says and what the architecture delivers. The dark space penalty is real money.
