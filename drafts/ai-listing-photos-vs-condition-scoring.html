The Listing Photos Looked Perfect. An AI Scored the Actual House a C4.

By Catherine Chen · June 6, 2026

A buyer in London scrolled through a listing on Winkworth Tooting's website this spring and saw a living room with soft furnishings, warm lighting, and clean walls. Lovely. Exactly what you'd hope for at the asking price. When she walked in, she found a property that hadn't been updated since the 1970s, with visible mold and a kitchen that was, in her words, "falling down." The listing photos had been generated by AI. A chimney breast that existed in the physical room had been erased from the digital one. Winkworth took the images down after she complained.

Three days later, on June 3, a California company called Veros Real Estate Solutions launched a product that does the opposite. VeroVISION feeds property photos through a computer vision model and scores the condition of each room and the overall property on a standardized scale. Its output, the Veros Home Score, correlates with human appraiser condition ratings at 93% and covers 97% of the U.S. residential market.

Two AI systems, operating on the same raw material, pulling in opposite directions. One makes a C4 house look like a C2. The other strips the cosmetics and tells you what grade the house actually earns.

What a Condition Grade Means for Your Money

Appraisers rate property condition on the URAR scale from C1 (new construction or recently renovated to like-new) through C6 (substantial damage requiring major repairs). Most resale homes land somewhere between C3 and C5. Each step on the scale represents real money. A C3-to-C4 shift on a $420,000 home, the approximate national median as of Q1 2026, typically corresponds to a 3% to 10% condition adjustment in the appraisal, depending on market and specific deficiencies. On that median home, the gap between one condition grade and the next is $12,600 to $42,000 in perceived value.

Here is the problem in plain terms: AI staging tools, available to any listing agent for $10 to $30 per photo, can visually upgrade a property by at least one condition grade in the photos a buyer sees online. The buyer forms a price expectation based on a C3 presentation, but the house is a C4. The appraisal comes back reflecting the actual condition. The buyer either overpays, renegotiates, or walks, and none of those outcomes are efficient, especially the one where the buyer closes without a correct appraisal and ends up holding a home worth less than they paid.

California Moved. Most States Haven't.

California Assembly Bill 723 took effect on January 1, 2026. It requires any real estate broker or salesperson who includes a digitally altered image in a listing advertisement to disclose that fact with a "reasonably conspicuous" statement on or adjacent to the image, along with a link to the original, unaltered photos. "Digitally altered" includes any image changed through photo editing software or artificial intelligence to add, remove, or change elements. AB-723 covers adding virtual furniture, brightening interiors, and replacing skies, but it also, critically, covers removing stains, cracks, damp marks, or structural issues. Staging and concealment are separated by a line that runs straight through the middle of what most AI photo tools can do.

New York's Department of State issued a trend alert warning homebuyers about AI-generated listing images. It cited existing rules under 19 NYCRR § 175.25(c)(9) against misrepresentation and General Business Law §§ 349, 350, and 350-a against deceptive acts and false advertising. No new statute. Wisconsin is drafting disclosure requirements for AI-altered listing images expected to take effect in 2027. North Carolina's REALTORS association published guidance clarifying that existing license law and Commission Rule 58A .0115 already prohibit using AI to misrepresent a property's condition, citing specific examples: covering stains or cracks, depicting damaged fixtures as new, presenting a digitally remodeled image as current reality.

That leaves roughly 46 states with no specific AI disclosure requirement and no published guidance on where virtual staging ends and misrepresentation begins.

Buyers Are Already Learning the Hard Way

Realtor.com reported that buyer agents are seeing clients walk out of showings within minutes because the property doesn't match the listing photos. Jenna Bailey, a trial attorney at Bailey Law Firm in Tempe, Arizona, described the legal line: "The issue is whether the images transition from being illustrative to falsifying. Images that have been too heavily manipulated that materially alter how a property looks could be considered deceptive advertising."

A study cited by New York's Department of State, published in the International Journal of Information Management, found that consumers perceive AI-generated real estate images as "less authentic, less professional, and misleading," which negatively affects purchase intentions, "particularly for high-involvement decisions like buying a home." The irony is thick. Agents are using AI to make listings more attractive, but the market is developing antibodies: buyers who distrust beautiful photos specifically because they've learned that beauty is now computationally cheap.

Meanwhile, the National Association of Realtors reminds members that Article 2 of its Code of Ethics prohibits exaggeration or concealment of pertinent facts, and Article 12 requires presenting a "true picture" in advertising. Existing NAR rules predate AI by decades. Whether they'll be enforced with the same vigor when violations are generated by an algorithm rather than a Photoshop brush is the question nobody has answered.

Who Gets the AI Condition Score

VeroVISION's Veros Home Score is available to originators, servicers, appraisal management companies, insurers, underwriters, and investors. Eric Fox, the company's chief economist, described the gap it fills: "For a long time, condition was the one variable an AVM could not accurately predict." The product launched the same day as LendlyX, a separate AI platform that does forensic photo and video analysis for private real estate lenders, using geofence validation and image verification to detect fraud in loan applications. LendlyX claims to address a $460 billion annual funding gap in private lending caused partly by inaccurate property assessments.

Notice who's missing from both customer lists. Buyers.

Lenders get an AI that can score condition from photos. Agents get an AI that can beautify those same photos. Buyers get neither tool and absorb the consequences of both. This is the shape of the current information asymmetry, and it's worth understanding clearly: the party with the most money at risk in a residential transaction is the only party without an AI tool to verify what they're looking at. VeroVISION is enterprise software, LendlyX serves lenders, and virtual staging platforms serve agents. Nobody serves buyers, at least not yet, and the regulatory framework in most states assumes the photos are photographs.

What a Buyer Should Do Right Now

First, assume the listing photos have been enhanced. Not because every agent is committing fraud, but because the tools cost $10 to $30 per image, they are ubiquitous, and there is no systematic enforcement mechanism in 46 states. Look for disclosure labels, which California law requires; elsewhere, their absence tells you nothing about whether the images are authentic.

Second, ask the listing agent directly whether any images have been digitally altered and request the originals. If the agent refuses, or if the answer is evasive, that is information. NAR's Code of Ethics, which binds all REALTORS regardless of state, requires a "true picture." You are entitled to ask what the true picture looks like.

Third, do not skip the home inspection, a point that was always true but matters more now that the gap between what listing photos show and what a physical walkthrough reveals has widened. An inspection runs $300 to $500, while the gap between a C3 and a C4 condition rating on a median-priced home translates to $12,600 to $42,000 in value. The math is not complicated.

Fourth, if you're working with a buyer's agent, ask whether they have access to any AI-powered condition assessment tools. The answer will almost certainly be no, but the question signals sophistication and may prompt more diligent photo verification on their end. If you're buying without an agent, which roughly 10% of buyers now do, the photo-to-reality gap is entirely your risk to manage.

The Best Case Against Regulation

Virtual staging existed long before AI made it cheap. Physical staging costs $2,000 to $5,000 per listing and has been standard practice for decades. Digitally adding furniture to an empty room is functionally identical to placing a rented couch in it. The line between "helping a buyer visualize potential" and "concealing defects" already existed, and existing consumer protection laws, NAR's ethics code, and state license regulations already address it. California's AB-723 may be solving a problem that existing law can handle, while creating compliance burdens that disproportionately affect small brokerages.

There's also a practical argument: VeroVISION's 93% correlation with human appraisers means a 7% discordance rate. On a $420,000 home, that's roughly $29,400 in potential scoring error. If a condition-scoring AI becomes the arbiter of property value, its errors will be systematically invisible in a way that human appraisers' errors, being idiosyncratic and reviewable, are not. Replacing human subjectivity with algorithmic certainty trades one kind of risk for another, and the algorithmic kind is harder to appeal.

Limitations

VeroVISION launched on June 3, 2026. No independent third party has validated the 93% correlation claim. Veros is the primary architect of the GSEs' Uniform Collateral Data Portal and a partner with the VA and FHA, which lends institutional credibility but does not constitute peer review. The UK examples cited are from The Times of London and may not generalize to U.S. market conditions, where disclosure norms and buyer expectations differ. The financial impact calculations use standard URAR condition adjustment ranges (3-10%), which vary significantly by market, property type, and appraiser. No systematic data exists on how frequently AI-altered listing photos cross the line from staging to misrepresentation, making the scale of the problem genuinely unknown.
