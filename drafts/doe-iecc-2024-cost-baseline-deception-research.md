# Research Notes: DOE's 2024 IECC Cost Analysis and the Baseline Deception

## Angle
The DOE says the 2024 energy code will add $14,000 per home and $9.2 billion annually. But that figure compares to a 2006 baseline — a code almost no state still enforces. The actual incremental cost from the codes most builders already follow is far smaller. Catherine Chen dissects the methodology and finds the real number buried in the political rhetoric.

## Journalist: Catherine "Code" Chen (Policy & Regulation)

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? **YES.** Every builder needs to know which energy code to target. Homebuyers hearing "$14,000 more per home" need context: most of that cost was already baked into the code their state adopted years ago. States have until Dec 30, 2026 to certify whether they'll adopt 2024 IECC. This is decision-relevant for every residential project in America right now.

## Primary Sources

### 1. DOE Press Release (June 26, 2026)
- Source: energy.gov/articles/energy-department-analysis-finds-proposed-international-building-codes-would-cost
- Key claims:
  - 2024 IECC would increase residential construction costs by $9.2 billion annually "compared to the 2006 code levels"
  - Up to $14,000 increase per typical single-family home
  - Payback periods exceed 10 years in most states, up to 22 years in some
  - Energy Secretary Chris Wright calls provisions "Green New Scam mandates"
  - DOE letter to ICC urges omitting: onsite energy generation, EV infrastructure, greenhouse gas avoidance requirements
  - DOE issued Request for Information on its methodology

### 2. DOE's Own Prior Determination (2024 IECC vs 2021 IECC)
- Source: energycodes.gov — "Determination Regarding Energy Efficiency Improvements in the 2024 IECC"
- The SAME DOE previously determined:
  - 2024 IECC vs 2021 IECC = 7.80% site energy savings, 6.80% source energy savings, 6.60% energy cost savings
  - THIS is the actual incremental improvement for states already on 2021 IECC
  - Much smaller than the headline $14,000 figure (which uses 2006 baseline)
- State certification deadline: December 30, 2026

### 3. State Code Adoption Status (ACEEE database)
- Current state of play (most states NOT on 2006 code):
  - Illinois: Already adopted 2024 IECC (effective 11/30/2025)
  - Colorado: Required to adopt 2024 IECC (or stricter) after July 1, 2026
  - Connecticut: 2021 IECC with amendments
  - Washington: 2021 IECC (state version exceeds national by ~5%)
  - Delaware: 2018 IECC
  - West Virginia: 2015 IECC
  - Oklahoma: Still on 2006 IECC (energy chapter) — ONE OF VERY FEW
  - North Dakota: 2021 IECC (voluntary, 91% population adoption)
- The 2006 baseline applies to almost nobody. Most states are on 2015, 2018, or 2021 IECC.

### 4. UpCodes AI Plan Review (June 2026)
- Source: morningstar.com / prnewswire.com
- AI-native plan review covering 11 million sections of locally adopted codes across 6,000+ jurisdictions
- Discipline-specific analyses: architecture, structural, MEP, fire protection, life safety, accessibility, energy
- Returns issues organized by severity linked to governing code section
- KEY TENSION: UpCodes can check compliance against ANY code version. The AI knows every code. The problem is political — which code will your jurisdiction enforce next year?

### 5. PNNL Technical Analysis (DOE Building Energy Codes Program)
- Source: osti.gov (PNNL-32140 and successors)
- PNNL conducts DOE's code impact analyses
- Each code cycle shows incremental improvements, not the cumulative 18-year gap DOE used

### 6. DOE 2018 IECC Determination (Federal Register Dec 10, 2019)
- 2018 IECC vs 2015 IECC = 1.68% site energy savings, 1.91% source energy savings, 1.97% cost savings
- Shows the incremental pattern: each code cycle adds modest improvements, not $14,000 shocks

## Original Contribution
Calculate what the 2024 IECC actually costs on TOP of the code most builders already follow.

Method:
- DOE's $14,000 figure is cumulative from 2006 through 2024 — roughly 18 years and 6 code cycles (2006→2009→2012→2015→2018→2021→2024)
- Most states are on 2018 or 2021 IECC
- Each code cycle adds ~$2,000-3,000 in construction costs (DOE's own prior analyses)
- The 2021→2024 increment is one code cycle
- DOE's energy cost savings of 6.6% for that increment = ~$150-200/year on a typical $2,400 annual energy bill
- At $3,000 upfront cost and $175/year savings, payback is ~17 years
- At $2,000 upfront and $175/year, payback is ~11 years
- VERSUS the headline $14,000 with 22-year payback — the real numbers are less dramatic but the payback period is roughly similar proportionally

Actually, the most honest calculation: if the average home energy bill is ~$2,400/year and the 2024 IECC saves 6.6% over 2021 IECC, that's ~$158/year. For the incremental cost to be $14,000, you'd need to be going from 2006→2024 in one jump. Most builders aren't doing that.

## Strongest Counterargument
The DOE's $14,000 figure isn't fabricated — it accurately represents the total cost of all energy code improvements since 2006. For the handful of states still on older codes, the full cost is real. And the payback math does favor builders who care about first cost over lifecycle cost. The housing affordability crisis is real, and every dollar added to construction costs matters. The question is whether the right response is to freeze codes at 2006 levels or to acknowledge that most states already absorbed most of these costs years ago.

## Limitations
- Cannot independently verify PNNL's cost models (proprietary energy simulation)
- DOE's June 2026 analysis document itself hasn't been fully reviewed (they issued an RFI suggesting methodology is still being finalized)
- State-by-state incremental costs vary enormously by climate zone
- Some states have local amendments that exceed IECC, making the comparison even more complex
