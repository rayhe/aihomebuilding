# Research: Pattern Book Homes — Who Draws the Catalog?

## Article #562 | Catherine Chen | July 18, 2026

## Angle
The 21st Century ROAD to Housing Act (became law July 11, 2026) includes a federal grant program for communities to develop "pattern books" — collections of preapproved home designs that bypass much of the permitting process. A dozen cities have already launched pattern book programs independently. The law is silent on how these designs get created. AI design tools — generative floor plan algorithms, automated code-compliance checkers — could mass-produce catalog pages at a fraction of the cost of architect-drawn plans. The question: does algorithmic efficiency produce algorithmic sameness? And who decides what a community's homes look like when the federal government is writing the check?

## Primary Sources

### 1. 21st Century ROAD to Housing Act (Law, July 2026)
- Became law July 11, 2026 without Trump's signature (passed House 358-32, Senate 85-5)
- 47 total housing supply provisions
- Pattern book provision: grants for communities to develop "pattern books" with preapproved housing designs needing fewer approvals before construction
- Environmental review exemption for infill between two reviewed buildings
- Manufactured housing: removes permanent chassis requirement (saves $5K-$10K/unit)
- Investor cap: entities with 350+ single-family homes barred from buying more
- Incentivizes local zoning reform: steers federal grants to localities that permit greater construction
- Source: northjersey.com, washingtonexaminer.com, usatoday.com, cnn.com, investopedia.com

### 2. Rogers, Arkansas — Pattern Zone Program (Launched June 2026)
- 13 preapproved residential designs (cottages to duplexes)
- Free downloadable plans, already through code compliance review
- Enter parcel data → see which designs fit your lot
- "We tried to think about the character of some of the older parts of town" — planner Amber Long
- Each plan includes a range of modifications before triggering full/partial review
- Builders select from typically approved materials (texture/look can vary, layout stays)
- Source: homes.com, rogersar.gov

### 3. Ohio — Housing Ohio Initiative (2025-2026)
- Led by Greater Ohio Policy Center + Ohio Realtors
- Statewide program focused on legacy cities (bleeding population/industry)
- Two architect-designed building plans: narrow single-family home + front-back duplex
- Plans fit 30-ft-wide lots (80% of Ohio's infill lots per advisory committee)
- Free, code-compliant plans with implementation guide
- 250,000+ unit housing deficit statewide
- Home prices 2.6x median income (highest since 2005)
- Designs by Pattern Zones Co.
- Source: cnu.org (Congress for the New Urbanism), midstory.org

### 4. Michigan — MEDC Permit Ready Housing Plans
- Pilot by Michigan Economic Development Corp + Michigan Municipal League + City of Grand Rapids
- 5 complete plan sets: 3 duplex + 2 quadraplex
- Open source stamped plans available to any Michigan community
- Pattern Book Homes manual with history of US housing development
- Plans designed by Driven Design, LLC
- Source: miplace.org

### 5. Overland Park, Kansas — Portfolio Homes (Launched Nov 2025)
- Cottages, small single-family homes, duplexes
- Duplexes: 854-1,658 sq ft; single-family: 1,224-2,197 sq ft
- Plans are free to download, no architect needed for design phase
- Building permit fees waived for approved applications
- Streamlines most unpredictable part of permitting timeline
- "Hard numbers on time and cost savings aren't available yet since the program just recently launched"
- Source: marc.org (Mid-America Regional Council)

### 6. Portland, Oregon — Certified House Plans
- Submit construction drawings via DevHub
- Once approved, can be used for New Single-Family Residence or ADU
- Changes cannot be made for individual sites without full revision
- Planning/Zoning reviewer still checks zone- and lot-specific standards
- Review time: 15 working days (normal schedule)
- Source: portland.gov

### 7. South Bend, Indiana — House Catalog
- One of the early pattern zone programs
- Facades from the house catalog designed to match neighborhood character
- Source: cnu.org

### 8. Sears Modern Homes (1908-1940) — Historical Precedent
- Sears shipped 75,000-100,000 precut kit homes by rail
- 1908 first catalog: 44 designs, $360-$2,890
- 447+ models over the program's life
- 30,000 pieces per home, 75-page instruction manual
- Pre-cut lumber reduced construction time by 40%
- Aladdin Company (Bay City, MI) was actually first (1906)
- Success factor: mass production reduced manufacturing costs
- Source: Wikipedia, midstory.org, thoughtco.com, morriscountynj.gov

### 9. AI Floor Plan Generation Tools (2025-2026)
- **PlanFinder**: Commercial AI generative space planning tool, Autodesk Revit 2026 addon. Custom ML model generates multiple solutions from geometric/functional constraints. Used in academic comparison study (Polish housing). BIM-ready floorplans.
- **CorbuAI**: Multimodal AI architecture platform. Floor plan generation, facade generation, interior style transfer. GAN-based.
- **Stanislas Chaillou GAN work**: Trained on 800+ Boston apartment layouts. Three-stage process: footprint → room layout → furniture. pix2pix GAN model.
- **Generative AI Framework for Adaptive Residential Layout**: Fine-tuned diffusion model on 20,000 curated images. Maps family life-cycle stages to floor plans.
- **RFP-A**: First evaluation metrics specifically for AI-generated residential floor plans (accuracy + diversity assessment)
- Source: mdpi.com (multiple papers), bricsys.com, architizer.com

## Thesis
The ROAD to Housing Act's pattern book provision is the federal government's most concrete attempt to solve permitting delays. But the law treats design as solved — pick an architect, draw some houses, fast-track the permits. It never reckons with the AI tools already generating residential floor plans at scale, or with the uncomfortable question of what happens when a dozen cities hire the same algorithm to design their "locally appropriate" home catalogs. The historical precedent (Sears) suggests standardization works when the market wants volume. The current market, with builder sentiment at historic lows and cancellation rates triple pre-pandemic norms, might want something else entirely.
