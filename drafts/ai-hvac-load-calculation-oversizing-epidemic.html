Your Contractor Sized Your AC With a Gut Feeling. The AI Used Your Actual Windows.

By Priya Greenwood · May 10, 2026

Andy Bell at Energy Vanguard ran Manual J load calculations on 40 homes in hot and mixed climates. He plotted the results against what contractors typically install. Not a single home in the dataset needed the amount of cooling capacity that the industry's rule of thumb would prescribe.

Not a single one, across a sample spanning the southeastern United States, Texas, California, and the Midwest.

1,431
Average square feet per ton of cooling across 40 Manual J calculations in hot and mixed climates, per Energy Vanguard's analysis. HVAC contractors typically use 400 to 600 square feet per ton, which means the industry standard is off by a factor of nearly three.

Most HVAC contractors in the residential market size air conditioning systems the same way their predecessors did 30 years ago: divide the square footage by 500, round up to the nearest half ton, and install whatever the distributor has on the truck. A 2,500-square-foot home gets a 5-ton system under this method, but according to Energy Vanguard's data, that same home probably needs something closer to 1.7 tons. Mike MacFarland of Energy Docs in California achieves 1,500 square feet per ton on retrofits routinely, and hit 3,350 square feet per ton on a new zero-energy home where the design temperature was 102°F. If a zero-energy house in triple-digit heat needs less than a ton of cooling, the five-ton condensers sitting outside American homes are not engineering but habit, a number inherited from the contractor's father or the distributor's recommendation sheet rather than from any measurement of the building they are attached to.

What Oversizing Actually Costs You

Allison Bailes, the building scientist behind Energy Vanguard, documents six distinct problems with oversized systems, starting with the most visible: a 4-ton heat pump runs $2,000 to $4,000 more than a 2-ton unit, money you spend on capacity the building will never demand.

But the money you waste at the point of sale is the smallest part of what oversizing takes from you, because an oversized system creates a cascade of operational failures that compound every month the system runs. Short cycling is the primary mechanism by which oversizing bleeds energy and comfort simultaneously. A system sized at twice the actual load satisfies the thermostat in roughly half the time a properly sized system would, then shuts off. Shuts on. Shuts off. Each start-up consumes a spike of electrical current, each shutdown halts dehumidification mid-cycle, and the house oscillates between blasts of overcooled air and rooms that start warming the moment the compressor stops.

Humidity is where this gets visceral, because an air conditioner removes moisture by running air across a coil cold enough to condense water vapor out of the air stream, and that dehumidification process requires sustained runtime that oversized systems never deliver. Your thermostat reads 74 but the air feels damp, so you drop the setpoint to 71, the system runs a fraction longer, the air temperature drops below comfortable, and now you live in a home that is both clammy and cold. Building scientists call this condition "cool and clammy," while homeowners call it "something is wrong with my AC," but nothing is wrong with the hardware and everything is wrong with the sizing decision that preceded it by a decade.

Bigger systems also demand bigger ducts. If your ductwork was not upsized to match the oversized equipment, the system pushes air through undersized passages at higher velocities, creating noise, increasing static pressure, and reducing equipment lifespan. EPA studies indicate that 60 percent of buildings have oversized HVAC systems, and NIST research shows improper installation can reduce energy efficiency by up to 30 percent.

Quantifying the National Waste

Let me run the arithmetic that nobody in the HVAC industry seems willing to publish. If the average properly calculated cooling load is 1,431 square feet per ton, and contractors routinely install at 500 square feet per ton, the typical new home receives roughly 2.9 times the cooling capacity it needs. For a 2,500-square-foot house, that translates to a 5-ton system installed where a 1.7-ton system would suffice.

Upfront cost difference: $2,000 to $4,000 in equipment alone, excluding the larger ductwork. Annual energy penalty from short cycling, oversized fan operation, and reduced dehumidification efficiency: $200 to $600, depending on climate zone, electricity rate, and how much the homeowner compensates by lowering the thermostat to fight the humidity problem the oversizing created. Over a 15-year equipment lifecycle, that totals $3,000 to $9,000 per home in combined waste. Multiply by approximately 1.5 million new single-family housing starts per year (Census Bureau), and the industry generates $4.5 to $13.5 billion in avoidable cost per equipment generation.

Those numbers assume new construction only. Replacement systems, which represent a far larger market, carry the same oversizing bias. Contractors rarely recalculate loads when replacing equipment. They match the nameplate tonnage of the dead unit, perpetuating the original sizing error for another 15 years.

AI Promises a Fix. Sort Of.

A new generation of AI-powered tools claims to automate Manual J load calculations, eliminating the rule-of-thumb contractor and replacing gut instinct with measured inputs. LennoxPros now offers a free Manual J calculator powered by CoolCalc that produces ACCA-approved MJ8 reports. Field Promax delivers preliminary load estimates meeting Manual J 8th Edition standards in under two minutes. AI platforms can auto-pull climate data by location, estimate missing insulation values using predictive models, and recommend specific equipment based on calculated loads.

Pacific Northwest National Laboratory went further. Their open-source BEM-AI bot, published in Energy and Buildings in March 2026, uses agentic AI to automate building energy modeling. The system breaks a modeling task into specialized sub-agents that independently handle data translation, model construction, and simulation execution. In tests on Florida commercial buildings, BEM-AI produced accurate energy savings estimates that previously required months of expert modeling time. The architecture is commercial-focused today, but the underlying approach translates directly to residential load calculation: multi-agent systems that can process floor plans, window schedules, insulation specs, and climate data without a human intermediary choosing to ignore the results.

Beam AI claims 90 percent time savings on HVAC takeoffs, with QA-reviewed outputs. The EDS platform integrates load calculations with Google Sheets for automated reporting. Across the sector, HVAC design software is growing at 7.25 percent annually.

Why Better Math Will Not Solve a Behavioral Problem

Manual J has been the ACCA/ANSI-recognized standard for residential HVAC load calculations since 1986, and the software to run it correctly has existed for decades in packages like Wrightsoft, Right-J, CoolCalc, and Elite RHVAC. The tools were already there, widely available and affordable, but contractors chose not to use them, or they used them and then overrode the results to install what they intended to install from the beginning.

Green Building Advisor documented the manipulation tactics in unflinching detail: inflate the window area on the input form, select the wrong insulation value, mark the house orientation incorrectly, and the software obediently produces a load calculation that justifies the tonnage the contractor wanted to install in the first place. Garbage in, garbage out, with an ACCA-approved stamp on the paperwork.

Why oversize deliberately? Because contractors face asymmetric consequences that make caution look rational even when the physics says otherwise. Install too small, the house doesn't cool on the hottest day of the year, the phone rings, and you are back on site for free. Install too large, and the homeowner never calls, because short cycling, humidity problems, and inflated electric bills go undiagnosed for years, attributed to weather, to the house being "drafty," to Texas just being hot. The larger system functions as insurance against a complaint the contractor would rather avoid, purchased with the homeowner's money without the homeowner's knowledge or consent.

AI load calculation tools do not change this incentive structure. A contractor who inflates window areas in Wrightsoft can inflate window areas in an AI-powered calculator. A contractor who rounds up from a 2-ton result to a 3.5-ton install will round up regardless of whether the 2-ton number came from a spreadsheet or a neural network. Automating the calculation does not automate the decision to act on it.

Variable Speed Is Not a Get-Out-of-Jail Card

Proponents of modern HVAC equipment argue that variable-speed and inverter-driven systems mitigate oversizing damage. A 4-ton variable-speed heat pump running at 50 percent capacity is roughly equivalent to a properly sized 2-ton single-stage unit at full capacity, with the added benefit of modulated comfort and potentially superior efficiency at partial loads. In theory, oversizing with variable-speed equipment is less harmful than oversizing with single-stage equipment.

In practice, variable-speed systems cost $3,000 to $7,000 more than single-stage equivalents. So you pay a premium to buy a system that compensates for the sizing error instead of paying less for a correctly sized system that doesn't need compensation, which is not a solution but a workaround that transfers the cost of the contractor's laziness to the homeowner's credit card, packaged as a premium feature rather than labeled as damage mitigation. It also assumes the variable-speed equipment is commissioned correctly, which NIST data on installation quality suggests is optimistic.

What Would Actually Work

2026 HVAC efficiency standards now require stricter load-based equipment selection under Manual S and Manual D. Updated SEER2 and EER2 metrics make it harder to hide oversizing behind inflated efficiency claims. That represents genuine progress at the regulatory level, though enforcement at the permit counter remains inconsistent across jurisdictions.

AI could contribute meaningfully if the tools evolve beyond automating calculations that contractors already ignore. Three developments would matter: First, AI-generated load reports submitted directly to building departments for permit review, removing the contractor as the sole intermediary between the calculation and the equipment selection. Second, computer vision systems that verify installed equipment matches the permitted load calculation by reading nameplate data from inspection photos, a capability well within current AI image recognition accuracy. Third, utility-integrated monitoring that flags homes where runtime patterns indicate oversized equipment and triggers automated efficiency audits.

PNNL's BEM-AI demonstrates that multi-agent AI can produce legitimate energy models autonomously. If that capability reaches the residential permitting pipeline, the contractor loses the ability to run a load calculation, ignore the result, and install whatever fits in the truck. The AI doesn't calculate the load for the contractor. It calculates the load for the building department, the utility, and the homeowner simultaneously, creating accountability that no spreadsheet ever provided.

What You Should Do Today

If you are building a home, demand a Manual J load calculation from your HVAC contractor, and not a verbal estimate or a square-footage rule of thumb or a software printout they cannot explain, but a document showing the input assumptions: what R-values they used for your walls, what window areas and orientations, what infiltration rate, what internal heat gains. Then compare those inputs to your actual plans. If the window area on the calculation does not match the window schedule on the architectural drawings, you have caught someone inflating inputs to justify oversizing.

If the contractor refuses to provide a load calculation, or provides one that implies less than 800 square feet per ton in a home built to current energy code, hire a third-party HVAC designer to run an independent calculation, because firms like Energy Vanguard and Energy Docs offer remote design services for $500 to $1,500, an investment that pays for itself if it downsizes your system by even one ton.

If you already own a home and suspect your system is oversized, check runtime on a hot day near design temperature for your climate, because your air conditioner should run nearly continuously under those conditions, and if it cycles on for 10 minutes and off for 15, the system is almost certainly too large. A smart thermostat can track runtime automatically. So can the data logging built into most modern communicating systems.

Limitations of This Analysis

Energy Vanguard's dataset comprises 40 homes, concentrated in the southeastern United States and Texas. It is not nationally representative. Climate, code stringency, and construction quality vary enormously across regions, and the 1,431 square feet per ton average should not be applied as a universal benchmark. The EPA's 60 percent oversizing statistic originates from commercial building studies, not exclusively residential data, though residential oversizing is widely documented by building science practitioners. My lifecycle cost calculations use national average electricity rates and equipment pricing, not climate-zone-specific figures, and actual waste per home will vary significantly based on local energy costs, system efficiency, and occupant behavior. No third-party study has validated AI load calculation outputs against measured post-construction performance. The technology is promising but unaudited.
