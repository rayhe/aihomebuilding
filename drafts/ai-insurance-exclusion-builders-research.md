# Research: AI Insurance Exclusion Coverage Gap in Residential Construction

## Core Angle
ISO/Verisk released standardized AI exclusion endorsements (CG 40 47 and CG 40 48) effective January 1, 2026. These endorsements give insurers a template to exclude coverage for any loss "arising out of, or attributable to, generative artificial intelligence." The exclusion applies to both bodily injury/property damage AND personal/advertising injury under CGL policies. ISO forms underpin ~82% of U.S. property and casualty insurance policies. Meanwhile, 74% of contractors view AI as an efficiency engine (ServiceTitan 2026), but most haven't checked whether their insurance still covers what AI touches.

## Key Sources

### 1. Gallagher Advisory (ajg.com) — ISO CG 40 47 analysis
- CG 40 47 01 26: "Generative Artificial Intelligence Exclusion"
- Applies to Coverage A (bodily injury & property damage) AND Coverage B (personal & advertising injury)
- Broad wording: may apply even when AI is only ONE contributing factor
- Applies whether AI used directly by insured OR indirectly through vendors, consultants, project systems
- Most construction contracts don't address AI usage or contemplate AI-related exclusions
- Quote: "For construction companies, the issue isn't whether AI will be used, but whether its use is recognized, managed and aligned with insurance and contractual obligations."

### 2. Cohen Seglias law firm (cohenseglias.com)
- ISO forms underpin approximately 82% of U.S. property and casualty insurance policies
- Generative AI defined as "a machine-based learning system or model that is trained on data with the ability to create content or responses, including but not limited to text, images, audio, video, or code"
- Flawed AI-generated structural calculation, hallucinated building specification, erroneous project schedule — all could lead to uninsured claims
- Small and mid-sized construction businesses face heightened exposure

### 3. Risk Specialty Group / FinancialContent
- Verisk forms (CG 40 47 and CG 40 48) took effect January 1, 2026
- Berkley Insurance: "absolute" AI exclusion, specifically names ChatGPT, Bard, Midjourney, DALL-E
- Hamilton Insurance Group: own generative AI exclusion
- Philadelphia Insurance and Hamilton Select: excluding AI from E&O entirely
- AIG, Great American, W.R. Berkley: filed for regulatory approval for comparable exclusions
- Stanford Law School: hallucination rates 58-88% general purpose, 20-33% specialized
- Recommends 4-step policy audit at renewal
- Carve-backs possible when firm documents AI governance program

### 4. ServiceTitan 2026 Residential State of the Trades Report (n=1,000)
- 74% of contractors view AI as efficiency engine
- Only 25% currently using AI
- 48% of early adopters report increased productivity
- 73% believe starting early creates competitive advantage
- Nearly half of contractors lack trust in AI

### 5. Warranty Week / PWSC
- U.S. homebuilders paid $1.071 billion in warranty claims in 2024 (27 public builders)
- Warranty reserves exceeded $2.2 billion in 2024 — double from a decade ago
- Lennar: $286M in claims. D.R. Horton: $119M. PulteGroup: $107M.

### 6. AIA Contract Gaps (Frantz Ward analysis)
- AIA A201-2017: no provisions for AI use, liability allocation, or disclosure requirements
- 5 specific gaps: standard of care, design doc responsibility, IP, third-party tech, insurance alignment

### 7. HCR Law (hcrlaw.com)
- CCDC 41 updated for drones but silent on AI
- Many AI errors result in economic losses falling into coverage gap
- Professional Liability insurance assumes human oversight — "black box" AI challenges this
- AI-generated construction records (meeting minutes, site diaries) will be challenged in litigation

## Original Contribution
Nobody has connected the insurance exclusion movement to the specific residential builder scenario — your general contractor used an AI estimating tool, the estimate was wrong, a structural element was undersized, and now neither your builder's CGL nor their E&O covers the resulting claim because AI was "in the causal chain." The homeowner is the ultimate victim. Residential builders are much less likely to have sophisticated insurance counsel reviewing endorsement schedules at renewal.

## Kill Test
Does this help someone building or buying a home? YES — this is about whether the insurance your builder carries actually covers defects when AI tools were involved. Every homeowner signing a construction contract needs to know this.
