## Critic 1: 🔍 General Editor — 8.5/10 ✅

**Strengths:**
- Headline is outstanding — specific, reader-directed, creates urgency without clickbait. "Your Mortgage Lender" puts the reader inside the story.
- Cold open is textbook: date, bill number, scope, then the two-word gut punch ("They should."). The reader is hooked before the second scroll.
- The insurance pivot is the article's best structural move — it takes a story the reader was ready to dismiss ("one state, disclosure only, cure period") and reveals why they can't. The coverage gap argument is genuinely surprising.
- The "7-point disclosure stack" is a strong original concept that gives the article intellectual weight beyond news reporting.
- Limitations section is among the best this publication has produced — specific, honest, not performative hedging.
- Closing line lands: "If you are waiting for certainty before acting, you are waiting too long."

**Issues:**
1. **Density in the long-sentence swings.** The 31+ word bucket holds 22 of 60 sentences (37%). While the rhythm gate passes comfortably, reading aloud reveals a few paragraphs where every sentence runs 30+ words without a breath. The paragraph beginning "Each of those steps involves..." is a single 78-word sentence. It works structurally but an editor would probably split it with a period after "the same buyer" for readability.
2. **The transaction walkthrough lists 6 AI-touched steps, not 7.** The stat callout claims 7 disclosure points, but the walkthrough paragraph names: (1) AI-enhanced listing photos, (2) AI underwriting, (3) Spectora AI inspection, (4) AVM appraisal, (5) satellite AI insurance, (6) AI compliance closing engine. Title search — implied as the 7th in the limitations section — never appears in the walkthrough. Either add it to the walkthrough or change the callout to 6.

**Suggested fixes:**
- Add title search as an explicit step in the walkthrough: "Gets a title report processed through an AI-assisted title search engine."
- Consider breaking the 78-word sentence after "the same buyer" into two sentences.

## Critic 3: ⚖️ Ethics Reviewer — 9.0/10 ✅

**Strengths:**
- The counterargument is stated at full strength before the pivot: "this is a disclosure law, not a prohibition, the cure period is generous, and well-run companies documenting their AI use will barely notice the compliance cost." Acknowledged with "All true" — no strawmanning.
- No self-congratulation. The article credits Spectora's candor ("to its credit, is unusually candid") and Cotality's research without positioning AI Home Building as the hero.
- The insurance angle is presented factually with specific forms, carriers, and percentages — not as fear-mongering.
- The limitations section is genuinely honest: admits the 7-point stack is "illustrative," that some steps may not qualify, that licensing board guidance is secondhand, and that compliance cost data doesn't exist.

**Issues:**
1. **"Nobody in Construction Noticed" (headline) is a strong claim.** Supported by "conversations with builders, inspectors, and real estate attorneys" — but how many? Two? Twenty? The article doesn't say. This is informal sourcing, and the headline elevates it to a sweeping claim. The limitations section doesn't flag this gap.
2. **The xAI v. Weiser stay and AG voluntary enforcement pause.** Per the Buchalter analysis, the AG has stated he will not enforce SB 26-189 until after rulemaking concludes — meaning the January 2027 effective date may not carry practical enforcement weight immediately. The article's urgency framing focuses on insurance and vendor preparedness (which is legitimate), but omitting the AG's own enforcement posture leaves out context that a fair reading would include.

**Suggested fixes:**
- Add a brief qualifier to the opening: "in conversations with more than a dozen builders..." or "in a half-dozen conversations..." — quantify the sample even roughly.
- In the "What We Don't Know" section, add one sentence noting that the AG has indicated enforcement will follow, not precede, rulemaking completion.

## Critic 5: ⚖️ Legal Accuracy — 8.5/10 ✅

**Round 1 corrections verified:**
- ✅ "$20,000 per violation" removed → replaced with discretionary CUPA enforcement (confirmed accurate per Aguardic statutory analysis)
- ✅ Governor Polis signing date corrected to May 14, 2026 (confirmed by Mondaq, Lexology, Snell & Wilmer, Holland & Knight, Buchalter, JD Supra)
- ✅ Stanford hallucination rates corrected to 17-33% for specialized tools (confirmed by Stanford RegLab paper)
- ✅ Carrier count changed to "more than a dozen" (12 named, claim now accurate)
- ✅ "80-100% regulatory approval rate" replaced with softer "Regulators in most states are approving those filings"

**Claims verified:**
- SB 26-189 passage date (May 12, 2026): Consistent with legislative timeline (session close May 13, signed May 14) ✅
- Effective date January 1, 2027: Confirmed across all sources ✅
- SB 24-205 repealed and replaced: Confirmed ✅
- "Automated decision-making technology" (ADMT): Correct statutory term ✅
- "Consequential decisions": Correct statutory term ✅
- 60-day cure period, AG-exclusive enforcement, no private right of action: All confirmed ✅
- Discretionary CUPA penalties: Confirmed (Aguardic: "The statute does not specify a fixed per-violation dollar amount") ✅
- Verisk CG 40 47 and CG 40 48 forms: Specific ISO form numbers, verifiable ✅
- CFPB AVM rule: Verified ✅
- x.AI LLC v. Weiser: Correct case name, confirmed by Buchalter ✅
- Six states with active bills + bill numbers: Cited with specifics ✅

**Remaining issues:**
1. **"$2.5 million and $6 million" compliance cost estimate lacks attribution.** This is a specific numerical claim about fiscal impact with no source. It may come from a Colorado Legislative Council Staff fiscal note or an industry coalition estimate, but the article doesn't say. STORY_GUIDE.md requires: "Every claim gets a source."
2. **"58 to 88 percent" general-purpose hallucination rate.** The Stanford study abstract states only that hallucinations are "reduced relative to general-purpose chatbots (GPT-4)" without giving GPT-4's specific rate. The 58-88% range may come from the full paper's body, but since the article attributes it to "Stanford Law School research" generally without a link to the specific figure, it's a soft attribution gap.
3. **"killed it" (referring to the federal court stay of SB 24-205).** The stay paused enforcement; SB 24-205 was repealed by SB 26-189 through legislation, not "killed" by a court. The phrasing conflates the judicial and legislative actions. Minor but imprecise for a legal article.

**Suggested fixes:**
- Add source for $2.5-$6M figure, or soften to "estimates ran into the millions" without specific numbers.
- Either link the Stanford paper directly for the 58-88% figure or soften to "substantially higher" for general-purpose tools.
- Change "killed it" to "paused it" or "effectively blocked enforcement" to be more precise about what the stay did vs. what the legislature did.
