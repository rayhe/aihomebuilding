# Research: AI Trust Collapse in Homebuying — When Staging Fraud Poisons Useful Tools

## Angle
Homebuyer trust in AI has cratered from 30% to 16% in one year (Cotality AI in Housing 2026 report, released April 2026). The cause: AI staging fraud — agents using AI to move walls, remove chimney breasts, hide damp and structural defects — has poisoned the well for every AI tool in real estate, including ones that could genuinely help buyers (like predictive inspection analytics and conversational home search). The tools that could rebuild trust are tarred by the tools that destroyed it.

## Primary Sources

### Cotality AI in Housing 2026 Report (April 2026)
- Trust in AI to help find a home: 30% (2025) → 16% (2026) — 14-point drop in one year
- 75% of homebuyers expect AI in the buying process but want "human in the loop"
- 68% say clear AI labeling for listings/mortgage recommendations is important/essential
- 37% say AI labeling should be mandatory (rises to 61% among Baby Boomers)
- 46% say unacceptable for lenders/insurers to conduct AI valuations without prior approval
- Tolerance for AI errors: 22% Gen Z, 19% Millennials, 11% Gen X, 9% Boomers
- 64% concerned AI may "recycle" unverified info vs validated first-party data
- Only 7% of Gen Z buyers would accept AI-generated info on property risk
- 50% of Gen Z say AI tools would help them feel MORE confident about buying
- AI could reduce loan processing by 1-3 months (Cotality estimate)
- 7M+ mortgages originated annually in the US
- 37% of originated loans go to buyers under 35
- Source: Cotality press release via BusinessWire, HousingWire, PYMNTS, RealEstateNews, MPAMag

### National Association of Realtors
- 70% of Realtors have used AI tools in some capacity
- Industry still lacks standardized guidelines for AI tool disclosure to buyers

### California Assembly Bill 723 (effective Jan 1, 2026)
- Requires real estate professionals to disclose when listing images are digitally altered
- Must provide link to original unaltered image (URL or QR code)
- Statement must be "reasonably conspicuous" and "located on or adjacent to the image"
- Enforcement: regulatory discipline, civil liability, criminal charges for willful violation
- "Digitally altered image" defined as altered via photo editing software or AI to add/remove/change elements
- Source: leginfo.legislature.ca.gov, Inman

### New York Department of State Trend Alert
- Secretary of State Walter T. Mosley: AI tools "may produce misleading or exaggerated representation of properties"
- Housing Commissioner RuthAnne Visnauskas: "rise of A.I.-generated images in listings threatens to undermine" transparency
- NY Real Property Law § 441-c: dishonest/misleading advertisements subject to disciplinary action
- 19 NYCRR § 175.25(c)(9): advertisements must include honest and accurate depiction
- NY Bill S09584: "No digital alteration shall add non-existent physical features or remove or conceal known material defects"
- NY also enacted synthetic performer disclosure law (S.8420-A), effective June 9, 2026 — $1,000 first violation, $5,000 subsequent
- Source: dos.ny.gov, LegiScan

### AI Staging Fraud Examples
- Winkworth Tooting (UK): AI removed chimney breast in listing photos; images pulled "after a customer expressed disappointment during a viewing"; disclosure was buried in PDF white text
- The Times (UK, June 2026): AI used to remove signs of damp, cracks, structural issues; "hadn't been updated since the 70s" but looked "fantastic, really lovely" online
- D.C. suburb (Feb 2026): AI staging created "demonic flesh monster crawling out of bathroom mirror" (went viral)
- Florida agent Katy McBrayer-Lynch: tested AI tools that "moved a wall, changed the window" — went beyond cosmetic
- University of Chicago research: AI-generated listing content triggers measurable buyer distrust, both sides lose efficiency

### Alpine Intelligence (Genuinely Useful AI Tool)
- Launched March 5, 2026 by Alpine Building Performance (Denver, founded 2016)
- Free AI-powered inspection risk forecaster for real estate agents
- ChatGPT-powered; analyzes MLS data, permit histories, property disclosures
- Generates: pre-inspection summary, system-by-system risk flags, talking points for agents
- Key insight: "What you see in an inspection is characteristic of when it was built and where it's located" — predictive analytics
- Example: pre-1940s Denver homes have 87% chance of lead-based paint
- "Definitely not meant to replace the inspection, but really to help the agent set expectations"
- Founders: Andrew Sams (owner), Mason Minor (business partner)
- Source: 5280 Magazine, PRLog press release

### Realtor.com RealAssist AI

- Plain-language home search → MLS data, affordability calculations, agent connections

- Source: Google Cloud press corner via PYMNTS

## Original Contribution
**Cost-of-distrust calculation:** If AI tools could shorten time-to-close by 1-3 months (Cotality's own estimate), and 7M+ mortgages are originated annually in the US, the 14-point trust collapse isn't just a survey finding — it's a measurable drag on the housing market. At average mortgage size ~$350K and average daily interest cost of ~$50, even a 1-month delay on trust-related AI avoidance across a fraction of the market creates billions in unnecessary carrying costs. The AI staging fraud isn't just a consumer protection problem — it's a market efficiency problem.

## Kill Test
✅ Does this help someone building or buying a home?
- Buyer learns which AI tools are useful (predictive inspection) vs which are there to mislead (staging fraud)
- Buyer learns their legal protections (CA AB 723, NY disclosure rules)
- Buyer gets actionable advice: ask for original photos, use predictive tools proactively, demand AI labeling
- Only 2 states have specific disclosure laws — 48 states have no equivalent protection
