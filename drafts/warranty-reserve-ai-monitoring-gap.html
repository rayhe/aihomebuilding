Frank "The Foreman" DeLuca July 19, 2026

Your Builder Budgets $2,348 to Fix Everything Wrong With Your New Home. One Camera System Would Save More Than That.

Somewhere in D.R. Horton's annual 10-K filing, between the $25 billion in inventory and the $31 billion in retained earnings, there's a line item that tells you exactly how much America's largest homebuilder expects to spend fixing your house after you move in. It's buried inside "accrued expenses and other liabilities," a catch-all category the company shares with legal reserves, insurance obligations, and employee benefits. You won't find it in the earnings press release. You have to dig.

Across the industry, the number works out to roughly $2,348 per home.

That's not a down payment on a repair, and it's not a deductible. That's the total. Workmanship callbacks, leaking windows, HVAC systems that rattle at 2 a.m., the crack in the foundation wall you'll notice fourteen months after closing, which is to say two months after the workmanship warranty expired, when the customer service line routes to voicemail and stays there. Everything the builder expects to pay to fix, across the life of a standard 1-2-10 warranty (one year for workmanship, two for mechanical systems, ten for structural), rolled into one actuarial estimate and disclosed to shareholders who never look at it twice.

Twenty-seven publicly traded homebuilders collectively spent $1.071 billion on warranty claims in 2024, according to WarrantyWeek, which tracks SEC warranty disclosures across U.S. manufacturing industries. That sounds large until you divide it by the number of homes those companies closed, at which point it sounds like your builder is placing a very specific bet: that your house won't need much work at all.

Over 22 years, the industry average warranty claims rate is 0.94% of product revenue. In 2024, it dropped to 0.70%. Accrual rates ran at 0.75%. Less than a penny on every dollar of revenue goes back into the product after it ships, and no other manufacturing industry with a comparable product lifecycle has a warranty rate that low. Automakers spend 2-3% of revenue on warranty obligations. Major appliance manufacturers run at 1-2%, even though a dishwasher costs a thousand dollars and is expected to last a decade, not five. Homebuilders, selling a product that costs ten to fifty times more and lasts decades longer, somehow manage with less than the dishwasher people, and nobody in the investor relations department seems troubled by the comparison.

What holds the system together is the clock.

One year for workmanship means your builder is responsible for the cabinet door that won't close, the paint that peeled, the grout that cracked, but only if you report it before month twelve. Two years for systems means the plumbing, electrical, and HVAC have an extra year of protection, and after that you're on your own with a furnace manual and a credit card. Ten years for structural sounds generous until you learn how narrowly "structural" is defined in practice: load-bearing elements that make the home unfit for habitation, a threshold so extreme that the defect essentially has to render the house uninhabitable before the builder picks up the phone. A foundation crack that's cosmetic but growing? Not structural, according to the contract. Water infiltration through a flashing detail that wasn't sealed correctly? That depends entirely on who reads the fine print, and homeowners typically discover this question at the exact moment they can least afford to litigate it.

Your warranty clock starts at closing, not at the moment the defect becomes detectable, and the most expensive defects in residential construction tend to declare themselves at year three, year five, year eight, right after the relevant coverage window closes. Moisture intrusion behind cladding that nobody sees for four years because the drywall looks fine from the inside. Insulation voids that compound energy costs so gradually you blame the utility company instead of the installer who skipped two bays in the attic. Slow foundation settlement that reveals itself as a diagonal crack creeping from the corner of a second-floor window, an inch wider every spring, until the day you call an engineer and learn that the $2,348 was never meant to cover what he's describing. Builders' actuarial model assumes the homeowner absorbs the long tail. The Hunterbrook Media investigation that interviewed sixty homeowners across sixteen states documented the same pattern repeating in every climate zone and price tier they examined, and the average homeowner spends somewhere between $10,000 and $18,000 on defect-related repairs in the first decade of ownership.

Now for the part that should make the industry uncomfortable.

AI-powered construction monitoring exists, it works, and the cost of deploying it is less per home than the warranty claims it prevents.

WINT Water Intelligence, an IoT and AI platform that detects leaks during and after construction, published data from a study comparing monitored and unmonitored sites across the same builder portfolios. The monitored sites represented 41% of those surveyed but accounted for just 11% of insurance claims: a 75% reduction in claim frequency, and the financial impact was even more lopsided, with protected sites generating only 4% of total insurance payouts and representing 90% less money flowing out per square foot built. Multiple insurance carriers now offer lower deductibles and premiums for buildings that run the system, and a few require it as a prerequisite for coverage, which tells you everything you need to know about how seriously actuaries take the data when they're the ones writing the checks.

Buildots uses hardhat-mounted cameras and computer vision to track construction progress against the BIM model in real time, catching approximately 60% of deviations that manual site walks miss, and their monthly cost runs $3,000 to $5,000 per project, not per home but per project, covering the full scope of the build from foundation to punch list. For a 100-home community constructed over eighteen months, that's $54,000 to $90,000 in total monitoring cost, which works out to $540 to $900 per unit delivered.

Compare that to the warranty reserve. If $2,348 is the expected cost per home and AI monitoring reduces claims by even 50% (half the rate WINT documented for water damage specifically), the savings are $1,174 per home against a monitoring cost of $540 to $900. The break-even math works at fifty homes. At a hundred, the builder is saving money and shipping a better product. At the scale D.R. Horton operates, roughly 90,000 closings a year, a 50% claims reduction would save over $100 million annually against a monitoring deployment cost of less than $80 million, and the margin isn't even close.

So why hasn't the industry moved?

Three reasons, and none of them will make you feel better about the house you're buying.

First, the warranty reserve is an accepted cost of doing business that nobody, not shareholders, not analysts, not the board, has incentivized builders to shrink. They've been budgeting 0.7-0.9% of revenue for warranty since WarrantyWeek started tracking in 2003, and the accrual rate hasn't changed meaningfully in twenty-two years of quarterly SEC filings, a stretch that includes a housing crash, a pandemic, a lumber price tripling, and a workforce shortage that would have broken any industry with tighter quality tolerances. It's a line item that shareholders don't scrutinize because it's small relative to revenue and stable enough not to trigger questions on the earnings call. Nobody asks whether investing in QA technology could push it lower still, because the number doesn't look like a problem from a boardroom; it only looks like a problem if you're the one living in the house, staring at a crack in the drywall and trying to remember whether you filed the warranty claim before or after the deadline.

Second, construction monitoring generates data that could become discoverable in litigation, and builders know this. Multi-Housing News, reporting on AI risk in construction in July 2026, warned that builders using AI tools "will be required as part of the discovery process to show the AI inputs and responses, including what documents or data the AI tool was trained on or used as inputs." If your AI camera system logged a framing deviation on Tuesday and the crew didn't fix it by Friday, that's a plaintiff's exhibit. Not deploying the system means not generating the evidence. The legal calculus today favors ignorance over documentation, and the industry hasn't resolved this tension because resolving it would mean rethinking liability at a structural level that neither insurers nor general counsel are prepared to touch.

Third, the labor shortage is making the break-even case stronger while simultaneously making deployment harder. NAHB's Home Builders Institute reported a 439,000-worker deficit in 2025. Fewer skilled workers means more mistakes during framing, roughing, and finishing. More mistakes means more warranty claims, which means the $2,348 reserve is under actuarial pressure from a workforce reality that the financial model hasn't absorbed. Builders are building faster with less experienced crews and budgeting the same fraction of revenue for repairs that they budgeted when the trades were fully staffed. Something in that equation is going to break, and it won't be the builder's margin.

If you're buying from a production builder, there's a check you can run yourself that takes about fifteen minutes. Every publicly traded homebuilder files a 10-K with the SEC that includes a warranty reserve note. Pull up the builder's EDGAR page, find the latest annual report, and search for "warranty." You'll find the beginning balance, the accruals for the year, the claims paid, and the ending balance. Divide the accruals by the number of homes closed (that's in the same filing), and you have the dollar amount the builder budgets to fix your house. If it's under $3,000, which it almost certainly is, that's the builder telling Wall Street it doesn't expect your home to need much fixing. You get to decide whether you're willing to take the same bet.

Then ask your sales rep whether the company uses computer-vision monitoring during construction, or IoT-based water leak detection, or any automated QA system that produces a record you could ask to see at closing. The answer, for all but a handful of builders operating in 2026, will be no. Proven technology exists. Clear math supports it. Insurance actuaries have validated the payoff with real claims data from real sites. But the industry hasn't moved because the current system, where homeowners absorb the cost of defects that surface after the warranty clock runs out, is cheaper for the builder than buying a camera.

Limitations. The $2,348-per-home figure is derived from aggregated SEC data across publicly traded builders and represents an industry average; individual builder accruals range widely (Hovnanian's claims costs rose 45% year-over-year while Toll Brothers' fell 33%), and private builders don't report warranty data at all, leaving the thousands of small and mid-size builders who close the majority of U.S. homes outside this analysis entirely. The WINT study compared participating sites against non-participating sites in the same portfolio, which introduces selection bias: builders who opt in to monitoring may already have better QA practices, inflating the apparent benefit of the technology. The 75% claims reduction figure is specific to water-related damage, which is the leading cause of construction insurance claims but not the only category of defect. Buildots' 60% detection rate is self-reported and has not been independently validated in a peer-reviewed study as of July 2026. The break-even calculation assumes consistent monitoring costs and does not account for integration, training, or the operational friction of deploying new technology on active job sites with high trade turnover. The NAHB's 439,000 worker deficit figure is a projection based on demographic trends and construction volume forecasts, not a direct headcount of unfilled positions.
