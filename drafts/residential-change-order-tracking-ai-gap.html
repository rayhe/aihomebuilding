Your Remodeler Lost $5,400 on One Kitchen Because Nobody Wrote Anything Down. The Tool That Would Have Caught It Doesn’t Exist.

By Frank DeLuca • July 24, 2026

A remodeler in the American Midwest finished a $150,000 kitchen and bathroom renovation last month. Good crew, clean site, happy clients at the walkthrough. Then his bookkeeper ran the job cost report and found $5,400 in work that was never billed. Pantry shelving the homeowner asked to reconfigure on a Tuesday morning: $900. A lighting plan revision after the fixtures had already been ordered: $1,200. Three rounds of tile layout changes plus the materials that ended up in the dumpster: $1,800. Mudroom coat hooks the wife mentioned while the trim carpenter was already there: $400. Painter touch-ups caused by all the other changes: $600. An extra outlet on the island that someone requested in a text message at 9 p.m.: $500.

Every single item started the same way: a verbal request, a nod, and then nothing. No documentation, no signature, no price discussion, no change order. The builder ate the cost because by the time anyone realized these were scope additions, the work was done and the relationship was more valuable than the argument.

That $5,400 represented 18% of the projected gross profit on the job, according to a case study published by Projul and analyzed by GO First's Grant Fuellenbach. Run the same pattern across eight to ten jobs a year, which is a modest workload for a residential remodeler, and you are looking at $25,000 to $50,000 in annual revenue that evaporates between the job site and the invoice. Not because the builder is careless. Because the system he operates in has no mechanism for catching scope changes before they become unbillable favors.

What Researchers Found When They Counted

A 2026 study published in MDPI's Buildings journal analyzed cost overrun factors across residential construction projects, disaggregated by project type. Variation orders, the industry term for change orders, were the single largest contributor to cost overruns in single-family homes at 23.5%. For comparison, the figure was 14.7% for low-rise condominiums and 10.9% for high-rise projects. Single-family construction is uniquely vulnerable because it involves fewer formal controls, more direct homeowner involvement, and a tolerance for informal communication that commercial construction abandoned decades ago.

Labor shortages ranked second at 18.3% across all residential types, and material price fluctuations contributed 10.2%, but those factors are external. A builder cannot control whether lumber prices spike or whether his best framer takes a higher-paying job. Change orders are different: they originate on the job site, in conversations between a homeowner and a contractor, and they follow a pattern so predictable that a machine learning model can identify them as the single most important variable in whether a project will exceed its budget.

A separate study, published in Emerald's Engineering, Construction and Architectural Management, tested fifteen machine learning algorithms against 836 construction projects to predict cost overruns. CatBoost, a gradient-boosting algorithm, outperformed all others. Its feature importance analysis was unambiguous: variation orders carried 41.16% of the predictive weight, more than double the next factor. Excessive quantities accounted for 21.86%, and budgeted costs contributed 20.96%. If you wanted to build a system that predicts whether a residential project will blow its budget, the single most useful input would be whether anyone is tracking the scope changes.

Nobody is.

The $5,400 Problem at Scale

Custom and semi-custom homes account for roughly 30% of new single-family housing starts, or approximately 300,000 units per year based on Census Bureau data. If the average project costs $400,000, the average cost overrun runs 15%, and change orders drive 23.5% of that overrun, then change-order-attributable waste on new single-family homes alone runs approximately $4.2 billion annually. That figure excludes production housing, where the problem is smaller per unit but multiplied across tens of thousands of starts.

Remodeling is where the numbers get uncomfortable. Harvard's Joint Center for Housing Studies pegs annual residential remodeling spending at roughly $600 billion. Industry surveys consistently show that 78% of homeowners exceed their renovation budget. Even if change orders account for only 20% of that overage, a conservative estimate given that the MDPI data puts new-construction single-family at 23.5%, the implied scope-creep waste in remodeling is on the order of $24 billion per year.

Combined, residential construction and remodeling lose roughly $28 billion annually to a problem that starts with a homeowner saying "while you're at it" and ends with a builder absorbing the cost or a dispute that poisons the relationship. Both outcomes damage the industry, both are preventable with documentation, and neither is currently being addressed by any tool designed for the residential market.

Commercial Construction Solved This Problem Years Ago

Procore, which went public in 2021 and now carries a market capitalization north of $12 billion, built its entire business on the premise that construction project data should live in one place and be accessible to everyone on the job. Its change order management module lets a superintendent log a scope change on a phone, attach a photo, route it for approval, and update the project budget before the concrete truck leaves the site. Autodesk Build, the successor to PlanGrid, does something similar at $290 per user per month, and it ties change management directly into BIM models so the cost impact of moving a wall shows up in the budget before anyone picks up a hammer.

These tools work because commercial construction uses them: the projects are large enough to justify the subscription, the contracts are formal enough to require documentation, and the legal exposure from undocumented changes is severe enough that nobody builds a hospital wing on a handshake. A general contractor running a $40 million office building would not dream of processing a scope change through a text message.

A general contractor running a $350,000 custom home does it every day.

What Residential Builders Actually Use

BuilderTrend, CoConstruct, and Projul occupy the residential project management market, and all three offer change order forms. But offering a form is not the same as catching a change. In every case, the builder has to recognize that a homeowner's request constitutes a scope addition, stop work long enough to open the app, type a description, assign a cost, and send it to the homeowner for approval before proceeding. On a job site where the drywall crew is waiting on an answer and the homeowner is standing in the kitchen pointing at a spot where they want an outlet that was not on the electrical plan, that workflow collapses. Not because the builder does not care about documentation, but because documentation is the enemy of momentum, and momentum is how residential projects stay on schedule.

What does not exist, anywhere, is a system that performs ambient scope change detection. No tool monitors the text messages between a homeowner and a builder for language that implies a scope addition. No system compares daily job-site photos against the original specification to flag work that was not in the plan. No platform parses an email thread and auto-generates a change order draft with an estimated cost impact based on the builder's own cost history. These capabilities are well within the reach of current natural language processing and computer vision models, and the underlying cost databases, RSMeans, CostCertified, Xactimate, already exist and are already used by the commercial tools.

Nobody has packaged them for a kitchen remodel.

Why the Relationship Problem Is Real

Every contractor who reads this will have the same objection, and it is a legitimate one: formalizing every verbal request into a change order will destroy the trust that keeps residential clients happy and referrals flowing. A homeowner who hires a remodeler for $150,000 expects a collaborative relationship, not a procurement process. When the homeowner says "can you move that outlet six inches to the left," and the builder's first response is to open an app and generate a $500 change order, the homeowner's perception of the builder shifts from partner to vendor, and in residential construction that shift is lethal: referrals dry up, reviews turn neutral, and the next kitchen goes to the guy who says "yeah, no problem" and eats the cost.

This is not a trivial objection. It is the reason residential construction operates the way it does. Commercial building has formal change order procedures because commercial clients are institutional buyers who understand procurement. Residential clients are families spending their savings on a renovation they have been planning for three years, and they do not experience a change order form the same way a hospital system's project manager does.

But the counterargument has a counterargument. A builder who absorbs $50,000 a year in undocumented scope changes is subsidizing his clients' spontaneity with his own margin, and at the industry's current average net profit of 5 to 6%, that subsidy is often larger than the profit itself. According to Fuellenbach's analysis, material costs are up 5 to 7% on top of already elevated post-pandemic pricing, labor is up 4% year over year with field craft averaging $36.54 per hour, and tariffs have pushed the effective rate on construction goods to 25 to 30%, a 40-year high. A builder who wants a 30% gross margin needs a 42.9% markup, not 30%, and most residential contractors have never seen that math laid out clearly enough to act on it.

In that environment, giving away $5,400 per kitchen is not generosity. It is slow insolvency.

What an AI System Would Actually Do

Picture it simply. A builder sends a text confirming a tile change. A system, running in the background on his existing phone, recognizes the language pattern as a scope modification, cross-references the original specification stored as a PDF or spreadsheet, and generates a draft change order: "Client requested substitution of 12x24 porcelain field tile with marble mosaic backsplash, approximately 18 square feet, with an estimated material delta of $840, labor delta of $360, and total impact of $1,200. Approval needed before work begins." The builder reviews it, sends it to the homeowner with one tap, and the homeowner approves or modifies before anyone picks up a trowel.

No confrontation. No awkward money conversation on the job site. Just a notification that says "this is what changed and what it costs," sent the same way the homeowner already communicates with the builder. The documentation happens in the background, the way spell-check happens in the background. You do not think about it until it catches something.

Job-site photo analysis would work on the same principle. A builder takes progress photos every day, which most already do for their own records or for lender draw requests. A computer vision model compares today's photos against yesterday's and against the original plans, flagging any work that appears outside the documented scope: a window that moved, a wall that shortened, a cabinet run that extended three feet. Each flag becomes a prompt, not an accusation, to either confirm the work was planned or generate a change order for it.

None of this technology is speculative, and every component already exists: NLP models parse construction documents today in products like Alice Technologies and OpenSpace, computer vision models compare as-built conditions to plans in products like Buildots and Doxel, and cost estimation APIs pull live pricing from databases updated weekly. They just exist separately, in products priced for commercial general contractors managing portfolios of $50 million projects, not for a remodeler in Ohio managing four kitchens and a basement.

The Math for a Builder

Assume a residential contractor runs ten projects per year with an average contract value of $200,000 and leaks 3% of revenue to undocumented change orders. That is $60,000 per year in lost billings. If an AI change order system captures even half of those lost billings, the recovery is $30,000 per year. At a subscription cost of $200 to $400 per month, the tool pays for itself in the first month and returns 6 to 12x for the year.

Why Nobody Has Built This Yet

Venture capital flows toward large addressable markets with high average contract values and clear monetization paths. Residential construction looks attractive from a distance ($600 billion in annual remodeling alone) but becomes complicated up close. Individual contractors are hard to acquire as customers, price-sensitive to subscriptions, and unlikely to adopt tools that change their workflow without immediate, visible payoff. The residential construction software market is fragmented across hundreds of small tools, none of which has achieved the kind of dominance that Procore holds in commercial. And the core product challenge of processing informal communications to detect scope changes requires integration with text messaging, email, and photo libraries in ways that raise privacy and consent questions that enterprise software does not face.

But 74% of contractors say AI matters to their business, according to industry surveys. Only 25% have done anything about it. The gap between stated interest and actual adoption is a market signal, not a contradiction. It means the tools that exist do not fit the workflow, and the tools that would fit the workflow do not exist yet.

Projul's conclusion from the case study that opened this article was five words: "Prevention is about systems, not willpower." That sentence describes the entire problem. Residential builders do not fail at change order management because they lack discipline. They fail because the system they operate in has no documentation layer that works at the speed of a job site conversation, and nobody has built one that does.

What This Means If You Are Building or Remodeling

If you are a homeowner starting a renovation, document everything yourself. Every request you make, even the ones that feel small, should be in writing: a text, an email, a note in whatever project management app your contractor uses. Not because your contractor is dishonest, but because without written records, neither of you will remember three months later whether the extra outlet was a favor or a billable addition, and that ambiguity is where disputes are born. If your contractor does not have a system for change orders, create your own. A shared Google Doc works, a running email thread works, and anything that creates a timestamp and a paper trail works.

If you are a builder, count your undocumented scope changes on your last three projects. Add up the labor and materials that went into work that was never invoiced. If the number is less than $1,000, you have unusually disciplined clients. If it is north of $5,000, which is where most honest remodelers land, you have a revenue problem that no amount of marketing will solve, because every new project carries the same leak.

If you are a software company looking at residential construction, the economics of this problem are not subtle. Twenty-eight billion dollars a year in avoidable cost overruns. A builder population that overwhelmingly wants AI tools. An existing technology stack that already does ambient document analysis, photo comparison, and cost estimation for the commercial market. The product gap is not technical but commercial: nobody has figured out how to sell a $300-per-month subscription to a contractor who is already losing $5,000 per project because he will not fill out a form.

Whoever cracks that distribution problem will own a market that does not know it exists yet.

Limitations of This Analysis

The MDPI cost overrun study draws on contractor evaluations from Thai residential construction projects. While the general pattern of change orders driving overruns is consistent across markets, the specific 23.5% figure for single-family homes may not transfer directly to U.S. conditions, where contract structures, labor markets, and regulatory environments differ. The CatBoost machine learning model that identified variation orders as the top predictor of cost overruns was trained on 836 Jordanian construction projects, not American residential work, and its feature importance rankings should be understood as directional rather than definitive for U.S. homebuilding.

Our $28 billion estimate of annual scope-creep waste combines new construction and remodeling figures using assumptions about overrun rates and change order attribution that have wide error bars. No large-scale randomized study has measured the actual effect of AI-driven change order tracking on residential cost outcomes, because the tool does not exist. The Projul case study, while detailed, describes a single project and should not be generalized to the industry without additional data.

We also acknowledge the relationship objection at full strength. Formalizing scope changes in residential construction carries real costs: slower job-site decision-making, potential friction with homeowner clients, and the risk that documentation becomes a barrier to the collaborative workflow that distinguishes residential from commercial building. An AI tool that optimizes for billing capture at the expense of client satisfaction would solve the wrong problem. Any viable solution must be invisible enough that the homeowner experiences it as professionalism, not bureaucracy.
