# Research: Homes Built Today Are Designed for Yesterday's Climate

## Angle
Building codes and HVAC design standards rely on backward-looking climate data (typically 25-year historical averages). But a home built today will stand for 50-100 years. AI-powered forward climate modeling tools reveal that many homes being constructed now will be severely undersized for cooling by mid-century. The mismatch isn't a future problem — it's baked into every permit issued today.

## Kill Test
**Does this help someone building or buying a home?** YES — directly. If you're building in Phoenix, Houston, or Miami, your HVAC system and building envelope are being designed for a climate that's already shifting. This article tells you what to ask your builder, what to demand from your HVAC contractor, and what the actual cost of climate-proofing is vs. the cost of retrofitting later.

## Key Sources

### 1. GAO Report GAO-17-3 (U.S. Government Accountability Office)
- "Selected standards-developing organizations generally have not used forward-looking climate information — such as projected rainfall rates — in design standards, building codes, and voluntary certifications and instead have relied on historical observations."
- This is the federal government saying building codes are backward-looking.
- Source: https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-17-3

### 2. EIA / Climate Central — 71% Increase in Household Cooling Demand by 2050
- U.S. EIA projects a 71% increase in household cooling demand by 2050 (referenced in AEO 2023).
- Climate Central analysis: 232 of 240 U.S. locations (97%) have experienced increasing cooling degree days (CDD) since 1970.
- Largest increases: Nevada, Arizona, Utah, Texas, Florida.
- Source: https://www.climatecentral.org/climate-matters/hotter-climate-higher-cooling-demand-2023
- EIA data: https://www.eia.gov/outlooks/aeo/

### 3. ASHRAE Climatic Design Data — Trend Acknowledgment
- ASHRAE 2021 Handbook update: "Globally there continues to be a persistent trend toward warming. This warming causes a knock-on effect: heating and cooling degree days decrease and rise, respectively, leading to changes in the classification of climate zones, resulting in code changes."
- ASHRAE uses 25-year historical averages for design conditions — explicitly backward-looking.
- 2021 edition introduced "historical trends" reporting for the first time, acknowledging rapid changes.
- Source: https://www.ashrae.org/news/ashraejournal/data-from-more-than-9-000-climate-stations-included-in-2021-handbook-chapter

### 4. Nature Communications — Inequalities in Global Residential Cooling Energy

- Academic review of cooling load increases under various climate scenarios.

### 6. Advanced Science (Wiley) — How AI Shapes Sustainable Building Design With Climate Change Challenges

### 7. Future Weather Files / EPW Morphing
- Building energy simulations use EPW (EnergyPlus Weather) files based on historical data.
- Tools exist to "morph" these files using climate projections (IPCC scenarios), creating future weather files for 2050/2080.
- Nature Scientific Data (2024) published typical and extreme weather datasets for studying building resilience to climate change and heatwaves.
- Source: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41597-024-03319-8

## Key Data Points

- **25-year lookback**: ASHRAE design temperatures use 25-year historical averages — a home built today is designed for the climate of 1999-2024, not 2026-2076.
- **71% cooling demand increase**: EIA projects 71% increase in U.S. household cooling demand by 2050.
- **97% of U.S. locations warming**: Climate Central found 232 of 240 locations have increasing CDD since 1970.
- **Building lifespan**: Average home lasts 50-100 years. A home built in 2026 will exist until 2076-2126.
- **Retrofit cost vs. upfront**: Upsizing HVAC ductwork during construction = ~$2,000-4,000 incremental. Retrofitting ductwork in a finished home = $10,000-25,000+.
- **Manual J calculations**: Standard HVAC sizing (ACCA Manual J) uses historical design temperatures. No provision for forward-looking climate adjustment.

## Skepticism / Counterarguments
- Some argue HVAC technology will improve (more efficient systems), offsetting the need for larger capacity.
- Building codes DO update every 3 years (ICC cycle), so they're not completely static — but they still use backward-looking data.
- Oversizing HVAC is already a problem (short-cycling, humidity control issues). The solution isn't just "bigger" — it's smarter design (variable-speed systems, better envelopes).
- The AI tools for this are early-stage — most residential builders don't have access to or training in future climate modeling.

## Actionable Takeaways
1. If building a new home: Ask your HVAC contractor if they sized using current or forward-looking climate data. The answer will be "current." Ask for a 15-20% cooling capacity margin.
2. If building in Sun Belt: Consider variable-speed HVAC systems that can ramp up as climate warms, rather than single-stage units sized for today.
3. Invest in envelope, not tonnage: Better insulation, low-e windows, and radiant barriers cost $3,000-8,000 upfront and reduce cooling load regardless of climate scenario.
4. Request future weather file analysis: It exists. EnergyPlus can run your home's model against 2050 projected weather. Your energy modeler (if you have one) can do this today.
5. Don't overbuild ductwork, but do oversize it: Larger ducts (one size up) cost marginally more during construction and accommodate future system upgrades.
