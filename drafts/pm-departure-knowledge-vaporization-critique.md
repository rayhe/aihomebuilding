## Critic 1: 🔍 General Editor — Score: 9.0 ✅

### Strengths
- **Exceptional cold open.** The Michael Torres scene (6:40 a.m., superintendent's trailer, $1.2M custom home in Denver) is vivid, concrete, and immediately establishes stakes. No market-size opening, no throat-clearing. The reader is mid-action from sentence one.
- **Tight narrative arc.** The structure follows a satisfying progression: anecdote → surprising data → what's actually lost (made concrete) → the cost → where knowledge lives → what could help → what you can do → honest limitations. Each section builds on the previous one without repetition.
- **Punchy micro-moments.** "All of it. Gone in three business days." / "This isn't bad luck. It's Tuesday." / "The builder ate $2,800." — these land because the longer surrounding sections have earned them. The contrast works.
- **Perfect callback closing.** "It happens to about one in seven." returns the reader to the probability calculation with a gut punch. No summary needed.

### Issues
- **Length (~2,039 words).** Exceeds the 800-1200 deep-dive target by ~800 words. The article is dense and each section earns its place, but the "What AI Project Memory Could Do" section could be tightened. The four-tool enumeration (Procore, OpenSpace, Buildots, Sitemetric) reads like a catalog — consider cutting one or two and keeping only what's needed to set up the "gap in the middle" argument. Trimming this section by ~150-200 words would bring the article closer to target without losing substance.
- **Minor pacing dip in "Where the Documentation Actually Lives."** The knowledge-channel breakdown paragraph (text 30%, email 20%, verbal 50%, with overlap caveat) is important data but reads slightly dense. The numbers overlap disclaimer, while honest, could be tightened.

### Verdict
Strong article. The hook works, the structure serves the argument, and the closing lands. Length is the main concern — trim the AI tools section if possible.

## Critic 3: ⚖️ Ethics Reviewer — Score: 9.0 ✅

### Strengths
- **No preachiness.** The article presents PM turnover as a structural reality ("It's a structural feature of the industry"), not a moral failing. PMs aren't villains for leaving. Builders aren't villains for not documenting. The framing is systemic, not judgmental.
- **Privacy concerns raised honestly.** The article flags that an AI tool reading PMs' text messages "raises legitimate questions about surveillance, especially on personal devices." It doesn't handwave this as a minor concern — it's listed as the first of three barriers, positioned as genuinely difficult.
- **Adoption paradox acknowledged without condescension.** "The PM who needs it most is the PM who is least likely to install it" is stated as an observation, not a judgment. The article doesn't mock PMs for working informally.
- **No self-congratulation about AI.** The technology section is skeptical — the tools don't exist for this market, and even if they did, privacy/adoption/visibility barriers are real. The article isn't selling a solution.

### Issues
- **Builder's efficiency counterargument could be slightly stronger.** The research file includes: "Small custom home builders argue that documentation overhead kills efficiency — they'd rather build houses than type notes." This isn't explicitly in the article, though the adoption barrier implicitly covers it. Stating it directly would strengthen the balance.
- **The opening anecdote's emotional weight is slightly one-sided.** We see the homeowner's loss in detail but not the departing PM's perspective (career growth, better opportunity, personal reasons). A one-sentence acknowledgment that departure is often rational for the PM would round it out, though this isn't strictly an ethics failure.

### Verdict
Ethically sound. Balanced treatment of a sensitive topic. No moral grandstanding, no AI hype, honest about limitations.

## Critic 6: 🔬 Research Rigor — Score: 9.0 ✅

### Strengths
- **Strong original contribution.** The article doesn't just synthesize vendor claims — it produces genuinely novel analysis:
  - The 1-in-7 mid-build PM departure probability (14.3% annual attrition × 12-month build)
  - The 78% portfolio probability for builders running 10 projects (1 − 0.857^10)
  - The $1,500-$3,750 reconstruction cost estimate from carrying costs and delay timelines
  - The 60/40 formal/informal knowledge split from the author's primary research (3-builder survey)
  - The "knowledge vaporization" framing itself — a novel concept that gives the article analytical backbone
- **Thorough limitations section.** One of the strongest in the pipeline. Explicitly acknowledges:
  - The benchmark covers all construction, not just residential
  - The cost estimate isn't from a controlled study
  - The 3-builder survey isn't representative
  - The portfolio probability assumes independence between departures (and notes this isn't realistic, since departures cluster)
  - Residential PM turnover could be higher OR lower than the commercial benchmark
- **Methodology is transparent.** The probability calculation shows its inputs (14.3% annual attrition, 12-month build window). The cost estimate shows its assumptions ($500K build, $3-5K/month carrying costs, 2-3 week delay). The informal knowledge breakdown cites its basis (3 builders). The reader can check the math.
- **Sources are traceable.** Every major claim is attributed to a named organization (Bridgit, CII, OpenSpace) with enough specificity to locate the source.

### Issues
- **The "most builds are fine" counterargument is implicit, not explicit.** The 1-in-7 stat means 6 out of 7 projects don't experience this problem. The research file notes: "The real counterargument: most builds complete without a PM change, so the risk feels academic. Until it happens to you." This framing would strengthen the article's intellectual honesty. Currently, the article treats the 1-in-7 as alarming without explicitly acknowledging the other side: that 86% of builds aren't affected.
- **The $280,000 OpenSpace figure is for commercial construction generally, not residential PM departures specifically.** The article notes this is "coordination challenges even when the team is stable" but the juxtaposition with the PM departure discussion could imply a more direct relationship than the data supports. A tighter qualifier (e.g., "even on projects where the team stays intact") would help.
- **The three-builder survey warrants a brief note on selection.** Were these three builders typical? Large or small? In one market or multiple? The Limitations section notes it's "not a representative sample" but even a sentence about who they were would add rigor.

### Verdict
The article meets every scholarly rigor requirement: original contribution, limitations, counterarguments (via AI adoption barriers), verifiability, and methodology transparency. The 1-in-7 probability calculation and the knowledge breakdown are genuine analytical contributions. The limitations section is exemplary.
