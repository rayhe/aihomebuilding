The Government Just Bet $10 Million That Robots Can Build Your House. The Private Sector Already Lost $3 Billion Trying.

HUD's new robotics-in-housing demonstration arrives after Katerra and Veev burned through billions. Factory-built homes now hold just 3% of the US single-family market, down from 7% in 1998. The survivors learned something the dead companies didn't.

Frank DeLuca July 10, 2026

In June, the U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development posted a notice that read like a recruiting flyer for the future: up to $10 million per award for companies or universities that can demonstrate how to build homes with robots and AI. The program, formally titled "Mass Market Solutions for Leveraging Robotics and AI Technologies for Home Construction Demonstration," wants applicants who can prove that factory-built housing components or fully volumetric modular homes can be manufactured faster and cheaper than conventional construction. HUD expects to pick winners by September.

I've managed residential construction schedules since before Katerra had a pitch deck. I've watched this particular movie enough times to know how the third act usually goes.

The Scorecard Nobody Wants to Read

Katerra raised more than $2 billion. SoftBank led a round. Foxconn invested. At peak valuation the company was worth $3 billion. It planned 14 distribution centers. It opened two. In June 2021, it filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy and laid off 7,500 people. Components left its Phoenix factory with manufacturing errors and came back unused. A former employee told The Information that "every day is a fire drill." A modular construction CEO who later bought Katerra's Tracy, California factory for $21.25 million described the failure as "spectacular."

Veev raised $647 million. It was a Bay Area unicorn. It shut down its U.S. operation in November 2024 after constructing 170 total units in 15 years. Scott Finfer, senior VP at KB Home, identified the fatal miscalculation in a LinkedIn post: Veev viewed itself as a supplier to the industry instead of a competitor. It built a factory when it should have been buying land and developing communities. Fifteen years and 170 units. A single mid-size production builder puts up that many in a quarter.

That's $3 billion in private capital across two companies. Both had robotic assembly lines. Both had AI-driven design tools. Both promised to revolutionize how Americans build homes. Neither got within shouting distance of profitability.

The Number That Should Be Going Up

The National Association of Home Builders tracks how many homes get built using modular or panelized methods each year. In 2024, the number was 28,000 out of 1,019,000 single-family completions. Three percent.

In 1998, it was 7%.

After a quarter century of venture capital, TED talks, glossy renderings, and at least a dozen startups promising to "disrupt construction," factory-built housing is losing market share. Not gaining it. Not holding steady. Losing. The multifamily segment fell from 7% in 2023 to 3% in 2024. NAHB's 2025 survey found that exactly 1% of single-family builders use AI to operate equipment on the job site.

The global market research firms still publish encouraging numbers. A $52 billion global market growing at 4.6% annually. Projections of $66 billion by 2030. Read those reports carefully and you'll notice they measure dollars flowing through the industry, not homes delivered or market share gained. Money in isn't the same thing as houses out.

Why It Keeps Failing

Peter Lewis, chairman of Wharton Equity Partners, explained the fundamental problem in an interview with Construction Dive: developers and contractors have "very sticky relationships with their own engineers, own suppliers, and their own construction relationships." When someone has $2 million on the line and a deadline from the lender, they call the framing crew that showed up last time. They don't cold-start a relationship with a factory in another state.

A modular construction executive put it more bluntly in the same piece: "Industrial construction is very unlike a car factory because each project is going to be different." Most modular companies have tried standardization. Pick from this one-bedroom, this two-bedroom, this studio, that's it. That has never worked. Nobody sticks to it. Homeowners want their kitchen island three feet wider. The lot slopes in a direction the factory floor plan didn't anticipate. The local inspector wants to see the framing connection detail in person, not in a photo from Ohio.

There are other headwinds. Transportation and assembly eat 15 to 20% of total project costs for volumetric modules. Building codes vary by municipality. Social stigma against "prefab" persists among buyers in the neighborhoods where margins are best. State manufacturing regulations don't always align with residential building codes. And the fundamental inflexibility of factory-built single-family designs clashes with a market that treats every house as a bespoke product.

What the Survivors Learned

Not everyone died. The companies still standing share a pattern that the dead ones missed.

Volumetric Building Companies bought Katerra's factory. They use it to build windows, cabinetry, countertops, trusses, and wall panels. Not whole houses. Components. They ship panels to job sites where local crews assemble them. The factory handles precision work that benefits from controlled conditions. The job site handles everything that requires adaptation to the specific lot, the local code, the homeowner's change orders.

Reframe Systems raised $20 million and operates something it calls "microfactories." Small, localized manufacturing facilities rather than mega-plants requiring cross-country trucking. Their first completed project was an all-electric ADU in Massachusetts, affordable enough for a low-income family without cost burden. They claim 35% cost reduction and 50% faster build times. Now they've expanded to Southern California for fire-resilient housing rebuilds, using noncombustible materials and airtight construction that factory conditions make practical. Their 20-year goal is one million housing units, and they plan to hit it by building more microfactories, not one bigger one.

Connect Housing Blocks fabricates stackable modular units with robotics at a facility in Ohio. They're focused on multifamily, where the same floor plan actually does repeat, and they've cut typical construction timelines from two years to ten months.

ABB Robotics partnered with Cosmic Buildings on mobile robotic microfactories in Los Angeles, achieving millimeter-level precision in panel production. They report a 70% time reduction and 30% cost reduction, though independent verification of those numbers remains thin.

The pattern is clear enough. The survivors stopped trying to replace the job site. They started feeding it. Components, not complete structures. Local factories, not centralized mega-plants. Multifamily and ADUs where standardization is a feature, not single-family customs where it's a bug.

What HUD's $10 Million Actually Means

The scale matters. HUD is offering up to $10 million per award, minimum $3 million. Katerra raised $2 billion. Veev raised $647 million. A single production builder spends more than $10 million on a tract development in Phoenix. This is not enough money to prove that robots can build houses at scale. It was never intended to be.

What it's intended to do, according to HousingWire's reporting, is provide federal validation. One industry executive described the funding as "a potential catalyst for increased private-sector engagement." The argument: if the federal government is writing checks for factory-built housing research, private investors may get comfortable writing larger ones. In other sectors, targeted government support has helped de-risk early-stage technologies and attract follow-on capital. Housing innovation advocates argue that construction technology deserves the same treatment, given that the nation is short somewhere between 3.8 and 7.2 million housing units, depending on who's counting.

The timing is notable for another reason. A TD Bank survey conducted at the U.S. Housing Community & Development Conference found that almost 80% of affordable housing projects were delayed, resized, or canceled in the past six months due to cost pressures. More than half of respondents cited limited government assistance as a barrier. The housing market is not gently encouraging innovation. It's generating the kind of sustained, industry-wide pain that sometimes forces adoption of things that were previously too unfamiliar to try.

What to Watch For

If you're a builder: the HUD program's September winner announcements will signal which specific technologies the federal government considers viable. Watch for panelized systems and component manufacturing over volumetric modules. The survivors' track record suggests that's where the near-term returns are. If you're running $2 to $5 million residential projects and you haven't priced factory-built wall panels against your framing crew, the economics have shifted enough in the last 18 months to warrant a call.

If you're a homeowner considering a modular or prefab home: ask the manufacturer how many completed homes they've delivered, not how many they've "designed" or "contracted." Ask whether local inspectors in your jurisdiction have approved their system before. Ask what happens when you want to move a wall. The companies that survive this next cycle will have straightforward answers to all three questions.

If you're an investor evaluating modular construction companies: the $3 billion in burned capital wasn't burned because the technology failed. Katerra's robots worked. Veev's panels were well-built. The technology was the easy part. Distribution, code compliance, customer acquisition, and the iron stubbornness of construction industry relationships were the hard parts. They still are. The companies worth watching are solving logistics and local adaptation, not inventing better factory equipment.

Twenty-five years of declining market share is a trend, not a blip. It takes more than a robot arm to reverse it.
