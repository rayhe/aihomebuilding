Eighty-Six Percent of Construction Workers Say They’re Ready for AI. The Industry Spent Twenty-One Cents Per Worker to Get Them There.

By Marcus Washington · July 21, 2026
← Back to AI Home Building

A framing carpenter in Central Florida opened his phone during a fifteen-minute break last April, found a ten-minute video about using ChatGPT to generate material takeoff lists, watched it with the sound low enough that the table saw behind him drowned out half the narration, and closed the app when the foreman whistled everybody back. According to the survey his employer filled out for a national industry study released three weeks later, he is now among the 86 percent of construction professionals who feel “somewhat or very prepared” to work with artificial intelligence on the jobsite.

That number comes from a DEWALT study published April 23, 2026, and it sounds like exactly the kind of headline that makes an industry feel good about itself. Eighty-six percent ready. Confident. Prepared for the future. Dig one layer deeper, though, and the confidence collapses under its own weight: only eight percent of those same respondents actually use AI in their day-to-day work.

Seventy-eight percentage points separate what construction workers believe about themselves and what they do. That is not a training gap. That is a fantasy.

YouTube Is the Curriculum

Where did the 86 percent learn what they think they know? The DEWALT study asked. YouTube: 40 percent. Online platforms like Coursera: 39 percent. Generic video tutorials: 42 percent. Not a single apprenticeship program, not a single NCCER-accredited curriculum, not a single community college construction management course was mentioned as a primary source of AI education in the study’s findings. DEWALT asked, and the answer was self-directed screen time.

87% of construction professionals say AI training should be embedded in trade schools and technical programs. As of July 2026, none is. — DEWALT / Stanley Black & Decker Study, April 2026

The workers themselves know this is absurd. Eighty-seven percent told DEWALT that AI training should be embedded in trade schools and technical programs, and 59 percent specifically called for hands-on, task-based training rather than the lecture-style content that dominates online platforms. They are describing an educational infrastructure that does not exist. Trade schools teach pipe fitting, conduit bending, blueprint reading, and OSHA compliance through multi-year apprenticeships that stack 8,000 or more supervised hours before a journeyman card is issued. AI training, in every practical sense, lives on an entirely separate planet: ten-minute tutorials made by software vendors who have never worn a hard hat, optimized for watch time, interrupted by ads for the very tools being reviewed.

That gap matters because construction is not an industry where knowing the concept is the same as knowing the skill. A carpenter who watches a framing video does not become a framer. An electrician who reads about conduit fill calculations does not pass the journeyman exam. Construction trades have spent a century building training systems that demand demonstrated competence before they confer credentials, and the entire AI training conversation operates as though watching a screen is the same as doing the work.

Twenty-One Cents Per Worker

DEWALT’s response to its own findings was a pilot training program with Associated Builders and Contractors Central Florida and a $75,000 commitment to ABC’s Trimmer Construction Education Fund for AI-related training grants nationwide. Seventy-five thousand dollars sounds meaningful until you set it against the scale of the problem.

ABC’s own 2026 workforce shortage analysis estimates the industry needs 349,000 additional workers this year alone. Divide DEWALT’s commitment across that need: $0.21 per worker. Twenty-one cents. Enough to buy roughly four minutes of a YouTube Premium subscription, which is, coincidentally, where most of the training actually happens.

As a share of revenue, Stanley Black & Decker posted $14.4 billion in 2025. That $75,000 AI training commitment represents 0.00052 percent of the total. This is not an investment in workforce development. It is a press release with a decimal point.

Partnerships Without Classrooms

DEWALT is not alone in converting a training crisis into a branding exercise. On March 11, 2026, OpenAI announced a partnership with North America’s Building Trades Unions to “support and expand training pathways into the skilled construction trades.” Sam Altman stood beside NABTU President Sean McGarvey. The announcement centered on ensuring that “the construction of AI-related infrastructure supports union careers” and “creates economic opportunity in the communities where this infrastructure is built.”

Read closely. This partnership is about building data centers, not about teaching electricians to use AI. OpenAI needs construction labor to pour the foundations of its compute empire, and NABTU needs to position its members as the workforce that does the pouring. Its deliverables—“policy development, project entitlement, workforce development, labor standards”—are policy language, not curriculum.

Six weeks later, Microsoft expanded its own partnership with NABTU, announcing “no-cost AI literacy courses and industry-recognized credentials” delivered through LinkedIn Learning. Microsoft had already trained 1,500 instructors through hands-on training centers, and the new initiative extended to TradesFutures, a nonprofit operating in 34 states.

8% of construction professionals use AI in their daily work. Ninety percent believe it will be indispensable within five years. — DEWALT Study, April 2026

LinkedIn Learning is a $29.99-per-month subscription product. “No-cost” means Microsoft waived the fee for union members, which is genuine access, but the platform was built for office workers learning Excel shortcuts, not for plumbers learning to interpret AI-generated pipe schematics. Construction-specific AI skills that would actually matter on a jobsite—drone photogrammetry, BIM model clash detection, AI-assisted quantity takeoff, computer vision quality inspection, point cloud interpretation from laser scanners—are not part of LinkedIn Learning’s course catalog. What Microsoft is teaching construction workers is how to use Copilot and prompt ChatGPT. Useful, perhaps. But a long way from the hands-on, task-based training that 59 percent of the workers themselves say they need.

What Trade Schools Would Actually Have to Teach

Imagine, for a moment, that an NCCER-accredited training center actually built an AI curriculum for the electrical trade. What would it look like? Not a Coursera module on “Introduction to Machine Learning.” Something a second-year apprentice electrician would use on a Thursday afternoon between conduit bending exercises and code lookup drills.

It would start with AI-assisted panel scheduling, because that is the most immediately useful application for a residential electrician: software that reads a load calculation and generates a breaker layout, and a student who can verify that the software got the 80-percent continuous load derating right instead of blindly trusting it. It would include drone-captured progress photography for rough-in documentation, because inspectors increasingly accept photographic evidence of concealed-work compliance, and the apprentice who can fly a DJI Mini, stitch a panorama, and annotate a junction box location has a career advantage that a YouTube video cannot confer.

It would require a Part 107 drone pilot certificate, which means an FAA knowledge test, which means study time and exam fees that no current apprenticeship program budgets for. It would involve reading AI-generated takeoff estimates and checking them against manual counts, because the estimation tools are getting faster but their error rates on residential work remain stubbornly high, and the person who catches the mistake is worth more than the software that made it.

None of this is theoretical. Every tool exists. Every workflow is in use at commercial scale. But transplanting them into a four-year apprenticeship program requires curriculum writers, equipment budgets, instructor certification, and coordinated adoption across hundreds of independent training centers. That is a multi-year, multi-million-dollar infrastructure project, and the industry has committed twenty-one cents per worker.

A Workforce With Nowhere to Learn

BLS projects approximately 649,300 annual openings in construction and extraction occupations through the mid-2030s, most of them replacements for retiring workers rather than new positions. ABC estimates 349,000 additional workers needed in 2026 and 456,000 in 2027. NCCER reports that 41 percent of the current construction workforce will reach retirement age by 2031. At 12.1 million total workers, that is nearly five million people walking off jobsites within five years, taking their knowledge with them and leaving behind a generation that was told AI would fill the gap.

$10.8B annual economic cost of the construction skilled-labor shortage: $2.7 billion in carrying costs, $8.1 billion in lost single-family home production (19,000 homes never built). — HBI Fall 2025 Construction Labor Market Report

The Home Builders Institute’s Fall 2025 report puts the annual economic cost of the skilled-labor shortage at $10.8 billion: $2.7 billion in higher carrying costs from stretched timelines and $8.1 billion in lost single-family production, representing 19,000 homes that were never built because there was nobody to build them. AI tools could help close part of that gap, particularly in estimation, scheduling, quality control, and documentation, but only if the people operating them know what they are doing, and right now, the training infrastructure is a YouTube playlist and a press release.

A NORC poll commissioned by Harbor Freight Tools for Schools found that 94 percent of voters believe the government should fund AI-resilient career training, and 95 percent believe more opportunities to study skilled trades in high school would better prepare students. Those numbers reflect a public that understands, intuitively, that the gap between knowing about AI and knowing how to work alongside it will define the next decade of construction. So far, the industry’s response has been to declare the workforce ready on the basis of nothing and to fund the declaration with pocket change.

An Honest Count

Here is what actually happened in the first half of 2026. Three major corporate partnerships were announced between technology companies and construction labor organizations. Total public investment commitments for construction-specific AI training: approximately $75,000 from DEWALT, an undisclosed but LinkedIn-Learning-shaped contribution from Microsoft, and zero dollars from OpenAI. Total construction workers who received formal, accredited, hands-on AI training through any of these programs: a single cohort of apprentices at ABC Central Florida’s Innovation and Technology Center, size undisclosed but described as part of a “NextGen leadership program.”

Meanwhile, 349,000 workers are needed this year. Five million will retire within five years. And 87 percent of the workers already on jobsites are asking for the one thing nobody is building: a real classroom.

Limitations

DEWALT’s study does not disclose its sample size, methodology, or margin of error in the public press materials. That 86 percent “prepared” figure may aggregate “somewhat prepared” and “very prepared” into a single number that masks widely different confidence levels. And the $0.21-per-worker calculation divides DEWALT’s $75,000 commitment by ABC’s 349,000 additional-workers-needed estimate for 2026; this is illustrative rather than a claim about per-worker spending, since the fund targets a smaller subset of ABC chapter applicants. Microsoft’s partnership may include in-kind contributions (platform access, instructor time) not captured in the dollar comparison. No comprehensive survey of trade school AI curricula exists; the absence of evidence is not the same as evidence of absence, though industry trade publications, NCCER materials, and ABC program descriptions through July 2026 contain no references to accredited AI-specific construction training modules.
