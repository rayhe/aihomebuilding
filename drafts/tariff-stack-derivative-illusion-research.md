# Research: The Tariff Stack on Your New Home's Materials

## Thesis
Everyone reported the 50% tariff on steel and aluminum. Almost nobody reported what happened underneath: the derivative product tariff rate dropped from 50% to 25%, but the base it applies to changed from the metal content of a product to the full product value. A $1,000 appliance with $200 of steel used to owe $100 in tariff. Now it owes $250. The rate went down. The duty went up. This structural change, combined with 45% Canadian lumber duties and the construction slowdown (single-family starts at an 8-month low, residential investment contracting for five straight quarters), is compressing builder margins into a zone where the math on new homes breaks for buyers and builders simultaneously.

## Key Data Points

### Current Tariff Stack on Residential Construction Materials (Jul 2026)
- **Steel & Aluminum:** 50% Section 232 tariff on commodity-grade (raw materials, coils, sheets)
- **Derivative products** (containing steel/aluminum/copper): 25% on FULL product value (appliances, HVAC, trucks, hardware)
  - OLD: 50% on metal content only → $1,000 product with $200 steel = $100 duty
  - NEW: 25% on full product value → same product = $250 duty (Cato Institute analysis, April 2026)
  - Net effect: many importers pay MORE despite lower rate
- **Copper:** 50% Section 232 tariff (wiring, pipe, plumbing fixtures)
- **Canadian softwood lumber:** 45.16% combined (35.16% anti-dumping/CVD + 10% Section 232)
- **Kitchen cabinets & vanities:** 25% (delayed from 50% to Jan 1, 2027 per Trump exec order, Jul 7 2026)
- **Upholstered furniture:** 25% (delayed from 30% to Jan 1, 2027)
- **Gypsum (drywall):** Subject to IEEPA tariffs (>50% of imports from Canada/Mexico)
- **Doors, windows, frames:** IEEPA tariff pressures (significant share from Canada/China)
- **Global baseline:** 10% under Section 122 of Trade Act of 1974

### Cost Impact Estimates
- **Brookings/Tax Policy Center:** Current tariffs add ~$30 billion to residential investment costs; 90% falls on new home construction
- **Center for American Progress:** 450,000 fewer new homes through 2030 due to tariffs
- **Yale Budget Lab:** Construction sector output could fall 4.1% over three years
- **PulteGroup CFO (Oct 2025 earnings):** ~$1,500/home increase starting 2026
- **NYC renovation firm Gallery KBNY:** Steel 50%, aluminum 50%, copper 50%, cabinets 50% (as of May 2026), Canadian lumber 45.16% — "meaningful, measurable pressure on every line item"
- **NAHB:** U.S. sawmills operating at just 64% of capacity, down steadily since 2017; will take years to ramp domestic lumber production

### Construction Slowdown Data (May-June 2026)
- **Single-family starts:** 882,000 SAAR, 8-month low, -1.9% MoM, -6.7% YoY
- **Total starts:** 1.17 million, 6-YEAR LOW, -15.4% MoM, -8.7% YoY
- **Multifamily starts:** -41.6% in a single month (486K → 284K)
- **Residential investment:** Contracted 5 straight quarters
- **Construction spending:** +0.1% MoM but -1.5% YoY (May 2026, Census Bureau)
- **New single-family spending:** -4.0% YoY
- **Builder sentiment:** Deteriorated in June (NAHB survey)
- **~60% of builders** offering incentives for 14 consecutive months
- **Mortgage rates:** ~6.5% (30-year fixed), rose 50+ bps since Iran conflict started Feb 2026
- **BMO:** "Little indication that U.S. home building will break to the upside anytime soon"
- **Lennar CEO Stuart Miller:** "Cost structure in the industry is pushing higher and is difficult to manage"

### Legal Architecture
- **Section 232 (Trade Expansion Act of 1962):** Steel, aluminum, copper, lumber — national security rationale
- **Section 122 (Trade Act of 1974):** 10% global tariff, capped at 15%, lasts 150 days unless Congress extends
- **IEEPA (International Emergency Economic Powers Act):** Broad duties on Mexico/Canada/China imports — Supreme Court invalidated use for tariffs Feb 20, 2026
- **Anti-dumping/CVD (Tariff Act of 1930):** Canadian lumber (longstanding trade dispute)
- **Executive order Jul 7, 2026:** Delayed cabinet/furniture tariff increases by one year (to Jan 1, 2027)

## Original Contribution
**The derivative tariff illusion.** Calculate the actual per-home tariff burden accounting for the base change on derivative products. A typical new home contains $15,000-$20,000 in imported appliances, HVAC equipment, and metal-containing fixtures. Under the old system (50% on metal content), the tariff on these items might total $1,500-$2,000. Under the new system (25% on full product value), the tariff totals $3,750-$5,000. The administration announced the rate went down. The bill went up. Nobody told the homebuyer.

## Counterargument (at full strength)
1. Domestic steel production capacity exists — tariffs are designed to shift production home, and once that happens, prices stabilize without import dependency
2. The kitchen cabinet/furniture tariff increase was just delayed, responding to industry and housing market pressure — this is the system working as designed
3. PulteGroup's $1,500/home estimate from Oct 2025 was conservative, and some builders have been able to absorb increases through supply chain adjustments
4. Lumber prices, despite the tariffs, are still well below their 2021-2022 peaks — the COVID lumber shock was worse
5. Builder incentives (mortgage buydowns, price cuts) are a market-clearing mechanism, not a crisis signal

## Limitations
- Per-home tariff calculations use estimated material import shares; actual import content varies dramatically by builder, region, and supply chain
- The derivative product base change affects many product categories differently; some products genuinely do pay less under the new structure
- Construction slowdown has multiple causes (mortgage rates, Iran conflict, overbuilding in the South); tariffs are one factor among several
- Sawmill capacity utilization (64%) suggests domestic supply constraints that tariffs alone cannot resolve in the near term
- The Section 122 tariff expires in 150 days unless extended; trade policy is volatile
