Your Drywall Crew Spent Five Days Mudding and Sanding. A Robot Did It in Two.

By Jake Kowalski · June 21, 2026
← All Stories

Drywall finishing is miserable work. Tape. Mud. Wait. Mud again. Wait again. Then sand everything while breathing dust that the federal government has classified as a carcinogen. Five to seven working days for a typical house, and the people willing to do it are disappearing faster than the industry can replace them.

A company called Canvas just eliminated three of those days.

Canvas, based in San Francisco, builds mobile robotic platforms that spray-apply joint compound in a single coat, let it dry overnight, then sand it smooth with a vacuum system that captures 99.9% of the airborne dust. A UR10e collaborative arm sits on a wheeled platform that fits through standard doorways, reads the room geometry with onboard sensors, locates the seams, and starts spraying without a single BIM file or 3D model upload, because the machine was designed for construction sites where nobody has time to prep digital plans for a robot that costs less to operate than the crew it assists.

"We have to build twice as much over the next 40 years, but at the same time, we have two people retiring from the industry for every one that comes in," Canvas CEO Kevin Albert told Universal Robots.

Five Days of Mud and Waiting

Here is what Level 4 drywall finishing looks like on a 2,000-square-foot house by hand. Walls and ceilings add up to roughly 8,000 to 9,500 square feet of gypsum board surface. A crew of two or three tapers works through the following sequence: tape every seam and embed it in a first coat of joint compound, then stop and wait twelve to twenty-four hours for it to dry, then apply a second coat, wider than the first, feathering the edges. Stop. Wait. A third coat, wider still, filling any remaining ridges. Stop. Wait. Sand everything to a uniform smoothness, wearing an N95 respirator if they are conscientious, a bandana if they are not.

Five to seven days minimum. Sometimes longer if humidity stalls the cure. At current rates, finishing runs $1.70 to $3.20 per square foot for Level 4, according to HomeGuide's 2026 cost data, which translates to roughly $13,600 to $30,400 for a typical single-family home's walls and ceilings at that standard, with labor eating 65 to 75 percent of the total per Angi's national estimates.

And then there is the dust.

What You Breathe

Joint compound contains crystalline silica. Sand it, and microscopic particles go airborne. Respirable crystalline silica causes silicosis, a progressive lung disease with no cure, along with lung cancer and chronic obstructive pulmonary disease. In 2016, OSHA tightened the permissible exposure limit to 50 micrograms per cubic meter over an eight-hour shift, codified in 29 CFR 1926.1153. Traditional mitigation on residential jobs means dust masks, wet sanding that slows everything down, and negative-pressure ventilation setups that most small crews skip because the equipment costs more than the bid margin.

Canvas solved this by not creating the dust in the first place. One spray pass instead of three hand-troweled coats means substantially less material to sand off. When sanding does happen, an integrated vacuum runs simultaneously, pulling particulate directly from the contact point. Result: 99.9% dust capture per the 1200CX product specifications. A human with a pole sander generates a cloud visible across the room. Almost nothing comes off the robot.

Not the Only Machine in the Room

Canvas is not alone. Okibo, a robotics company with operations in Europe and the U.S., sells the EG7, a fully autonomous finishing robot. It weighs 800 pounds, rolls on wheels, stands 27 inches wide, and navigates through standard residential doorways using AI-powered 3D scanning. It paints, applies plaster, and finishes drywall at 1,000 square feet per hour, roughly ten times the pace of a human finisher. The EG7 does not need building information models or layout drawings. No pre-programming. It scans the space, identifies the surfaces, and starts working. A quick-change system swaps between spray heads and sanding tools.

Both machines share a design philosophy: bring the automation to the job site, not the panels to a factory. This matters for residential work, where every house has slightly different wall heights, ceiling slopes, window offsets, and closet geometries that make standardized prefab panels impractical for interior finishing.

On the exterior side, Tennessee-based PaintJet has deployed the Bravo, a robot that paints building facades at fourteen times the speed of a human crew, using computer vision to control spray patterns and reduce paint waste by 25%. Its Alpha Shield coating claims twice the lifespan of conventional paint with a 9% reduction in cooling costs, though independent verification of that thermal claim is thin.

What It Actually Costs

Neither Canvas nor Okibo publishes per-unit lease or purchase pricing on their websites. Both companies operate through direct contracts with general contractors and drywall subcontractors. Canvas has told construction media that its systems are available through project-based engagements rather than outright sales, which means the cost shows up as a line item in your sub's bid rather than as capital equipment you own.

The math that matters is on the schedule side. A general contractor running a single-family residential build carries daily overhead: superintendent time, portable toilet rental, insurance accrual, construction loan interest, temporary power, tool maintenance. Estimates vary widely by market, but $500 to $1,500 per day is a reasonable range for a production home. If a robot shaves three to four days off the interior finishing phase, that is $1,500 to $6,000 in carrying costs you never incur, plus the cascading benefit of handing the house to painters three days sooner, which moves flooring up, which moves trim up, which moves final clean up, which moves certificate of occupancy up.

Giatec, the concrete sensor company, quantifies job-site delay cost at $10,000 to $15,000 per day for commercial projects. Residential numbers are lower but the principle holds. Dead days cost real money.

What Does Not Work Yet

Corners. Inside corners and outside corners remain difficult for robotic arms, and both Canvas and Okibo systems currently require human workers to finish corner beads, angles, and any junction where walls meet at non-standard geometry. On a typical residential job, corners, soffits, and ceiling transitions represent roughly 15 to 20 percent of the total finishing work. The robot handles the broad field areas. Humans handle the fussy stuff.

Stairs and split-levels create access problems for wheeled platforms. Canvas has a 12-foot reach on the 1200CX and a 15.5-foot reach on the larger 1550, but vaulted ceilings, two-story foyers, and stairwell walls still demand scaffolding or stilts. Residential architecture loves exactly the kind of vertical variation that robots handle poorly.

And there is the question nobody can answer yet: what happens when the robot misses a spot and nobody catches it until the painter sprays a flat sheen that highlights every imperfection? A Level 4 finish viewed under raking light is unforgiving. Human tapers develop an intuition for reading surfaces by shadow. Whether a camera-and-algorithm system matches that intuition over hundreds of different lighting conditions, drywall types, and compound brands is unproven at residential scale. Canvas has deployed primarily on commercial multifamily and hospitality projects, where walls receive texture or heavy paint that hides minor undulations. A smooth-finish living room wall in a $700,000 custom home is a different standard.

The Labor Problem Is Real

The UK's Construction Industry Training Board found that the country needs 250,000 additional construction workers by 2028 to meet housing targets. In 2023, more people left the British construction industry than joined it. The U.S. trajectory is similar. The Bureau of Labor Statistics projects a need for roughly 61,000 new construction laborers per year through 2033, but the industry's median age keeps climbing and apprenticeship completion rates have stagnated.

Drywall finishing sits in a particularly painful spot. It demands skill. A good taper takes years to develop. It is physically brutal on shoulders, necks, and wrists. The silica dust exposure adds a chronic health dimension that younger workers are increasingly unwilling to accept, especially when warehouse and logistics jobs pay comparably with less bodily risk. The result is a trade with an aging workforce and a recruitment pipeline that cannot keep up.

AUAR, a UK construction automation company whose portable micro-factories produce timber framing panels robotically, frames the situation bluntly. "Automation isn't replacing jobs," co-founder Mollie Claypool told CNN. "Automation is filling the gap."

That may be the most honest description of where drywall finishing robots fit right now. They do not eliminate the taper. They handle the broad, repetitive field work that consumes 80 percent of the hours and generates the worst dust exposure, freeing the human finisher to focus on detail work. On a residential project, that still means one experienced finisher on site. You just do not need two or three anymore.

Should You Care?

If you are a general contractor running production homes, track Canvas. Their 1200CX fits through residential doorways and handles Level 4 field finishing in a fraction of the time. The schedule math alone likely justifies the cost on any project where you are carrying more than $800 per day in overhead. Request a bid from a drywall sub that uses robotic finishing and compare it against your current per-square-foot rate.

If you are building a custom home and your walls are getting a Level 5 smooth finish with satin paint, wait. The robots are not there yet for that standard, and you will still need a skilled human to bring the surface to the quality that $700-per-square-foot construction demands.

If you are a homeowner who just wants to know why your builder's drywall bid came in $4,000 lower than the last quote you got, this might be why. The labor component of finishing is compressing, and the robots are one of the forces pushing it.

Limitations

Canvas has deployed primarily on commercial projects. Independent verification of the company's claims about schedule reduction and dust capture on residential-scale builds is not available in published literature. The cost comparison table above uses industry-average manual finishing rates from HomeGuide and Angi, not controlled experiments comparing robot-assisted and manual finishing on identical homes. Okibo's 1,000-square-foot-per-hour throughput figure comes from the manufacturer and has not been independently benchmarked in peer-reviewed testing. PaintJet's 9% cooling cost reduction claim for its Alpha Shield coating lacks published third-party testing data. Drywall surface area estimates assume standard interior partitioning for a roughly rectangular 2,000-square-foot floor plan. Actual surface area varies significantly with floor plan complexity, number of rooms, and ceiling height. The OSHA silica standard (29 CFR 1926.1153) applies to all construction activities involving silica exposure; whether specific drywall compound formulations exceed the PEL during typical residential sanding depends on compound composition, ventilation, and sanding method, and is not a blanket conclusion. Labor shortage data from the UK CITB is not directly transferable to U.S. markets, though BLS data confirms a parallel trend domestically.
