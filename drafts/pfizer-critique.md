## 5. ⚖️ Legal Accuracy — 8.5/10

**Rationale:** The article is careful with legal language. It attributes all factual claims about the incident to named sources (WSJ, Berman, Sammons, Mamdani). The Grace bankruptcy is stated as public record with explicit "no party has been accused of wrongdoing" language. The article does not make legal conclusions about cause. The incentive misalignment discussion is framed as personal opinion from experience, not legal finding. The policy recommendation (mandate SHM for load path modifications) is presented as a proposal, not as an interpretation of existing law.

**Issues:** The article references "the criminal investigation launched by NYC's Department of Investigation" — the DOI is an executive investigative agency, not a criminal prosecution body. If DOI has referred the case, saying "criminal investigation" is accurate; if not, "investigation" alone would be safer. This is a borderline factual precision issue. At 8.5, it passes, but tightening "criminal investigation" to "investigation" would remove any ambiguity. Also, the mention of MetroLoft "being sued over a separate conversion in Tribeca" lacks specifics (case name, court) — though the WSJ attribution provides a verifiable trail.

## 6. 🔬 Research Rigor — 9.0/10

**Rationale:** The article's original contribution — calculating SHM cost as a percentage of the $720M project loan (0.04%–0.08%) — is a genuinely novel analysis that nobody else has published. The methodology is transparent: bridge SHM cost data scaled to a 37-story building, covering specified floors 15–37, with itemized components (strain gauges, fiber-optic sensing, wireless transmission, cloud analytics, engineering interpretation). The limitations section is honest and specific: cost estimates are extrapolated from bridge data (no building conversion SHM case study exists), the failure mode hasn't been determined, and sensor placement effectiveness depends on engineering decisions. The counterargument is presented at full strength — cost burden on smaller projects, existing code review milestones. Sources are named and verifiable: arXiv Dec 2025 (Cho et al., Bayesian neural network), Nature Scientific Reports 2026 (IoT SHM architecture), CBRE June 2026 data, CommercialEdge CFI.

**Issues:** The $300K–$600K SHM cost range could benefit from more granular methodology showing how bridge data was scaled (cost per sensor × number of columns × floors). The arXiv study is on reinforced concrete, not steel — the article bridges this gap implicitly but doesn't flag the material difference as a limitation. These are minor.
