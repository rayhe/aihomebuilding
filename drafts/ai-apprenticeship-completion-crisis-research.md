# Research: AI Apprenticeship Completion Crisis

## Angle
The construction industry needs 349,000 new workers in 2026. Apprenticeship completion rates are below 35%. Now the DOL and Microsoft are bolting AI curricula onto a system that can't retain students through the existing program. Nobody has asked: if you add AI modules to a 4-year electrical apprenticeship that already loses 65% of its students, does completion go up or down?

## Kill Test
Passes — directly affects anyone building a home. Labor shortage = longer timelines, higher costs, lower quality. Builders need to know which training investments actually produce workers.

## Original Contribution
Cross-reference: DOL says <35% completion rate. DOL simultaneously launching AI curriculum for these same programs. Nobody has calculated the marginal burden of adding AI modules to already-failing retention. If a 4-year IBEW apprenticeship loses most students by year 2, adding AI training in year 3 doesn't help anyone.

Simple math: 349,000 workers needed × <35% completion = need ~1M apprenticeship starts to net 349K completions. Current registered apprenticeship system has ~600K active apprentices total. The pipeline is structurally incapable.

## Strongest Counterargument
AI training might INCREASE completion by making programs more engaging and relevant. If apprentices see AI as career advancement (higher wages, less dangerous work), retention could improve. Microsoft's free LinkedIn Learning removes the cost barrier. The DOL initiative could modernize musty curricula that bore young workers.

## Limitations
- No direct data on how AI curriculum additions affect completion rates (too new)
- Completion rate data is from 2021; more recent data may differ
- ABC model is a projection, not actual hiring numbers
- Randstad data covers all skilled trades globally, not just US residential construction
- NABTU/Microsoft partnership just launched; no outcomes data yet
