# STORY_GUIDE.md — AI Home Building Editorial Standards

## Voice & Tone

### What We Sound Like
We're a trade publication for people who build things. Not a tech blog. Not a McKinsey deck. Not a corporate communications office. We explain complex technology to smart people who have sawdust in their hair and a $500K project on the line.

### What We Don't Sound Like

**Banned phrases and patterns:**
- "Here's the thing:" / "Here's where it gets interesting" — lazy transitions. Delete.
- "The kicker:" / "The bottom line:" as section headers — find less formulaic closings.
- "Let's be clear" / "Make no mistake" — this isn't a political speech.
- "And it's not even close" — overused AI filler phrase. Banned.
- "The [industry] is at an inflection point" — meaningless. If everything's an inflection point, nothing is.
- "X isn't about Y. It's about Z." — the AI reveal formula. You get one per career. You've used it.
- "For comparison," / "To put that in perspective," starting every contrast paragraph — vary your transitions or just let the numbers speak.
- "The [noun] industry is a $X billion market" as an opening line — every AI-written article starts this way. Start with a person, a job site, a surprising number, or mid-action instead.
- Every section following the same structure (claim → data → company example → "but here's the catch") — break the pattern.
- Exactly 3 or exactly 5 items in every list — real reporting has messy counts.
- Uniform paragraph lengths — three sentences, three sentences, three sentences. Real writing has one-sentence punches and eight-sentence builds.

### What Good Writing Looks Like

**Vary sentence length dramatically.** Short punches. Then longer, more complex constructions that unfold toward a conclusion the reader doesn't see coming until they're already committed to the sentence and it's too late to look away. Then short again.

**Don't telegraph your conclusions.** Let the data surprise. If the reader knows where you're going by the second paragraph, you've failed.

**Have actual opinions.** "This tool is overhyped given the evidence" is journalism. "The market is evolving" is cowardice. We're not neutral. We're fair and evidence-based, but we have a point of view.

**Cut the throat-clearing.** Don't start with a paragraph of context-setting. Start where the story gets interesting. If your first paragraph is deletable, delete it.

**Include skepticism.** Not every AI tool works. Not every startup will survive. The graveyard of construction tech is deep — Katerra ($2B), Veev ($647M), and counting. When the evidence is thin, say so.

**Read it aloud.** If it sounds like a corporate blog post, a press release, or a consulting slide deck, burn it and start over.

## Article Template

### Structure (flexible — break this when the story demands it)
1. **Cold open** — scene, anecdote, or surprising data point. NOT market size.
2. **The problem** — what's broken, expensive, or dangerous.
3. **The technology** — what exists, who's building it, what it costs.
4. **The evidence** — data, case studies, results. Be specific.
5. **The skepticism** — what doesn't work, what's unproven, who's failed.
6. **The stakes** — what this means for the person reading this.

### Data Standards
- Every claim gets a source. Name the study, the company report, the government dataset.
- Use specific numbers, not ranges when possible. "$4,500/year" not "thousands per year."
- If a number is uncertain, say so: "ICON claims 24-hour print times; independent verification is thin."

### Length
- 400-800 words for most articles.
- 800-1200 for deep dives and investigations.
- Shorter is usually better. Cut anything that doesn't earn its place.

### Headlines
- Specific > clever. "Your Architect Missed a Code Violation. The AI Didn't." > "AI Is Transforming Code Compliance."
- Include a surprising number or comparison when possible.
- Address the reader directly ("Your," "You") — this is their money and their home.

### Dates
- Always use today's actual date. NEVER future dates.
- Format: Month DD, YYYY (e.g., "March 11, 2026")

### Hero Images
- Generate via imagine skill, landscape orientation.
- Style: realistic construction/architecture scenes, warm lighting.
- Avoid: stock photo clichés (handshakes, generic office shots, clip-art robots).
