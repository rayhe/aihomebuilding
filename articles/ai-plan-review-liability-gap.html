The AI Reviewed Your Building Plans in 30 Minutes. Nobody Knows Who Pays When It’s Wrong.

By Catherine Chen · May 28, 2026

Honolulu's building department used to spend 90 minutes reviewing a single residential plan submission. That was the fast version. The one where the reviewer didn't get pulled into a meeting and the applicant had filled out every field correctly, which almost never happened, because applicants fill out forms the way drivers fill out DMV paperwork: badly, resentfully, and with at least one critical omission. After deploying Clariti's CivCheck AI plan review tool, the same process takes 20 to 30 minutes, a 70% reduction in review cycles and a 64% cut in total review time.

Denver signed on in May 2026, with Seattle and Calgary running pilots close behind. AutoReview.AI, a University of Florida spinoff that processes plans against the entire 800-page Florida Building Code in 30 minutes flat, replaced a review process that took Altamonte Springs staff several weeks per submission; Pasco County added it for zoning and landscape review. Symbium made Bakersfield the first U.S. city with AI-powered instant permitting, handling 500 permits in six months at 10 to 15 minutes each. After the LA wildfires, Governor Newsom deployed Archistar's AI e-check software free to LA City and County, available on a statewide contract any municipality can access.

Twenty-five municipalities across three countries, and Clariti alone serves communities across 26 states. The speed gains are real, but the staffing crisis demanding them is worse.

Here is the question nobody deploying these systems has answered: when the AI reviews your building plans and misses a code violation, who pays? Not rhetorically. Whose insurance writes the check?

The Chain That Existed Before

Construction liability has been litigated for centuries. Imperfect, but knowable. Your architect carries errors and omissions insurance, typically $1 million to $5 million per claim, covering design defects that make it through their professional review. Your general contractor carries commercial general liability for workmanship failures. And if the municipality's plan examiner approved plans that violated code, the city was shielded by governmental immunity under the discretionary function doctrine, a legal framework holding that certain government decisions involve policy judgment and therefore cannot give rise to tort liability, because plans examiners were government employees making professional assessments that fell squarely under that umbrella.

Not elegant, not always fair, but everybody knew where they stood, and insurance products existed for each link in the chain.

The Chain That Doesn't Exist Yet

AI plan review breaks every link simultaneously, starting with the vendor. Clariti, AutoReview.AI, Symbium, CodeComply.Ai, Archistar: each sells software as a service. Standard SaaS end-user license agreements limit vendor liability to the fees paid during the contract term, sometimes a fixed cap, and disclaim consequential damages entirely. A $50,000 annual software contract does not cover a $2 million foundation failure. It certainly doesn't cover one caused because the AI misinterpreted a vague provision of the Florida Building Code, which is exactly the kind of provision that AutoReview.AI's own developers acknowledge is "flexible" and requires human judgment to parse. If you've ever scrolled past Terms of Service on a software product, you've seen the language. It probably wouldn't survive a motivated plaintiff's attorney in construction defect litigation, but nobody has tested it yet.

Then consider the municipality, where governmental immunity has historically protected plan review because the review involved discretionary judgment. Does an AI-generated compliance report qualify? A court could plausibly hold that the city's decision to adopt the software was discretionary, but the output itself was ministerial: a mechanical application of code to drawings, not protected by the doctrine. No court has decided this, and no state legislature has addressed it. Not one. Florida's Section 553.791 allows private providers to perform alternative plan review and inspection, requiring them to carry professional liability insurance, but AI software is not a licensed private provider, and it occupies a category that the statute never contemplated.

Then the architect: if an AI plan review tool flags a design as code-compliant, does that shift the standard of care? An architect whose plans were "approved" by the city's AI system has an argument: they reasonably relied on automated compliance confirmation, the same way they might rely on a plan examiner's stamp. Whether courts will buy it depends on whether AI compliance output gets treated as equivalent to a human professional's judgment. It almost certainly isn't, legally. But the practical effect on E&O insurers is identical: more claims, more coverage disputes, longer litigation.

The Insurance Industry Is Already Flinching

AIG, Great American, and WR Berkley have submitted requests for regulatory approval to limit their liability for claims arising from AI systems. The reason, per Insurance Business Magazine: "growing concern over the potential for multibillion-dollar claims tied to AI-related errors or harm." If approved, responsibility shifts more heavily onto the businesses deploying AI, which in the plan review case means municipalities and the architects downstream of their AI-generated approvals.

What does that mean concretely? Cities adopting AI plan review could face claims their insurers decline to cover. Architects could discover E&O policies that explicitly exclude AI-assisted errors or cap them at a fraction of the policy's face value. Some property policies already cap AI-related claims at $500,000 on a $10 million policy, according to the American Bar Association: half a million dollars on a ten million dollar policy.

Munich Re's aiSure is the only insurance product on the market specifically designed to cover AI underperformance, paying out when an AI tool fails to meet specified performance guarantees, available since 2018 and covering both vendor and user losses, though it requires individual due diligence on the AI systems involved. Munich Re declined to provide pricing details, but the scarcity speaks for itself: one product, in the entire world, for a technology being deployed across 26 states to review the structural safety of buildings where people live and sleep and raise their children.

Meanwhile, Florida Is Removing Oversight Entirely

While some jurisdictions add AI to their plan review process, Florida's legislature went the other direction entirely. House Bill 803, passed by both chambers and awaiting the governor's signature, requires local governments to waive building permits for single-family home projects valued below $7,500. Fewer permits means fewer inspections, and the Florida Association of Counties objected on those grounds, arguing the provision strips local inspection authority, while Mike Silvers from the Florida Roofing and Sheetmetal Contractors Association flagged the obvious gaming risk: contractors pricing jobs at $7,400 on paper and collecting the rest off the books.

Insurance carriers lose the permit data they rely on for claims analysis and subrogation when unpermitted work causes property damage, and in a state where hurricanes are annual and construction quality is a life-safety issue, this is not a theoretical concern.

Separately, Senate Bill 1434 restricts local authority over building on contaminated sites, and a third measure directs the Florida Building Commission to standardize permit applications statewide by July 2027. Individually, each bill is a reasonable deregulation argument, but taken together they represent a systematic reduction in the inspection and review infrastructure that insurance, liability law, and building safety have depended on for decades.

What a Builder Should Know Right Now

If you are a general contractor or custom home builder in a municipality using AI plan review, your risk profile changed and you may not know it yet. Your municipality's AI-generated plan approval is not equivalent to a human plan examiner's stamp for liability purposes, and no court has said otherwise. Insurers are working to make sure no court ever will. If you rely on an AI compliance check as confirmation that your design meets code and it doesn't, the AI vendor's EULA limits their exposure to the software subscription cost, the municipality may claim immunity, your architect may argue reliance, and you're the one standing on the jobsite when the inspector finds the problem.

So what do you do? Verify critical code compliance independently, regardless of what the AI report says. Structural, fire egress, and accessibility requirements are the three categories that AutoReview.AI's own research found most frequently violated. Ask your municipality whether their AI plan review tool is advisory or dispositive. Advisory means it's a screening tool, and the traditional human review process should still backstop it. Dispositive means the AI's output is the final word. If dispositive, ask who carries the liability.

You will likely not get a clear answer. That silence is the answer.

The Strongest Case for AI Plan Review

This article's thesis has a counterargument that deserves full weight.

The Bureau of Labor Statistics counts 147,600 building inspectors in the United States, but employment is declining and all 14,800 annual openings come from retirements and departures, not growth. The median age and required five-plus years of trade experience mean the pipeline is starving. HUD fired its entire team of building inspectors in late 2025, every last one of them, leaving no federal staff to coordinate inspections for five million housing units, some of which have gone uninspected for more than five years due to pandemic-era backlogs.

So the alternative to AI plan review is not careful, thorough human review; it is months-long backlogs, overwhelmed reviewers missing violations at higher rates than any software system, or no review at all. Clariti's Honolulu deployment suggests AI review may actually be more consistent, applying identical rules to every submission without the cognitive fatigue that builds across a reviewer's fortieth plan of the week, and an AI system that catches 90% of violations in 30 minutes may genuinely outperform a human reviewer catching 85% in 90 minutes, especially when that human has 60 more plans stacked behind the one in front of them.

That argument is probably correct, but it does not solve the liability question. Speed and accuracy improvements are worth pursuing, but they do not create a legal framework for allocating losses when the 10% of missed violations includes a structural defect that costs someone their home, and no amount of processing speed resolves the question of whose insurer pays when the automated compliance report was wrong about the load-bearing wall that failed eighteen months after occupancy.

What This Analysis Did Not Prove

No publicly available data compares AI plan review error rates against human reviewer error rates on identical plan sets, and nobody has run that study. Vendor claims of 70% time reduction are verified by municipal partners like Honolulu, but speed tells us nothing about accuracy differentials. No vendor has published its EULA terms for public scrutiny; the liability analysis here rests on standard SaaS contract structures, not specific agreements between Clariti, AutoReview.AI, or Symbium and their municipal clients. Governmental immunity varies dramatically by state, with some applying broad discretionary function protection and others narrowing it to near-irrelevance. And the insurance market for AI-related construction losses is so nascent that pricing signals barely exist: Munich Re's aiSure and Orbital Witness's London-based real estate guarantee are the only two products this research identified, neither designed specifically for construction plan review liability.

The liability gap is real, but its resolution is not. That will require a construction defect lawsuit in a municipality using AI plan review, a denied insurance claim, and an appellate decision interpreting AI output under governmental immunity doctrine. That case hasn't been filed, which means every municipality, architect, builder, and homeowner operating under AI plan review is doing so in a legal vacuum that everyone acknowledges and nobody is filling.
