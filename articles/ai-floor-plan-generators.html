You Drew Your Dream Home on a Napkin. The AI Drew It Wrong.

By Elena Vasquez · March 12, 2026

A woman in Portland typed “three bedrooms, open concept, 1,800 square feet, Craftsman style” into Maket and received, in eleven seconds, a floor plan that looked like something out of Dwell. Clean sight lines. A mudroom off the garage. Windows aligned to catch the afternoon light.

She brought it to her architect. He stared at it for about thirty seconds.

“The load-bearing wall runs through the middle of your open concept,” he said. “This bathroom has no ventilation path. And the staircase violates IRC egress width by four inches.”

She’d spent two months falling in love with a plan that would fail its first inspection.

The Tools Are Real. The Plans Are Not.

Generative AI floor plan tools have crossed from novelty to genuine consumer product. Maket, the most focused of the bunch, lets you describe a home in plain language and receive multiple layout options in seconds. Their v2 platform—currently in waitlist—promises editable, dimensioned plans generated from natural language. Over a million people used v1.

Planner 5D takes a broader approach: a full design suite with AI-assisted room arrangement, 3D walkthrough rendering, and Apple Vision Pro support. Their AI interior tools auto-furnish rooms based on style preferences. Homestyler adds AI-powered auto-planning that generates full home layouts from room counts and dimensions, plus photorealistic rendering that turns flat plans into magazine-quality visualizations.

These are not toys. The renders are beautiful. The layouts feel intuitive. People are making real decisions with them.

Which is the problem.

What the AI Doesn’t Know

Building codes. Structural loads. Soil conditions. Plumbing stack locations. Setback requirements. Fire separation distances. Egress window sizing. ADA clearances if you’re in a jurisdiction that requires them for new construction. Stair geometry that complies with IRC R311.7 (7¾-inch max riser, 10-inch min tread, 36-inch min width). Mechanical chase routes for HVAC ductwork. Header sizing over openings.

None of these consumer tools check any of that. They generate arrangements—rooms in spatial relationship to each other—not buildings. The distinction is the gap between a sketch and a set of construction documents, and it is a gap that kills projects.

Where This Actually Helps

I’ve spent enough years reviewing architectural drawings to know that most clients walk into their first meeting with an architect having no idea how to communicate what they want. They say “open concept” and mean seventeen different things. They reference Pinterest boards that mix Scandinavian minimalism with Tuscan farmhouse and expect the architect to reconcile them.

AI floor plan generators solve that problem better than anything that came before. A homeowner who’s generated twelve layout options in Maket arrives at the architect’s office with spatial vocabulary. They’ve learned, through iteration, that they actually want the kitchen facing east, not west. That the mudroom needs to be closer to the laundry. That the guest bedroom they thought they wanted is better as a study.

This is enormously valuable. It collapses weeks of schematic design exploration into an afternoon. Multiple architects I’ve spoken with—off the record, because admitting that a consumer app does something useful is still heresy in some firms—say clients who arrive with AI-generated options are faster to work with and more satisfied with the final product.

The tool isn’t replacing the architect. It’s replacing the blank page.

The Dangerous Middle

The risk is the homeowner who skips the architect entirely. Who takes the Maket output to a contractor and says “build this.” A licensed contractor in most states needs stamped construction documents to pull a permit, which should catch the problem. But in the 17 states that don’t require architect licensure for single-family residential—including Texas, where half the nation’s new homes are being built—the barrier between an AI-generated layout and a poured foundation is alarmingly thin.

I asked Maket about this. Their site disclaims the plans as “for inspiration purposes” and recommends consulting licensed professionals. Fair enough. Planner 5D’s terms similarly note their output isn’t construction documentation.

Disclaimers don’t redesign load paths.

What Comes Next

The obvious play is a tool that generates layouts and checks them against code. This is technically possible—the International Residential Code is a finite, parseable document, and several startups are already doing automated code checking for commercial projects (Archistar, Autodesk’s Forma platform, TestFit for multifamily). Nobody has shipped it for consumer residential yet. The jurisdiction fragmentation is brutal: over 20,000 local code authorities in the US, each with their own amendments.

Until someone does, the AI floor plan generator is a magnificent sketchpad connected to nothing. Draw as many rooms as you like. Just don’t pour concrete.
