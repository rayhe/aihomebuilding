You Evaluate 150 Lots a Year. An AI Just Screened 183,000. The Difference Isn’t Speed.

By Frank DeLuca · April 7, 2026

Land is the longest line on the schedule. It always has been. And nobody plans for it properly.

I have watched projects blow deadlines by six months because a land deal died at due diligence. Contamination nobody flagged during the initial screen. A utility easement cutting through the only buildable footprint. Zoning that looked residential on the county website but turned out to be conditional use, with the condition expired in 2019. Each dead deal resets the clock. On a construction loan at 7.5%, every month of reset costs real money.

According to the NAHB's 2025 Housing Market Index survey, 64% of single-family builders report lot supply as low or very low. That is down from the 76% peak in 2021, but still above anything the industry saw before 2016. Lots are harder to find. And the evaluation process has not changed in decades.

How a Lot Pipeline Actually Works

A mid-size builder doing 75 homes a year needs to close 75 lots. Funnel ratios run 10:1 to 20:1, so you are evaluating 150 to 200 parcels annually to find those 75 winners. Each evaluation takes two to six weeks: drive the parcel, pull zoning records, check flood maps, order a Phase I environmental if anything looks questionable, review soil reports where they exist, call the planning department, and then wait three to five business days for someone to tell you what they will actually approve versus what the code technically allows.

That is your land coordinator's entire job. Maybe two people if you operate across multiple counties. Methodical. Necessary. Brutally slow.

What D.R. Horton Did

In November 2025, the largest homebuilder in the United States deployed Prophetic AI across its entire operation. Across 30-plus states, the platform screened 183,000 parcels and flagged 3,000 high-potential development sites.

Prophetic's core product, ZoneAI, claims to interpret zoning codes across 20,000 U.S. municipalities with 99% accuracy. Instead of calling a planning department and waiting days for a callback, the platform tells you whether R-1 in Maricopa County allows the density you need before you have left your desk.

3,000 → 75
Gove Group went from evaluating 100–200 parcels/year to 3,000 in its first 12 months on Prophetic. That is not a faster version of the old process. It is a different business.

What Slow Evaluation Actually Costs

I ran the carrying cost math for a mid-size builder. Every lot under contract ties up capital during due diligence. At current acquisition financing rates, that capital has a measurable price.

That $213,600 is real but misleading as a headline number, because it assumes every lot's due diligence compresses uniformly from six months to one. In practice, the zoning and screening phases compress dramatically while environmental review, title work, and entitlement hearings stay on their own timeline. A more conservative estimate: AI eliminates two to three months of the front-end process, saving roughly $85,000 to $128,000 per year for a 75-lot builder.

But carrying cost is not the main story.

If you evaluate 150 parcels and close 75, you are picking from a shallow pool. If you screen 5,000 and close 75, you are selecting from a population 33 times larger. Better soil conditions. Fewer entitlement headaches. Shorter drives from your subcontractor base. Each of those factors compounds through the project schedule in ways I have seen kill margins on dozens of builds. I cannot prove the margin difference in a spreadsheet. But 20 years of managing schedules taught me this: projects that go sideways usually started with a lot someone settled for, not a lot someone chose.

Who Else Is Building This

Acres.com covers 150 million parcels with AI-powered search and zoning intelligence. Deepblocks combines zoning interpretation with financial feasibility modeling and 3D massing, so you can screen whether a parcel pencils before you ever drive it. TestFit raised $20M in Series A funding and counts 6,200 users doing site planning 30 times faster than traditional methods, connecting pro forma analysis directly to site design.

None of them publish pricing, which tells you the market has not stabilized. A builder trying to run a basic ROI calculation cannot do it without requesting a demo and negotiating. Annoying, but typical for enterprise construction software in year two.

What You Should Do

Builders closing 50+ lots per year: Request demos from Prophetic and Deepblocks. Bring your last 12 months of dead deals. Ask each platform to retroactively flag the issues that killed them. If the AI catches even a third of those failures at the screening stage, the tool pays for itself in reduced dead-deal carrying costs alone.

Smaller builders (10 to 30 homes/year): Acres.com's search interface can replace a significant amount of manual county-record pulling. Start there before committing to an enterprise platform you may not need.

Homebuyers: Ask your builder how many lots they evaluated before choosing yours. "We looked at a few options" means they settled. "We screened 400 parcels and this one scored highest on soil stability and entitlement risk" means they chose.

Why Relationships Still Win

Zoning codes are text. Municipal politics are not.

An AI can tell you a parcel is zoned R-3 with a 35-foot height limit. It cannot tell you that the planning commissioner's neighbor organized 40 residents against the last variance request on that street. A builder with three decades of relationships in one county knows which planners move fast, which soils engineers the county trusts, which neighborhoods have unrecorded covenants that will surface six weeks into entitlement. Local knowledge compounds in ways that are invisible to any database.

Use AI to generate the long list. Use your relationships to close the short list. Builders who think they can replace either one with the other will lose deals both ways.

What This Analysis Did Not Prove

Prophetic's 99% zoning accuracy claim across 20,000 municipalities is self-reported. No independent audit exists. Gove Group's 3,000-parcel throughput is a single case study from one company. My carrying cost calculations assume typical acquisition financing at 7.5%, which varies significantly by builder size, market, and lender relationship. No published peer-reviewed study compares AI-assisted land acquisition outcomes against traditional methods over a meaningful sample of projects.

And the biggest gap: I estimated the value of better lot selection from a larger screening pool, but I cannot quantify it. Someone needs to publish a dataset linking lot screening methodology to project outcomes. Until then, the selection quality argument rests on operational experience, not controlled evidence.
